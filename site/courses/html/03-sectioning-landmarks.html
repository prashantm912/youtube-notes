Module: Sectioning and Landmarks

Landmarks and sectioning elements are the map that screen readers, search engines and your future self read instead of your CSS — this module shows which container means what, and why the fabled document outline never actually existed.

Knowledge Library → Courses → HTML → Sectioning and Landmarks

What this module covers

This module is about giving a page structure that machines can read: the landmark elements (<main>, <header>, <footer>, <nav>, <aside>) that assistive technology treats as a navigable map, the sectioning-content elements (<section>, <article>, <search>, <address>) that group and label content, and the heading ranks (<h1>–<h6>, <hgroup>) that carry the real hierarchy. It ends with the single most misunderstood topic here — the document outline — and what actually determines accessible structure.

Topics

Landmark elements: main, header, footer, nav, aside

Landmarks are the top-level regions assistive technology exposes so a user can jump straight to a part of the page. <main> wraps the dominant, document-specific content and there must be only one visible <main> per page. A page-level <header> becomes the banner landmark (branding, site search, primary nav) and a page-level <footer> becomes the contentinfo landmark (copyright, site-wide links). <nav> marks a major block of navigation links — not every list of links needs one — and <aside> marks complementary content such as sidebars, pull-quotes and related links that are only tangentially connected to what surrounds them.

Why it matters: screen-reader users navigate by landmark the way sighted users scan a layout; skip them and the page is one undifferentiated blob. The subtlety interviewers love: <header> and <footer> are the banner/contentinfo landmarks only when they are not nested inside <main>, <article>, <section>, <aside> or <nav> — nested, they are just generic introductory or footer content for that section.

<body>
  <header>      <!-- banner landmark: logo + site nav -->
    <nav aria-label="Primary">…</nav>
  </header>
  <main>        <!-- one per page: the unique content -->
    <article>…</article>
    <aside aria-label="Related">…</aside>
  </main>
  <footer>…</footer>  <!-- contentinfo landmark -->
</body>

Sectioning content: section, article, search, address

<article> is a self-contained, independently distributable unit — a blog post, a product card, a comment, a forum reply — something that would still make sense if it were syndicated on its own. <section> is a generic thematic grouping used only when no more specific element fits, and it should almost always carry a heading. <search> (Baseline since 2023) wraps the controls of a search or filtering form and gives them the search landmark without a manual role="search". <address> holds contact information for its nearest <article> or the page as a whole — an author's email or a company's details — not arbitrary postal addresses in running text.

Why it matters: the most-tested question in this whole module is section vs article vs div. Ask "could this block stand alone and be reused elsewhere?" Yes → <article>; a thematic slice of a larger whole → <section>; a grouping that exists purely for styling with no semantic meaning → <div>. Choosing wrong never breaks rendering, which is precisely why interviewers use it to separate people who memorise tags from people who understand them.

<search>
  <form action="/results">
    <label for="q">Search</label>
    <input id="q" name="q" type="search">
  </form>
</search>

<article>
  <h2>Sectioning content explained</h2>
  <section><h3>When to use section</h3>…</section>
  <footer><address>By <a href="mailto:a@x.com">the author</a></address></footer>
</article>

Headings and hierarchy: h1–h6, hgroup

Heading elements <h1>–<h6> express a document's structure as six ranks, and it is the rank — not the font size — that conveys hierarchy to assistive technology and search engines. <hgroup> groups a single heading with one or more associated <p> lines (a subtitle, tagline or alternative title) so they read as one unit, while only the heading inside it counts toward the structure.

Why it matters: jumping to headings is one of the primary ways screen-reader users move through a page, so ranks must form a logical, gap-free outline — never skip from <h1> to <h3>, and in modern practice use exactly one <h1> as the page title. Because default heading sizes decrease with rank, beginners pick a level for its appearance; that is a CSS decision and choosing a rank for its size is a classic anti-pattern.

<h1>Sectioning and Landmarks</h1>
  <h2>Landmark elements</h2>
    <h3>The header trap</h3>   <!-- h2 -> h3, no gap -->
  <h2>Headings</h2>

<hgroup>
  <h1>Frankenstein</h1>
  <p>Or: The Modern Prometheus</p>  <!-- subtitle, not a heading -->
</hgroup>

The document outline: reality and accessibility

HTML once specified a "document outline algorithm" that would compute nesting depth from sectioning elements — the idea being that every <section> could restart its headings at <h1> and the browser would work out the real levels. No browser and no screen reader ever implemented it, and it was removed from the WHATWG HTML Standard in 2022. The structure users actually get is built from the flat sequence of <h1>–<h6> ranks plus the landmark roles — nothing recomputes your levels for you.

Why it matters: sectioning elements give you landmarks and machine-readable grouping, but they do not auto-adjust headings, so you must choose every rank by hand to match the visible and logical hierarchy. The practical checklist: one <h1>, no skipped ranks, give an accessible name (via aria-label or aria-labelledby) when there are several landmarks of the same type, and remember that a bare <section> only becomes a region landmark once it has such a name.

Interview questions

Most-asked sectioning and landmark questions in Indian interviews — semantic HTML and accessibility are a reliable screening round from service companies to product deep dives:

Beginner What are semantic elements and why prefer them over div?

Semantic elements name what their content is (<nav>, <article>, <main>) rather than being neutral boxes like <div>. That meaning is consumed by assistive technology (landmark navigation), search engines, reader modes and future maintainers. A <div> is a styling wrapper with no meaning; reach for it only when no semantic element fits.

Beginner Difference between <section> and <article>?

<article> is self-contained and independently reusable/syndicatable — a blog post, product card or comment. <section> is a generic thematic grouping within a larger whole and should carry a heading. Test: if it makes sense standing alone somewhere else, it is an article; if it is just one part of the surrounding page, it is a section.

Beginner Can a page have more than one <main>?

Only one visible <main> per page. You may have additional <main> elements only if all but one carry the hidden attribute (for example in a single-page app that swaps views). It holds the page's dominant, unique content — not the header, nav or footer.

Beginner What does <nav> represent, and does every set of links need one?

<nav> marks a major block of navigation — primary menus, a table of contents, pagination. Not every group of links needs it (a list of related links in a paragraph does not). A page can have several; give each an accessible name with aria-label so users can tell "Primary" from "Footer" navigation.

Beginner What is an <aside> for?

Content only indirectly related to what surrounds it — sidebars, pull-quotes, related-article lists, ad units. At page level it exposes the complementary landmark. Do not use it merely for parenthetical text inside a sentence; that stays in the normal flow.

Intermediate When should you use <section> instead of <div>?

Use <section> when the block is a distinct thematic part of the document that would logically appear in an outline and can take a heading. Use <div> when the wrapper exists only to hook CSS or JavaScript with no semantic grouping. Rule of thumb: a section almost always has a heading; if you cannot name it with one, it is probably a div.

Intermediate Does <article> have to be a news or blog article?

No. It is any independently distributable unit: a blog post, a product card in a grid, a user comment, a forum reply, an interactive widget. Articles can even nest — a post plus its comments, where each comment is its own inner <article>.

Intermediate What problem does the new <search> element solve?

It wraps the controls of a search or filtering operation and exposes the search landmark natively, replacing the old pattern of <div role="search">. Baseline across browsers since 2023. It holds the form/inputs, not the results — results belong in <main>.

Intermediate Why must you not skip heading levels, and how many <h1> should a page have?

Screen-reader users navigate by heading and rely on rank to understand nesting; a jump from <h1> to <h3> implies a missing level and breaks that mental model (WCAG 1.3.1). Modern best practice is exactly one <h1> as the page title, with ranks descending without gaps. Never choose a level for its default size — that is what CSS is for.

Intermediate Are <header> and <footer> always landmarks?

No — only when they are direct children of <body> (or a sectioning root). Then <header> is the banner and <footer> is contentinfo. Nested inside <article>, <section>, <main>, <aside> or <nav>, they are just generic introductory/footer content for that section and expose no landmark.

Intermediate What is <hgroup> and when is it useful?

It groups a single heading (<h1>–<h6>) with associated <p> content — a subtitle, tagline or alternative title — so a title and its strapline read as one unit. Only the heading contributes to the structure; the paragraphs do not become headings. Useful for article titles with subtitles.

Pro Is there a document outline algorithm? What actually determines heading structure?

No. The sectioning-based outline algorithm was never implemented by any browser or screen reader and was removed from the WHATWG standard in 2022. Accessible structure comes from the flat sequence of explicit <h1>–<h6> ranks (plus landmarks). Nesting a <section> does not demote its headings, so you must set each rank manually — an inner heading has to be, say, <h3> by hand, not a "reset" <h1>.

Pro How do HTML5 elements map to ARIA landmark roles, and why does it help users?

Implicit roles: <header> (page level) → banner, <nav> → navigation, <main> → main, <aside> → complementary, <footer> (page level) → contentinfo, <search> → search, and a named <section> → region. Screen readers list these as a jump menu, so users skip straight to navigation or main content instead of tabbing through everything. Prefer the native element over role= on a <div>.

Pro When does a <section> expose a landmark, and how do you disambiguate repeated landmarks?

A <section> is only a region landmark when it has an accessible name — via aria-label or aria-labelledby (often pointing at its heading); without one it is generic and invisible to landmark navigation. When a page repeats a landmark type — two <nav>s, several regions — give each a distinct name so assistive-technology users can tell "Primary navigation" from "Breadcrumb" and "Related products" from "Latest news".
