Gym Training Fundamentals: Purpose, Safety, and Realistic Results

Champion bodybuilder and certified trainer Yatinder Singh on why most gym-goers train wrong — and how purpose, protocol, and patience build a body that lasts.

Start with purpose, not your friend's program

Yatinder Singh — Mr. World 2015, Asian champion 2018, Champion of Champions 2022, and a certified trainer with 23 years in the fitness industry — estimates that around 60 percent of people in gyms today do not actually know why they are there. They joined because friends did, and they copy whatever their friends lift. His first rule: before stepping into a gym, define your purpose. Muscle building, weight loss, and general fitness each demand a different plan, and even "I just want to move" is a valid purpose — as long as you name it.

  • Get a coach. You spend twenty-two hours a day on everything else; give one or two to a qualified trainer, tell them your purpose, and let them structure the work. A coach also supplies motivation on the days you have none. Singh still trains under a coach at championship level and credits his mentor for everything he has achieved.
  • Expect soreness — and go back anyway. Muscles worked for the first time will hurt and stiffen. Skipping the next sessions makes the stiffness last longer; the best remedy for a sore body is returning to the workout. Push through consistently and within two to three weeks the released "happy hormones" — lower stress, better mood — make training something you look forward to.
  • Right age to start lifting: around 15–16. Before that, children should build fitness on the ground — running, cycling, and actual sports — rather than under a barbell.
  • Feeling self-conscious is normal. Everyone compares themselves to fitter people on the next treadmill. Singh's advice: accept and love the body you have today first, then improve it. Don't look left or right — focus on yourself, befriend the machines and your purpose rather than the crowd, keep the phone away, and follow your schedule without interruption. Do that, and in six months to a year you will be near your goal.

The session protocol: warm up, train, cool down

Singh insists every workout follow a three-part protocol. Skipping the first and last parts is the most common mistake he sees in younger lifters.

  1. Warm-up (10–15 minutes). Cycling, walking on the treadmill, jumping jacks — anything that tells your heart work is coming. A resting heart rate of about 72 beats per minute may need to reach 120–130 during exercise; sprinting on a treadmill at full speed without preparing the heart first is asking for trouble. Warm up until the whole body feels ready and blood is flowing to the muscles.
  2. Strength training. A beginner without a trainer can build a simple circuit of six to eight exercises covering every major muscle: lat pulldown for the back, bench press for the chest, dumbbell press for the shoulders, barbell curls, triceps pushdowns, and free squats. Run the circuit three times at 10–15 repetitions per exercise. Bodyweight is a perfectly good starting load — and progress gradually: if you lifted 2 kg today, tomorrow is not the day for 20 kg.
  3. Cool-down. Stretch the muscles you just trained to release them. Done properly, stretching helps lactic acid accumulate less and clear faster, which softens next-day soreness.

On duration: a genuinely moderate-to-high-intensity session cannot stretch much past an hour of focused work — roughly one hour ten minutes of strength plus up to 30 minutes of cardio. Someone "training" for three hours a day is training at low intensity and overtraining at the same time; a body that exhausted stops responding and results actually slow down.

Impatience is the enemy: ego lifting, injury, and real timelines

Ego lifting is training to impress rather than to progress: your gym partner of two years deadlifts 200 kg, so you — two months in — try to match it, or you chase a heavy bench press just to have a number to tell people. Singh calls it the single biggest cause of gym injuries and says most youngsters he watches in the evening are doing it. His rule: lift only as much as your body allows. If Monday's set was 50 kg, try 55 next week; maybe you manage 58. That is progressive overload — strength built gradually, backed by good eating.

He learned this the hard way. Around 2004–05, a heavy lift went wrong and damaged the discs in his spine. After surgery on five damaged discs, doctors told him he would never lift weights again, and he spent around 18 months in a wheelchair. He came back by studying — an ACSM qualification, anatomy, kinesiology — and by working with doctors, chiropractors, and physiotherapists to learn exactly which movements his spine could and could not tolerate: how deep to squat, single-leg instead of bilateral leg presses, when to hold and release breath. In 2010 he won Mr. India again with a best-improved-bodybuilder title. The lesson he draws: avoid injury if you can, but if it happens it can be managed — with professional help and a mind that refuses to give up.

How long results really take

  • No abs in three weeks, no body in three months. Reels promising six-pack abs in weeks are selling something that does not exist. Abs become visible only when body fat drops below roughly 10 percent — and dropping from, say, 18 percent takes on the order of five disciplined months.
  • Abs are 100 percent diet and 100 percent gym. Not a split — full effort on both, or the fat percentage never moves.
  • A real physique takes a minimum of three years, and up to ten. Anyone who needs a body in three to six months, Singh says bluntly, should not join a gym — because the only route that fast is illegal substances.
  • Competition prep is a lifestyle. For his own events he trains around four and a half hours a day across two sessions for 20–22 weeks, with six planned meals and daily check-ins with his coach — and even that sits on top of year-round discipline.

"Steroid is equivalent to slow poison. You will die — not all at once, but a little every day."

Yatinder Singh

Steroids deliver fast muscle, strength, and recovery — and then take back more than they gave. Stop the cycle and you fall below your natural baseline, which pushes users into repeat cycles that, in his words, end at the graveyard. Natural diet and training can build an excellent physique unless you are competing at elite level. And if someone in your gym suggests a substance, treat that as proof they are not on your side — keep your distance.

Heart attacks and the gym: what actually goes wrong

After widely reported heart-attack deaths during garba season and on gym treadmills, Singh pushes back on blaming the gym or protein. Nobody asking "is the gym dangerous?" knows those individuals' age, health issues, or lifestyle. People die daily of diabetes and road accidents; the outrage arrives only when someone collapses on a treadmill. The real failures, he argues, happen before anyone touches a machine.

  • Get a medical test before joining a gym. Stressful lives hide problems in the heart, liver, and lungs that you cannot feel. Only after a baseline test should you decide whether — and how — to train.
  • Gyms should screen at enrolment. If a member arrives without a medical test, the gym should at minimum record past history, medical conditions, and injuries before enrolling them.
  • Follow the protocol. Warm-up, training, cool-down — the same rules that prevent injury also protect the heart. No arriving and immediately sprinting at speed 12–15 on the treadmill.
  • Your lifestyle counts more than the machine. Drinking until 1 a.m. leaves the body dehydrated and under-slept; showing up at 6 a.m. to run hard on a treadmill after that is, in Singh's words, inviting your own death — and neither the treadmill nor the gym is at fault. The same applies to smoking 20 cigarettes a day and then matching a non-smoker friend's pace.

Before hard training: six to seven hours of sleep is mandatory, and if you drank the night before, do not step into the gym until your body is rehydrated. Fitness protects you only when the other 22 hours of the day cooperate.

A day of eating that supports training

Asked to define a healthy plate, Singh refuses to make it fancy: the harder a diet is to follow, the faster people quit. Everything below is available in an ordinary Indian kitchen.

Component Daily target Simple sources
Water About 3 litres The body functions well on 2.5–3 litres; the 8-litre figure pushed by influencers is overhyped
Fruit Two pieces, regularly Local fruit — apple, pomegranate, chikoo, banana, orange — no need for imported berries
Vegetables One portion greens plus salad Saag, methi, green vegetables; cucumber and kakdi as salad
Carbohydrates Three portions spread over the day Oats in the morning, rice at lunch, two home-made rotis at night — carbs are the body's fuel, like petrol in a car
Protein 0.8–1 g per kg of body weight (basic need) Moong dal, eggs, paneer or tofu, fish or chicken, a scoop of whey on busy days
Good fats About 15% of calorie requirement Flaxseed oil, fish oil, coconut oil, olive oil drizzled on salad, a single spoon of desi ghee in dal

A 90 kg person needs roughly 90 grams of protein a day just for normal body function — gym or no gym — and requirement can rise to about 1.8 g per kg depending on training. On wheat: if you have eaten roti since childhood and have no medical condition, two rotis at a sensible hour are fine — don't get lost in English-language food fears. Structure the day as three main meals plus two small ones, and when hunger pops up between meals reach for fruit or a whey shake instead of tea and coffee. Eat only as much as your body can digest; a heaped plate it cannot process helps nobody.

Timing around workouts and sleep

  • Solid pre-workout meals — two hours before. Eggs, oats, rice, paneer, potatoes, or sweet potato need that long to digest and convert to energy. Eating a banana while walking into the gym does nothing: your workout will be over before it becomes fuel.
  • Quick snack — 45 minutes before. A piece of fruit if you need something closer to the session.
  • Fat loss: train fasted in the morning. If the goal is burning fat, fasted morning cardio helps — provided the previous day ended with a good dinner and good sleep. It is not a hard rule; evening trainers can still lose weight.
  • Dinner two to two-and-a-half hours before sleep. A body busy digesting cannot enter deep sleep, and deep sleep is where growth hormone production and stress recovery happen. Sleep at 11? Eat by 9. A late sleeper who turns in past midnight can dine at around ten and still be fine.

Supplements: what's worth taking

Singh's baseline stack for anyone training with discipline is just three items: whey protein to close the protein gap, a multivitamin to cover the vegetables nobody eats enough of, and fish or flaxseed oil for good fats. Muscle-building goals can add creatine and BCAAs on top.

Whey protein

Whey is derived from milk — if drinking milk is safe, whey is safe. It is a natural, 100-percent-safe product; only the dose differs by person, from half a scoop for a lightly active homemaker to two scoops for a hard trainer, ideally under supervision. Its second job is post-workout repair: a fast-digesting protein when the body needs rebuilding. Requirements don't vanish on rest days — if travel or meetings will make you miss a meal, a shake is the sensible move. On labels: the powder should actually be protein — roughly 68–85 percent for concentrate, above 90 percent for isolate — not half protein, half sugar. One to three grams of carbs and a gram or two of fat per 100 g is nothing to fear; a clean, short label is what matters, and added BCAAs or glutamine are a bonus.

Creatine

The most-researched supplement there is, and something the body already produces itself. For workout quality, muscle, and strength, 3–5 grams a day is the right range depending on training intensity — keep doses average and sustainable rather than loading 5–10 g. Drink extra water while using it, keep training, and keep the diet good.

Pre-workout drinks

Beginners do not need them — food is more than enough. At intermediate and advanced levels, where the real need is focus rather than energy, a pre-workout taken 15–20 minutes before the session can help. Choose one with around 200 mg of caffeine (the prescribed dose) rather than 350 mg, ideally with L-glutamine and the BCAAs leucine, isoleucine, and valine: that profile gives constant energy instead of an instant spike. A formula that turns your ears red and sets your skin tingling is not a sign it is "working" — it will disturb your training, and it is not a good product. Because caffeine disturbs sleep, take pre-workout early in the day; beginners and intermediates should avoid it after 5–6 p.m.

Intra-workout drinks

The colourful sippers young lifters carry are mostly fashion. For beginners, plain water — or water with a pinch of Himalayan salt and a little honey — is more than enough. Heavy intermediate-and-above trainers can use a flavoured drink mid-session, preferably one supplying essential or branched-chain amino acids, which support muscle recovery and growth when demand is high.

After 40: the brisk-walk plan

Many over-40s say "we walk" — and mean strolling. Strolling is not physical activity, Singh says; it is pleasant morning air. The shift that matters is to the brisk walk, which he calls the world's best exercise: it needs nothing but you and some music you enjoy.

  • The talk test. Walking with a partner? Agree not to talk for 40 minutes. To check your pace, try a normal conversation: if you can chat comfortably, speed up. You should be breathing hard.
  • Make it a habit: 40–45 minutes, early morning on an empty stomach if possible.
  • Add breath and flexibility. Yoga once or twice a week — even as a beginner, even with a trainer — teaches control over breathing, which he considers today's most needed skill. Swimming is an excellent full-body option.
  • Add light weights. A little resistance work, even at home, keeps muscles strong so they can support weakening joints in later years — the difference between struggling and moving freely.

Together that covers heart health, flexibility, muscle strength, and breathing — a holistic plan. But it only works if eating changes with it: samosas, pakoras, and parathas will undo it. Shift toward green vegetables and fruit, in balanced portions the body can actually digest.

Myths vs. facts

"Muscle turns to fat when you stop strength training"

Myth. Muscle never converts to fat, and fat never converts to muscle. Muscle actually keeps your metabolism better. Stop training and you lose some muscle, but muscle memory means you rebuild strength far faster than a beginner when you return.

"Only morning exercise keeps you fit"

Myth. Fitness is not tied to the clock.

"Never exercise more than an hour"

Partly true. Trained with full focus — no phone, no chatting — a session can barely exceed an hour anyway, though it may stretch to about an hour and twenty minutes.

"You must fast before cardio"

Depends. It is not compulsory. Fasted cardio is suggested specifically when the goal is lowering body-fat percentage.

"Only heavy weights build a body"

Myth. Wrong outright — load must match your level and build gradually.

"People who work out shouldn't masturbate"

Myth. There is no connection between masturbation and workout results — though Singh suggests youngsters chasing muscle keep self-control.

"Heavy lifting stops height growth"

Myth. Height is overwhelmingly about genetics. If you are due to grow, lifting won't stop it; if you are not, no exercise choice was the cause.

"Which split is best for muscle building — PPL or something else?"

Answer. For building muscle, Singh favours a single-muscle-group split — chest, back, shoulders, legs on separate days, about 45 minutes per muscle without overtraining it. After around three months, progress to pairing muscles (chest with triceps, back with biceps), ideally guided by a coach who watches your progress.
