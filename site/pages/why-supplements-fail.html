Why Most Supplements Fail: The Truth Behind the Labels

Formulator Palak Midha spends up to two months and several textbooks building a single supplement. Her argument is blunt: most products that disappoint were never dosed, formulated or sourced to work in the first place — and the label rarely tells you which is which.

The label game: fillers, dose and price

The most common reason a supplement does nothing is that it barely contains anything. A single capsule can hold up to about 650 mg — and a larger "00" or "000" size can carry up to roughly 1,600 mg — yet many makers put in only 100 to 200 mg and pad the rest with filler. The commercial logic is that one under-dosed ingredient per pill lets a brand sell ten different bottles instead of one that works, which is also why people end up swallowing ten to fifteen supplements a day.

Dose is the variable clinical research actually studies. There is published work on the specific dosages at which, for example, liver cells begin to regenerate, and a serious formulator matches those trial dosages rather than guessing. Because a competitor can copy a printed label in a minute but cannot copy the underlying dose research, she sometimes writes "proprietary blend" on the back to protect it — while still giving the exact dosage to any customer who asks.

Cheapest is a warning sign. Quality actives, absorption enhancers, standardisation and heavy-metal testing all cost money, so a genuinely good supplement is not the cheapest on the shelf. Two failures compound the problem: buyers shop on price and self-prescribe, and many doctors and dietitians never deep-dive into the therapeutic dosing of a supplement either. Treat what goes into your body as something closer to sacred than to a bargain purchase.

The right form of every nutrient

After dose, the second biggest reason a supplement fails is the wrong chemical form. The body absorbs some forms directly and struggles with others, and if your gut cannot perform the needed conversion, a cheaper form simply passes through. The rule she gives is to choose the bioactive, directly usable form even when it costs more and takes longer to make.

  • Vitamin A — retinyl palmitate over beta-carotene. Beta-carotene needs a gut enzyme to convert into the usable form; with certain gut issues that conversion never happens, so the pre-converted form is given instead.
  • B12 — methylcobalamin, the directly absorbable form rather than plain B12.
  • B9 — folate, not folic acid. Folate is a methyl donor that lowers homocysteine and supports heart health.
  • Selenium — selenomethionine, not selenate.
  • Thiamine — benfotiamine, a form she notes is hard to source in India.
  • Alpha-lipoic acid — the stabilised R-form (R-ALA) rather than ordinary ALA. Extracting it in a stable form took her team six months.

She reports being criticised for these choices — asked, for instance, why she uses retinyl palmitate instead of beta-carotene — and answers that she deliberately delivers the form the body can use without a conversion step it may not be able to perform.

Herb quality: which part, which extraction, which contaminants

A herb with strong clinical backing can still fail if it is sourced carelessly. Ashwagandha is the example: across 26 human clinical trials it is described as lowering cortisol and raising testosterone by around 17%. But the leaf contains toxic alkaloids, so an extract must use the root only — and the common mistake is reading about ashwagandha and then buying the cheapest plain extract. Her products use a branded, standardised ashwagandha (KSM-66) rather than a generic one.

When shortlisting a supplier, she asks two questions first. What is the extraction process — is it methanol or alcohol-based, or the cleaner water, CO₂ and spray-drying route? And will they run heavy-metal testing? Beyond that, she checks the active percentage: with turmeric, curcumin is the active component, so its percentage is what matters, not the raw weight.

Heavy metals are not a fringe worry. A 2022 FSSAI report is cited as finding that 70% of supplements on the Indian market carried heavy metals. A reputable brand will have heavy-metal reports for its actives available to view. If you are learning to buy well, the first habit is to know which part of a herb should be used and to educate yourself on the active you are actually taking.

Gut supplements: probiotics, prebiotics and postbiotics

Probiotics dominate the market but are treated here as a double-edged sword: no single probiotic works for everyone. She cites a study in which participants taking a yogurt-based probiotic gained weight over eight weeks, and points out that yogurt itself is not universal — it fails people with histamine or lactose intolerance. Her own preference runs to prebiotics and postbiotics.

Prebiotics mean feeding your own unique bacterial colony with fibre. Ordinary food — dal, rice, roti — supplies that fibre, but fibre backfires for some people; she references a book called The Fiber Myth and notes their clinic runs a genetic test that flags people sensitive to excess fibre as a gene. Where fibre bloats, a pre-digested prebiotic fibre can grow the colony first, after which fibre is reintroduced slowly — described as fertiliser for the bacteria. A cheaper route to the same resistant starch is cooling cooked rice or potato before eating. The working method is a process of elimination: two steps forward, one step back.

Postbiotics answer a further question — whether a probiotic ever reaches the colon at all, and in what spore form. Postbiotics are the metabolites that probiotics produce, so taking them delivers the end product directly, the same logic as choosing a pre-converted vitamin form. Examples she works with include butyric acid (a short-chain fatty acid found in butter), the longevity molecule C15 (which she wanted to launch but found too expensive, noting only about 0.2% is needed and that butter is a source), and raw cacao folded into a protein shake in place of a synthetic chocolate flavour.

The liver: detox supplements that hold up

Liver support is described as her company's best seller, and the claim behind it is specific rather than vague. She reports reversing her mother's third-grade fatty liver, with liver enzymes now normal. The formulation pairs NAC (N-acetyl cysteine) with milk thistle; she cites studies of over 600 participants with fatty-liver issues who, given NAC and milk thistle over three months on a controlled diet, saw liver enzymes normalise and fatty liver improve considerably on ultrasound. In a more extreme case, high-dose NAC is credited with bringing patients back from liver failure without a transplant — relevant because excess paracetamol can drive liver toxicity and failure.

Supplements are only part of a detox protocol built to relieve a high toxic load, much of it now indoor: carpets, paint, furniture, air fresheners and scented candles.

  • Infrared sauna. Framed as non-negotiable and traced to firefighters exposed to intense toxicity at the World Trade Center attack, treated by a detox specialist. Compared with a normal sauna, infrared sauna sweat is said to excrete far more heavy metals — she cites six to ten times as much. Her routine is around 20 minutes; she notes a longer protocol of 30 minutes in, a 10–15 minute cool-down, then another 30 minutes, twice a week, for heat-shock proteins and cellular rejuvenation.
  • Charcoal, inside the sauna. Two medical-grade charcoal capsules (from coconut shell) bind toxins as they mobilise so they are not reabsorbed, which matters because detox pathways get compromised with age. Sugar-free electrolytes go alongside, since sweat also loses electrolytes.
  • Contrast therapy. An ice bath in the cool-down window can accelerate fat loss and is described as producing a strong skin flush afterward.
  • Cruciferous vegetables. Cauliflower, broccoli, cabbage and brussels sprouts supply DIM and I3C, which — combined with calcium D-glucarate — support estrogen balance. Estrogen dominance is flagged as common when the liver is overloaded, and is noted in around 80% of breast-cancer patients.

For antioxidant load she points to a "Stop Aging" formula: two capsules said to carry the resveratrol of about 15 glasses of wine, the quercetin of about 20 apples and the EGCG of about 26 cups of green tea. The resveratrol is specifically trans-resveratrol at 250 mg — described as roughly 96% more bioactive than ordinary resveratrol because it is a linear molecule, and the form used across the longevity clinical studies.

Thyroid caution on cruciferous vegetables: people with thyroid conditions need not cut them out. The guidance offered is up to three times a week, paired with iodine — ideally guided by an iodine patch test — taken as a supplement such as sea kelp. The strong reversal claims for fatty liver and liver failure are presented from clinical experience, not as guaranteed outcomes; both hosts stress consulting your own physician before self-prescribing.

Strategic versus lifelong: how long to take a supplement

A supplement failing can also mean using it wrong — taking a short-term tool forever, or dropping a lifelong need too soon. The distinction she draws is between supplements the body needs continuously and those introduced strategically until the body can take over.

Some are for life. Omega-3 is the example: the body needs it daily, and getting it purely from fish carries heavy-metal toxicity, so a clean supplement makes sense long-term. She stresses EPA and DHA specifically, noting a newborn draws DHA out of the mother's brain for its development — a roughly 2% loss linked to why many new mothers feel low and foggy, and a reason to supplement across multiple pregnancies. An ideal omega-6 to omega-3 ratio is given as about 3:1, with 16:1 described as acceptable in India; her son once tested at 133:1, after which a targeted omega-3 corrected it.

Others are strategic. Ox bile can be given while the body relearns to use fat for energy, then weaned off; postbiotics can be taken for a period until your own system kicks in. The principle is to change the lifestyle, get the body ready, and then step off the supplement rather than depend on it indefinitely.

Supplements that are mostly hype

Asked which products are effectively a scam, she names several, and separates genuine molecules from their marketing.

Fat burners

Scam. Listed first among products that do not deliver what they promise.

Hair gummies

Scam. A gummy holds very little active — she was told a gummy could carry only about 150 mg, versus the 1,600 mg she would give a client. That is enough for a children's multivitamin, not a therapeutic hair dose.

Collagen shots and supplement "shots"

Skip the format. Collagen is tasteless and can simply be stirred into tea; paying for a "shot" adds no value.

Probiotics, as generally sold

Overhyped. They should be matched to the individual through testing. Without that, fermented foods — sauerkraut, kimchi, fermented cucumber — are the simpler route, with the same caution that fermented foods fail people with histamine intolerance.

BCAAs

Upgrade instead. She favours essential amino acids (EAAs) combined with L-glutamine and creatine — with no artificial sweetener or colour — as the mix that actually helps build the body.

Shilajit

Real molecule, misleading marketing. Its value lies in humic and fulvic trace minerals once supplied by well water, but it has been marketed like Viagra. It can raise male testosterone and libido over about 90 days, yet if zinc and vitamin D are low, addressing shilajit before the root cause will not work. She prefers Ionicplex — a patented trace-mineral complex from an Arizona company that isolated the minerals shilajit carries — and plans to use it, not shilajit, in a testosterone formula.

Melatonin

Fact, at a low dose. She does not take it herself with a high sleep score, but points to research on melatonin helping rejuvenate stem cells in the kidney, and to a functional-medicine physician who keeps her parents on it. The emphasis is a very low dose — around a milligram, with her own reference nearer 2 to 3 mg.

Smart protein, not just more protein

Protein is where she tells the unmotivated to start, since a deficiency can blunt the "happy hormones" and neurotransmitters built from amino acids — but the form and pairing still decide whether it helps or backfires.

  • Do not take whey alone. On unregulated blood sugar, plain whey can drive a large insulin spike and more cravings and weight gain across the day. Combining it with seeds, a spoon of MCT oil or powder, or a bowl of vegetables and fibre blunts that effect. Straight whey suits bodybuilders eating in a calorie surplus throughout the day, not everyone.
  • Choose complete, bioavailable sources. Vegetable protein is lower and comes in a non-heme, less bioavailable form; if you eat non-vegetarian food, a complete amino acid profile is easier to reach. Red meat — lamb is her first preference — is called especially helpful for menstruating women.
  • Eggs as the safe default. With whey unreliable and chicken carrying hormones, eggs are named the safest protein source in India — she suggests at least four a day, up to eight to ten, delivering choline, fat and protein together so there is no insulin spike.
  • With age, digest it better. If movement is good, the body synthesises and digests less protein, so a digestive enzyme taken with protein can make a noticeable difference.

The same integrated build shows up in her thyroid formula, offered as an example of dose-and-form done deliberately: ashwagandha for cortisol, L-tyrosine, two forms of iodine, sea kelp, benfotiamine and a zinc-to-copper ratio of one to ten — the kind of researched combination a single cheap extract cannot match.
