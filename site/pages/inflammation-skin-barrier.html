Inflammation and Your Skin: How Pollution, Water and Diet Damage the Barrier — and How to Defend It

Dermatologist and aesthetic expert Dr. Rashmi Shetty traces most modern skin and hair trouble — irritated cheeks, hair fall, pigmentation, acne — back to one root: inflammation from outside aggressors and from what we eat, and shows how simple barrier care beats expensive fixes.

At a glance

  • Skin is the body's largest organ and its largest defence barrier. The bare minimum it deserves is a basic routine: a good moisturizer, a reliable sunscreen and an effective lip balm.
  • Pollution harms skin two ways — particulates irritate it from the outside, and finer particles penetrate to trigger oxidative stress, inflammation and DNA change. A moisturizer is your physical shield; washing it all off at day's end completes the defence.
  • Hard water, dirty scalps, wrong protein supplements and helmet sweat all feed inflammation around hair follicles — a major, overlooked driver of hair fall alongside genetics.
  • Sunscreen matters even indoors and even for brown skin: aim around SPF 50, a two-finger quantity, reapplied up to three times a day, with sticks or powders for sweaty commutes.
  • What goes in shows up on the skin: clean water, colourful fruit and vegetables, less sugar, and good sleep — poor sleep raises cortisol and can push you to eat up to 900 calories more the next day.
  • Beware unlabelled "fairness" creams sold in little tubs: the chalky, pearl-white ones are usually steroids, and they thin the skin, destroy the barrier and cause lasting damage.
  • For a wedding or big event, start with a dermatologist three to six months ahead. With only ten days left, never experiment with anything new — there is no time to correct a reaction.

How pollution inflames the skin — outside and in

Understand what pollution does, and the protective steps become obvious. Particulate pollution — the kind that hangs over Delhi or any dusty city — works on the skin in two ways. First, the particulates themselves irritate the surface, which can produce irritant contact dermatitis. Second, the finer particles penetrate through the skin, where they trigger oxidative stress, inflammation and DNA change.

The defence follows directly from the mechanism:

  • Block it physically. A mask is the best option. If you will not wear one, apply a good moisturizer — it becomes a barrier between the dust and the skin, and keeps the skin barrier itself intact so less penetrates.
  • Layer in the daytime. Modern sunscreens often double up with moisturizer and antioxidants, covering sun and pollution defence in one product.
  • Wash it all off. At the end of the day, remove everything. On a field job with heavy pollution, don't wait — wash multiple times through the day and reapply moisturizer each time. Sunscreen needs reapplying; so does the moisturizer it drains into.
  • Support from within. Good nutrition helps the skin fight the damage; a good antioxidant and anti-inflammatory supplement can be added support.

Children are not spared

Dermatologists now see far more atopic dermatitis in children than before. Dr. Shetty attributes the rise to two triggers: external pollution, and what she calls internal pollution — processed, nutrition-poor food. Children who use no skin care at all still arrive with red, irritated cheeks and a rising load of allergies and irritant dermatitis.

Sunscreen: the rules that actually matter

Indian skin carries protective pigment — a boon that shields against severe sun damage such as cancers — but that same melanin darkens readily in sunlight. Sun exposure also drives oxidative stress and speeds ageing: it cracks collagen and hardens elastin, making skin look tougher. Hence sunscreen, every day the sun is up.

Why indoors counts too

We live under harsh lights, in front of computers and phones emitting blue light, beside large windows — and India's sun is up early and sets late in every season. Wherever you are, if the sun is up, wear your sunscreen.

The practical rules

  1. SPF around 50. Ten points more adds no meaningful protection; a lot less may protect too little.
  2. Reapply. Three times a day is ideal — early morning, lunchtime and coffee time. If not three, at least twice.
  3. Use enough. A tiny dab of SPF 50 will not help. Apply at least a two-finger length.
  4. Sweat-proof the habit. In India's humid heat, sunscreen powders and sticks suit commuters and heavy sweaters better than creams alone.
  5. Pair with internal support. Antioxidant and anti-inflammatory supplements act as an "internal sunscreen" that backs up the topical one.

Most misused product. Asked which skin care product Indians overuse incorrectly, Dr. Shetty's rapid-fire answer was sunscreen itself — owning it is not the same as using enough of it, often enough.

Hard water and hair fall: the follicle-inflammation link

What hard water does

Fact. Hard water genuinely damages skin and hair. It is laden with minerals unfriendly to both; the irritation it causes sets off inflammation, which leads to hair fall, redness, barrier damage, eventual pigmentation, and can trigger acne.

Fixes that do not require an expensive filter:

  • Fill a bucket at night and let it stand — most minerals settle, and you use the top of the water.
  • Water-softening tablets dropped into a bucket absorb the hard minerals.
  • Simple spout-fitting filters (easily bought online) — Dr. Shetty recommends these to patients travelling to hard-water cities such as London.
  • At minimum, wash your face and scalp with drinking water from a can.

Why hair is thinning in the 20s and 30s

Stress, diet and products all contribute — and so does heredity, showing up differently than people expect:

  • Genetics arrive early now. Dr. Shetty sees genetic patterns like irritated skin and hair fall appearing in this generation at least ten years earlier than in their parents. And fathers and uncles often simply never noticed their own early shedding — there was no Instagram trained on their hairline.
  • Look at the maternal side. Androgenic alopecia is mostly carried from the mother's side — check the men in your mother's family, not just your father.
  • Excess or wrong protein. Protein that is not digested properly, or the wrong protein, is inflammatory and contributes to hair fall.
  • Perifollicular inflammation. Hair fall is not just genetics — it is also the inflammation around each follicle. A dirty, unwashed, yeast-laden scalp with dandruff drives exactly this inflammation.
  • Changed habits. Men of the previous generation washed their hair with every bath. Today's men skip washes (misled by the myth that washing causes hair fall) while adding styling gels and heat their fathers never used.

Daily riders, mind your helmet. Helmets hold sweat against the scalp, and most people never clean the inner sponge. Wash your hair every single day so no inflammation builds around the follicles; if you oil your hair, wash it off before long hours in a helmet, or folliculitis — little pimples on the scalp — can follow. Wash your face and apply a neat moisturizer before putting the helmet on, so you start sweat-free with a barrier between the sponge and your skin.

Feeding the skin from within

Dr. Shetty jokes that she has collected one-star reviews for talking about weight, eating patterns and inflammatory diets when patients came in for skin — but everything on the skin and hair comes from within. The skin, she says, is a tool for diagnosing what is happening inside. The daily checklist from the conversation's nutritionist co-host, Ryan, endorsed by Dr. Shetty:

  1. Hydration first. Better-quality water, with no sugar and no caffeine in it. The cleaner the water, the better the skin.
  2. Eat colour. Fruits and vegetables that do not come in a packet — and not just tomato and potato. Research shows their polyphenols and flavonoids protect the skin; he calls them nature's natural internal sunscreen.
  3. Avoid sugar. Processed foods are loaded with it, and even restaurant staples like idli, sambar and curries get excess sugar and salt added.
  4. Prefer organic where possible. Insecticides and pesticides in the food chain force the liver to work overtime on top of sugar and empty calories — and the skin is the organ that takes the beating.
  5. Portion control is step two after eating cleaner.
  6. Sleep is the new diet. Research shows less sleep means higher cortisol and anxiety for the skin the next day — and can force you to eat up to 900 calories more. Dr. Shetty adds that sleep is when collagen repair happens.

Getting off sugar without suffering

Cutting sugar is partly a psychological battle of roughly 12 to 15 days. The nutritionist's toolkit: stevia and allulose are the only sweeteners he is comfortable with, used only for a two-to-three-week transition while the palate adjusts (taste buds are programmable — in his experience, recalibration takes around 21 to 75 days). Magnesium supplementation helps because electrolyte and mineral imbalances themselves drive sugar cravings; peppermint and chewing gum blunt cravings; and the ancient Indian herb gymnema sylvestre (mishri shringi) reduces the craving for sweetness. Dr. Shetty adds that bitter or pungent foods also cut sugar cravings.

Supplements the experts lean on

  • Antioxidants and anti-inflammatories are the starting point, because oxidative damage alters DNA and causes inflammation. Molecules named: resveratrol, glutathione and its precursors, zeaxanthin, astaxanthin, coenzyme Q10 and curcumin.
  • Vitamin C — regular supplements are ascorbic acid and can cause gastric reflux with daily use. Dr. Shetty formulated her own from berry and acerola extracts precisely because whole-fruit polyphenols improve functionality, bioavailability and absorption, the way vitamin C in a kiwi or lemon resists oxidation. Vitamin C is a coenzyme to multiple reactions, is needed to bind amino acids into collagen, and supports blood flow and arterial health.
  • Collagen. Asked point-blank whether collagen supplements are genuinely effective or hype, her verdict: genuinely effective, with white papers to prove it — and worth taking even when young. Her own formulation pairs it with edible hyaluronic acid (sodium hyaluronate), lutein and zeaxanthin.
  • For a 26-year-old building muscle: choose your protein supplement carefully — the wrong protein, the wrong quantity, or protein with zero carbs to help assimilation shows up as acne on the back, shoulders and face, plus hair fall. Do a food-intolerance blood test to find the protein source that suits you. Add omega-3 with EPA and DHA above 500 mg, plus hyaluronic acid, vitamin C and zinc for skin integrity. Essential micronutrients for skin and hair include K2, zinc, selenium, vitamins A and E, and the B vitamins.

"Supplements are a nutritional convenience for the nutritional indiscipline. A 26-year-old should try and learn to get more salads in their diet, rather than only looking at protein shakes."

— Ryan, nutritionist

Myths, fairness creams and other red flags

"Fairness creams can make you fairer"

Myth — with a caveat. Asked for the biggest skin care myth Indians still believe, Dr. Shetty's answer: that you can become fairer than you are. Skin tone sits on the Fitzpatrick scale of one to six; Indians mostly fall between two and five, and no cream moves you from a five to a three. What legitimate "lightening" products actually do is deliver a more even tone and undo damage — by regulating skin turnover (the roughly 28-day shedding cycle slows with age, and retained clumped melanin makes skin look darker) and by reducing the activity of the enzymes that make pigment. Skin also darkens over the years because pollution, sun and friction keep triggering melanocytes — aging melanosis, maturational melanosis, frictional melanosis. A brighter, more even, one-shade-lighter tone is the realistic ceiling.

The unlabelled tub cream: stay away. A cream sold in a little unlabelled tub — very white, chalky, pasty, almost pearl-white — is usually a steroid emptied out of a tube. For a few days you look alabaster-fair and pores seem to vanish. What is really happening: the skin is losing pigment (turning white, not fair), then thinning, then literally cracking as the barrier is destroyed. Blood vessels start showing through, acneiform eruptions follow, and extra facial hair can grow. High-percentage hydroquinone and steroid creams cause lasting damage — avoid them.

Stretch marks after big weight change

Prevent; you cannot erase. Stretch marks form when unlubricated skin cracks — the elastin fibres give way. If you are starting a weight-loss journey, lubricate the skin throughout: plenty of moisturizer, coconut oil, or a simple cold cream. In-clinic radiofrequency can stimulate collagen and hold the skin tight as weight drops. Once formed, marks can be reduced with microneedling, radiofrequency, deep peels and lasers — but no one can restore clean, unmarked skin, and no one can 100% erase a stretch mark or an acne scar. Be wary of anyone promising otherwise, and cautious about aggressive weight gain and loss in the first place.

Mixing actives as a beginner

See a doctor first. Anything beyond sunscreen, face wash and moisturizer — the actives — deserves a professional consultation rather than a mixing chart. Of the three most-searched ingredients — hyaluronic acid, vitamin C and retinol — never mix all three. Vitamin C can irritate; retinol can irritate; and hyaluronic acid, marketed as pure hydration, will itself burn and irritate skin whose barrier is already compromised. The combination is riskiest on sensitive, dry skin.

Does sex or masturbation cause acne?

Myth. No influence — if anything, Dr. Shetty suggests it should help acne, since sex relieves stress hormones and releases happy ones.

Daily routines that hold the barrier

The framework is three words: clean, nourish, repair. Cleanliness is the best thing for skin.

First, know your skin type

  1. Sleep overnight with no skin care at all — ideally no air conditioning, fan and windows open. Wash your face clean and go to bed.
  2. In the morning, press a blotting paper or tissue on your face and lift it off.
  3. Read the result: tissue slips straight off — dry skin. Tissue sticks or shows an oily patch — oily skin. Oily in some zones, not others — combination.

Then simply buy what the tube says — face wash for oily, combination or all skin types — from a credible company. No need to decode ingredient lists.

How often to wash

Skin typeFace washes per day
DryTwice is plenty
CombinationTwo to three
OilyThree, maximum four

Do not keep splashing water beyond that — constant washing signals the body that the skin is drying out, and it responds by producing more oil.

The under-two-minute routine

  1. Clean: the face wash that suits your skin type, around three times a day — for example after lunch, at 5 o'clock tea, and at night.
  2. Nourish: moisturizer if your skin is dry, topped with sunscreen. If you are a typical oily-skinned 25-year-old (testosterone keeps young male skin oily), a single mattifying sunscreen does both jobs — one product, two seconds, done.
  3. Repair: at night, products with salicylic or glycolic acid lift excess oil and leave the face bright for morning. (Persistent problems — excessive oil, acne, pigmentation, early hair fall, back acne — are a separate conversation with a doctor.)

Soap bar or face wash?

Cleanse with anything rather than nothing — but prefer a bottle. If soap is all you have, cleanse with soap; cleansing matters most. But a bar sits exposed, most soap bars contain fatty acids, and fat is a good growth medium for bacteria — and you cannot be sure you are the only one touching it. Anything dispensed from a bottle, tube or squeezy pack is as clean as a single use. It does not even have to be a dedicated face wash — a body wash from a bottle is acceptable.

Preparing skin for a wedding or big event

The right way: three to six months out

Dr. Shetty's plea: do not arrive ten days before the wedding. Skin, like a diet, needs time to respond — three months minimum. Book a video or online consultation with your chosen dermatologist three to six months in advance, and work the plan:

  1. Correct nutrition first — lower inflammation, calm skin irritation, get weight and insulin resistance right.
  2. Blood investigations: key vitamins (B12, D3), iron and ferritin, thyroid, insulin resistance, and functional male or female hormones. Then correct gaps with supplementation, nutrition and lifestyle changes so the glow is built from within.
  3. A staged skin care regime matched to your current climate and the wedding venue's, graduated by the doctor as the date approaches.
  4. Start locally what you can — a facial, a clean-up, laser hair removal — or fly in to your doctor monthly if you can.

If you genuinely have only ten days

  • Experiment with nothing new — not even a new cream. If it reacts, there is no time to correct it.
  • See a dermatologist on day one; reset your skin care and nutrition. Even ten days can lower inflammation, improve insulin resistance, and correct D3 or B12 (a shot if levels are very low), with an antioxidant and anti-inflammatory started.
  • Do any deep cleansing, peel or laser on day one, leaving ten days to recover.
  • Do not chase pigmentation now — it can be covered with makeup, and chasing it will irritate the skin and look flaky under makeup. Treat what makeup cannot hide: barrier problems like eczema, and bumpy, painful acne.
  • Hydration is the key, because makeup dries the skin.
  • Biostimulator microinjections — combinations of hyaluronic acid, glutathione and copper peptides placed in tiny amounts across the skin — can deliver supple, hydrated, glowing skin within ten days, faster than any cream could. But go to a good doctor; done badly, it is a disaster.
