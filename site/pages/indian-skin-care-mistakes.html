The Skin Care Mistakes Indians Make — and What Actually Works

Dermatologist Dr. Aanchal Panth, an AIIMS graduate, maps skin quality to three levers — food, sun and lifestyle — and clears away the diet myths, DIY remedies and viral trends that quietly damage South Asian skin.

At a glance

  • Skin quality tracks three broad factors: food, sun exposure and lifestyle. Most skin disease begins with inflammation somewhere in the body or the skin itself.
  • Sugar and dairy are the two dietary triggers with the clearest link to acne; both raise insulin-like growth factor, and dairy also raises androgens that push oil glands to overproduce.
  • Sun protection is the single biggest lever for Indian skin, which is genuinely prone to dark spots because it carries more melanin. SPF 30 is the floor; SPF 50 suits pigmentation-prone skin.
  • Lemon, orange peel and apple cider vinegar do not lighten skin — they irritate it and make it more sun-sensitive, worsening the very dark spots people are chasing away.
  • Collagen breakdown becomes evident after age 25; sun protection, adequate protein and a clean diet do more than any cream, and stress, smoking and pollution all accelerate ageing.
  • Physical scrubs, gritty homemade masks and viral "facials" damage the skin barrier; a simple, consistent routine of cleanser, moisturiser and sunscreen beats every shortcut.
  • A basic dermatologist consultation in your teens or early twenties, and roughly once a year after, catches triggers such as melasma and pre-diabetic skin changes early.

Food, inflammation and the skin

Inflammation is where most disease starts — internal heat causes a mild swelling that shows up in skin and hair. Some skin conditions arise from inflammation inside the body's organs; others are confined to the skin. As a working principle, what is healthy for the body is healthy for the skin, and what is not, is not. A holistic diet aimed at the whole body pays off visibly on the face.

Sugar: the fastest route to inflammation

Cut it. Sugar raises insulin-like growth factor, which drives more acne. It also dries and dehydrates the skin, makes wrinkling look more prominent, and — over the long term — produces advanced glycation end products: sugar binds to the collagen in your skin, stiffens it, reduces its elasticity, and both delays healing and speeds ageing. Reducing sugar helps from an acne perspective and an ageing perspective at once.

Practical substitutions that Dr. Panth's interviewer has found workable: keep desserts and pastries largely out of daily life; satisfy a sweet craving with a little fruit, preferably in the morning rather than at night; and lean on stevia, monk fruit or a small amount of jaggery instead of table sugar. Cravings are hardest in the first four to six weeks — once the body adjusts, they fade. Indian sweets combine sugar and milk, which is exactly why they are worth treating as an occasional exception rather than a staple.

Dairy: the city-milk problem

Reduce it if you have inflamed acne. Studies link dairy to acne: milk raises androgens and insulin-like growth factor, both of which stimulate the sebaceous glands to make more oil. Stopping milk often reduces breakouts within two to three weeks. The milk sold in cities appears to be the main culprit; whether fresh, natural cow's milk closer to source behaves differently is not something the transcript settles.

Two dairy foods get a pass. Paneer is safe: when it is made, the lactose and whey separate out into the water that drains away, so the solid you eat lacks the acne-causing components. Curd is fine for the same reason. It is precisely that leftover water — the whey — that concentrates into protein powders, which is why whey protein is a common hidden cause of sudden back and chest acne in people who start the gym. If breakouts appear there, look at your protein source; paneer is a safer way to get the protein.

Genetics set the response. Some people drink milk with no effect at all. Dietary guidelines come from studying large groups, so they hold on average — but every individual's genetic make-up reacts differently, and "my grandmother did this and was fine" does not disprove the general link. Sugar and milk are inflammatory on the whole; how much they affect you personally will vary.

Alcohol, processed food and salt

Both are inflammatory — the less, the better. After a heavy night of drinking, the next morning brings dryness, stretched-looking skin, puffiness and more prominent eye bags. Alcohol is inflammatory and dehydrating, and long-term drinking has a clearly negative effect on skin. The old line that a daily glass of red wine is good for you is largely a busted myth: the flavonoids and antioxidants in red wine can be had far more effectively elsewhere — including from the grapes the wine is made from.

  • Rehydrate correctly. Oral rehydration solution (ORS) — a proper proportion of sugar, salt and water — helps the body absorb electrolytes and rehydrate the skin better than plain water or lemon water the next day.
  • Practical limit. The ideal is not to drink; the practical advice is no more than two drinks a week, and for beer no more than about two pints.
  • The second hit. Alcohol lowers inhibitions, so people abandon their diet and snack on unhealthy food — a further negative effect layered on top of the alcohol's own sugar.

Processed, packaged food carries a heavy load of preservatives, salt and sugar to extend shelf life. Excess salt is as harmful as excess sugar: it raises blood pressure, dries the skin and causes water retention. The common thread across popular diets — paleo, keto, carnivore, vegan — is simply removing processed food and eating fresh, and that alone brings quick improvements to fitness, skin and hair. No single diet suits everyone worldwide; eat what your local market offers, keep protein intake adequate (Indian diets often fall short), and go local.

Sun protection: the biggest lever for Indian skin

The skin has two layers: the epidermis on top and the dermis below. At the base of the epidermis sit melanocytes, which make melanin. In sunlight these cells become more active, produce more melanin and distribute it more widely, so it rises through the skin and causes darkening. Because Indian skin is genuinely prone to dark spots, the more you avoid the sun, the better it stays. If only two products were allowed, they would be a face wash at night and a sunscreen in the morning — with a moisturiser added, that is close to ideal.

Why the sun matters more than any wrinkle cream

Fact. A landmark photograph of a truck driver — one side of his face aged roughly 20 years more than the other after 28 years of one-sided sun exposure — is used in dermatology teaching for a reason. Sun exposure does not just cause dark spots; it breaks down collagen, produces more wrinkling, alters the skin's healing, and in that image also shows sagging, a drooping eyelid and eyebrow-hair loss on the exposed side. It also stakes out the case that sunscreen and sun avoidance genuinely slow ageing.

Sunscreen use is far lower among men, partly from the notion that it feels sticky or leaves a white cast. Older formulas did; modern gel-based sunscreens are lightweight, leave no residue and do not feel greasy or sweaty. Choosing one you enjoy makes the habit stick — and the habit is the whole point.

Reading SPF, and the numbers brands inflate

Choose the number for your skin, and the brand for its honesty. SPF — Sun Protection Factor — is measured on human skin: sunscreen is applied, UV exposure is given, and the dose needed to redden protected skin is compared with unprotected skin. That ratio is the SPF.

SituationMinimum SPF
Regular, everyday useSPF 30
Skin prone to dark spots (typical Indian skin)SPF 50
Long outdoor exposure — beaches, Rajasthan, GoaSPF 50, plus water- or sweat-resistant

Testing has repeatedly found products labelled SPF 50 or 100 delivering less than 25, or even below 20. Reputable brands that have been on the market for a while — the conversation names Lakmé and Pond's as examples — run proper, ongoing quality control and human testing to make sure the bottle delivers what the label claims. For water exposure, look for a water-resistant component so a dip does not wash it off completely. Many good sunscreens also include niacinamide, which helps fade dark spots, so a single product can protect and treat at once.

Sunburn and high altitude

Every sunburn is an injury. A burn is skin injury, and continuous injury means continuous inflammation, which drives ageing and extra collagen breakdown. The more you protect against sunburn, the more you slow the ageing process. Indian skin types are somewhat less likely to burn, but very high exposure still burns — and at high altitude, such as Ladakh, the thinner ozone layer and harsher rays make burns far more likely.

Sun protection is broader than sunscreen alone. Seeking behaviour matters: carrying an umbrella, and avoiding the sun between roughly 11 a.m. and 3 p.m., are important parts of protection. For anticipated heavy exposure — a beach holiday or a high-altitude trek — oral antioxidants such as vitamin C tablets and astaxanthin reduce melanin production and sun damage. Antioxidants act as scavengers of the free radicals that every internal process generates and that would otherwise damage cells; unchecked free-radical damage drives inflammation, ageing and, over the long term, raises cancer risk.

Dark spots, pigmentation and the lemon mistake

Hyperpigmentation is an umbrella term for anything that raises melanin and leaves dark spots. The most common cause in Indian skin is melasma. Next is post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation — the dark mark left behind after any injury or inflammation, including acne. Age spots and sun spots appear over time, and because cell turnover slows with age, these marks linger longer, leaving the skin looking uneven.

Why lemon and kitchen acids backfire

Myth — and an actively harmful one. There is a widespread belief that anything from the kitchen must be harmless on skin. Lemon is a photosensitiser: it makes skin more sensitive to the sun, so if you already have dark spots it makes them worse. Being acidic, it irritates the skin further; the same goes for crushed orange peel and apple cider vinegar. People apply lemon expecting a brightening or bleaching effect — that comes from mild irritation and exfoliation — but the side effects and irritation dominate, and in acne-prone skin the resulting dark spot lingers even longer.

"Please do not apply lemon. It actually makes the skin more sensitive towards the effect of the sun."

— Dr. Aanchal Panth, dermatologist

What actually fades a dark spot

Serums plus relentless sun protection. Serums containing kojic acid or alpha arbutin lighten dark spots. Sunscreen is the most important step of all when treating pigmentation — the more you avoid the sun and the more you protect, the faster spots heal and the shorter the mark left by acne. A sunscreen that also contains niacinamide fights the dark spot while it protects, so a separate product may not be needed. There are three ways to reduce a dark spot, and good treatment uses all of them:

  • Increase cell turnover so pigmented dead skin is shed faster.
  • Reduce melanin production with tyrosinase inhibitors that suppress the enzyme making melanin.
  • Reduce melanin distribution — niacinamide limits how far the pigment spreads.

Ageing well: collagen, stress and lifestyle

The dermis is held together by a mesh of collagen and elastin that gives skin its structure. As collagen breaks down, skin sags, pores look larger, and fine lines and wrinkles appear, leaving skin looking aged and leathery. Babies have more collagen — and more fat — which is why their skin looks so taut. With age, fat is lost and the fat pads shift and droop, so a defined jawline becomes less prominent. Collagen loss and shifting fat are the two biggest drivers of an aged appearance.

When to start anti-ageing

Sun protection from the teens; actives from about 25. Collagen breakdown becomes clearly evident after age 25, which is when antioxidant serums and retinol earn their place. Before that they add little. From 25 onward, a workable framework is an antioxidant serum in the morning, a retinol-based cream at night, and sunscreen throughout — used regularly, this genuinely slows the ageing process. An exfoliating serum once a week can be added for more advanced routines, skipping retinol that same night. Exfoliation removes the outermost dead layer (the stratum corneum), whose turnover slows with age, leaving skin fresher and stimulating collagen; the build-up of dead skin around hair follicles, called hyper-cornification, can also drive pimples, so weekly exfoliation helps acne-prone skin.

Stress, smoking and pollution

All three speed ageing. Stress raises cortisol and starts inflammation in the body, so chronic overthinking visibly accelerates ageing. Smoking increases free radicals and drives more collagen breakdown. Pollution breaks down skin collagen and darkens skin, because pollutant particles settle on the skin and trigger inflammation and reactions that lead to dark spots and ageing — a real concern for anyone in an industrial or high-pollution area.

A pollution-fighting routine: an antioxidant serum in the morning to scavenge pollution-generated free radicals, a moisturiser to keep the barrier intact and protected, and sunscreen — a zinc-oxide-based one forms a barrier so pollutants sit on top rather than penetrating. At night, cleanse thoroughly to remove settled dust, and always remove makeup properly before sleep.

Exercise, and the limits of face massage

Movement helps; massage relaxes but does not reshape. Cardio increases blood flow and gives skin a healthy flush; strength training three times a week has been shown by biopsy to increase skin collagen; and yoga aligns body and mind, with its relaxation showing up on the skin. Lymphatic drainage massage genuinely reduces morning puffiness. But facial massage, gua sha and jade rollers will not sharpen a jawline, raise cheekbones or counteract sagging — facial ageing happens across many layers, from bone resorption and an enlarging eye socket to shifting fat pads and slackening muscle, none of which surface massage or a cream can address. If it feels relaxing, enjoy it; do not expect it to change your structure.

Sleep and dark circles

Structural first, then holistic. Under-eye concerns are common from Tamil Nadu to Kashmir because they are largely structural — a high incidence of sunken eyes and tear troughs, naturally thin under-eye skin through which vessels show, all worsened by rubbing and by age. Sleep becomes more important the older you get, and fixing sleep often does more than any eye cream. Because the causes differ, so do the treatments: hollows need filler, dark skin needs lightening agents, thin and wrinkled skin needs retinol or collagen-stimulating creams, and puffiness responds to caffeine-based products. Allergies, sinusitis, asthma, dry eyes from screen time and uncorrected vision all deepen dark circles too. At home, chilled cucumber or potato slices soothe, and frozen green-tea bags — steeped, then frozen — de-puff and calm with their mild anti-inflammatory effect.

Home remedies, masks and viral trends

Desi masks are not all equal, and the internet's worst trends are genuinely harmful. The dividing line is simple: soothing, non-abrasive applications are usually fine; anything gritty that you rub into the skin is not.

Multani mitti, besan and physical scrubs

Clay is fine; scrubs are not. Multani mitti (fuller's earth) absorbs oil and leaves oily, open-pored skin feeling fresh, with pores looking temporarily smaller — acceptable to use. Besan (gram flour) can suit very oily, non-irritated skin as a mild physical exfoliant. But dermatologists do not recommend physical scrubs at all: fine grains rubbed into the skin create micro-tears, and in Indian skin that injury can stimulate more melanin and leave dark spots. Homemade sugar scrubs, ground-dal scrubs and coffee scrubs all fall in this harmful category.

Barrier damage is real. Dr. Panth has seen skin barriers wrecked by homemade masks — ground dal or other gritty pastes rubbed in — leaving inflammation, redness, itching and lasting damage. People also scrub the neck, underarms, elbows and knees hoping to expose "fairer" skin underneath; that is the wrong way to treat skin. If you want to exfoliate, use controlled chemical exfoliants such as glycolic or lactic acid rather than abrasion, which irritates unevenly and tears the skin. This kind of experimentation is most common in teens and the twenties.

Soothing masks are a different matter — rose water, curd, or mashed papaya or fruit cause no harm and can be applied for relaxation. It is only the gritty, rub-in preparations to keep off the skin.

Korean masks and products

Good for sensitive skin — but they will not give you Korean skin. Korean products are extremely popular, and there is real science behind them: Korean research leads the world in cosmeceuticals, using growth factors, exosomes and PDRN (salmon DNA) at the cutting edge, funded by enormous domestic demand. Their everyday products are rich in soothing, anti-inflammatory ingredients — centella asiatica, propolis, snail mucin — which is why sensitive or dry skin loves them; studies suggest sensitive skin is more common in the Korean population. What they cannot do is transplant Korean genetics, bone structure or skin type. Glass skin is light reflecting off a smooth, even surface, and Indian skin, with its natural tendency toward darkening, unevenness and dark circles, simply is not built the same way.

The trends to never try

Do not do these. Menstrual-blood facials, bird-poop facials and cat-litter facials are all circulating online, and all should be avoided. The menstrual-blood idea seems borrowed from PRP — the "vampire facial" made famous by a celebrity — in which blood is drawn, centrifuged and the plasma re-applied under microneedling. Menstrual blood is not the same thing; it carries bacteria and fungus naturally present in the vaginal canal that have no place on your face. Clay-style trends like the bird-poop mask merely absorb oil and give a temporary tightening effect that a conventional clay mask provides safely. Stick to a conventional, good moisturiser and sunscreen.

Getting ready for a wedding or party

Start early — or keep it gentle. For a wedding, begin a basic routine and a dermatologist visit as soon as the date is set: treating acne takes three to six months, and dark spots may need laser sessions or lightening creams, so there must be time to show improvement. Never introduce new treatments or creams in the month before the event, because there is no time to correct anything that reacts. For a party the same day, a sheet mask with hyaluronic acid worn for about 15 minutes gives an instant hydrated, plumped glow. Malai (milk cream) is fine for a quick moisturised look thanks to its mild lactic acid — but, again, no lemon.

Everyday habits that help or harm

Touching and popping

Both make things worse. Constantly touching the face transfers everything your hands have touched onto the skin. Popping a pimple is worse still: trying to squeeze out a red pimple pushes its contents deeper, causing more inflammation, a larger nodule, a higher chance of scarring, and — by damaging the barrier — a darker mark left behind. If a clear pus point shows, it can be released very gently, but the safer moves at home are a salicylic-acid serum on a painful pimple and a pimple patch over a pus point to dry and heal it. Managing stress helps too: teenagers flare worst during exams, and deep breathing that lowers cortisol can reduce the inflammation of a coming breakout.

Shaving, and men's skin

Technique and cleanliness decide the outcome. Shaving causes problems when done with an unsterile or dirty blade, without cleaning the skin before and after, or against the direction of hair growth — always shave with the grain, and moisturise afterwards. Aftershave is antiseptic and fine to use, but its alcohol content can dry and irritate skin (a form of irritant contact dermatitis), so following it with a moisturiser helps. Men's skin is thicker with larger oil glands, so blackheads and open pores show more on the nose; because a beard forms a barrier, dermatologists often pick slightly thicker, gel-based actives that penetrate through it.

Ageing is perceived differently by gender. Men's thicker, oilier skin wrinkles a little more slowly, and men tend to accept ageing as part of life; a beard also hides lower-face changes such as marionette lines and a sagging jawline. Women face far more societal and media pressure to fight ageing — a pressure worth questioning rather than answering with so much Botox and filler that the face no longer looks like itself. Taking good care of yourself and staying healthy is the goal; obsession is not.

Water: how much, how hot, and how hard

Drink enough, wash cool, and mind hard water for hair. Around 2 to 2.5 litres a day — roughly 8 to 10 glasses, about one an hour — is the guide. But drinking water hydrates the body, not the outer skin: dry skin needs a moisturiser and can benefit from omega-3 supplements that help retain moisture. For washing, room-temperature or cool water beats hot, which dries the skin and strips its oils; cool water soothes and reduces puffiness. Anyone with eczema, psoriasis or very dry skin should avoid hot showers entirely and keep showers short, around two minutes.

  • Ice water dunk vs direct ice. Dunking the face in ice water briefly shrinks pores, smooths makeup application and reduces puffiness — but it is not for dry, sensitive or rosacea-prone skin, or skin with prominent veins and redness. Never rub an ice cube directly on the skin: ice burns are common. Wrap it in a thin towel, or use ice globes for gentle cooling and de-puffing.
  • Hard water. It affects skin only mildly, but clearly harms hair: its minerals coat each strand, leaving hair dry, frizzy and brittle. For dry, damaged or colour-treated hair, a water softener is worth considering.

Warning signs, routines and the dermatologist's role

Moles, skin tags and skin-cancer warning signs

Know what to watch for. Moles are collections of melanocytes and are largely genetic; sun protection modestly reduces how many form but cannot stop them. They come as flat junctional nevi, raised skin-coloured dermal nevi, and dark, raised compound nevi, and can be removed — small ones by a dermatologist (roughly ₹2,000–3,000 for a small mole, ₹8,000–10,000 for medium, more for large), larger or cosmetically sensitive ones by a plastic surgeon; a deep nevus must be fully excised or it recurs. The medical caution matters more than the cosmetics: watch for a non-healing ulcer, roughness, bleeding or blackening, and any mole that suddenly changes colour, grows, bleeds or ulcerates — those changes warrant examination. Australia's high skin-cancer rates illustrate the mechanism: fair, low-melanin skin, strong UV and repeated childhood sunburns raise the risk of melanoma, an aggressive skin cancer, and of squamous cell carcinoma.

Skin tags can signal pre-diabetes. Before diabetes sets in, insulin runs high, thickening the epidermal keratinocytes and producing skin tags in the body's folds along with darkening and thickening at the neck, underarms and groin — a pattern called acanthosis nigricans. Treat it as a prompt to cut sugar, exercise, stay active and maintain a healthy body weight.

A routine you can actually keep — by age and budget

Simple is enough; escalate with guidance. Good skin care need not be expensive — competition has made budget products genuinely effective. Match the routine to your stage:

  • Students and under-25s: a gentle cleanser, a moisturiser and sunscreen in the morning; cleanse again at night and apply a moisturiser before bed. Add a serum if you have some spots. That is enough.
  • Ages 25–35: a night routine of retinol, a hyaluronic-acid product and an anti-acne serum, with a peptide added to signal the dermis to build collagen, and a weekly exfoliating serum in place of retinol that night.
  • The high-spend end: growth-factor and peptide serums, plant-extract formulas, skin-booster injections and collagen-stimulating tightening lasers. Basic skin care at this level runs about ₹15,000–20,000 a month, with treatments adding a similar amount or up to ₹50,000 — entirely optional, not necessary for good skin.

When to see a dermatologist

Consulting fees depend on the doctor's experience and city; a dermatologist with more than ten years' experience typically charges more, and metros run higher than smaller cities. Finding the right one takes a little time — it may take a visit or two to know whether they align with your requirements. A basic consultation in your teens or early twenties helps you understand your skin type and catch triggers such as melasma early, and a check roughly once a year keeps you on track. Active acne warrants visits every two to three weeks, or every two to three months, until it settles, then perhaps once every six months. A good dermatologist treats the concern you came in with, rather than pointing out new insecurities. Looking ahead, dermatology is moving toward minimally invasive, non-surgical procedures and better-penetrating molecules; AI may help diagnose, but the empathy and experience of a doctor remain hard to replace.
