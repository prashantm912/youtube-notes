Fatty Liver and Gut Health: The Mistakes That Quietly Do the Damage

Gut complaints and fatty liver are surging in India, driven by modern lifestyle, stress and a flood of supplements sold as quick fixes. A leading gastroenterologist cuts through the noise: what actually causes fatty liver and how to reverse it, why most probiotics and PPIs are overused, and how to manage acidity, constipation and diarrhoea sensibly.

At a glance

  • Gut problems are rising for several reasons at once: more awareness, modern lifestyle and stress, and more people reaching out to doctors instead of ignoring symptoms. Any kind of stress — financial, occupational or personal — has a real impact on the gut.
  • The three most common presentations in clinic are gas (reflux, acidity, constipation, diarrhoea), fatty liver, and abdominal pain people cannot explain.
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease — now termed metabolic-associated steatotic liver disease — has become a pandemic of sorts, and most of it is lifestyle-driven and reversible if caught early.
  • Grade 1 and 2 fatty liver can usually be fully reversed with diet, exercise and losing 5 to 10% of body weight. Medications reverse liver function test numbers, not the disease itself.
  • Probiotics, prebiotics and most supplements are heavily marketed but rarely necessary; a balanced diet and natural sources like curd and buttermilk are enough for most people.
  • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) are India's most over-prescribed drug and carry real long-term risks — they should be short-term and supervised, not indefinite.
  • Three foundations decide gut health: physical activity, a balanced home-cooked diet, and mindfulness or stress reduction. Almost everything else is supplementary or unnecessary.

Fatty liver: why it has become a modern epidemic

Fatty liver is now one of the most common findings in clinic, largely because routine health checks and ultrasounds are picking it up in ever more people. In earlier years, the two causes doctors reached for when they saw a fatty liver were alcohol and chronic hepatitis B and C — viruses that predominantly infect the liver. Increasingly, though, the picture is non-alcoholic fatty liver disease: something other than alcohol is driving the fat into the liver.

The terminology has been updated to metabolic-associated steatotic liver disease, and what it means in practice is that people with diabetes, hypertension or obesity tend to deposit fat primarily in the liver, where it seems to cause the most trouble. The result is a lifestyle-driven epidemic of fatty liver, most of it non-alcoholic.

Catching it early — before symptoms appear

By the time a fatty liver becomes symptomatic, the disease is usually far advanced. Jaundice, a decreased appetite, or swelling in the legs and abdomen mean the disease has probably gone past the point of easy reversal. The whole aim is to catch people early, and that is where annual health checks earn their place: an ultrasound will typically report grade 1, 2, 3 or 4 fatty liver, and a liver function test that comes back deranged is a further pointer that something is wrong.

For those who do not get regular checks, certain groups are especially prone: people who are diabetic, hypertensive or obese, and particularly those with central obesity. In the Indian population, that means a waist circumference above 90 cm in men and above 80 cm in women flags a higher risk of fatty liver disease.

Why fatty liver is worth taking seriously. It can become chronic and progress to chronic liver disease; there is a rising incidence of liver cancers linked to fatty liver disease, sometimes even before chronicity sets in; and it acts as a harbinger for problems elsewhere, such as in the kidney or the heart. Catching it early can make a real difference.

How often to screen

For anyone above roughly 30 to 35, an annual health screen is worthwhile. Those with high-risk factors — a thyroid issue, diabetes, hypertension or obesity — benefit from an earlier yearly ultrasound alongside a liver function test. Basic blood tests such as a lipid profile, cholesterol and sugar levels deserve frequent checks too, with screening intervals tailored to each person's risk. It is also important to screen for the hepatitis viruses: hepatitis C can now be virtually eliminated, and hepatitis B can be kept in check with simple medication.

Reversing fatty liver: lifestyle comes first

The first step after a diagnosis is to see a doctor — and not to panic. Many people arrive alarmed at a grade 1 or grade 2 result, but those grades are generally not a cause for worry. Grade 3 and 4 make a far bigger difference, and doctors also look at other parameters such as the liver enzymes (ALT and AST); if these are elevated, there is more reason for concern.

For most people who present with fatty liver, lifestyle modification is the first step, and it comes down to two things: diet and exercise. Getting the diet right, getting good exercise, and losing anywhere between 5 and 10% of body weight is usually good enough to reverse the majority of fatty liver disease. Alongside this, controlling risk factors such as diabetes and hypertension matters.

What medications can and cannot do. Most medications for fatty liver do not reverse the liver disease — they only reverse the liver function test numbers. Some reduce fat content or inflammation and so have an indirect effect on those parameters, but there is still no medication that delivers complete reversibility. That is why lifestyle modification remains the most important intervention, with medications added only when lifestyle change alone is not helping.

Reversal is realistic: grade 1 and grade 2 fatty liver can be completely reversed with lifestyle modification. The doctor speaks from personal experience, having had grade 1 fatty liver himself that resolved with adequate exercise and diet — and having seen the same in many patients once they lose some weight.

Alcohol and smoking

There is now enough evidence that even the smallest amount of alcohol is not encouraged; the old idea that a glass of wine is good for the heart no longer holds. No doctor will name a "safe" amount, and part of the harm is not the alcohol alone but what goes with it — carbonated mixers loaded with sugar that spike fat conversion, and the junk food eaten alongside. As a broad position: no amount of alcohol is truly safe. That said, grade 1 or 2 fatty liver is not a reason for panic over a rare occasional drink; a very occasional 30 to 60 ml, perhaps once in three to six months, is not a disaster — though it is better avoided. Smoking has no direct impact on the liver but plenty of indirect harm, and offers no benefit whatsoever.

The gut microbiome and the antibiotic problem

The gut microbiome is not a new concept — it has been discussed for ages. It matters because it acts as a sample by which the body learns what is harmful and what is beneficial; the gut is always in a state of alertness, and the goal is for it not to over-react to normal stimuli. The microbiome begins forming at birth: in a normal delivery the mother's bacteria are passed to the child, and even in a caesarean the child is exposed to skin flora. As the child grows, the environment and the food it is given shape a stable internal milieu over time.

The main way this gets disrupted today is unnecessary antibiotics. When a child has a fever for a day or two, parents often go straight to a pharmacist and start antibiotics, which destroy the normal gut flora. In India this is compounded because antibiotics can be bought without a prescription, and people repeat old prescriptions without consulting a doctor — driving antibiotic resistance when courses are not completed or are self-prescribed for too long.

In children this carries a particular cost. Developing infections is natural and important, because the immune system is learning how much to react and when. Repeatedly disrupting that process with antibiotics can leave the immunity with problems over time. This is thought to contribute to the rise in food allergies and autoimmune conditions that were far less common in the Indian population before, some of which are associated with changes in gut microbiota — though the exact science and how to modulate it are still not understood. What helps most is simple: a balanced diet as the child grows does more for the microbiome than anything else.

Probiotics, prebiotics and the supplement trap

Probiotics have unfortunately become a commercial product. Gastroenterologists rarely prescribe them, and generally never unless absolutely necessary. Whether the many probiotics available — off the shelf and on prescription — actually help is still unknown, and so is whether they can cause harm: they are live organisms being ingested, and they can carry resistance to antibiotics. Even for a patient with diarrhoea, or suspected dysbiosis (an imbalance of good and bad bacteria), it is unclear how much survives the acidic stomach and the bile, and how many actually reach the patient to any benefit. Probiotics are also expensive, and people sometimes take them instead of the actual medication they need.

Where gut-directed treatment has genuinely proven useful is faecal microbiota transplantation — transplanting another person's stool — and even there, only in two conditions: Clostridium difficile infection and ulcerative colitis, and usually only after standard treatment has failed. In inflammatory bowel disease such as ulcerative colitis, a couple of well-researched probiotics can be of some benefit in very specific circumstances. Beyond that, giving probiotics regularly is hard to justify. In practice, after acute gastroenteritis it can take weeks to months for the gut microbiota to recover, and giving a probiotic makes no measurable difference to that recovery — so there is little reason to prescribe one.

If you want probiotics, get them from food. Natural probiotics such as curd and buttermilk can be taken freely — they are natural food and carry no such concerns. Along with other natural sources, that is enough for most people.

The same logic applies to supplements generally. People increasingly add probiotics to a stack of vitamin D, zinc and iron taken without any real indication. Supplements should supplement an actual diet, not replace it — the healthier default is to fix the balanced diet first and reserve supplements for a genuine, identified need.

Abdominal pain, acidity and reflux

Abdominal pain is one of the most common reasons people visit a gastroenterologist, and it has several typical causes. One is acid peptic disease — often driven by H. pylori bacteria in the stomach, which increases acid production; people usually describe a burning sensation rather than true pain, and the two are genuinely hard for patients to tell apart. Gallstones are another important cause, classically in a middle-aged woman after childbirth presenting with upper or right-sided pain — the old teaching of "fat, female, fertile" — and are far more common than people expect. A third bucket is non-specific abdominal pain that, after thorough evaluation, is labelled irritable bowel syndrome.

Importantly, if 100 patients come to the outpatient clinic, 60 to 70% of them will not have a real identifiable cause. That does not mean they are all labelled with IBS. Irritable bowel syndrome is a diagnosis of exclusion with defined criteria — abdominal pain associated with a change in bowel habit — reached only after basic tests such as a complete blood count and inflammatory markers like CRP, and sometimes faecal calprotectin to look for gut inflammation. Endoscopy and colonoscopy are generally not needed in younger people, but are used where there is strong suspicion of inflammatory bowel disease, which is being picked up far more often now.

Gastritis and the reasons behind it

Genuine gastritis — burning in the upper abdomen — most commonly traces to H. pylori, which needs to be checked and treated. Other common irritants are painkillers and over-the-counter drugs, including some ayurvedic and homeopathic medicines, that inflame the bowel lining; alcohol and smoking are known causes too. When no reversible factor is found, many cases are functional GI disorders — no identifiable cause but real symptoms. Because stress features heavily, stress reduction is part of the treatment, and for those who cannot manage it, cognitive behavioural therapy alongside a psychiatrist or psychologist can help modulate the discomfort. Even so, potentially serious causes like gallstones must be ruled out before anyone is labelled "functional".

PPIs, antacids and the drugs to be cautious with

Proton pump inhibitors are India's most commonly prescribed drug, written by every field of medicine and by people outside medicine too — often when they are not really required. The better approach is to go back to basics, using simpler medication such as famotidine for a short duration (a week or two), then identifying and treating the underlying problem rather than continuing a PPI long-term. The one clear place for long-term PPIs is genuine gastro-oesophageal reflux disease with heartburn — and even there, endoscopic therapy or minor surgery can now reduce reflux episodes.

Long-term PPIs carry real risks. They can increase infections, reduce nutrient absorption, and cause osteoporosis, and have been linked to kidney disease in some people. Certain combinations are worse: PPIs combined with levosulpiride can cause irreversible Parkinsonism if used long-term, and have caused galactorrhoea in women. PPIs are best avoided unless truly needed, and then only under supervision and monitoring. They are frequently co-prescribed with antibiotics or painkillers when most people do not need them — a better practice is to give the option and use it only if symptoms actually appear.

Antacids and effervescent products offer little. Magnesium-based antacids are not particularly helpful and are unnecessary as a routine after every meal. Eno is essentially a carbonated drink that produces gas you then burp or pass, giving temporary relief but no real benefit — as with soda and carbonated drinks, mostly it delivers a sugar high. None of these should be a reflex response to a heavy meal.

Constipation and diarrhoea, decoded

People use "constipation" for many different things — passing stools daily but not feeling satisfied, going repeatedly, or simple discomfort. By definition, constipation means not passing stools for about three days, and it is not one homogeneous condition. There is normal-transit constipation (the bowel is fine but the person is constipated), slow-transit constipation (motions move through the bowel more slowly, sometimes due to thyroid or simply by birth), and — the one most often forgotten — pelvic floor dyssynergia.

Pelvic floor dyssynergia is potentially reversible: the act of passing motions is poorly coordinated, so instead of the rectum contracting while the sphincters relax, the opposite happens. Laxatives may not help here because it is an anatomical, mechanical issue. It needs to be identified by a doctor and then treated with biofeedback training programmes that use machines to make a subconscious act conscious — teaching people how to relax the sphincters and contract the abdomen at the same time. Someone may take laxatives for a very long time without benefit precisely because they are actually struggling with something else. In elderly patients who become constipated, a colonoscopy is warranted, because the chance of a growth or cancer is higher.

Constipation in children

In children, being constipated is often a normal part of growing up, and 90 to 95% of the time it is functional constipation that improves as they get older; undue worry is usually unnecessary, though it is still worth getting checked for one or two conditions. Two practical points help: do not overload children with water, because water is largely absorbed in the small intestine while constipation is predominantly a large-intestine problem; and consider posture. The Indian squatting position aligns the anatomy for passing stools better than the western commode. A western commode can be converted simply by placing two stools beside it to rest the feet on — a step or raised platform under the feet achieves the same alignment, and it is known to improve the act of passing motions for adults too.

Diet for constipation

Beyond posture, a high-fibre diet helps — and that mostly just means more vegetables and more fruit, which is enough without prescribing exact quantities. Additional fibre such as isabgol (psyllium) can help a little in the short term. These are not meant for the long term: anything taken for a long time risks habituation, where the body stops passing without it, so the aim is to keep people off medications as much as possible.

Diarrhoea

Diarrhoea is defined as passing more than three times a day. The first split is acute versus chronic. Acute diarrhoea (under about a week) is most often an infection, usually viral and self-limiting, and generally does not need antibiotics — those are reserved for people with fever or who are very unwell. Chronic diarrhoea is the greater concern: it is worked up to rule out endocrine causes such as thyroid, inflammatory bowel disease (ulcerative colitis and Crohn's), and occasionally tuberculosis. When basic tests are clear, it may be labelled a functional bowel disorder or IBS, where stress often plays a major role, so de-stressing and diet both make a difference — commonly by coming off dairy or identifying individual trigger foods (some people react to eggs). Prebiotics and probiotics are not generally needed; natural comfort foods like curd rice give the same effect with no downside.

Food fads versus facts

A large part of gut anxiety comes from internet advice. The recurring principle here is moderation and personalisation — different people react differently, and religiously avoiding foods often causes more harm than the food itself.

Does avoiding gluten help if you don't have celiac disease?

Mostly placebo. Only around 1 to 2% of people who avoid gluten without celiac disease genuinely benefit; the rest experience a placebo effect. Gluten is an important staple, especially in the north of India, and cutting it unnecessarily risks nutritional deficiencies. If you suspect gluten sensitivity, get tested rather than following a fad diet.

Is food allergy testing worth it?

Not recommended. The tests are not specific enough, and people end up avoiding all sorts of foods that may not be the cause. Many get tested without consulting a doctor, then avoid foods for vague reasons — one person with gas-related headaches actually had migraine. Intolerances such as celiac disease (wheat) and lactose intolerance, and true allergies to peanuts or seafood, are pathological conditions with proper symptoms and lab findings, which patients usually work out and avoid themselves.

Do spicy and oily foods increase acidity?

Partly true. There is no real science that spicy food increases acid production, and it varies from person to person, so it needs personalising. Oily food, however, does aggravate reflux: it relaxes the valve between the food pipe and stomach, letting acid travel back up. Spicy and oily food is generally not healthy and is better limited regardless.

Do rajma and chole cause gas and bloating?

Only in excess. Anything in moderation is fine. If you need the protein and are comfortable with them, there is no reason to view rajma and chole only as gas-producing foods — overdoing it is the problem, not the food.

What about onion and garlic?

Personalise it. Garlic generally tends to help, and onion should not be a problem for most people. If someone consistently reacts to them, it is better for that individual to avoid them — but you cannot apply one person's diet to everyone.

Is milk a problem?

For some. Milk is a complicated subject. Among people with troublesome symptoms, around 30 to 40% report feeling much better after stopping milk and milk products — partly because lactose digestion declines with age, and partly because many products are adulterated. This is not a blanket recommendation to stop milk; it is a trial for those with symptoms. Diabetics may also benefit from avoiding milk because of its contribution to sugar spikes. A practical test is to switch to black tea or coffee for a couple of weeks; if there is no change, milk is not the culprit.

Is whey protein causing your problems?

Sometimes. Many people who start the gym and whey protein together develop diarrhoea, and improve when they stop. That does not make all whey protein bad — the fix is often to build up gradually rather than jumping to three or five scoops at once. But for someone not training for professional competition, natural dietary protein is usually enough, and a reported figure of around 70% of protein products being adulterated — sometimes with lead or arsenic — is a serious reason for caution when consuming them daily.

Artificial sweeteners, lectins and detox drinks

Skip them. Artificial sweeteners are not helpful and are known to cause harm, with studies linking them to cancers; avoiding refined sugar itself matters far more. Lectins are over-discussed — well-cooked food in moderation should cause no problem, and avoiding them strips away nutritional value. Lemon water for detox is not helpful. Colon cleanses are strongly discouraged and can cause serious harm, with people ending up in emergencies. Apple cider vinegar shows no real benefit beyond placebo. Turmeric water and similar remedies do not help; in large quantities turmeric has itself been linked to liver failure, so only minimal amounts are safe.

Coffee is the exception among drinks. Black coffee and caffeine are known to be good for the liver, including fatty liver disease, where a proven study shows cups of coffee help. The catch is timing: keep coffee to the morning and avoid it in the afternoon and night, since it hinders good sleep. Coffee with milk is better avoided if you have other lifestyle issues. Green tea is far less certain — one cup a day at most, and not morning, noon and night; the doctor stopped drinking it after seeing a case of liver failure in a heavy green-tea consumer.

On snacking and meal patterns: constant snacking between meals is not particularly physiological — we are among the few animals that eat by the clock rather than when hungry. Whole fruit is better than juices or purées, because the fibre (and even the skin, as with mango) is valuable, and at least one fruit a day is worth having whenever you like. Fermented food is fine to include, roughly one meal a day. Deep-fried food should be avoided — the oils are reused and the process strips nutrition — while slow-cooked foods such as idli and upma are a better bet. Outside food can never match a home-cooked meal, where you control the ingredients and the quality of oil.

Warning signs and the three foundations of gut health

Some symptoms mean you should see a gastroenterologist promptly rather than reaching for a home remedy:

  • Bleeding — vomiting blood, or passing blood in the stool.
  • Unexplained weight loss without dieting or extra exercise, especially in people above 45 to 50.
  • Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) food or liquids.
  • Significant abdominal pain — anything rated above roughly 4 or 5 out of 10.
  • Chronic symptoms, such as loose motions persisting for 3 to 6 months.

Do not rely on home remedies for undiagnosed discomfort. It is genuinely hard, even for professionals, to interpret abdominal symptoms — some people who report "indigestion" are actually having a heart attack, pancreatitis or gallstones. Get checked first; if everything is clear, you can try whatever you like at home.

The take-home is three foundations. First, physical activity — being more active at least four or five days a week, with at least 20 minutes of any form of exercise. Second, a balanced diet built on good home-cooked meals, eaten mindfully. Third, mindfulness and stress reduction, which is closely tied to physical activity: a good run or workout brings stress levels down on its own. On meal frequency, well-balanced meals matter more than the number — one, two or three meals a day is fine as long as you cope well, with small frequent meals reserved for specific conditions like reflux. Intermittent fasting may benefit the gut by giving it rest, but the science is still emerging; it must be approached carefully by people with medical conditions such as diabetes, and sequential daily fasting is not necessarily good — roughly once a week is enough. Beyond these three foundations, most of the rest is supplementary, complementary, or most likely not needed.

This article summarises the professional views of a practising gastroenterologist on liver and gut health. It is for general education only and is not a substitute for personalised medical advice, diagnosis or treatment. Fatty liver, persistent digestive symptoms and warning signs such as bleeding, unexplained weight loss or difficulty swallowing should be assessed by a qualified doctor, and any medication or supplement — including PPIs, probiotics and fibre — should be used under professional supervision.
