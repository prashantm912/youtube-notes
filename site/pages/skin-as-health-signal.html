What Your Skin Is Telling You: Reading Health From the Outside In

Dermatologist Dr. Aanchal Panth, in practice for a decade, explains why most skin complaints start on the inside — and how stress, diet and everyday habits show up on your face long before you feel them.

At a glance

  • Skin is a diagnostic window. A trained eye reads stress, hormonal imbalance, iron or thyroid problems and even early insulin resistance from the face and feet before the patient says a word.
  • Recurring acne, worsening dark circles and dull skin should not be normalised or simply covered up — they are clues to a deeper trigger that needs addressing.
  • Health of skin is about luster and moisture, not colour. Dark skin can be healthy and radiant; fair skin can be dry and unhealthy.
  • No superfood fixes skin. A balanced local diet with enough protein does the work; excess sugar, salt, ultra-processed food, and — for acne-prone people — milk are the things to cut back.
  • Most people over-wash and over-treat. Hot showers, daily full-body soap and stacks of fancy products harm the skin more than they help.
  • Stress speaks through the body first — changed bowel movements, morning puffiness, redness and hair fall are early signals of rising cortisol.
  • The whole routine reduces to CMS: cleanse, moisturise, sunscreen. When in doubt, keep it simple and see a dermatologist.

Reading the skin: what the face reveals about health

A dermatologist attentively examining a patient can pick up a great deal before any complaint is voiced. Many of these signs trace back to the nervous system — increased sympathetic ("fight or flight") activity and autonomic dysfunction that a stressed body produces.

The signs of a stressed body

Stress shows first around the eyes: dark circles look more prominent, the eyes appear sunken, and the skin beneath them turns dry. Skin overall looks drier, dull and sometimes slightly pale. Dry lips are common — stressed people often pick at or lick their lips, leaving them dry and sometimes cracked at the corners. Hair fall is another giveaway; there is even a characteristic way people tie their hair when they are shedding it. Taken with overall demeanour, these markers signal that someone is going through a stressful period.

Red flags that point deeper

Some signs mean look inside the body. Certain skin changes raise suspicion of a metabolic or organic problem and prompt further blood tests.

  • Acanthosis nigricans — velvety darkening and thickening of the skin in body folds: the neck, underarms, groin, sometimes under the breast, and in severe cases the crook of the elbows and the sides of the forehead. Patients often describe their neck or underarms as looking "dirty." It points to insulin resistance, now very common because of inactivity, obesity and poor diet — and increasingly seen in children as young as 12 to 14.
  • Skin tags in the same areas prompt a check of insulin and blood sugar. People who have skin tags have a higher propensity to develop diabetes later, so they are treated as a prompt to monitor blood glucose and change the diet — lifestyle changes at this stage can push diabetes away.
  • Sudden severe acne, especially large eruptions along the jawline with hair thinning and increased facial hair, points to hyperandrogenism — a cluster of features (facial acne, scalp-hair thinning, excess facial and body hair in a male pattern, irregular periods) seen in women with excess androgen, often PCOS.
  • Pale, tired-looking skin in someone with low morning energy suggests iron deficiency; correcting the levels tends to improve the appearance.
  • Generalised dryness can point to thyroid abnormalities such as hypothyroidism.
  • The feet tell a lot about diabetes control — skin quality, prominence of vessels, any ulcers or signs of vasculitis.

Dark circles and dull skin: sensitive, not specific

Real signals, but never a single cause. In medicine some signs are "sensitive but not specific" — they tell you something is wrong without telling you exactly what. Dark circles are a complex, multifactorial condition, not one problem with one cream.

South Asian facial structure contributes a natural hollowing and slightly deep-set eyes, so shadowing makes circles look more prominent. Add puffiness, thinning skin that lets underlying vessels show through as a bluish tinge, wrinkling worsened by rubbing (often from allergies), and a tear-trough depression, and it becomes clear why no single product resolves it. Poor sleep does not cause dark circles but exacerbates existing ones, partly through reduced oxygen flow to the area. Iron deficiency, excess salt or sugar, dehydration, a night of binge drinking, viral infection or any stressful event can all deepen them the next day. Some dark circles are simply genetic and hard to treat — even a healthy person can have them.

Dull skin works the same way. When someone says their skin has suddenly lost its glow and no longer reflects light as it should, that points toward stress, hormonal imbalance or nutritional deficiency, and warrants a detailed history. Dull skin, dark circles and dry lips are best read not as single entities but as clues for digging deeper.

Colour is not health — luster is

The colour of skin has nothing to do with its health. The moisture-retaining capacity and the amount of oil the skin produces determine its quality far more than its shade. Healthy skin exists in every colour: dark skin can be shiny, healthy and lustrous, while fair skin can be dry and unhealthy — much as a person can be heavier and healthy, or thin and unhealthy.

This is why "bright" or "even-toned" skin is not the same as "fair." What people perceive as brightness is really luster: light reflection, moisture-holding capacity and healthy oiliness. A sudden patchy darkening in certain areas of the face is different — that may signal post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation or a pattern like acanthosis nigricans, and is worth examining.

Acne is not something to cover up

Treat the trigger, not the surface. The sebaceous follicular unit behaves almost like an organ of its own — highly responsive to hormones, sensitive to insulin levels and to the food you eat. When that balance is off, acne keeps coming. Topical creams and moisturisers will not solve it until the triggers are found and addressed.

An occasional pimple — one or two eruptions every couple of months that clear without leaving marks — is fine. But continuously developing new acne, or even a few that leave behind dark spots or scarring, absolutely needs evaluation. Many people dismiss teenage acne as a passing hormonal phase, then seek help at 20 to 25 once scars have set in — and once there is a scar, there is little that can be done. The aim is always to prevent new acne: understand the triggers, follow a simple routine and make the lifestyle changes that reduce eruptions.

Hormones and the menstrual cycle

Because the follicular unit is so sensitive to hormones, the monthly cycle shows on the skin. After ovulation around day 14, a surge in progesterone increases oil production and predisposes to acne — which is why many people report one or two pimples appearing 7 to 10 days before their period, on otherwise clear skin.

Food, and the skin it builds

There is no superfood — only a balanced diet. A healthy balanced diet with a good portion of protein, carbohydrate and fat is what skin needs; no single drink or food "fixes everything." If you are healthy overall, it reflects on your skin and hair. Eat what is local and available rather than chasing marketed imported produce, include some nuts and seeds without obsessing, and start meals with a bowl of salad — small additions matter more than a complete lifestyle overhaul.

What to cut back

  • Excess sugar harms skin short and long term. It produces advanced glycation end products: sugar binds to collagen fibres and makes them brittle, slowing collagen regeneration and making lines and wrinkles more prominent. Keep added sugar under about 25 grams a day.
  • Excess salt causes water retention and puffiness of the face, alongside other health effects.
  • Ultra-processed food — biscuits, cookies, chips, reused and ultra-refined oil — offers little nutrition, causes inflammation and does nothing good for skin or gut. Reduce it rather than banning it outright; a cheat meal once a week is fine if it is not too fried or processed.

Avoidance itself can be harmful. A pure avoidance mindset brings its own stress and negativity — constant worry over every meal. Balance is the point: if a child wants a packet of chips once in a while, that is fine; just don't finish the whole packet or stock the pantry with unhealthy food.

The milk question

There is a real correlation with acne. Not everyone who drinks milk gets acne, but for a sudden eruption that resists regular treatment, cutting milk is worth trying. Milk raises insulin-like growth factor, which stimulates the oil gland to make more oil, increasing skin oiliness and worsening acne. Skimmed, fat-free milk has an even stronger correlation — so it is not the fat that is the problem. The combination of milk and sugar in Indian sweets (mithai) is a particular aggravator.

Because milk is a valuable protein source, especially for vegetarians, complete elimination is not always necessary — even cutting down helps improve acne.

Vegan diets and the B12 trap

Watch the B12 supplement. A vegan diet lacks vitamin B12, which then has to be supplemented. Vitamin B12 supplements and injections can trigger sudden acne eruptions — a documented effect. They shift the skin's anaerobic environment, increase the growth of acne-forming bacteria and raise porphyrins on the skin, unbalancing the microbiome. Anyone taking B12 for a deficiency should be alert to this.

Gluten is not the villain

Myth. Gluten has been villainised for no good reason. In rare conditions — celiac disease and dermatitis herpetiformis — avoiding it makes a real difference, and people genuinely allergic to gluten (a small minority) should avoid it. For everyone else, gluten is a staple; it does not cause acne or mess up skin. Go ahead and eat it.

Delivery apps and pollution

Food-delivery apps are convenient but have quietly reduced home-cooked meals and removed the friction that once curbed cravings — a 10 p.m. ice-cream urge that would once have passed by morning can now be satisfied in ten minutes, so cravings are indulged rather than fought. Air pollution matters too: pollutant particles irritate the skin and create oxidative stress, driving free-radical damage, dryness, darkening and premature ageing (all disease begins with inflammation, which these particles cause on the skin's surface). To protect, use an air purifier where possible, add an antioxidant serum to reduce oxidative stress, moisturise to form a layer between skin and pollutants, and use a zinc-oxide-based sunscreen — the same barrier-forming zinc oxide found in diaper-rash creams — to trap particles before they irritate.

The bathing and hygiene habits people get wrong

Hot showers and daily soap

Both dry the skin out. Hot water extracts oil from the skin's surface and dries it further — best avoided by anyone with eczema, psoriasis or dry skin. Lukewarm is good enough; a cold shower is hard to sustain, especially in winter. If hot showers are non-negotiable, keep them very short — around two minutes — and apply coconut oil before stepping in.

There is a widespread misconception that you have not bathed unless you have lathered foaming soap over your whole body every day. You have not: skin needs soap only in the groin and underarms daily. For the rest of the body, water alone is enough, and soap once a week is plenty. Think of dry skin like a dry tabletop — dust brushes off easily; you only need to scrub where there is oil or grease. Following a short, soap-light shower with a moisturiser applied while the skin is still damp brings a noticeable improvement in how dry the skin feels.

Bathe at night, and change your clothes

A night bath beats a morning one. If you bathe once a day, do it at night. Dust collected on the skin through the day otherwise settles into the body folds overnight and irritates them — a particular problem for people with allergies, eczema, psoriasis and atopic dermatitis. Coming home, bathing, changing into night clothes and sleeping controls allergies and itching far better.

Why night clothes and clean pillow covers matter. Day clothes carry the day's dust into your bed, transferring it to the mattress, pillow and blanket — bedding that gets washed far less often than clothes. Overnight hair oil coats the pillow cover and transfers to the face, aggravating acne in acne-prone skin. Changing into fresh night clothes also cues the brain into rest mode, part of sleep hygiene alongside dimming the lights before bed. Fresh linen, and a little lavender oil, can further improve sleep quality.

Coconut oil: good, with conditions

Excellent on the body, cautious on the face. Coconut oil is a great emollient — prescribed for eczema and psoriasis, where a layer of moisturiser can be sealed in with coconut oil, and plain coconut oil works well too. For hair it is a good pre-wash conditioning agent: leave it on for half an hour to an hour before washing to reduce frizz and dryness. But it is somewhat comedogenic, so people with acne-prone, oily or combination skin may not enjoy it on the face.

Stress, rest and the skin

Stress is like drawing a bow: pulling the string back is useful for shooting an arrow, but if it stays pulled back all the time, the bow breaks. You have to let the string relax again and again — in neurological terms, moving from sympathetic back to parasympathetic. People who plan to "rest after retirement" have it wrong; the body needs rest every day. A good night's sleep should provide it, but most people are not sleeping enough.

Scrolling is not resting. A crucial skill is taking small rests in the middle of the day — five minutes of breath work, even two minutes of eyes shut, deep breathing and relaxed muscles — instead of going from stress to stress to stress. Picking up the phone on a break and seeing a friend's holiday or a new car keeps the body stressed rather than relaxed. Over-stimulation is a leading driver of stress and anxiety, so a real break needs a proper definition.

The body signals before you feel it

The body registers stress well before the mind does — waiting until you feel stressed means you are already late. The gut talks first: changed bowel movements signal that the autonomic system is shifting. The skin follows. Morning clues of rising cortisol include puffiness around the eyes and skin that looks stretchy, dry, or slightly red and irritated. Cortisol disrupts the skin barrier, lowers the skin's water-holding capacity, can trigger acne, and leaves the cheeks looking red or the acne more inflamed than usual — a sign of poor rest or unmanaged stress.

Hair fall as a stress signal

A major indicator of stress. Hair is a vestigial organ, not required for life, so under stress the body diverts blood and circulation to major organs and away from the skin and hair. The result is telogen effluvium — hair shedding in chunks — appearing two to three months after a stressful event such as dengue, typhoid, a viral fever, exams or childbirth (postpartum hair loss). Losing 50 to 60, even up to 100 hairs a day is normal; beyond that needs evaluation. The correlation is strong enough that treating a patient's migraines can stop their hair fall once the headaches settle.

Recovery is real

Just as stress dulls the skin, coming out of it restores it. Patients seen a year after a stressful period — including grief over a lost loved one — are almost unrecognisable: better moisturised, more luminous, better at reflecting light, with lighter dark circles and more hydrated skin. As the mind recovers, the skin recovers too, typically over six months to a year.

Filters, celebrities and a skewed mirror

The flawless skin online is not real. Behind film actors' poreless faces sit makeup, photography, lighting and Photoshop, and above all extreme discipline plus resources — nutritionists who plan exactly how to satisfy a craving without a sugar spike, and a whole team to produce that look. In real life, no one's skin is flawless, poreless and uniformly bright at all times. Normal skin has pores, fine hair, slight unevenness, faint dark circles and lips that are not uniformly pink. Comparing your skin in the mirror to a screen is comparing against something that does not exist.

What genuinely impresses is good skin at 45 and beyond — that reflects years of consistency and discipline, unlike good skin in the twenties, which takes little effort. A healthy, active life shows: fitness and skin can hold up well at an older age.

Filters distort self-perception. Phones now come with built-in filters, so the filtered photo becomes what people believe they look like — creating a dissonance with the mirror. Patients arrive convinced their skin has dulled, then show a heavily filtered photo as the baseline. Because a filter makes you look like a slightly better version of yourself rather than someone else, you don't dissociate from it — you start chasing an unattainable standard. The concern is greatest for children, whose self-image and self-worth are still forming, and much of what they learn comes from watching how their mothers view and present their own skin. Build self-worth, seek help for genuine skin conditions, and draw the line between healthy self-care and obsession.

Much anxiety comes from a gap between perceived and actual reality — and both anxiety and depression trick the mind into believing a problem is permanent. It is not. If your skin is poor now, that does not mean it will be poor in six months; there is a long list of things you can do, and your entire metabolism can change from the inside in that time. Which is also why quick results are the wrong goal: skin turnover, new cell growth, fading spots and lifestyle change all take time, and results that come fast rarely last. Give the skin about three months.

A routine you can actually keep: CMS

Cleanse, moisturise, sunscreen. The take-home for anyone confused about skin care is CMS. The full framework is cleanse, then treat, then moisturise, then protect with sunscreen — but the minimal version covers most needs: a gentle cleanser and sunscreen in the morning, and a cleanser and moisturiser at night. To do more, add an antioxidant serum in the morning and a retinol serum at night before the moisturiser, boosting collagen and softening fine lines. This is the same for men as for women.

Men's skin ages differently

Men tend to have thicker skin with more sebaceous glands, so it is oilier, shows fewer early fine lines, and facial hair helps shield it from UV — meaning men often show slower initial ageing, though they develop deeper wrinkles over time. Women's skin, by contrast, is shaped by monthly and life-stage hormonal shifts, with a sense of sudden ageing common in the perimenopausal 40s and 50s. This is why a wife doing an elaborate routine may still envy a husband who does nothing — much of the difference is anatomical and hormonal.

The core steps stay the same across ages; the products change.

Prefer a lighter, gel-based moisturiser for oilier skin; see a dermatologist once to set a routine if acne-prone. Choose a thicker moisturiser with ceramides and hyaluronic acid, plus barrier-repair and soothing ingredients like allantoin and panthenol.

The recurring lesson is that skin care is misunderstood largely because people overdo it: the correct product, in the correct quantity, at the correct frequency, decides how skin responds.

Movement compounds the benefit

Self-care reinforces itself — better sleep improves skin, which improves self-image, which improves how you eat and train. Exercise helps skin not only directly but through the involuntary choices that follow a voluntary workout: after a hard 30-minute run, most people won't reach for the chocolate or sugary drink that would undo it. Starting to exercise tends to pull healthier eating along with it.
