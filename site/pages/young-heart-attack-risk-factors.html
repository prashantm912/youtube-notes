Why Young Indians Are Getting Heart Attacks

Reports of people in their thirties and forties dying of heart attacks have become disturbingly common in India. Cardiologist Dr. Vijay, who practises in Bangalore, explains what is driving the shift to younger ages, how to catch coronary disease early, and the lifestyle and medical steps that lower the risk — from screening tests and blood-pressure targets to diet, exercise, and the emergency response that can save a life.

At a glance

  • The risk factors behind heart disease are not new — their frequency has risen over the past decade. The three most impactful recent drivers are lifestyle change, rising chronic disease (obesity, diabetes, hypertension), and stress.
  • "Heart disease" is broader than a heart attack: it includes muscle disorders (cardiomyopathies), rhythm disorders (arrhythmias), coronary artery disease, and peripheral artery disease.
  • Indians develop coronary artery disease 5 to 10 years earlier than Western populations, largely a genetic and ethnic tendency.
  • Screening can start at 20 to 25 with a family history and around 30 without risk factors; intervals tighten with age, and specific cardiac tests — not just a routine blood test — are needed.
  • Diabetes is called a "silent killer" that can damage vessels for years before symptoms appear; controlling it protects the heart, kidneys and stents.
  • For most people LDL ("bad") cholesterol should sit below 100, and below 55 after a cardiac event; triglycerides below 150.
  • Per World Heart Federation 2022 data there is no safe limit for alcohol, now regarded as a carcinogen; smoking raises cardiovascular risk two- to four-fold.
  • Mild plaque can be reduced with good lifestyle changes — a warning to act on, not a reason to despair.
  • In a suspected heart attack the first hour is the "golden hour"; angioplasty is the gold-standard treatment and is now usually done through the wrist.

Why heart attacks are striking younger Indians

News of people below 40 to 45 dying of heart attacks has become routine, and Dr. Vijay attributes much of it to how people now live. The contributing factors are lifestyle, demographic and environmental influences, together with family history. On the lifestyle side he lists unhealthy, highly processed and high-fat diets — especially trans fats found in processed foods with heavy cheese and butter — sugary soft drinks, a sedentary routine with rising screen time and phone addiction, more tobacco, alcohol and substance use, growing obesity, diabetes and hypertension, and a lack of sleep he singles out for emphasis. Genetics play a part too.

His central point is that none of these factors is new. What has changed is their incidence: each has increased over the past few years, and that rise is why heart attacks are climbing in the young. Asked to name the three most impactful drivers of the past decade, he points to change in lifestyle, the rise in chronic diseases such as obesity, diabetes and hypertension, and stress.

What counts as "heart disease"

The cardiovascular system, he explains, is the heart muscle, its walls, and the blood vessels. Disease can strike at any of these levels, so heart disease is far more than a heart attack (myocardial infarction):

  • Cardiomyopathies — disorders of the muscle, whether enlargement (hypertrophic cardiomyopathy) or weakening (dilated cardiomyopathy).
  • Arrhythmias — an abnormal increase or decrease in heart rate or rhythm.
  • Coronary artery disease — disease of the blood vessels supplying the heart.
  • Peripheral artery disease — the same vessel disease occurring away from the heart.

Modifiable versus non-modifiable risk

Risk factors are divided into those a person can change and those they cannot. Modifiable factors include diet, smoking and alcohol, acquired high cholesterol, being overweight, and raised blood sugar or blood pressure — all of which can be improved through lifestyle changes and, where needed, medication. Non-modifiable factors include age (coronary artery disease rises exponentially as people get older), gender (post-menopausal women become more prone), and race or ethnicity. On that last point, Dr. Vijay notes that Indians experience the onset of coronary artery disease 5 to 10 years earlier than Western populations — a difference he attributes to genetic and ethnic factors.

Women's specific heart risk

Beyond the traditional risk factors shared with men, women carry additional, sex-specific risks. Dr. Vijay highlights the rising rate of polycystic ovarian disease or syndrome (PCOS), and pregnancy-related conditions such as eclampsia, pre-eclampsia, gestational diabetes and gestational hypertension.

The menopause is a turning point. Before it, the hormone estrogen has a protective effect on the arteries; after menopause that protection is withdrawn, leaving women markedly more susceptible to coronary artery disease. Layered on top of the usual factors — lifestyle, diabetes, hypertension and the rest — this is why heart disease is rising in women.

Oral contraceptive pills and clotting. Oral contraceptive pills are thrombogenic — these hormonal preparations increase the blood's clotting capacity, raising the tendency to form a clot anywhere in the body. Dr. Vijay notes that in any woman presenting with a thrombosis — pulmonary embolism, deep vein thrombosis, or stroke — one of the histories routinely elicited is the use of such pills.

Screening and early detection

A recurring theme is that a routine blood test is not enough to screen the heart; cardiac screening uses specific tests, and the timing depends on risk. With a family history of familial hypercholesterolaemia, the National Heart and Lung Institute suggests starting as early as 11 to 12 years. Most other evidence points to starting at 20 to 25 where there is a family history of any heart disease, high cholesterol, diabetes or hypertension, and around 30 for low-risk people with no risk factors.

How often to screen

For people without risk factors, Dr. Vijay gives the following intervals; those with risk factors or a strong tendency to heart disease should be screened at much shorter intervals:

Which tests, and why the full lipid profile matters

At 20 or earlier, screening centres on a lipid profile — but not total cholesterol alone. Some labs report only total cholesterol as part of a package checkup, whereas it is the split components that matter: HDL (good cholesterol), LDL (bad cholesterol), and triglycerides (fats). Newer markers of coronary artery disease — lipoprotein(a) and apolipoprotein B — are also important and should be included. Alongside the lipid profile, blood sugars and an ECG are done at this stage.

An echocardiogram is added when there is a strong family history of muscle-related disease such as hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, which often produces no symptoms and can cause sudden cardiac death — including the person who collapses during a gym workout. The echo shows the heart's structure, function and muscle thickness. From 30, sugars and cholesterol are standard; from 40, Dr. Vijay favours ECG and echo, with or without a treadmill test, plus cholesterol and sugars.

The limits of a treadmill test — and the CT calcium score

Risk is assessed with a cardiac risk score (the ASCVD score) based on factors such as family history, smoking status and cholesterol. For people in the intermediate-risk category, Dr. Vijay recommends a CT coronary calcium score, a test that has come into use in recent years. He stresses it because of a real limitation of the treadmill: about a 10% false-negative rate, meaning slowly developing blocks can be missed. He describes a patient who walked well on the treadmill with a normal ECG and no symptoms, only to have a heart attack a week later. Where family history is strong or a person has risk factors such as smoking, substance abuse, diabetes or hypertension, he suggests getting at least one CT coronary calcium score — with or without an angiogram — once past 40.

The metabolic drivers: diabetes, blood pressure, obesity and cholesterol

Diabetes: the silent killer

Diabetes is a silent killer. It is the disease which can affect any organ in the body.

Dr. Vijay, cardiologist

Diabetes can strike the heart at every level: weakening the muscle (diabetic cardiomyopathy, which can lead to heart failure), affecting the coronary arteries and causing a heart attack, disturbing the conducting system, or damaging the kidneys and secondarily the heart — the cardiorenal syndrome, in which diabetes and kidney disease behave, in his phrase, "like a brother and sister." A dangerous feature is the early "honeymoon phase," when the body compensates and a person can seem well for years without medication, even as silent microvascular damage is under way. That vessel-wall injury (microvascular dysfunction) is the base on which cholesterol and then calcium deposit, gradually narrowing the artery like a pipe until blood supply is hampered — first noticed as chest pain or breathlessness on exertion, which he calls a warning sign of heart attack.

Control matters even after treatment. Take two diabetic patients who undergo angioplasty on the same day for a single-vessel block: the one with good control (HbA1c under 7, ideally 6.5 to 7) keeps the stent open far longer, while the poorly controlled patient (HbA1c around 8 or 9) may return within two to three years with stent thrombosis or restenosis. Dr. Vijay notes the common denial — patients who insist their sugar only rises when they eat mango — and stresses that after a certain point diet alone is not enough and medication becomes necessary.

Hypertension: the pressure that damages everywhere

Blood pressure is the pressure exerted on the vessel wall; as it rises it damages the protective endothelial lining throughout the body. Faced with chronic pressure overload, the heart muscle first compensates by thickening (concentric left ventricular hypertrophy on the echo), then — like an elastic band stretched too far — begins to weaken. Hypertension can drive heart failure, coronary artery disease, peripheral artery disease, stroke and renal dysfunction.

Diagnosis follows a protocol: a single reading is not enough. Dr. Vijay wants a minimum of three readings on three different occasions, both arms checked at the first visit, the patient rested calmly for 10 minutes with feet on the ground and no caffeine in the preceding half hour. Hypertension is now defined at any age as above 140/90; recent studies flag 130/90 as "high normal," a prompt to begin lifestyle changes. On when to medicate, he is measured:

  • At 140/90 on three occasions, even in a person of 30 or 40, he first advises lifestyle changes, home monitoring and review in three months rather than immediate medication.
  • Above 160/100, medication has to be started — lifestyle change alone will not deliver that degree of correction.

Misconception. Many people fear anti-hypertensive medicines or prefer alternative remedies over "English medicine." Dr. Vijay points out that these are time-tested drugs taken by enormous numbers of people, that there are now more than 8 to 10 groups with known side-effect profiles, and that many do more than lower pressure — ACE inhibitors and ARBs also protect the kidneys in diabetes by helping prevent nephropathy, while sacubitril/valsartan he calls a near-miracle drug in heart failure with reduced ejection fraction. He also notes beta blockers are sometimes prescribed after a heart attack for rate control and remodelling, not for blood pressure at all.

Obesity: it is the fat you cannot see

Obesity is defined by a body mass index above 30. The extra load makes the heart work harder and can lead to secondary hypertrophy; obesity can also cause pulmonary hypertension (cor pulmonale), partly via obstructive sleep apnoea, and is itself a state of chronic inflammation that drives coronary artery disease, stroke and heart failure. In men, abdominal obesity is measured by a waist-hip ratio above 0.9. The danger is not the visible outer fat but the metabolically active fat inside the organs; more fat cells mean more insulin resistance, and so a greater chance of developing diabetes, hypertension and heart disease.

Cholesterol and triglycerides: the numbers to know

HDL (good cholesterol) is often low in Indians, but studies show raising it with medicines gives no cardiovascular benefit — it is best raised through brisk exercise and weight loss. LDL (bad cholesterol) drives the events, and the targets are clear: below 100 in a person without heart disease, and below 55 in anyone who has had a cardiac event such as a heart attack or stroke. Triglycerides — a fat that indirectly reflects insulin resistance — should stay below 150; above 400 to 500 they raise the risk of pancreatitis, a dreaded complication.

Myth. Patients often arrive fearing that statins will give them diabetes. Dr. Vijay's answer: at very high dose over a long duration there may be a slightly increased risk, but he frames the balance as roughly 99.9% benefit against about 0.001% risk. Statins such as atorvastatin, rosuvastatin and pitavastatin lower baseline cholesterol by about 25 to 35% depending on dose (from 10 to 40 or 80 mg); fenofibrates are used to bring down triglycerides. For familial hypercholesterolaemia where LDL can reach 400 and statins are insufficient, PCSK9 inhibitors — a recent injectable advance — can drive LDL as low as 20, though at present each injection may cost around ₹90,000.

Eating for the heart: oils, foods and supplements

The Indian diet leans heavily on spices, ghee and butter, so cooking oil is a natural place to start. Dr. Vijay's general rule: any oil in a lesser amount is safer. Beyond quantity, he points to the smoke point — the temperature at which an oil starts burning and releasing harmful substances. Because Indian cooking involves a lot of frying rather than the Western habit of simmering or saucing, higher-smoke-point oils are better for the heat.

He suggests it is reasonable to rotate two or three oils — one or two for simmering and dressing, others for frying — to draw on different nutrients. On the debate over "seed oils," he notes that canola and corn are seed oils that also carry omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids; he ranks vegetable seed oils among the best after olive oil, followed by refined peanut, safflower and sunflower oil. On dairy fats: ghee has a high smoke point and suits frying, whereas butter has a lower one and is better kept for dressings; skimmed dairy is acceptable for the heart.

Heart-healthy foods

  • Colourful fruits and vegetables, valued for their antioxidants — a "glowing heart" to match glowing skin.
  • Legumes, whole grains, and fatty fish such as salmon and mackerel for their omega fatty acids.
  • Flax seeds (omega-3 and omega-6), which can be powdered into wheat flour for chapati or roti.
  • Chia seeds, high in fibre with water-retaining capacity and omega-3.
  • Nuts — about three to four almonds (badam) and one or two walnuts a day, walnut being especially good for the heart.
  • Avoid processed food and high-butter dairy products.

Not shown to help. Asked whether soaking almonds rather than eating them dry brings any benefit, Dr. Vijay says no.

Add-on, not a replacement. Supplements such as fish oil, omega-3 capsules and coenzyme Q10 can lower cholesterol, triglycerides and blood pressure, but they are not sufficient alone. They should be an add-on to lifestyle changes — never a substitute for them — and then they work better.

Exercise, stress and the myths about sudden collapse

How much exercise, and at what intensity

From a cardiology perspective, the minimum is 150 minutes a week — about 30 minutes a day, five days a week. Dr. Vijay recommends a mixed programme: roughly three days of aerobic (cardiac) training to build stamina, stay lean and reduce fat, and at least two days of resistance training, which increases muscle strength and mass, improves insulin sensitivity and thereby lowers diabetes risk while improving posture. Intensity should be controlled so that heart rate does not frequently exceed the maximum for the person's age, calculated as 220 minus age — for a 40-year-old, a maximum of 180.

A raised resting heart rate reflects sympathetic overactivity, while athletes may sit at 35 to 40 because of high vagal tone; a lower resting rate is associated with greater longevity. Encouragingly, people with a high resting heart rate can bring it down with regular exercise.

Stress, yoga and the mind

Yoga, meditation and mindfulness are described as strongly protective, because mental health now matters as much as physical health. Chronic stress — from work-life imbalance and family pressures — is a significant driver of cardiac disease, hypertension and diabetes in this generation. Mechanistically, stress triggers an adrenergic overdrive; the surge of stress hormones damages the vessel lining microscopically, and over time that site inflames and begins to form a block. Stress can also rupture the membrane over an existing 40 to 50% block, precipitating a heart attack. Calming the mind delays the onset of all of these.

Smoking

Smoking raises cardiovascular risk two- to four-fold, and second-hand smoke exposes non-smokers to the same coronary risk. Nicotine, tar and other chemicals damage the endothelium, accelerate atherosclerosis and injure the scavenger cells in the vessel wall, driving formation of lipid-core plaque. On recovery, at one year after quitting the risk falls to about 50%, and it may take 10 to 15 years to approach a pre-smoking state — the textbook figure being about 20 years to resemble a non-smoker. His emphasis: quitting at any age helps — beneficial at 60, at 70, and even more so at 20.

Alcohol

No safe limit. Earlier studies suggested moderate alcohol was good for the heart, but per World Heart Federation 2022 data there is no safe limit, and alcohol is now considered a carcinogen. It harms the heart in three main ways: weakening the muscle over time (alcoholic cardiomyopathy, leading to heart failure); triggering arrhythmia — atrial fibrillation — after binge drinking, the "holiday heart syndrome" named for weekend and holiday bingeing, in which heart rate can climb to 300; and, with long-term use, coronary artery disease. This is why an apparently fit person who exercises can collapse after a single binge. For longevity and health, the safe limit is zero.

Gym culture, steroids, fasting and protein

When fit, muscular people die suddenly during workouts, several factors may be at play. Some carry undiagnosed genetic conditions such as channelopathies — invisible on the outside and detectable only with in-depth genetic evaluation — that exercise can precipitate into fatal arrhythmias. Anabolic steroids, taken for appearance, promote not only skeletal but cardiac muscle growth, raising the risk of heart failure and arrhythmias; coronary artery disease is cited as about 3% higher in those taking such supplements. Poor sleep, unusual diets (intermittent fasting, keto, heavy fried animal protein) and severe dehydration compound the danger — dehydration in particular being implicated in gym deaths.

Not the exercise itself. The lesson is that being fit does not by itself protect against sudden death; the culprit is usually an underlying condition, steroid use, dehydration or diet — not the act of exercising.

Short-term gain, long-term risk. Intermittent fasting shows good short-term results in uncontrolled diabetes and obesity — lowering LDL and insulin requirements and improving sugar control. But long-term studies are cited showing 91% of those with underlying heart disease (a prior heart attack or heart failure) dying of cardiovascular disease, so the long-term outcome is not good for that group. On protein, there is no direct effect on the heart, but very high intake burdens the kidneys and gut — bloating and renal strain — which can affect the heart secondarily.

Infection, not the vaccine. On the fear that COVID vaccines caused heart deaths in young men, Dr. Vijay cites research in about 21,800 patients in Europe showing that people who had COVID-19 infection had more than a twofold increased risk of coronary artery disease for the first three years afterwards, and an ICMR study finding no relation between the COVID vaccine and coronary artery disease. He notes the vaccine question is still not fully settled for every subgroup, but that current reports point to the infection — with its tendency to increase clot formation — rather than the vaccine as the driver of post-COVID heart disease.

If a heart attack strikes: emergency care and reversing early plaque

After a heart attack, every minute counts, and the first hour is the "golden hour" — the earlier treatment is given, the more heart muscle is saved. Where a cardiologist is not immediately available, as in some district or town settings, clot-dissolving anticoagulant injections such as streptokinase, tenecteplase or reteplase can be given once bleeding risk is ruled out. Where a catheterisation lab is accessible, the patient should be taken there at once.

An emergency tablet packet. Dr. Vijay suggests keeping three tablets on hand to give immediately when a heart attack is suspected: aspirin, sold as Disprin, 325 mg; clopidogrel, four 75 mg tablets; and atorvastatin 40 mg. This is general information from the interview, not a personal prescription — anyone at risk should confirm suitability, dosing and contraindications with their own doctor in advance, and emergency medical care should be sought without delay.

In a heart attack the first step is an angiogram — a minimally invasive procedure that has evolved over the years. Where 10 to 15 years ago it was done through the thigh, now 95 to 99% is done through the hand: a small catheter is passed from the wrist up to the aorta and heart, the vessel is engaged, contrast is injected, and the whole angiogram takes around 10 to 15 minutes. If a block is found, angioplasty is often performed in the same sitting — a wire and balloon open the block and a medicated stent is placed. This is the gold-standard treatment after a heart attack and takes roughly half an hour to an hour.

Recovery after angioplasty

Patients typically spend about 24 hours in the ICU — the first day carries a slightly higher risk of stent thrombosis — then move to the ward and are often discharged the next day. Two to three days of rest follows, meaning ordinary routine activities but no walks or heavy lifting; after a week, routine activity resumes. Exercise restarts after a week with a short walk, building gradually to brisk walking. The main caution is to avoid heavy lifting immediately — no more than 5 to 10 kg for a while — and to pause after each floor when climbing stairs.

Who should take low-dose aspirin

Not for everyone. An older belief held that low-dose aspirin benefited everyone over 40 or 50; recent evidence says otherwise, because in low-risk people the bleeding risk — particularly gastric irritation and GI bleeds — outweighs the benefit. Aspirin is reserved for high-risk people: those over about 60 to 70 with a previous cardiac issue, strong family history, diabetes, hypertension or smoking. Those already on aspirin should continue; anyone not started should discuss it with a cardiologist rather than beginning it on their own.

Early plaque can be reversed

A closing note of encouragement: a mild plaque that is not yet blocking flow can definitely be reduced with good lifestyle changes — plaque volume and burden can come down. Dr. Vijay urges patients not to be discouraged by a 20 to 30% plaque on a CT scan, but to treat it as a warning and a motivation to adopt a good lifestyle and prevent future events. His parting message returns to fundamentals: healthy food, being regularly active, managing stress and good sleep — these four things reduce the risk of heart disease and much else.
