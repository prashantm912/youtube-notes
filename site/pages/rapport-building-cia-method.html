The CIA Rapport Cycle: Two Questions, One Validating Statement

A former CIA intelligence officer breaks down the agency-taught conversational cycle that rapidly builds trust and influence — why it makes people feel interesting and understood, how it lets you learn everything while revealing nothing, and the manipulative edge to watch for.

Key ideas

  • Most people undermine their own likability by spending conversations talking about themselves — their history, experience, qualifications and ideas.
  • The CIA teaches a simple process for rapidly building trust and influence: ask two questions, make one validating statement, then repeat the cycle.
  • Asking questions and validating another person's ideas subconsciously makes them think you are like them, and makes them feel interesting, important and relevant — producing a dopamine rush that makes them feel good around you.
  • The cycle produces what intelligence officers call informational superiority: you learn a great deal about the other person while revealing nothing real about yourself.
  • Because the human brain is predictable, the process works in any language and at any age — with clients, customers, potential dates, in-laws and business partners.
  • The same effectiveness is what makes it ethically double-edged: the validating statements can be entirely fabricated, and some people find the pointed questioning uncomfortable.

The cycle: how the technique works

The former officer describes his pre-CIA self as someone who thought he was a good conversationalist and an above-average social talent — until training revealed that he spent most of his time talking about himself. What the agency taught him instead was a very simple, repeatable process:

  1. Ask a question about the other person.
  2. Ask a second question — often a follow-up that goes deeper into their answer.
  3. Make one validating statement that affirms what they shared, typically by pointing to a similarity with your own life.
  4. Repeat the cycle.

The cycle is flexible rather than rigid. In the demonstration described below, the final round used one question plus a follow-up before the validating statement — the structure bends to the conversation while the question–question–validate rhythm stays intact.

Why it works: the psychology of feeling interesting

According to the former officer, the process of asking questions and validating other people's ideas works subconsciously. It makes the other person think that you are like them, and it makes them feel that they are interesting, important and relevant. That feeling triggers a dopamine rush, and the dopamine makes them feel good being around you.

What most people actually want from a conversation is for someone to be interested in them, to hold space for them, and to reflect them back — and to discover little sparks of similarity. There is nothing people love more than the sense that another person understands them. The cycle systematically manufactures exactly that experience: interest through questions, understanding through validation, similarity through the way the validating statement is framed.

Because the human brain responds to this predictably, the technique is not situation-bound. The former officer describes it as a systematic process that works all the time — with clients, with customers, with possible future dates, with in-laws, with business partners — in any language and at any age.

The cycle in practice: a live demonstration

The former officer demonstrated the technique in a live conversation with a woman he knew only a little about — that she was a mother who had been through a divorce. The demonstration shows several tactical details worth noting.

Meet them where they are

His opening question — what did you do first thing this morning with your kids — turned out not to fit her actual morning: one son was in college and the other was at his father's house. Rather than letting her substitute a hypothetical example, he insisted on starting from her real situation: "I need to meet you where you are." The follow-up became: what is it like to wake up in an empty house when you know you're a parent?

Validate by finding the similarity

Her answer — a mix of sadness at the invisible string connecting her to her children and relief at not having to make lunches by 7 a.m. — was met with a validating statement built on similarity: he said she had just described what he wonders about his own future, that when his children leave he expects to feel both sad and happy.

Keep cycling deeper

The next rounds followed the same shape: questions about what her mornings mean for her work productivity and what her rhythm looks like, then — when she mentioned starting her day with a protein smoothie — a validating statement claiming he also starts his day with a protein smoothie and prefers vegan proteins for digestibility and long-term muscle growth. Three cycles in total, and the rapport was visibly building.

What it felt like on the receiving end

Asked how the exchange felt, she reported the intended effect alongside an honest note of discomfort. It felt like a connection — "you're relating to things, you're finding the similarities, not the differences" — like being understood, like wanting to share more, even like she would accept a date. But her first instinct had been wariness: it initially felt like a stranger at a bar working an angle, and she noted that she personally dislikes talking about herself when asked pointed questions, which made the position awkward.

Informational superiority: the asymmetry underneath

The twist the former officer revealed afterwards is the real point of the technique for an intelligence officer. Every validating statement he had made was fabricated: he does not have protein in the morning, and he had never imagined his children leaving the house. Yet by the end of three cycles he knew a great deal about her — how she starts her day, that she likes to move slowly, that she has one child in college and one at her ex-husband's house, that her podcast once had a protein sponsor — while she had learned nothing new and true about him.

Now you feel good being around me — but you don't know anything about me.

— a former CIA intelligence officer, on the tool the agency calls informational superiority

This is why the technique suits fieldwork: it establishes rapport, trust and a felt connection without the operator having to reveal anything of themselves. The telltale sign from the other side is a familiar one — the moment you realize, after a warm conversation, that you actually know nothing about the other person.

The ethical edge: what to watch for

The same tool cuts both ways. The demonstration's validating statements were invented, and the recipient's honest first reaction was suspicion of being manipulated. Used with fabricated similarities, the cycle is a deception technique; used with genuine ones, it is simply disciplined curiosity. And not everyone enjoys the receiving end — people who dislike talking about themselves can find direct, personal questioning awkward rather than warming.

  • Notice the asymmetry: if someone leaves you feeling wonderful but you cannot name a single real thing about them, you may have been on the receiving end of this cycle.
  • Validation built on invented similarities creates trust the other person has not actually earned evidence for.
  • The felt connection is a dopamine response to being asked and affirmed — it is not, by itself, proof of compatibility or sincerity.

Putting it to use

  1. Enter the conversation intending to learn, not to present. Resist the pull to talk about your own history, experience and ideas.
  2. Ask a question grounded in the other person's real, current situation — meet them where they are, not where your prepared opener assumes they are.
  3. Ask a follow-up that goes one layer deeper into whatever they actually said.
  4. Make one validating statement, ideally surfacing a genuine similarity between their experience and yours.
  5. Repeat the cycle, letting each round build on the last.

The payoff the former officer promises is a predictable one: the other person feels interesting, important and understood — and feels good being around you.
