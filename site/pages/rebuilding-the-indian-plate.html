Rebuilding the Indian Plate: The Fundamentals of Eating Well

Certified nutritionist Sangeetha Aiyer strips health back to its basics — how to measure where you actually stand, how to load an ordinary Indian plate with protein and fibre, how to protect your gut, how to see through health-food marketing, and how to build habits that outlast a Monday's motivation.

At a glance

  • Change the conversation from weight loss to health: fix your internal markers, sleep and circadian rhythm, and a healthy weight follows as a by-product.
  • Start with honest data — waist circumference, a waist-to-height ratio, a one-week food diary, and an annual blood panel from age 25 — before setting any goal.
  • Indian meals are carbohydrate-heroes. The two missing components are protein (aim 15–20 g per meal) and fibre (25 g for women, 35 g for men); most household meals deliver only about 3 g of fibre.
  • Cut junk food first. Roughly 800–1,000 unnoticed calories a day come from it, and it is the single change with the biggest return for the least effort.
  • Protein needs run from a bare minimum of 0.8 g/kg up to 1.6–1.8 g/kg for trained people, and rise with age and at key life stages — puberty, pregnancy, lactation and menopause.
  • Your gut microbiome shapes cravings, skin and mood. The three things wrecking it today are ultra-processed food, circadian-rhythm disruption and sitting all day.
  • A protein bar with more sugar than protein, or a millet biscuit, is still junk — food marketed as healthy can be more dangerous than plain junk because you eat more of it.
  • Get your "why" right. Ninety-five percent of dieters regain the weight within two years; slow, sustainable habits stacked one at a time are what actually change a life.

Health, not weight: start with honest data

Health has been over-complicated, and the first job is to simplify it. Most people live in slight denial — "I don't really eat that much," "I'm quite active" — until they actually break it down and find they neither eat well nor move much. So the conversation has to shift away from weight loss, weight gain and looks. Aesthetics take centre stage because of constant exposure to social media, but being slim does not mean being healthy: lean people turn up with PCOS, and thin people develop diabetes. The goal is to be healthy whatever the number on the scale.

It also helps to separate weight loss from fat loss. Eat fewer calories and expend more energy and weight will drop — but that loss can be water, muscle or bone-mineral density rather than fat. The only thing you want to lose is excess body fat; muscle mass and bone-mineral density, once gone, are hard to regain depending on your age. Fix your internal markers, mental well-being, circadian rhythm, sleep cycle and stress, and the right weight — along with good skin and hair — arrives as a by-product. It is a journey and a process, not a destination you reach and then stop.

Before changing anything, gather three or four data points — some qualitative, some measured — so that you understand where you stand and have something actionable to act on:

  1. Look at your waist. Stand in front of a mirror and measure your stomach circumference at the narrowest part of the waist, where the curvature is — typically just above the navel, neither upper nor lower. The National Institute of Nutrition and other bodies classify a healthy stomach circumference as 80 cm for women (about 32 inches) and 90 cm for men (about 34.5 inches). The further past this you go, the more surely you are moving into the unhealthy bucket.
  2. Work out your waist-to-height ratio. Divide waist by height in the same unit. Above 0.55, you are heading towards unhealthy.
  3. Keep a one-week food diary. For a week — weekdays and weekend, because habits swing sharply between them — write down everything in a notebook or a calorie app: the éclair from the fridge, four nuts, one biscuit off someone's plate. You will be surprised by your average intake, and by how much of it is not whole food. If more than 10% of your total calories come from packaged food (anything out of a wrapper, versus home-cooked roti, sabzi and fruit), you are progressing towards an unhealthy body and lifestyle.
  4. Get an annual blood panel. Humans rarely act without a little healthy fear, and a data point provides it. At least once a year — now recommended above age 25, where it used to be 40-plus — check your blood pressure, blood sugar, lipid (cholesterol) profile, liver and kidney function, and basic vitamin status including vitamin D and B12, since many conditions manifest from vitamin deficiency. This matters especially if your family carries any history of metabolic dysfunction — diabetes, cardiovascular risk or fatty liver on either parent's side.

Without actionable data you either do nothing, or you do something that lasts two days. The point of the diary and the panel is to open the poll on where you really are.

Rebuild the plate: protein, fibre and hidden oil

The traditional Indian diet is carbohydrate-oriented — and that suited an earlier era. After colonisation and famine, the priority was providing enough calories for many people doing hard physical labour, so rice, cereal and pulses became the mainstay with a little oil to cook them. Today purchasing power is higher and jobs are sedentary, so the plate proportion has to shift towards nutrient-dense food rather than merely calorie-dense food. A medu vada is calorie-dense — many calories for few nutrients; a moong dal chila with paneer stuffing carries calories too, but its nutrient density is excellent for those calories.

Indian diets have a two-way problem: an inadequacy of protein and an inadequacy of fibre at the same time. When both are missing from the plate, the one thing you over-eat is carbohydrate. Two useful rules of thumb: aim for at least 15–20 g of protein in every meal, and 8–10 g of fibre per meal — a daily total of 25 g of fibre for women and 35 g for men. A regular Indian household meal often delivers only about 3 g of fibre and 5 g of protein.

Fix breakfast first

The most common Indian breakfasts — poha, upma, idli-dosa, vada — are overwhelmingly carbohydrate. How healthy a poha is depends entirely on how it is made: an Indori style over sev, with a little sugar in the cooking, is a very tasty carb-heavy snack. Even "improved" with peanuts and a cup of curd it is not automatically healthy. A 150 g poha is almost 90% carbohydrate; the peanuts add healthy fat, not protein (10 g of peanuts gives perhaps 3 g of protein — peanuts are a source of healthy fat, not protein), and 100 g of curd adds about 3 g of protein. An idli-vada plate is roughly 80% carbohydrate — the urad dal in an idli yields about 2 g of protein, and the vada is deep-fried on top.

The better way to eat these:

  • Reduce the quantity of the grain and add a protein on the side — tofu or paneer for vegetarians, two eggs for non-vegetarians.
  • Make it rich in fibre with plenty of vegetables (carrot, peas, cauliflower, beans) — aim for a roughly 1:1 ratio of grain to fibre, so your glucose stays steady without needing a continuous glucose monitor.
  • Use hung curd rather than loose curd: draining the water takes 100 g to about 6–8 g of protein, helping the start of your day reach around 15 g.
  • Chutney is fine — coconut chutney is good fat, peanut chutney is not a problem — but always keep something alongside for protein.

Vegetarian clients who are conscious of refined-carb intake (anything derived from rice or wheat) prefer other breakfasts: moong dal chila stuffed with paneer or tofu and grated vegetables, with a little dahi, easily reaches 20 g of protein; missi roti made with tofu and a little oil, with sabzi and dahi; or hummus with paneer and no extra carbs. Re-imagining a plate you have eaten for thirty years takes time, but it is healthier for the way we live and work now.

Fix the thali

A typical vegetarian household plate is one or two rotis, a little rice, a dal made the usual way (about 5 g of protein), and a small amount of sabzi — both roti and rice being refined carbohydrates from wheat and rice. The correction is not to ban rice or roti, but to change the proportions so carbohydrate falls to about a quarter of the plate and the other three-quarters is non-carbohydrate that actually carries nutrition:

  • Add a big bowl of salad — it does not have to be the fancy Western concept; tomato, carrot and beetroot is enough.
  • Add a concentrated protein: paneer, or a legume such as chana with good curd, or chicken with dal (which brings soluble fibre). The paneer bowl is superior to the chana bowl, because chana also carries carbohydrate.
  • For non-vegetarians, chicken — and if that is expensive, two to three eggs. Non-vegetarians eat meat only a couple of times a week anyway, so the everyday plate still needs a plant or dairy protein.
  • Keep either rice or roti, not a heavy serving of both, and let salad, dahi and the protein fill the rest.

This is sustainable because nothing is banned outright. It is not just for the woman of the house trying to lose weight — grandparents and children need the same change.

The invisible oil

Indian cooking has no built-in sense of how much oil goes in — a tadka can be done in 5 g or in 20 g. Our flavour comes from two things unlike other cuisines: spices and oil. Spices are not the problem — some are antioxidant and protective depending on quantity — but the oil for taste needs to come down. The oil allowance defined by the WHO and the American Heart Association is 500 ml of cooking fat per person per month; for a standard four-member household that is 2 litres of all oils combined — olive oil, ghee, peanut oil, everything. Because Indian food is genuinely hard to cook in 500 ml, allow a little leeway up to about 700 ml per person; most households actually use around 1.5 litres per person.

The bigger issue is hidden oil, the oil you are not adding yourself. A packaged biscuit, chips, Parle-G and bhujia are all full of oil; a food-delivery order carries double or triple. This is why so many people have high LDL cholesterol despite low saturated-fat cooking and long-standing ghee awareness: it comes from the outside and junk food, typically fried in palm oil. Palm oil is much misunderstood — it is not the worst oil, it simply has a high amount of saturated fat, and it is constant consumption that raises your numbers.

A one-line test. Before anything goes into your mouth, ask what nutrition it is giving you — how much fibre, protein, carbohydrate and sugar. If you cannot tell, the label (or a quick check) will. Ninety percent of your entire nutrition should come from whole foods.

Protein through every life stage

The bare minimum to avoid deficiency and disease is 0.8 g of protein per kilogram of ideal body weight — the figure the ICMR, National Institute of Nutrition and WHO recommend so that even the most marginal, below-poverty-line population is covered. It is a floor, not the level at which you thrive. In practice, eat at least 1 g/kg (about 70 g for a 70 kg person), because at 0.8 g the stomach is not satisfied and you compensate with hard-to-eat volumes of fibre. Depending on activity, the range runs from 1.2 to 1.8 g/kg; most trained people sit at 1.6–1.8, which takes a 70 kg individual to roughly 95–96 g — and that is not excess. Beyond 1.8 g/kg, more protein simply means more food with no added benefit, and is seen only in the bodybuilding community.

The recommended daily allowance is a population minimum, not a ceiling. There is also a real difference between a 20-year-old and a 48-year-old consuming the same 8 grams — absorption ability and need diverge with age. The older you grow, the higher you actually need to go, to preserve muscle mass and bone-mineral density and to stop sarcopenia. Grandparents who "can't get up once they sit down" are showing a lack of strength in bone and muscle, and need more protein, not less.

In percentage terms the requirement is nearly identical for men and women, though absolute amounts differ because women generally carry less muscle. If anything, women should be more careful of their protein intake: less muscle by nature, and a sharp risk of falling bone-mineral density after menopause, with bone being one-third protein — not just calcium.

The stages when protein is non-negotiable

Protein is the macronutrient that promotes structural development — the building block of every cell, organ and tissue. Certain life stages make it non-negotiable:

  • Puberty and adolescence: sex hormones are developing — a testosterone surge builds muscle differently in boys; estrogen in girls supports muscle and limits excess fat gain.
  • Pregnancy, first trimester: maintenance calories stay the same (an Indian woman is usually 1,800–2,000 calories). You do not need to eat for two; you need to change the composition of those calories towards protein — you are building a life.
  • Pregnancy, second trimester: only about 350 extra calories, most of it from protein and healthy fat (nuts, seeds, legumes). This is when gestational diabetes is checked; a GDM diagnosis is taken very seriously (insulin or metformin) and raises the future diabetes risk of both mother and child.
  • Pregnancy, third trimester: only about 450 extra calories, again largely protein — not ice creams and laddoos, which add weight and risk.
  • Lactation: about 400–600 extra calories — a few more nuts, an egg, some seeds — and a good time to gently regain health, since energy expenditure is high.
  • Menopause: estrogen — the body's protective shield for hair, skin, muscle, bone, cardiovascular health and stress — tanks. Women begin gaining central belly fat "on the same calories," and need to crank up protein, control carbohydrates, and do impactful resistance training (not yoga, zumba or marathon running) to protect muscle and bone.

A healthy pregnancy weight gain, by WHO charts, is about 8–12 kg (roughly 9–11 kg), depending on your starting BMI; gaining more than 11 kg is considered a risk pregnancy, which is alarmingly common in India. In her own pregnancy, when a scan showed the baby's growth lagging, the doctor advised raising protein — warning of a drip otherwise — and eating around 100 g of protein daily through the third trimester corrected the growth naturally.

On "excess" protein. The protein-police claim that anything above the minimum is excess is wrong. Somewhere between 0.8 and 1.8 g/kg is simply the range of healthy protein you need. What most people, and older relatives especially, are actually eating is far too little.

Gut health: fibre, the microbiome and cravings

Gut problems have risen sharply — acidity, gas, bloating and IBS are so common that around one in three people arriving for help sit somewhere on that spectrum, and many have normalised it, living on antacids. But the gut, sometimes called the second brain, is arguably the first: it holds trillions of microbes with access to your brain, skin, mood and hormones (most of your serotonin is made there). A good or bad gut can make or break not just mental health but what you crave.

Cravings are a signal. When bad bacteria take over, they effectively call the brain and demand sugar — which is why "I need something sweet after every meal" is a sign your gut needs changing, not a character flaw. For strong cravings the answer is not moderation but abstinence: stop completely for 30–40 days and use that window to populate the gut with good, healthier food, so the bacterial balance shifts over 30–60 days and the cravings fade. Gut and mental health run both ways, too — anxiety and IBS frequently travel together, and severe, chronic IBS wrecks quality of life because the person is forever thinking about their stomach.

Three things ruining gut health

  1. Ultra-processed food — hotel food, food-app delivery and now quick commerce; street food is the worst.
  2. Circadian-rhythm disruption — sleeping and waking out of sync with sunlight. You do not have to sleep at sunset, but if you are not getting 7–8 hours by around half past ten or eleven at night and waking by about seven, with some daylight, everything disrupts. The first casualties are your hunger signals — ghrelin (hunger) and leptin (satiety) — followed by cortisol, which raises insulin, which raises hunger, a vicious cycle. One study found that every two hours of sleep disruption led to about 360 extra calories consumed the next day, without the person realising.
  3. Sedentary behaviour — eating and sitting with no movement. Exercise changes the gut microbiome for the better irrespective of diet; if you feel bloated or heavy, a 15-minute strength or floor session can visibly change your stomach within an hour.

Fibre — the food for your gut

Fibre comes in two kinds. Soluble fibre (in lauki, and in isabgol) is one humans can partly handle. Insoluble, indigestible fibre — in chole, chana, rajma and cruciferous vegetables like cabbage, cauliflower and broccoli — is fibre for your gut microbiome: what you do not digest, you feed to the healthy bacteria. The more nutritional diversity and the greater the number of plants you eat, the more diverse and healthy your microbiome. There is essentially no study where fibre caused harm — unless you have a pre-existing condition such as IBS, IBD or SIBO, where insoluble fibre may not be tolerated and a low-FODMAP protocol (eliminating certain starches and sugars for about 90 days, then carefully reintroducing) is used to turn the microbiome over. Fibre also lowers LDL cholesterol and provides volume: protein delivers long-term satiety, fibre delivers short-term satiety, which is why the plate needs both.

Probiotics, prebiotics and what is worth your money

A probiotic is a living micro-organism — strains of yeast and good bacteria such as lactobacillus, found mostly in fermented foods — that resembles what is already in your gut. A prebiotic is simply food for those microbes: your insoluble fibre. The most reliable route is a diverse, seasonally changing diet, which primes the gut for all of this. Some caution on the rest:

  • Probiotic supplements largely do not reach the colon where the bacteria reside — stomach acid is strong enough to kill most of them — so much of the benefit is a placebo (feel-good) effect.
  • Three things are described as scams for the money going into them: gene testing / nutrigenomics ("nutrition based on your genes"), and microbiome (stool-sample) testing — even the experts cannot reliably say which bacterium is missing or what to add.
  • Better to fix your food, sleep and exercise first, then experiment strategically with fermented foods: kimchi, sauerkraut, kefir, and India's own kanji (in many regional forms, including beetroot kanji) — and chaas is already there.

Raise protein and fibre in increments

Whenever you change something, do not jump from 10 to 100. If your Indian thali currently gives 20–25 g of protein a day, taking it suddenly to 70 g will cause problems — the same is true of fibre. Move in steps: 25 to 40, then to 60 over two to three weeks, then to 70, then stop. Protein always needs fibre and hydration to keep the bowel cycle regular (it can otherwise cause constipation), and is better digested with a little fat — in nature protein and fat come together (an egg is roughly 1:1 by mass). Young gym-goers are misled into 100 g of protein, all from whey shakes, on day one; the gut cannot be treated that way. Hyper-using any one nutrient — including fibre — has consequences and can cause deficiencies. Balance is the point.

Marketing traps: protein snacks, millets, supplements and vegan diets

As protein awareness grows, an entire segment has opened up — protein shakes, bars, biscuits, chocolates and wafers.

Food marketed as if it is giving you a health benefit is more dangerous than junk food, because you will eat more of it.

— Sangeetha Aiyer, nutritionist

Adding a little protein to an ultra-processed food does not automatically make it healthy. If a normal wafer is ₹10 and the "protein" version is ₹50, with a celebrity endorsing it, the makeup of everything else in it has not changed. The reframe: if you were eating a small packet of chips as an occasional treat you knew was wrong, you will now buy four protein wafers guilt-free because they "have protein" — turning a Sunday indulgence into a daily habit.

Protein snacks versus real protein

Not food. Most protein bars are not even protein bars — they are energy bars, where carbs and sugar (even "natural" sugars like dates and raisins) always exceed the protein. There is yet to be a protein bar in India under 200 calories carrying more than 20 g of protein, because it would not sell or taste good. Carry a good whey shake or, at worst, an occasional bar when travelling and nothing else is available — but never as an everyday snack.

Whey and plant proteins, by contrast, are recommended. A whey scoop (~30 g, 200–212 calories) or a plant-protein isolate scoop (~35 g, 215–225 calories) both deliver 24–27 g of protein that is complete across all nine amino acids, in the right proportions (leucine above its threshold, so muscle protein synthesis can occur), and close to 100% bioavailable because isolates have nothing attached. Three concepts matter when judging a powder: its protein-to-calorie ratio, its complete amino-acid profile, and its bioavailability. Class-1 proteins — egg, paneer, meat, soy, tofu — give the most protein for the fewest calories.

Viral home "protein" powders of sattu and mixed nuts fail on all three. Their macros are mostly carbohydrate and fat with the least protein; 20–25 g of any whole nut is already 150 calories, so a 30 g mixed-nut powder can reach 350 calories — a weight-gain formula. It is a lovely thing to give underweight or picky children to finish their calorie requirement, but as a calorie-dense whole food, not as a protein supplement.

Millets

Healthy only whole. Millets are healthy in their whole form — a jowar roti on your plate is a good thing. Turned into millet noodles, biscuits, chakli or ice cream, there is no difference from maida. Eat food as close to nature as possible, with minimum processing steps: soaking whole millets for an upma or pulao is fine; grinding to a batter for idli-dosa is one more step; grinding further with added sugar and oil into a cake or biscuit defeats the point.

Eat any meal with awareness of its consequences instead.

Vegan diets

The one diet not recommended. A vegan diet is largely ideological — rooted in activism against animal cruelty, a stance worth respecting — and ideological beliefs cannot be debated with logic or evidence. As a whole-food plant-based diet it is scientifically possible to do healthily, but it is impractical, elitist and expensive. For an Indian giving up milk, dahi and eggs, more than 100 g of soy-based protein becomes non-negotiable, plant-protein isolate supplementation is needed to reach 100 g, carbohydrates climb because plant sources carry them, and you must understand nutrition pairing — plant iron needs vitamin C and even then absorbs at only 10–20% versus readily available heme iron. Iron is already the biggest deficiency among Indian women and children; among vegans it is a leading deficiency after B12 and zinc, and vegan diets show the most deficiencies in ranking studies. Anyone choosing it should design it with a nutritionist rather than assume "dal, rice and sabzi is already vegan." Note too that many vegan celebrities built their physique in their formative years first — nutrition never shows its effect in six months or a year.

Two kinds of obesity — and why it matters

Health agencies operate on Body Mass Index. It is highly debated whether BMI is the right measure, but as a first population-level filter it is a workable marker: above 25 is overweight, above 30 is obese, and above 35 is morbid obesity — these are medical terms. Layered onto this is central obesity, defined earlier by the 80 cm (women) and 90 cm (men) waist thresholds.

India has two kinds of obesity. One is the classic definition — a person visibly larger, with high weight and high BMI. The other, more insidious, is TOFI: thin on the outside, fat on the inside. It is a South Asian problem — Vietnam, the Philippines, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka and the subcontinent — where BMI reads normal but fat is disproportionately distributed centrally, with thin arms, legs and face. This matters because of where the fat sits:

  • Subcutaneous fat — under the skin — is not metabolically damaging. You can be 10 kg overweight with subcutaneous fat and still be relatively healthy.
  • Visceral fat — around the organs, as fatty liver, fatty pancreas and fat around the heart — is dangerous. Just 1 kg of extra visceral fat starts causing problems, which makes TOFI metabolically more dangerous than classic obesity.

As lower-middle-class families move up into the middle and upper-middle class, more of their spending goes on ultra-processed food, eating out and ordering in — obesity here is aspirational, and rises with income. The consequences are serious enough to act on:

  • Cancer: after smoking, obesity has among the worst outcomes — smoking is linked to some 14 kinds of cancer, obesity to about 11.
  • Metabolic dysfunction: it may not kill in months like a tumour, but it degrades the quality of your next 20–30 years, so that even in your 40s you cannot travel and move as you would want.
  • Hypertension and diabetes: obesity links directly to hypertension, then diabetes; between them, 95%+ of chronic kidney disease in India — and worldwide — is related to these two conditions.
  • Diabetes complications: nephropathy, neuropathy and brain stroke — there is no area of the body that sugar does not damage.
  • Cardiovascular disease: obesity plus a poor lipid profile opens the door to CVD, and Indians already carry among the highest CVD and diabetes risk — even immigrant Indians in the US show the highest CVD risk of any ethnicity. On top of a TOFI baseline, added obesity is something the population cannot afford.
  • Mobility: a large share of knee and hip replacements trace to obesity, and low muscle leads to falls and fractures.

On immunity. You cannot boost your immunity through everyday food. There is no substance, kadha or chyawanprash you can eat or drink to boost it — you are not smarter than your immune system.

Building habits that last: get your "why" right

Most people fail because their "why" is wrong. Search any health topic on YouTube and you get "lose weight in 21 days," "lose belly fat in 15 days," "drink this magic drink." Health does not fall into your lap in three or six months. When the why is long-term and genuinely believed, the "how" also becomes long-term: you stop looking for shortcuts, and whatever plan you follow — even a slow one — becomes sustainable, and sustainability is what changes your habits and behaviour. If your why is "fit into this lehenga in three months," the why itself is wrong.

A healthy person never says they are "on a diet" — they say "this is what I eat, this is how I train," and the good body is an outcome. People who look good after 5, 10 or 15 years have had their routine and lifestyle in place 90% of the time. The hard statistic: 95% of dieters who lose weight regain all of it, if not more, within two years. So the challenge is not weight loss but weight maintenance; not fat loss but fat maintenance and habits. Show up 80% of the time, and do not rely on motivation — it is the most privileged thing on the planet, here today and gone tomorrow.

Habit stacking

Do not change every habit overnight — first-day "no sugar, no junk, go to the gym" does not work. Start with the lowest-hanging fruit that makes the biggest difference, and stack one habit behind another so each becomes more effortless:

  1. Cut junk food. The easiest change with the largest effect: roughly 800–1,000 calories a day, completely unnoticed, come from junk. A Hide & Seek packet is an easy 500 calories (500–650 for ten biscuits) — the same 500 calories as two rotis, 100–120 g of cooked rice, a thick dal, two vegetables and 150 g of curd, which keeps you full for four hours where the biscuits leave you ready to eat again in 15–30 minutes. Replace junk with nuts, a little popcorn, curd and fruit, or chaas (flavoured with jeera and ajwain, no sugar) and fresh nimbu pani with salt, not sugar.
  2. Add movement. If you are sedentary — as most laptop-based jobs are — 10,000 steps is 1 hour 10 minutes at a normal pace, which you will not do. Tell yourself instead: for one week, I will get up and walk for 30 minutes. Set yourself up for success.
  3. Sleep on time, which is the habit that makes waking at 7 possible, which makes the morning walk possible.
  4. Stack a healthy meal — "I will have 20 g of protein" — behind the walk, without touching lunch and dinner yet.
  5. Then a packed lunch three days a week, and later, when the client themselves asks, strength training — the request should come from them, driven by small wins.

Small wins reinforce the brain: you are walking, your protein is right, you are not eating so much junk, and 2.5–3 kg comes off in the first month. This is why strength training is not pushed from day one — a simple 45-minute walk succeeds, and succeeding matters more than the ten-day impact.

Be kind, and start again

Good habits gratify later; compromised, bad habits gratify immediately — so remove instant gratification from the equation. Be kind to yourself: even a film star with an extreme deadline should stay healthy, sustainable, happy and well-slept. And understand that every meal and every day is a fresh start — the body forgives. If a Saturday goes wrong, or you miss a Sunday, do not write off the whole week; but do not let long periods pass either, because old muscle memory is strong and the longer the break, the harder the restart. One day off, two days off — come back immediately to the habit stack. Ultimately it is the power of routine and the consistency of doing the boring, basic things — not longevity buzzwords, biohacking, magic foods or superfoods — that actually works.

Common questions

  • High protein on a tight budget: soya chunks are the cheapest protein — three servings a week with skim milk or home curd can reach 120 g of protein cheaply (though soya chunks eaten too often can trouble the gut). Fish is easy on the coast (Mumbai, Bengal), harder inland; goat meat (mutton) is expensive, so once or twice a week. Otherwise protein is not expensive — it is a matter of priorities.
  • Most nutritious fruits and vegetables: whatever is local and seasonal. You do not need to chase blueberries or frozen raspberries.
  • Overfed but undernourished: eat better and you eat less; eat poorly and you eat more while staying unnourished — the calorie-dense, nutrition-poor biscuit is the classic example.
  • Best energy and metabolism: take your basal metabolic rate, honestly add your activity level, add daily movement (NEAT), and that is your maintenance. For fat loss go 300–500 calories below it; to gain and build muscle, 300 calories above.
  • Sugar cravings: sugar is like an itch — scratch it daily and there is no limit. Let 24 hours pass without scratching, and little by little it settles.
  • Cholesterol through food: total cholesterol alone means little; read the parts — HDL, LDL, VLDL, triglycerides. High triglycerides usually mean excess carbohydrate or sugar; low HDL means too little protein or too little exercise; high LDL means high saturated fat — which comes not mainly from your mother's ghee on the roti but from junk food, palm oil, fried and hidden fats. Fix the outside food and the ordering before shouting about the two spoons of ghee at home.
