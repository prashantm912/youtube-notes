Why the Face Ages Faster After 40: Bone, Fat, and the Trade-offs of Treatment

Aesthetic physician Dr. Rajani argues that facial aging is a structural problem — bone, fat pads and ligaments, not skin alone — and that understanding the architecture explains why one change on a face always forces another.

At a glance

  • Faces age from the inside out: bone narrows and thins, deep fat pads shrink and descend, ligaments loosen, and the dermis gets thinner. Skin quality is only the surface layer of the problem.
  • As fat descends and bone recedes, a woman's face tends to become more square — more masculine — which is why the narrower "V-shaped" lower face reads as younger.
  • Every change has an equal and opposite reaction. Adding cheek filler can lift jowls but crowd the eyes closer together; filling folds can widen the widest part of the face. Who assesses your face matters more than which product is used.
  • Fillers add volume but do not lift; biostimulators such as Sculptra are what stimulate the structure that lifts.
  • You want to remove some fat — the jowl and under-chin fat — and preserve the good fat in the cheeks and lips.
  • The lower your aging rate, the longer any treatment lasts. Bone care, hormones, retinol with vitamin C, red light and lifestyle are what slow the clock.
  • Two people can both be 62 and look decades apart. Skin type and lifestyle — sun, smoking, hormones — decide far more than the number.

Aging is structural: the four layers that give way

The face people see is draped over an architecture that changes with age. Imagine pulling skin tight over bone that has shrunk — it will not look right, because the angles the skin once followed are gone. That is why a facelift alone cannot return a face to its twenties: it addresses the drape, not the frame beneath it.

Bone

Bone is most robust in the twenties, when everything aligns over it. From the twenties into the thirties the face still remodels, but not yet in an osteoporotic way. Later, the jawbone loses density, the jawline narrows, and the maxilla involutes — it pushes inward — which flattens the mid-face and squares off the lower face. Men lose less facial bone than women, partly because of daily shaving stimulus and partly because women lose the estrogen that protects bone.

Fat pads

The deep fat pads are more important than they were once thought to be. A decade ago the conversation was all about bone; now the fat pads are understood to produce estrogen and mesenchymal stem cells and to keep a face looking younger. As they shrink and descend with age, the face deflates and drops. Losing facial fat is aging, which is why crash weight loss — including rapid loss on GLP-1 medications — shows up in the face.

Ligaments and dermis

Facial ligaments loosen, and that laxity is what produces jowls. Dermal thickness also declines. When early "bunny lines" or thinning appear in one area, they are a harbinger: the skin is thinning elsewhere too, and structural support is being lost across the face.

Why aging looks like masculinization — and why the V-shape reads young

Faces carry masculine and feminine features, a difference called dimorphism, and there is plasticity to it. As a woman's fat descends and her jaw bone narrows, her face moves toward a squarer, more masculine shape. The single biggest fight in the clinic is taking that squared face and reshaping it back toward a V — an oval or heart-shaped outline.

The width across the angles of the jaw — the bigonial width — is central. A wider, more parallel jaw looks older and more masculine; a narrower lower face looks more youthful. When people are shown two versions of the same face, the majority prefer the narrower V-shaped lower face, especially once they understand what they are looking at. Cultural taste layers on top of this: a strong, wide "Texas jawline" is prized in some places, while a narrow V-line is the goal in others. As the saying goes in the clinic, we all want what we do not have.

The trap: because the fight is to lift a heavy lower face, providers often balloon out the upper and mid-face to create the illusion of lift. It does lift the lower face — at the price of an over-large upper face. Volume in the wrong place ages the result.

Reading a face: the angles that signal youth

An experienced injector assesses a face in seconds, and much of that assessment is about angles seen from the side — not just the front. If your provider never looks at your profile, key information is being missed.

  • Nasolabial angle — the angle where the nose meets the upper lip should sit around 100 degrees, ideally a little more acute so the lip comes slightly forward. When the maxilla involutes, this angle flattens, and a facelift will not correct it.
  • Nose-to-lip distance — a short distance from the base of the nose to the upper lip is an aging feature, but whether it is truly "long" can only be judged against the chin length below it. This is why lip lifts are so often misjudged.
  • Jaw (gonial) angle — a female jaw angle is more obtuse and sloped, roughly 110 to 120 degrees; a male angle is more upright, closer to 90 to 100 degrees. Filling the lower face can push a female jaw toward the more masculine, upright look.
  • Forehead slope — a female forehead is fairly upright before it slopes back, which reads as feminine and attractive; a male forehead has forward frontal bossing and then a stronger slope. A provider who cannot say whether a female forehead should be upright or sloped is a warning sign.

Every change forces another: the trade-offs of filler

For every change made on a face there is an equal and opposite reaction somewhere else. This is the reasoning that separates a careful plan from a cascade of treatments, and it is why the first thing offered in a clinic — cheek filler, then lip filler, then folds — is not automatically the right thing.

Cheek filler can crowd the eyes

The cheekbone signals youth and fertility, so people who have their cheeks filled often want more. But widening the cheekbone area laterally moves the visual width outward, which makes the eyes appear closer together — and the eyes are the first thing anyone looks at. On a face that already has a wide cheekbone, adding more out to the sides is not a good look, even though it does pick up the jowls.

Filling folds can widen and lengthen the wrong things

Filling the nasolabial folds can be reasonable on a face with good mid-face volume, but it can also add width to the widest part of the face — the exact opposite of the V-shape goal — and make an already long nose-to-lip segment more noticeable, with structural changes that show when the face moves.

Filling the jowl can masculinize the chin

Filling the jowl is often worthwhile, but it can create the illusion of a wider chin, which reads as more masculine. A female chin width is judged from a line dropped down from the nasal ala; a male chin is wider. Straightening the jawline with a firm, high-structure filler such as Radiesse can masculinize the front view even as it improves the side.

None of this is an argument against filler — it is an argument for using the least amount that achieves the goal, and for the person planning the face to weigh each move against the others.

What treatments actually do — and do not

Choosing between named products matters far less than matching the tool to the deficit. The same request produces completely different plans on different faces.

ToolWhat it doesWhat it will not do
Hyaluronic acid fillersAdd volume; can straighten a jawline or restore a foldDo not lift; too much or too superficial shows and can widen the face
Biostimulators (Sculptra, Bellafill, diluted Radiesse)Stimulate the skin and structure; biostimulators are what lift, and this is studiedSculptra adds little immediate structure; cannot be dissolved if a nodule forms
CO2 and other resurfacing lasersRemove sunspots and sun damage; give a little skin tighteningDo not lift the face; can over-treat the neck and reveal hypopigmentation
Radiofrequency and energy fat reductionReduce jowl and under-chin fat; radiofrequency works on all skin typesNot for areas where fat should be preserved, such as cheeks and lips

Remove the right fat. The goal is to kill fat only where it ages you — the jowl and the submental fat under the chin — while protecting the good fat in the cheeks, lips and vertical lip-line area. In-clinic radiofrequency reaches around 67 degrees Celsius without the fat shut-off that home devices use for safety, and options such as Kybella or liposuction target the same under-chin fat.

Sunspots are treated separately from lift: even after a facelift, sun damage remains and needs resurfacing or light-based treatment. Two rounds of CO2 might run several thousand dollars, and taking on hollow temples, folds, lips, jawline straightening and resurfacing together is how a plan climbs to tens of thousands of dollars quickly.

Injection safety: the cheek is relatively safe, but the region between the eyes, the forehead and the area near the nose are the dangerous zones. The facial artery and the vessels feeding the back of the eye run here, and misplaced filler carries a risk of blindness. Home lasers are judged not yet good enough to be worth the money for the results they deliver.

Slowing the aging rate at home

The single most useful idea for the budget-conscious: the slower your aging rate, the less treatment you need and the longer any treatment lasts. Someone who protects bone, avoids excess sun, does not smoke and does not crash-diet may not need a top-up at two years, because the result was maintained.

Skin: retinol needs vitamin C

Retinol is the staple, paired with an alpha hydroxy acid as the number one and two combination — the AHA does more for brown spots. But retinol needs vitamin C to build collagen, and much of the tightening people credit to a harsh retinol is really inflammation, which drives facial fat loss and ages the skin. A gentler approach uses more than one form of retinol — such as retinaldehyde alongside retinol and retinyl palmitate — with a stable, fat-soluble vitamin C (THD ascorbate) so the collagen-building step is actually supported.

Structure: bone, hormones, light and habits

  • Near-infrared and red light reach to the level of the bone; near-infrared is wanted, but staying under about 900 nanometers is advised.
  • Oral and gum health protect the alveolar bone that holds the teeth — lose that bone and you lose the jawline, which is a major reason women age around the mouth.
  • Bone support through vitamin K2 and D3, and hormone replacement where it is safe and appropriate, address the estrogen-driven bone and fat loss directly. A DEXA scan reveals where your bone stands.
  • Gua sha, used briefly with a face oil and focused on the neck, jawline, cheekbone and brow, supports lymphatic drainage and blood flow.
  • Sunscreen matters when sun exposure is excessive, and the strong afternoon summer sun is aging — but the body also needs some sun, so the point is judgment, not blanket avoidance.

Preventing the loss, or maintaining it with home care, can cut the amount of in-clinic work needed to a fraction. That is where patience pays.

Why two people at 62 look decades apart

Age alone tells a provider almost nothing. Two women can both be 62 and require completely different plans. One may have good mid-face volume, loose under-eye skin and hooding; the other, with a skin type carrying more melanin, may have thicker dermis, a perfect glow with no sunspots, but heavier skin, more sag and under-chin fat — and would be harder to lift with injectables, needing fat removal at the jawline rather than lasers.

Lifestyle is the deciding variable. A 62-year-old who smokes shows dramatic hollowing, more sun damage, lip volume loss and jowling — the visible cost of what smoking, sun, alcohol and estrogen loss do to skin, fat and bone. This is why there is no single answer to "what should I do at 62." Protect the skin, the fat and the bone; the number on the birth certificate is the least informative thing about the face.
