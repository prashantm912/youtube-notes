Reverse-Aging Biohacking: A Science-Led Playbook

Biohacking expert Palak Midha argues that looking a decade younger is not luck or genetics but a decade of small, compounding choices. This is her framework for optimising the body's core systems — supplements, macros and blood sugar, the gut, sleep, training load, inflammation and skin — using the secrets of science to gain an unfair advantage over ageing.

At a glance

  • Biohacking is not just prevention — it is super-optimisation: using scientific tools to push performance, health and appearance well beyond the default.
  • Our bodies evolved over millions of years for a hunter-gatherer life; the last 10,000 years since the farming revolution changed everything, so modern life works against our biology. The fix is one small step at a time, and the sooner you start, the greater the compounding.
  • The best biomarker Palak tracks is not an expensive telomere or metabolic-age test — it is her own energy, which she describes as feeling like a 25-year-old at 40.
  • Five nutrients are the foundation of an Indian supplement stack: vitamin D3 (with K2), omega-3, magnesium and B12, with zinc and boron layered in for testosterone.
  • Prefer direct supplement forms over ones that require the body to convert them — direct retinyl palmitate over beta-carotene, methylated B vitamins over standard ones.
  • Health rests on four pillars: food, exercise, sleep and thoughts. Supplements add roughly a 20–25% edge on top of the pillars, not instead of them.

The supplement foundation most people are missing

In order of priority, four nutrients are most commonly lacking in Indian diets, with two more added for specific goals.

Vitamin D3 — closer to a hormone than a vitamin

Myth. The idea that you will simply get your vitamin D from sunlight does not hold for most people. The darker the skin tone, the harder it is to convert; fair skin can make vitamin D in 20–25 minutes, but our lifestyle keeps us out of the sun, and for people with compromised livers, prolonged sun exposure drives pigmentation rather than health.

The dosing is widely misunderstood. As a rough guide, the requirement is around 1000 IU per 12 kg of body weight, which is why a 5000 IU capsule is a sensible baseline for an average Indian adult. It has to be D3 in a bioavailable form, and it should be paired with K2 — ideally a full spectrum such as K1, MK4 and MK7. Even five minutes a month of near-full-body sun exposure helps activate the receptors, which matters as much as the dose itself.

Omega-3 — plant sources fall short

Myth. The belief that flax and chia seeds cover your omega-3 needs is misleading. Those seeds contain ALA (alpha-linolenic acid), and less than 1% of ALA converts to DHA — the fat your brain and cell membranes actually need. Eat chia and flax for fibre, not for omega-3.

A single cod liver oil capsule does little. For a therapeutic effect you need roughly 1000 mg of combined EPA and DHA daily, and 2–4 grams if you are deficient. Omega-3 also works as a natural blood thinner and helps correct the skewed omega-6 to omega-3 ratio in the Indian diet, which should sit nearer 4:1 rather than the far higher ratios most people carry.

Magnesium — an absorption problem, not an intake problem

Magnesium is not usually missing from the diet — sunflower seeds and cashews supply it. The problem is that it is poorly absorbed in the gut, and a stressful lifestyle makes the body leach more magnesium out through urine. A chelated form such as magnesium bisglycinate, where the magnesium is bound to two glycine molecules, is better absorbed; glycine itself lowers body temperature and supports fat burning, which is why taking magnesium at night helps you relax into deeper sleep.

Vitamin B12 — deficient and hard to absorb

B12 is one of the deficiencies where a single dose can feel almost magical, because so many people are depleted. It is largely absent from Indian vegetarian food; in non-vegetarian diets it is highest in red meat and especially liver, which is why chicken liver once a week is a useful addition for those who eat it. Crucially, a swallowed tablet is poorly absorbed in the gut — it works far better as a chewable that dissolves in the mouth, or as an IV or injection, and ideally in a methylated form.

Zinc and boron for testosterone. The number one supplement combination for raising testosterone is zinc together with boron — start zinc and testosterone often begins to rise. A bodybuilder or high-testosterone individual needs more zinc than others; for most people a good intake of seeds and nuts (sunflower seeds are rich in zinc) can cover it, with a supplement supporting the lifestyle rather than replacing it.

Choose direct forms over conversions

A recurring principle: favour the form the body can use immediately over one it has to convert. Prefer direct retinyl palmitate over beta-carotene, which the body must convert to vitamin A. Prefer methylated B vitamins. The reason is partly biochemical and partly behavioural — when a supplement works within a day or two, people are far more likely to stay consistent with it.

Vitamin K2 and the calcification problem

With age, arteries stiffen and calcium deposits build up, regardless of whether blood pressure or cholesterol is high. At a sufficiently high dose — using both MK4 and MK7 forms — K2 helps move calcium away from the arteries and back toward the bones, effectively pulling plaque away from arterial walls. This is why, in Western practice, newborns are given D3 and K2 drops from day zero. The urgency is old news in cardiology: research on the bodies of young World War II soldiers, some only 17 or 18, already showed sticky cholesterol beginning to clog their arteries — so plaque can start forming from the mid-teens and, once calcified, become dangerous by one's thirties or forties.

Balancing the plate: macros, carbs and blood sugar

The single biggest dietary mistake is that meals are not balanced. Any food — protein or carbohydrate — can harm you depending on quantity. Eat a bowl of poha in the morning and only about half a bowl is needed to refill the glycogen in your liver and muscles; the rest converts to sugar in the body. The same applies to protein: eat too much tandoori chicken with no activity to follow and, through a process called gluconeogenesis, the excess is converted into sugar too.

The macro split differs by person and goal. Women need relatively more fat — for fertility and hormonal signalling — while a man's plan might carry slightly less. A practical example: a plain potato dosa is a double dose of carbohydrate, so the fix is a smaller dosa with more paneer inside, which adds the fat and protein while keeping carbohydrate to what the body actually needs. Indians tend to eat emotionally and skip the protein entirely at breakfast; adding some fat, in the form of desi ghee for instance, and a protein source rebalances the meal.

Ghee, in this framing, is close to a superfood: it supplies the raw material for hormones, is a source of butyric acid and short-chain fatty acids, and supports brain energy. Industrial vegetable oils, by contrast, are treated as something that belongs in machines rather than in food.

Blood sugar is the master biomarker

So many problems — including women's hormonal issues such as PCOS — trace back to blood sugar. When insulin runs high you drift toward insulin resistance, hormones swing, and inflammation and glycation (the formation of advanced glycation end-products, an ageing driver) increase. A continuous glucose monitor worn on the arm makes this visible, showing exactly which foods spike insulin.

Three simple habits stabilise it:

  1. Do not sit after eating — take a 15–20 minute walk. On a glucose monitor, a post-meal spike that stayed high for two hours without a walk stabilised within roughly 30 minutes with one.
  2. Eat fibre before carbohydrate — a bowl of vegetables or seeds first. Roti is not counted as fibre here; the useful fibre comes from vegetables and seeds.
  3. Eat carbohydrate last, and keep portions modest — one roti rather than roti plus rice.

Persistently high blood sugar behaves like a blade on the nerves, which is the mechanism behind diabetic neuropathy — the numbness, prostate and cataract issues, non-healing foot wounds and even sciatica-like nerve pain that patients experience. Cinnamon and berberine can help regulate blood sugar; berberine has been compared to metformin for this purpose and also has anti-ageing effects. Swapping wheat roti for besan (gram flour) roti lowers the glycaemic load to roughly 25–30 and removes the gluten, but the carbohydrate still has to be balanced against dal and potato in the same meal.

Healing the gut and the gut-brain axis

Before biohacking sleep or anything else, fix the gut — because the gut-brain axis sits underneath so much of it. Around 90% of serotonin is produced in the gut, and serotonin drives melatonin production, influences libido and motivation, and shapes sleep. Blue-light blockers, a cool bedroom and an elevated bed all help, but none of it works without a healthy gut first.

Palak's three-part gut protocol:

  1. Fermented vegetables first. Fermented cabbage, beetroot and similar are the starting point. Not a single study shows fermented vegetables harm the bacterial colony, whereas curd (yoghurt) does not suit everyone.
  2. Prebiotic fibre next. Prebiotic fibre is food for whatever unique bacterial colony you already have, letting the good bacteria grow.
  3. Postbiotics. Rather than introducing live bacteria blindly, supply the metabolites the good bacteria produce — short-chain fatty acids and butyric acid, found in butter and ghee.

Partly true. Probiotics are a double-edged sword. In a study, lean mice given yoghurt improved while some mice became obese on the same yoghurt — the response depends on the individual's existing colony. Ideally a gut test would map your bacteria before any probiotic; until affordable testing arrives, prebiotics and postbiotics are the safer bet because they feed the colony you have rather than gambling on foreign live bacteria that may be rejected.

Resistant starch is an easy source of prebiotic benefit: cook rice or potato, cool it so the starch becomes resistant starch, then reheat and eat it — it also raises blood sugar less. For bulletproof-style coffee, the butter must be blended, because blending is what generates the butyric acid.

What damages the gut

The main gut disruptors are well known: gluten, dairy and industrial vegetable oil. Modern wheat is part of the problem — the agronomist behind the high-yield short-wheat varieties earned a Nobel Prize for ending famine, but the modification made the gluten harder to digest, which is why stone-milled older wheat is gentler for some people. When large particles like gluten cross a leaky gut lining into the blood, the immune system attacks them and can turn on the thyroid and joints, driving autoimmunity. Fibre is itself double-edged: pushing a big raw salad on someone whose gut is not ready causes bloating, so portion sizes of raw vegetables should be built up gradually as the right bacteria establish.

Recovery, sleep and the nervous system

Sleep is the master regulator — of hormones, metabolic health, brain and organ health. Palak sleeps around four and a half to five hours plus a 40-minute midday session closer to meditation and deep breathing, with a tracked sleep efficiency between 98 and 100%. Her stated logic is that we sleep to let the body clear waste and inflammation; by reducing the sources of that load, the amount of sleep the body demands falls. She treats energy, not an expensive test, as her real biomarker.

Her sleep levers:

  • Eat early and low-carb at night. Finish eating about three hours before bed and keep carbohydrate low, so blood sugar does not crash overnight and wake you — some night waking is hypoglycaemia. Favour good fat, protein and modest complex carbs.
  • Walk after the meal. A 15–20 minute walk stabilises the post-meal glucose.
  • Hack the light. No television in the bedroom; shift lights to orange after sunset and use red light for any night-time need, because white light enters through the eyes and suppresses melatonin, hiding from the brain the signal that night has arrived. These light changes alone helped reverse a case of sleep apnoea in her father.
  • Lower the temperature gradually. A cooler room supports deep sleep; she brought her parents from around 24°C down to 20°C over a year rather than all at once. Magnesium and glycine assist by lowering body temperature.

Training without tipping into damage

The first 45 minutes to an hour of a workout is where the benefit lives: heart rate rises, blood pressure regulates, toxins clear through sweat, muscles get their stimulus, and insulin and blood sugar normalise. Beyond that point, stress hormones climb — cortisol, and specifically glucocorticoids that begin breaking down muscle for fast energy once glycogen is spent. Past roughly 45–60 minutes of intense training you are in a catabolic state; for natural bodybuilding, less is more.

Sudden cardiac events in the gym are often mislabelled. They can be triggered by arrhythmias — irregular heartbeats that went unnoticed — combined with silent arterial blockages, calcification and a low VO2 max, so the heart cannot supply enough oxygen when a person overpushes. Stacking stimulants makes it worse: caffeine before a workout, or an ECA stack (ephedrine, caffeine, aspirin), overdrives the sympathetic nervous system, and everything lands on the heart. Magnesium helps here too, by preventing arrhythmias and supporting pumping efficiency.

The deeper skill is balancing the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems — knowing which days to overpush and which days to recover. On a high-stress day, choose walking, a good fatty-and-protein meal and rest over lifting. As one high-profile example, Cristiano Ronaldo, who tracks his readiness, skips the gym on days his cortisol is high. Overpush repeatedly through your twenties without balancing recovery and the damage surfaces later as chronic fatigue, hormonal decline and a mid-life crash.

Slowing ageing: antioxidants, inflammation and toxic load

One driver of ageing is reactive oxygen species (ROS) — the reactive by-products of metabolism, breathing and especially intense exercise, which damage cells from within. Antioxidants neutralise them. The mistake is trying to get all your antioxidants from food alone, because the modern liver is overwhelmed: it evolved to handle food, breath and a little natural pollution, not the pesticides in food, fluoride in toothpaste, microplastics in teabags, and heavy metals in lipstick it now meets daily.

On concentrated antioxidants, Palak points to potent polyphenol sources: a formulation she describes carries the polyphenols of about 10 cups of green tea in two tablets, with EGCG at 45% and 250 mg of trans-resveratrol — the bioavailable, longevity-associated form found in grapes. She also favours quercetin, which strengthens the heart and the immune system and can calm seasonal allergies.

Myth. Imported berries are not the antioxidant win they appear to be. Berries are cold-climate fruit; the versions sold in hot climates often taste wrong and carry pesticide concerns, so she stopped eating fresh imported berries. If you want berries, frozen berries are the safest bet. Better local options: dark chocolate at 80% or above (good for stem-cell regeneration and libido), pomegranate eaten in modest portions because of its fructose, and superfoods such as chlorella and matcha — a single matcha can carry the antioxidant load of many cups of green tea, and its L-theanine smooths the effect.

Inflammation is the common thread

Around 70% of chronic diseases have inflammation as an underlying factor. Cancer cases in India rose from roughly 14 lakh in 2022 to 15 lakh in 2023, and the striking figure is that about 70% are non-genetic — driven by inflammation and lifestyle. We all carry cancer cells; they proliferate when fed, which is why controlling insulin and chronic inflammation matters. Chronic inflammation frequently starts in a leaky gut, so the first moves are to reduce gluten, dairy, vegetable oil and sugar. Sugar is singled out: it sticks to collagen — the structural protein that holds skin and joints together — degrading it and adding to inflammation, which is why replacing sugar with stevia is worthwhile while still honouring the sweet taste.

Homocysteine — a marker worth tracking

Homocysteine is an amino-acid-derived marker that causes constant irritation to the endothelial walls of arteries; the resulting micro-cracks are where LDL and triglycerides — which are really just transport vehicles — get lodged. It should sit below 15; Palak's own reading came in at 25, and she found the tendency ran in her family, linked to an MTHFR gene mutation and poor methionine handling. A methylated B6, B9 and B12 combination lowers it quickly by donating methyl groups. The B9 should be methylfolate, not folic acid, because in people with the MTHFR mutation folic acid can trigger cancer-related genes. Homocysteine is also an attacker of brain cells, and in roughly 90% of heart attacks the levels are high — yet it is treated far less often than cholesterol.

Reducing the toxic load

Microplastics are now pervasive — a recent finding described a credit-card-sized quantity accumulating in the brain — and they can cross the blood-brain barrier and the placenta, having even been found in babies. Palak's response is behavioural and incremental: carry your own glass water bottle, avoid plane and packaged food served in plastic and foil, and change one habit at a time until it becomes automatic. Cosmetics are a hidden source of xenoestrogens: the fragrances and some preservatives and non-food-grade colours in creams go straight into the blood when applied, and she links them to heavier periods and breast-cancer risk. What you apply can matter as much as what you eat.

Read the label on chewing gum. Xylitol after a meal helps kill harmful mouth bacteria, but most gums are around 20% xylitol and 80% aspartame — worth checking before assuming a gum is doing you good.

Skin from the inside out

Skin is the largest organ and the first place ageing shows, so the internal work comes first: supporting the liver, adding antioxidants, healing the gut (directly linked to acne, mainly via dairy), adding omega-3 and vitamin E. Cell turnover — the shedding of dead cells — slows with age, and practices such as intermittent fasting and autophagy, which clear zombie cells, contribute to a natural glow. Dull, ashen skin often reflects internal health more than any topical product.

Chemical exfoliants can backfire. AHAs, BHAs, PHAs and retinol speed cell turnover, so at 25–27 the skin looks brighter as new cells surface — but the first layer of protection against sun is stripped, the skin barrier is disrupted, and the risk of pigmentation rises. With many products now carrying 1–2% of these acids, Palak expects pigmentation to become a widespread problem in a few years, and skin sensitivity that once appeared in one's seventies to arrive by the thirties. The priority is repairing the barrier, not repeatedly removing the stratum corneum.

On active ingredients she prefers signalling molecules over aggression. Peptides tell the skin to produce more collagen, and sodium PCA outperforms hyaluronic acid on hydration. For brightening she favours a night-time tyrosinase-inhibitor serum, which stops melanocytes from activating, over hydroquinone — cheap to synthesise but heavily caveated and banned in Europe on safety grounds. Newer plant-derived extracts, sourced from apple and peach by specialist European suppliers, can outperform hydroquinone without disrupting the barrier, though they are expensive and change colour, which is why large global brands avoid them. Her own minimal routine is four products: a morning antioxidant serum, a night tyrosinase-inhibitor serum, and a day and night cream, with no added colour or fragrance.

Because ingested collagen is distributed by the body wherever it is needed most — with skin receiving only around 5–10% — topical, targeted treatment is needed to boost collagen in the face specifically. Microneedling can help stimulate it. She is not a fan of radiofrequency microneedling on the face, as it can affect the facial fat pads and, in her view, drive ageing rather than reverse it.

For everyday stress, which keeps the sympathetic nervous system switched on, the ideal answer is breathing, meditation and gratitude journalling. When there is no time for that, adaptogens and amino acids help: ashwagandha (root only, standardised to over 5% withanolides and branded for heavy-metal testing, since the leaf carries toxic alkaloids that can harm the liver), tulsi (holy basil) extract, L-theanine, GABA, L-tryptophan and magnesium. L-theanine paired with coffee is a standout — it extends the alertness and prevents the caffeine crash.

The four pillars — and where supplements fit

The closing framework is four pillars of health: food, exercise, sleep and thoughts. If even one pillar drops, health suffers — and the fourth, thoughts, is the one most people neglect. You breathe and you think all day long, so working on mindset, relationships and belonging compounds more than any single supplement. In practice, when wealthy clients had every biohack available and still saw improvement stall, the missing variable was their thought patterns.

Give more time to food than to exercise, more time to sleep than to food, and more time to your thoughts than to sleep.

— Palak Midha, biohacking expert

Supplements are the parallel layer, not the base. Fix the pillars first, then use supplements as needed — an anxiety-support blend during a demanding stretch, melatonin while travelling, iron around a period, a protein shake when intake drops — and wean off them as life settles. On top of solid fundamentals, supplements add roughly a 20–25% edge, which is exactly why athletes use them: not to skip the basics, but to capture every advantage after the basics are done. And whatever you change, change one thing at a time — the aggregation of many small 1% improvements is what eventually moves your health and energy.

The overarching message is to become educated and aware about what your body needs to function optimally, identify what you are and are not getting, pick the top few things you must address, and allocate your budget accordingly. Prevention — through diet, lifestyle and targeted supplementation — is always better than cure.
