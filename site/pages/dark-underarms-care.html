Dark Underarms: Causes, Daily Care and Treatments That Work

Dermatologist Dr. Aanchal explains why the underarm area darkens, when it signals something that needs medical evaluation, the everyday habits that make it worse, and the creams and clinic procedures that gradually lighten it.

At a glance

  • Underarm skin one to two shades darker than the surrounding skin is normal — it is folded skin that experiences friction, much like the elbow.
  • Get evaluated if the area is more than three shades darker, has become thick or velvety, shows a sharp border against normal skin, or if other body folds are darkening too.
  • The main causes are frictional dermatitis, post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation from waxing, hair removal creams, spray deodorants and irritating skincare, and acanthosis nigricans linked to insulin resistance.
  • Daily care is simple: loose clothing, a thin moisturizer twice a day, gentle soap without scrubbing, and a soothing roll-on instead of spray deodorant.
  • A 6–10% glycolic acid cream once a week, milder lactic acid lotions, or roll-ons with kojic acid, niacinamide or licorice extract can lighten the area over three to six months.
  • For stubborn cases, in-clinic options include light chemical peels, Q-switched laser and laser hair reduction — and if you are overweight, losing even 4–5 kg makes a real difference.

When darkening is normal — and when to get it checked

Underarm skin will always be slightly darker than the rest of the body, and that is not abnormal. Dr. Aanchal sees many patients convinced their underarms are very dark who, on examination, turn out to be completely normal. One to two shades darker is considered normal, because the underarm is folded skin that experiences friction. The elbow works the same way: forearm skin is stretched while elbow skin folds, so the elbow always looks a little darker. Compare your underarm with the stretched skin on the inner arm and some difference is expected.

Mild darkening of one to two shades needs no treatment at all. But certain signs mean the area should be properly evaluated:

  • More than three shades darker than your normal skin.
  • Thickened or velvety skin along with the darkening. This suggests acanthosis nigricans, a condition in which the skin thickens, folds more, and therefore looks darker and velvet-like.
  • A sharp, defined border — a line-like demarcation between the underarm and the surrounding normal skin. This can be a bacterial or fungal infection. Many patients assume it cannot be an infection because there is no itching or discomfort, but bacterial infections can sometimes be asymptomatic, so a well-demarcated dark patch should always be examined.
  • Other body folds darkening too. If the neck and other folds are also dark, consult a dermatologist — it is important to rule out insulin resistance as the underlying driver.

Note: If your neck is dark along with your underarms, the pattern points toward acanthosis nigricans. Having it also raises your chances of developing diabetes in the future, so it deserves proper evaluation rather than cosmetic treatment alone.

Why underarms darken: the three main causes

Frictional dermatitis

Repeated rubbing gradually darkens the area over time. Friction increases when:

  • You are overweight — the two skin folds rub against each other far more.
  • You wear tight clothes — constant fabric contact in the underarm irritates the skin, and with time it darkens further.
  • The skin is lax — whether from weight loss, age, or naturally loose skin, lax folds rub more and can darken over time.

Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation

Here the skin was injured or inflamed, and darkening followed as it healed. Common triggers:

  • Waxing. The most common cause. Wax that is too hot, or the pulling of the strip itself, injures the skin, and over time this leaves darkening.
  • Hair removal creams. Even with regular use and no obvious reaction, the area can darken over time — sometimes with visible redness after application, sometimes without any.
  • Spray deodorants. Deodorant comes out of a pressurized pump, carries a high alcohol content and heavy fragrance, and all of this strongly irritates underarm skin. The alcohol gets trapped between two skin folds where there is already moisture and sweat, the skin gets irritated, and it heals leaving darkness behind. Dr. Aanchal advises staying away from deodorant sprays; if you need to control body odor, roll-ons are a better option.
  • Irritating skincare. Exfoliating the underarms with glycolic acid toners or home remedies can inflame the skin, and the darkening that follows can be worse than what you started with. Do not apply irritating products to this area.

Acanthosis nigricans

This condition is quite common in the Indian population because insulin resistance is seen more often here. The skin of the folds thickens and looks dark and velvety. If you have acanthosis nigricans, your chances of developing diabetes in the future also increase, and darkening in the underarms typically comes with darkening of the neck and other body folds.

Daily habits that protect and improve the area

  1. Wear loose clothes. Especially with synthetic fabrics, tightness is a problem: synthetic cloth holds sweat for a long time, the trapped sweat macerates the skin, injures it, and leaves darkening behind.
  2. Moisturize twice a day. Underarms are usually skipped, but a thin layer of moisturizer gives the skin some protection and keeps it from getting irritated.
  3. Wash only with a gentle soap. No scrubbing, no loofah. Aggressive scrubbing may make the area look lighter for a short while because dead skin comes off, but in the long run it only irritates and darkens the skin. Care for the area gently.
  4. Use a soothing roll-on. Choose one with soothing ingredients that calm the skin, repair the barrier, and reduce excess sweating and odor without irritation.

Follow these consistently and the darkness will stop getting worse — and slowly start to improve over time.

What to stop doing

  • Waxing — completely. Even with mild darkening, do not wax; in Dr. Aanchal's experience, darkening definitely worsens with waxing. Hot wax, cold wax, wax strips, Rica wax, chocolate wax — every type has the same impact on underarm skin.
  • Hair removal creams. They significantly irritate the skin and can leave darkness behind.
  • Reverse shaving. Shaving itself is fine, but shave in the direction of hair growth. Shaving against the grain cuts the hair too close to the surface, raising the chances of ingrown hairs — and with them, irritation and further darkening.
  • Scrubs and home remedies. No gritty particles, no besan (gram flour) scrubs, orange peel or lemon juice. Even homemade remedies will irritate this skin over the long term.

Creams that can help — and how to use them

Glycolic acid creams (6–10%)

A glycolic acid cream at 6–10% usually works well for underarm skin — but only once a week. Using it more often will not bring faster results; it can irritate the skin, the darkness will not budge, and it can sometimes get worse. Once or twice a week at most, and in Dr. Aanchal's view once is enough. Glycolic acid removes the dead upper layer of skin; the newer skin underneath is lighter, and the darkness reduces gradually. Expect to use it regularly for three to six months before you notice slow lightening — results are not quick.

Lactic acid lotions

Lactic acid is milder and gentler on the skin. It is the better choice if your underarm skin is currently irritated, has been over-irritated in the past, or has ingrown hairs — it exfoliates gradually without aggravating the area.

Lightening roll-ons

Roll-ons containing ingredients such as kojic acid, niacinamide or licorice extract can gradually improve underarm skin. A roll-on alone will not erase the darkness, but it can bring considerable improvement over time — especially if the darkening was caused by deodorant use in the first place.

Limits of creams: If the underarm skin is distinctly thickened, roll-ons will not improve it, and creams alone will not deliver much change either. Thickened skin needs proper weight loss, careful daily care, and possibly in-clinic procedures to reduce the thickness. If the darkening is mild, however, these creams will definitely help.

In-clinic procedures

Chemical peels

Light chemical peels exfoliate the surface so that the creams you use penetrate better and the darkness reduces over time. Expect four to five sessions, about once a month, depending on how thick and how dark the skin is.

Q-switched laser

If the skin is not thickened at all and the darkening is mild, a Q-switched laser can bring considerable improvement. This too may take four to five sessions, roughly once a month.

Laser hair reduction

This works indirectly: once the hair is gone, you stop waxing, stop shaving and stop hair removal creams, and without that constant monthly irritation the skin gradually improves. Dr. Aanchal has seen many patients who came in with darkening look much better after laser simply because the repeated waxing injury stopped. If you have thick underarm hair and wax every month or two, laser hair reduction is well worth considering — underarm hair is thick, so the laser works particularly well there.

Plan for six to eight sessions, once a month: hair looks noticeably finer after about four sessions, and after eight it can reduce by up to 90%. Note that this is hair reduction, not removal — 100% of the hair will not go, but the few hairs that remain grow so thin and fine that they are barely visible, needing only a trim every three to six months.

Safety: Always get laser done under the supervision of a qualified professional, at a proper clinic where a dermatologist oversees the treatment. If you are not improving, a doctor needs to be there to adjust the parameters.

The four priorities, in order

  1. Lose weight. Weight loss reduces friction, reduces skin thickness and reduces insulin resistance, so the darkening comes down substantially. Dr. Aanchal has seen that losing even 4–5 kg visibly reduces darkening of the neck and underarms. Losing weight should be your number one priority.
  2. Moisturize lightly and shield the area from rubbing. This alone brings considerable improvement.
  3. Avoid friction and injury. No waxing, no reverse shaving, no hair removal creams, no tight clothing.
  4. Use a gentle exfoliating cream once a week to gradually improve the skin.

This is a slow process — there is no overnight improvement — but within three to six months underarm skin improves considerably.

This article is for general education and does not replace personalized medical advice. Consult a dermatologist for diagnosis and treatment of persistent skin darkening, especially with thickening, sharp borders or darkening of multiple body folds.
