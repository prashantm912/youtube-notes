Choosing the Right Face Wash, Moisturiser and Sunscreen for Your Skin Type

Dermatologist Dr. Sarin explains how to identify your skin type at home and match each of the three cornerstone products — cleanser, moisturiser and sunscreen — to it, with specific product picks for oily, dry, combination and sensitive skin.

First, know your skin type

Everything else follows from this. Using products that were not made for your skin can cause reactions, keep you from healthy skin in the long run, and deny you the results you are working for. A common casualty: people who have never had a pimple in their life start breaking out simply because their products do not match their skin type.

The check happens at the mirror when you wake up:

  • Oily skin: the skin looks distinctly oily and shiny in the morning.
  • Dry skin: after washing your face or showering, the skin immediately feels tight, stretched, almost cracking — you feel you must apply something right away.
  • Combination skin: neither of the above happens and the skin behaves normally; typically the T-zone produces a little oil while the rest of the face stays normal to dry.

A basic routine rests on three cornerstones: cleansing, moisturising, and protecting with sunscreen. Everyone should follow these three steps; anything beyond them depends on your skin's needs, your goals, and any specific concern you are treating.

Cleansing: why it matters and how to do it right

A cleanser removes the oil that deposits on the skin, pollutants from the environment, and any makeup or skin products you have applied. Getting these off matters for two reasons:

  • Clogged pores. On oily or acne-prone skin, product and oil build-up can block pores, let bacteria grow, and raise the chances of pimples. The less time these sit on the skin, the better — so wash once in the morning and once at night with a cleanser suited to your skin type.
  • Oxidation. Pollutants are generally oxidants. Like a cut apple turning brown and shrivelling, they oxidise the skin — including its DNA — which brings on ageing and pigmentation earlier.

Technique: wet the face with water, take a coin-sized amount of face wash, and massage it in thoroughly — into the corners of the nose, eyes and lips and up to the ears — so oil is lifted from every spot. This matters most for oily skin. Very dry skin can skip the morning face wash altogether, since it barely secretes oil overnight; if the skin still feels dehydrated, use a moisturising cleansing lotion instead.

Double cleansing: optional, and there are three ways

Double cleansing, a Korean skin care concept now trending, is not an essential step. It is for people who do not feel sufficiently clean after one wash — who sense makeup, sunscreen or pollutants still sitting on the skin. Step one removes oil, makeup and pollutants; step two is your regular cleanser for deeper cleaning. Three product families cover step one:

  1. Cleansing milks and lotions — suit every skin type and are very gentle. The Moiz cleansing lotion and Cetafresh lotion both work here: take one or two pumps without any water, blend into your sunscreen or makeup, rinse with water, then follow with your regular face wash.
  2. Cleansing balms and oils — for very dry skin. Their higher oil content blends with the oil component of sunscreen and makeup so it lifts off cleanly. Picks: Clinique Take The Day Off, and a cleansing balm from Quench. Oily skin should stay away from balms and oils — they can themselves clog pores and trigger acne — but may still use them on stubborn eye makeup or lipstick, since those areas generally lack oil glands and clog far less.
  3. Micellar water — ideal for oily, acne-prone skin with blackheads and whiteheads. Its tiny micelles bind oil (from makeup or sunscreen) on one side and water on the other: wipe the whole face with a soaked cotton pad, rinse the remainder off with water, then use your regular face wash.

Moisturiser: your skin needs water and a seal to keep it in

Like the rest of the body, skin needs water. It holds water naturally, but environmental conditions evaporate it — which is why skin builds its own surface oil layer to preserve moisture. The right moisturiser depends on which half of that system your skin lacks:

  • Dry skin produces too little oil, so its water escapes. It needs oil-rich moisturisers that deliver water and form a cover that keeps it in. Ideally moisturise two to three times a day.
  • Oily skin has plenty of oil but may lack water. It needs lightweight, water-rich, low-oil formulas — at least once a day. Dr. Sarin's suggestion for very oily skin that hates the sticky daytime feel: apply a little moisturiser at night over your serums, wake up with plumper, dewier skin, and skip the morning application. Night moisturisation is an essential step.

What about facial oils?

Partly true. Oils can help hydrate — but only for very dry skin, and never alone. Coconut oil contains no water, and skin needs both water and oil: apply a good water-containing moisturiser first, then the oil on top, and dehydration resolves faster. Coconut oil is generally considered safe for skin, but in India's sticky, humid weather it can clog pores — so apply it before a bath and wash off the excess with soap and water.

Oils to avoid. If you have brown skin, do not use almond oil or mustard oil on your face: both contain molecules that make skin sun-sensitive. You will not notice at first, but pigment slowly builds where they are applied, and over the years those areas darken — a condition called lichen planus pigmentosus that studies link to mustard and almond oil application, especially in Indians. And olive oil is to be avoided by every skin type: despite its premium image, it can destroy the skin's natural barrier, and studies back this up.

Sunscreen: the step people skip — even indoors it matters

Sunscreen is the third crucial, mandatory step: you have cleaned the canvas and hydrated it — sunscreen is what protects it going forward. Patients often ask why they need sunscreen when they stay home all day. The answer: if daylight reaches you indoors — if you can see without switching a light on — visible light is reaching you, and where visible light reaches, UV rays reach too.

  • UVB — the burning rays — burn the skin and cause redness. Window panes in homes and cars filter UVB out.
  • UVA — the ageing rays — cause fine lines, wrinkles, dark spots and pigmentation. Window glass does not filter UVA.

For Indians, ageing is more of a concern than burns: melanin-rich darker skin rarely burns or develops skin cancers, but ageing and pigmentation trouble almost everyone. If future fine lines, tanning or pigmentation concern you, wear sunscreen at home too.

What to check on the label

  • SPF 30 or above — this indicates the level of UVB protection.
  • PA+++ or PA++++ — the PA factor indicates UVA protection, which matters most for Indian skin.
  • Tint or iron oxide if you sit in front of laptops and screens: tinted sunscreens with a slight brown colour, or ones containing iron oxide, filter blue light.

Beyond the basics: exfoliation and serums

Exfoliation

As we age, dead skin cells stop shedding as fast as they did in youth and start accumulating, so exfoliation becomes essential after you cross 20–25. The signs: dullness, more tan around the forehead and mouth, or a bumpy, non-smooth texture when you run a finger over the skin.

There are two kinds of exfoliants, and dermatologists always prefer chemical over physical. Physical exfoliants act like fine scratches on the skin, and as those micro-injuries heal over time they can leave pigmentation. Chemical exfoliants — acids — dissolve the glue holding dead cells on; they can occasionally over-exfoliate, which is exactly why choosing strength and frequency carefully matters.

  • Alpha hydroxy acids (AHAs) dissolve the cement between skin cells — best for tanning and black or brown marks. Options: glycolic acid, mandelic acid, and lactic acid, which is very good for sensitive skin.
  • Beta hydroxy acid (salicylic acid), usually at 1–2%, goes inside the oil glands and dissolves the oil there — it does little for the surface. It is for whiteheads, blackheads, roughness on the nose, and acne.

Frequency depends on your concern and the acid percentage. Dr. Sarin's general example for glycolic acid: at 5–6% (the common strength), use it three times a week; at 2%, daily use is fine; at 15–20%, only once every one to two weeks.

Serums

Serums are also an additional step, in brief: niacinamide and vitamin C are good for pigmentation control; retinol works for acne and anti-ageing; vitamin C helps with anti-ageing too. Exfoliating acids also come in serum form.

Two rules that make any routine work

  1. Patch test every new product. Apply a little behind the ear or on the forearm, leave it for a day, and watch for irritation — redness, itching. Only if nothing happens should the product go on your face.
  2. Stay consistent. Whether your routine has three steps, five or ten, results come only with consistency. Use something for a month and a half and then drop it, and your skin sees no lasting change. Follow the basic face wash–moisturiser–sunscreen regimen for three to four months and your skin's health improves considerably — always with products chosen for your skin type.
