Eat for Your Biology: Personalised Nutrition, Gut Health and Metabolic Age

Sports and clinical nutritionist Ryan Fernando argues that there is no universal diet — only your diet. His method treats food as fuel, the gut as the seat of mood, and testing as the way to replace guesswork with a personal blueprint.

At a glance

  • Food is fuel before it is pleasure. The taste buds are a tempter, not a guide; the gatekeeping question for anything you eat is "Is this food worthy to enter my body?"
  • Everyone is bio-individual. What suits one person's genes and gut can harm another's, so a single "correct" diet does not exist.
  • The gut microbiome ferments your food and releases molecules — including serotonin precursors — that reach the brain, tying eating directly to mood and mental health.
  • Genetics decide a lot: lactose and gluten tolerance, magnesium absorption, cancer-risk genes and more. Testing turns years of trial and error into a blueprint.
  • You can also read your own body without a lab — through a food diary, heart and breathing rate, and honest attention to burping, farting, skin and stool.
  • Sugar is not the villain by itself; the dose and your genes are. In excess it accelerates aging by attacking hemoglobin and DNA.
  • Fasting is a powerful healer, but "vanity fasting" for weight and "pain fasting" for disease are different, and medical conditions require professional supervision.
  • The body is the most expensive real estate you own — the one asset you can never replace.

The mindset: food is fuel before it is pleasure

Most people treat the tongue as the final judge of what to eat — if it tastes good, it is good. Ryan Fernando's starting point is the opposite: the tongue is only a tool to tell you what tastes better, not what your body should receive. He traces India's tangled relationship with food to its history — generations of scarcity made abundant, sumptuous eating a marker of affluence and reward, and that emotional programming was handed down by parents and grandparents without an element of science attached.

The correction is not to shame people into eating well but to educate them, because health is deeply personal. He frames the taste buds as a mistress constantly trying to tempt you, and offers a single line as the gatekeeper for every meal:

Is this food worthy to enter my body?

Underneath the philosophy sits a physical claim he returns to throughout: what you eat and how you sleep is the seed point for everything the body then becomes — brain, muscle, skin, blood. A seed has to be watered and fertilised to become a tree; you cannot skip the process and demand the result. And the stakes are framed in ownership terms — your body is the most expensive real estate you will ever hold, an SUV or Rolls-Royce you can never trade in for a new one, so the standard for what you put in it should be correspondingly high.

He also positions physiology as the foundation of psychology: set the body straight and the mind becomes better aligned, more able to perform, and happier as a result. His own first exposure to nutrition came through a friend whose long depression lifted only after he changed what was happening in his gut — a story that seeds the rest of this approach.

Your gut is running the show

The claim that unsettles people most is that mood and mental health begin in the digestive tract. Fernando explains the machinery in plain terms. The stomach has no bacteria — it is an acid mixer that breaks big molecules down. The small intestine breaks food further into amino acids, glucose and fatty acids. The action happens in the large intestine, a long "stadium" packed with bacteria: there are more bacterial cells in the body than human cells, and roughly 2.5 to 3 kilograms of bacteria in the gut alone.

Those microbes feed on your food and release molecules back into the gut, which are then absorbed into the bloodstream and carried to the brain and muscles. Feed good bacteria nutritious, clean food and they release good molecules — including serotonin, which he calls the body's happiness and confidence index. Feed bad bacteria cheap, pesticide-heavy, taste-first food and they throw out bad molecules. Over two or three years of eating poorly, performance and mental health decline; fix the eating and within about six months the good bacteria crowd out the bad.

He cites two studies to support the food–mind link, with the caveat that he does not recall the exact figures and that the studies could be found and verified:

  • A prison study (America or Australia) in which one block of prisoners was given only a multivitamin and an omega-3. Incidences of aggression, murders and suicidal tendencies fell in that block compared with the control block.
  • An Australian study finding exercise to be roughly 1.5 times more powerful than medication and counselling for depression.

His practical model: depression can begin in the gut, quietly building for years before it registers as a mental problem — at which point people reach for counselling and medication. He urges checking earlier for genetic deficiencies in absorbing B-complex, omega-3 and magnesium, and for gut health, rather than treating only the downstream symptom. He notes that even gastroenterologists are increasingly turning to diet, and one prominent gut-health doctor has asked him for a nutritionist because clinicians now recognise diet needs correcting.

Context, not a cure-all. Fernando presents the gut angle as a frequently missed contributor to low mood — not as a replacement for mental-health care. He is explicit that he is a nutritionist, that the studies he cites should be looked up, and that people with medical conditions need professional guidance.

Bio-individuality: why your genes decide your diet

The through-line of Fernando's practice is that no two people are nutritionally identical, so no single diet is "correct." The food most people eat, he argues, was never scientifically designed — it comes out of love and culture — and modern farming compounded the gap by optimising crops for yield rather than nutrient density. He cites a paper on apples showing much higher iron content in the 1960s than today, which he links to earlier generations having better hemoglobin. Nutritional deficiencies, in this view, are partly built into the food supply and partly written into each person's genes.

His own genetics illustrate the point. He carries a gene that absorbs magnesium poorly, which he connects to cramping during sport as a teenager, chronic constipation (magnesium softens stool), and the observation that people with higher magnesium tend to have larger brains. He also carries genes flagged for cardiomyopathy and hemochromatosis that, in his case, are recessive and not expressed — what he calls "winning the genetic lottery" — but which prompted him to have his father tested too.

Milk: a North–South genetic split

Milk is nutritious, Fernando says, but only for people who can digest it — and many cannot. He offers a climate-and-history explanation: in colder North India milk kept longer and was consumed fresh, so the gene for digesting lactose stayed strong (he estimates more than 50% of the North Indian population digests milk). In hotter South India milk spoiled fast and was eaten fermented as curd, where lactobacillus has already broken lactose down — so the South Indian gene for digesting milk directly is weaker. Refrigeration and cosmopolitan inter-region marriages have since scrambled these inheritances, leaving many people drinking milk their genes were never built for. His advice is simply to get tested rather than assume.

Gluten and the case for eating like your ancestors

Fernando wrote a book, Wheatless, after discovering through genetic testing that he is allergic to wheat — a realisation that came only at 36, after decades of adult acne he now attributes to bread and milk. Gluten, the protein in wheat, barley and rye, is one that some people simply cannot digest, and millions do not know it. He is blunt that doctors — including in dermatology and even oncology — telling patients to "eat what you want" is wrong, because bio-individuality is real. Humans historically ate only six to eight foods available to them; the modern all-foods diet outran the genome, which changes only over thousands of years. For those drawn to veganism, especially Indians, he suggests plain vegetarianism is closer to ancestral eating and avoids heavily processed fake-meat substitutes — and wonders aloud how much common IBS traces to processed food, excess sugar, gluten or lactose.

The tests worth doing

Testing, for Fernando, is how you stop running blind laps around the stadium. He values that in India many of these can be ordered directly from a lab without a doctor's prescription, calling that democratisation important — while acknowledging doctors who counter that it can breed health anxiety. He describes eating flagged foods on purpose every six months and watching whether they rise in his food-allergy blood test — his wheat now reads "green" after a decade of avoidance, though his genetics still mark him allergic. He also raises harder-edged genetic findings: the MTHFR gene, which governs the DNA-protecting process of methylation and which many people carry on the "back foot"; a personal genetic sensitivity to toxoplasmosis (prompting advice to avoid raw meat, raw fish and sushi); and the BRCA1/BRCA2 breast-cancer genes, which he argues women — especially before fertility treatment — should consider testing for, referencing Angelina Jolie's preventive mastectomy as documented precedent. These claims about genes and cancer risk are presented as reasons for awareness and professional testing, not as diagnoses.

Read your own body — without a lab

Not everyone can afford a nutritionist or the tests, so Fernando offers an "idiot's guide" to feeling your way to good nutrition. The core idea: become Sherlock Holmes to your own body by isolating one food and observing what it does. Take a food you eat often — his example is soya chop (soya and wheat) three to four times a week — and track your response systematically.

  1. Resting heart rate: average it over the week you are eating the food.
  2. Peak heart rate: note your top heart rate during exercise.
  3. How you feel after eating: at the moment, then 2, 3 and 6 hours later as it passes through the gut — noting pain, burping and heaviness.
  4. The next morning: is your nose blocked, are you sneezing, is there phlegm you have to clear?
  5. Compare: drop the food the next day and watch how digestion changes.

He adds respiration rate as a marker — a good resting rate is roughly 14 to 18 breaths per minute, and eating the wrong food pushes breathing rate, blood pressure and heart rate up. In elite performance the goal is the lowest possible resting heart rate, so that the body has headroom when pushed; the everyday version is to notice which foods give you shanti (calm) and which give you taap (agitation). A small food diary captures all of it.

Some of the loudest signals are the ones people ignore or misread. Fernando notes that many Indians define burping as "acidity," when burping is actually wind and gas — from talking too much and swallowing air, or from foods (like milk) that ferment when they are not digested. Frequent burping and farting, acne and pimples, and explosive stool are all markers that something you are eating is not sitting well. His own example: after cutting rice from about fourteen times a week to roughly four, his stool improved noticeably — a crude but real indicator of gut health.

Simple markers to watch: frequent burping or farting, acne and pimples, blocked nose or phlegm in the morning, and stool that comes out fast and explosive rather than slow and steady. Fernando treats these as everyday, no-cost feedback on whether a food suits you.

Sugar, carbohydrates and fats

Not the culprit. Fernando refuses to convict sugar — or any single food — outright. Sitting as a "judge," his verdict is that no food is inherently the bad molecule; what matters is the dose-dependent serving. The problem with sugar is that it strongly activates the brain's primordial pleasure centre, and that modern life removed every natural limit on it.

In ancient times, he points out, sugar meant fruit — available for only a few weeks or months a season, earned by walking to find it, and delivering vitamins and minerals alongside the fructose. Today you can eat mango 365 days a year, and readymade sugar is available every hour of the day. The culprit is not the molecule but the constant, year-round intake — a specific case of his recurring first principle: the human race is eating too much. Genes still haven't caught up, since it takes two to five thousand years for them to change.

Dose and genetics are individual. A larger, more muscular person's sugar tolerance differs from a smaller person's, and there is both a diabetes gene and an anti-diabetes gene — some people (he cites his orthopaedic-surgeon father-in-law) eat copious sugar without becoming diabetic, while others develop it at 25. When people remove sugar and feel a big improvement, that itself proves they were taking too much; if removing it changes nothing, their intake was already moderate. He connects his own rising uric acid, as he approaches 50, to eating more sugar while bulking, and notices that three or four days without sugar stops his joints aching.

Sugar and aging

The reason many performance-focused people give up sugar is aging. The HbA1c test measures glycation of hemoglobin — hemoglobin being attacked by sugar — and glycation is itself a form of aging. Sugar also attacks DNA, and aging is partly the DNA unravelling. DNA is protected by methylation, which needs methyl-donor foods: amino acids such as glycine and cysteine, and B-complex, chiefly vitamin B9. The MTHFR gene governs this, and Fernando likens it to the protective film on a new car — people born with a compromised MTHFR gene who eat a lot of sugar are like a car with no paint, rusting instantly, the rust being aging of the DNA.

Carbs versus fats: test, don't guess

It depends on you. Asked which diet is better — low carb or low fat — Fernando's answer is to test. He wore a continuous glucose monitor and found that he spikes on orange juice while his brother, same genes and upbringing, does not. So he advises trialling low-carbohydrate, low-carb high-fat, low-fat low-carb and higher-carbohydrate approaches and tracking the outcome in your own biochemistry, rather than trusting a population study done on a different genetic group.

His own data: a moderate carbohydrate-and-protein diet (around 1 to 1.5 grams protein per kilogram) keeps his entire blood chemistry perfect, whereas low-carb, high-protein, high-fat throws his vitamin profile off — he watches the outcome even where he cannot explain the mechanism. The markers he reads include inflammatory ones (ESR, C-reactive protein, D-dimer, IL-6), a full lipid profile (LDL, HDL, VLDL, lipoprotein A and B, and where available cholesterol particle size), and the vitamin and mineral profile. For sugar specifically he points people to HbA1c, fasting glucose, and the HOMA insulin-resistance score, with a health coach, endocrinologist or diabetologist if the numbers are off — noting India is effectively the diabetic capital of the world.

Fasting: two kinds, and when to be careful

Fernando calls fasting the greatest healer, precisely because the human race is eating too much — but he splits it in two. "Vanity fasting" is intermittent fasting for weight and looks: a 16-hour fast with an 8-hour eating window, or 20 hours fasting with a 4-hour window. It works largely through calorie restriction, and he is candid that urban (and much of rural) India simply eats too much. "Pain fasting" is for people already broken by disease — diabetes, hypertension, cancer — and, he says, works even better; research suggests that once a fast reaches around 24 to 30 hours, autophagy sets in and the body gets a better metabolic outcome, with the claim that under 30 hours certain cancer cells can "hide."

He is firm on quality and safety. He promotes a water-based fast over a dry fast, reserving dry fasting for elite athletes (his example is finishing the Marathon des Sables across the Sahara), not ordinary people. And he pointedly refers medical cases to professionals: a diabetic who fasts can drop blood sugar dangerously low or slide into ketoacidosis, and those with cancer-risk genetics need real oversight.

His own story is a caution and a curiosity at once. Facing an idiopathic urticaria breakout that medication and elimination diets did not fix, he eliminated everything down to water and salt. The rash disappeared 72 hours into the fast — then reappeared the moment food was about to arrive at hour 76–77, before he had even eaten. He extended to a four-day water fast and felt great, describing the third day as almost elite and even spiritual. He is emphatic that he does not advise this without proper medical support, calling himself "a young idiot who often makes mistakes." He also advises against breaking any fast with outside food.

Get medical support first. Fernando repeatedly stresses that anyone with diabetes, cancer risk, or other medical conditions should consult a good medical dietician or doctor before extended or therapeutic fasting. Extended and dry fasting, in his telling, are not do-it-yourself projects.

Getting started: the smallest bites

Fernando accepts that only a small fraction of any audience will truly transform, so he favours giving people the smallest bite that changes something visible. His three simplest entry points:

  1. Fast a little. Try a 16-hour fast with an 8-hour eating window, or simply skip one or two meals across the week. He notes traditional Indian, Christian and Muslim practices all built in fasting days that modern generations have forgotten.
  2. Hydrate better. Drink better-quality water with a pinch of Celtic or Himalayan salt; skip exotic beverages like kombuchas and fermented coffees. Glacial or natural spring water is his ideal, since the body is about 60% water.
  3. Walk. Build up to 6–7 km a day — around 45 minutes — not necessarily in one shot. He argues a billion Indians need to walk, since not everyone has the time or money for a gym but almost everyone can walk. Walking calms the heart rate, gives time to think, and "informs" the gut microbes; morning exercise also makes you less likely to overeat through the day.

He is realistic about how the walking can backfire — a person who walks an hour then eats a 500-calorie cake as a "reward" cancels the benefit — but says that over one or two years of consistency, people begin to self-correct.

Habits take longer than you think

Myth. The popular "21 days to build a habit" figure is, in Fernando's telling, a misread of the science: 21 days was the time for the simplest tasks. For diet and exercise, he cites roughly 66–67 days for disciplined people and about 254 days for the undisciplined — which is why 30-, 60- and 90-day "transformation" promises so often fail on patience. Separately, he gives the gut lining about 90 days to fully turn over, so a serious elimination protocol runs at least that long; around 90% of clients, he says, feel "like a million bucks" by then.

A tomorrow-morning checklist

Asked what a healthy 28-year-old should do starting tomorrow, Fernando's list runs:

  • Eat organic where you can, and eat less.
  • Get a weighing scale that shows body-fat and visceral-fat percentage, so you can watch for excess.
  • Do a blood test once a year.
  • Keep one hour a day with no technology around you.
  • Get 7-plus hours of sleep in one stretch.
  • Eat your last meal about 3 hours before bed.
  • Get exposure to sunlight as the sun is setting — it helps deeper sleep.
  • Walk.

The motivational frame he leans on is metabolic age: at 48 he claims a metabolic age of 33, and describes many clients who are 48 on their licence but 65 metabolically. Because your body is the one asset you can never replace, and muscle is (in his words) the only age-reversible organ, the payoff for eating to your own biology is aging slower — living, as he puts it, with minimum charging and maximum performance, like the winning battery in an electric vehicle.
