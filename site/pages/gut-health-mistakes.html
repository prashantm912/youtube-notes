Gut Health, Decoded: The Habits That Quietly Harm Your Digestion

Gastrointestinal surgeon Dr. Dhaval Mangukiya explains what the gut actually is, why acidity is misunderstood, how to read the warning signs of reflux and GI cancer, and which popular "gut-cleansing" fads are more likely to land you in surgery than improve your health.

At a glance

  • "Gut" is a broad term for the gastrointestinal tract from mouth to anal canal, with the liver, gallbladder and pancreas as supporting organs whose secretions help digestion.
  • The gut microbiome — the bacteria, viruses and yeast living in the intestine — breaks food into an absorbable form. Unhygienic food and illness are the two big things that disturb it.
  • Acidity is not a disease. Stomach acid is essential — it is the first checkpoint that makes food digestible. Around seven in ten people experience hyperacidity at least once a week.
  • Carbonated drinks, soda and Eno-type remedies do not relieve acidity — they stimulate more acid secretion. Sharp pain, bleeding or chest pain that mimics a heart attack are reasons to see a doctor.
  • IBS is a diagnosis of exclusion, driven partly by the gut–brain axis. Bowel frequency varies between people; it only counts as a problem when it disrupts daily life.
  • Probiotic supplements and gluten-free or lactose-free diets help only the people who genuinely need them — most of the time, natural food and a proper diagnosis matter more.
  • Colon cleanses, deep enemas, juice and bitter-juice "detoxes", liquid-nitrogen food gimmicks and prolonged fasting can cause ulcers, infection and even intestinal rupture requiring surgery.
  • GI cancers are rising and hard to detect early. Unexplained weight loss, bleeding and a lasting change in bowel habit warrant a check-up.

What "gut health" really means

"Gut" is a layperson's term for the gastrointestinal tract. It runs from the mouth right to the last part of the intestine, and includes the oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, rectum and anal canal. The liver, gallbladder and pancreas are supporting organs: they introduce secretions into the tract that help digest food, so they too affect digestion even though the word "gut" mainly points to the intestine.

The microbiome, in plain terms

The microbiome is the population of bacteria, viruses and yeast that lives inside the intestine, working with the body in a synergistic relationship. Food is not simply absorbed the moment it touches the intestinal lining. These organisms first digest it and convert it into metabolites — an absorbable form — which the body's cells can then use. This is why the diversity and balance of the microbiome matters so much to overall gut health.

Prebiotic, probiotic and postbiotic

Three related ideas sit on this concept:

  • Probiotic — the microbiome itself: the natural bacteria and resident cells inside us. When their population is disturbed, the body's metabolism is affected in time, which is why a probiotic supplement essentially transfuses microbiome into a person.
  • Prebiotic — food for those organisms, prepared into a form that supports both the microbes and the human host.
  • Postbiotic — the metabolites the microbiome produces after converting that food.

What disturbs the microbiome

Two things stand out. The first is unhygienic food: whatever we take in through the mouth exposes the intestine directly, so contaminated food is the first thing that can disturb the gut organisms. The second is illness of any kind — the body's responses become hampered, and the population and functioning of the gut organisms suffer, which in turn worsens the side effects of the illness.

Common presentations seen in day-to-day practice are bloating, acidity, and gastric disturbances in the form of constipation or diarrhoea. The surgeon notes that gastrointestinal disease is rising — attributed to the westernisation of diet, pollution, and professional stress — and predicts that over the next couple of decades GI-tract illness and cancers could overtake other systems in medical impact.

Acidity and reflux: what's normal, what's not

"Acidity is a disease"

Myth. Acidity as such is not a disease — that has to be understood first. Acid is made in everyone's stomach, and it is needed: it is the first checkpoint that begins breaking food down and churning it into a form fit to be absorbed. Just as people differ in height, they differ in how much acid they make. The problem begins only when secretion rises past a certain level, and the acid starts to cause symptoms — most commonly a burning pain in the upper abdomen.

What GERD actually is

Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GERD) is, in simple terms, the stomach's contents and secretions flowing backward. A valve normally sits between the oesophagus and the stomach so that food which has entered does not return. If that valve is weak or there is an anatomical issue, food and secretions travel back up — sometimes all the way to the mouth. Because that refluxed acid reaches the chest and oesophagus, it can cause severe chest pain that mimics a heart attack and panics the patient. Since cardiac problems are far more dangerous, anyone with repeated sharp pain or chest discomfort should seek a consultation so it can be diagnosed correctly.

Alarm signs that need a doctor. Simple burning pain is the common presentation. Escalate if you notice: a sharp, shooting, severe pain in the upper abdomen (a possible sign of ulcers); bleeding, which can appear as blood in vomit or as black-coloured stool; or nutritional deficiency and anaemia. If reflux is constant and stopping you from carrying out your routine, see a gastroenterologist rather than self-treating.

"Soda or Eno will cure my acidity"

Myth. Many people reach for Eno, soda or carbonated drinks expecting relief, but carbonated drinks actually stimulate more acid secretion. They should never be used to control acidity. If the episode is transient and settles on its own, nothing needs to be done at all.

Antacids and PPIs: how far is safe

Antacids, anti-acidity syrups and gels are available over the counter, and many people take them like chocolate after meals. Used on and off — because of food habits, lifestyle or professional stress — easily available antacids are completely fine to take. Roughly seven out of ten people experience hyperacidity at least once a week, so this is a very common reality. But if you need the medicine very frequently, at least one doctor consultation is warranted.

Proton pump inhibitors (the "-prazole" drugs) are the most commonly used acidity medicines, and this is where the habit turns risky: people visit a doctor once and then continue the same drug for years, even lifelong, on their own. There are correctable factors behind acidity that make continuous use unnecessary. One important cause is H. pylori infection — a common organism whose alteration raises acid secretion and damages the stomach's mucosal lining, producing small ulcers. Treated with a proper course, a person can be completely relieved of that acidity, which is why continuous self-medication should prompt a proper investigation and diagnosis instead.

The lifestyle drivers

Anything in excess harms the intestine — sweet, spice, masala or oil alike. Excessive spicy or oily food raises acid secretion, so the entire diet needs balance. Habits like staying up late and eating late at night also affect patients and increase acidity, and are worth moving away from. As for home remedies, there is no genuine remedy that suppresses acid; the most reliable lever is diet modification.

IBS, constipation and the gut–brain connection

"Any irregular bowel habit is IBS"

Myth. Irritable bowel syndrome is a diagnosis of exclusion, and it is over-labelled. Bowel frequency naturally varies: one person may pass motion once in two days, another four times a day — both are normal, just as some people feel hungry twice a day and others four times. Society has built a habit of insisting the bowel must clear every morning, so anyone whose bowel opens in the evening gets told they are abnormal. These are absolutely normal variations, and many people get labelled with IBS because of this belief.

The gut–brain axis

IBS does exist scientifically, and it works on the gut–brain link. A very common experience is needing to rush to the toilet just before an exam or an important meeting: signals travelling from the intestine to the brain — and central activity in return — increase intestinal motility. The scientific basis is real. Whatever is absorbed through the intestinal lining is ultimately converted, via the liver, into the energy sources the brain also needs, and the brain is connected to the whole body through nerves. The gut's absorption levels and the immune response are tied to the brain as well. This is early science with a lot of research still ongoing, so it is too soon to claim a food will make the brain stronger or younger — but the connection is now entering the scientific data.

IBS is labelled a disease only when it disturbs day-to-day activity and work. Someone going three or four times a day who is otherwise working absolutely fine needs no treatment or consultation. It is more often reported by young, working professionals — not because it is age-related, but because their symptoms interfere with output and efficiency, so they notice and report it more.

The FODMAP idea and food triggers

FODMAP refers to fermentable elements. For someone with IBS, a low-FODMAP diet is advised. Broadly, simpler forms of carbohydrate are more advisable than complex ones — raw sugar, very heavy sweet foods and some fruits such as apple fall into the high-FODMAP category. Lists of low- and high-FODMAP foods are easily available to look up and note down.

Because the same plate does not suit everyone — one person may not tolerate milk, another paneer, another curd — the practical step is to identify the trigger yourself with a food diary. When a patient is too overwhelmed by symptoms to pinpoint the trigger, medicines that help maintain the gut microbes and motility are prescribed early on, but ultimately education about the triggering factor matters most.

Stress and anxiety

After food, stress and anxiety are among the most important — and most uncontrollable — influences on IBS. Food anyone can control; stress, tied to professional and daily life, is harder. Before reaching for anti-anxiety or psychiatric drugs, the surgeon suggests drawing on culture, family bonds and friend circles to reduce stress and anxiety, which can cut off many stomach- and intestine-related issues.

Constipation: definition and the two-part fix

Constipation is not about a number. If a bowel movement — whether once in two days or twice in a day — leaves you feeling satisfactorily evacuated, that is not constipation. Conversely, someone going three or four times a day who still feels incomplete and full is constipated. The label should come from a doctor rather than from neighbours. The same drivers as acidity apply: stress, food habits, smoking and alcohol.

Two simple things prevent it. First, plenty of fluids — an adult body needs roughly five to six litres of liquid a day. Second, green leafy vegetables and a high-fibre diet. Follow these well and constipation usually will not occur.

On remedies: isabgol (psyllium) is a stool bulker — taken daily it does not cause intestinal issues, does not hamper motility, and does not increase secretion, so it need not be avoided. Laxatives are different: some increase intestinal secretion or motility, and should be avoided long-term. With a high-fibre diet and plenty of fluids, you need no churan, ayurvedic powders or other supplements.

Lactose, gluten and the elimination-diet trend

Lactose intolerance and gluten sensitivity belong to a broader group of conditions called malabsorption syndromes. In coeliac disease, for example, the intestinal wall's absorption capacity is reduced or lost, so certain elements cannot be absorbed and others block the absorption of the rest — which is where the gluten-free concept comes from.

Lactose intolerance

Lactose intolerance is usually diagnosed at an early age, sometimes genetically in children. Once someone is genuinely intolerant, it generally has to be avoided lifelong. Some autoimmune conditions — such as inflammatory bowel disease, in which ulcers form in the small and large intestine — also mean lactose is controlled or avoided (no milk or dairy) as a lifelong measure. The key caution: do not assume it and do not imply it for the whole household because one person does not tolerate milk. It must be properly diagnosed rather than self-assumed.

"Everyone should go gluten-free"

Not for everyone. Gluten-free diets have become a trend, but they help only the populations that need them — coeliac disease, the skin condition dermatitis herpetiformis, or cases labelled as IBS that respond to nothing else. For those people it is worthwhile. Applied universally to anyone with a label, it does nothing: no health benefit and no harm. If you have been diagnosed and will genuinely benefit, go for it — otherwise there is no need.

Probiotics, antibiotics and the supplement habit

When probiotics actually help

Prebiotic, probiotic and postbiotic products all focus on the intestinal microflora. Supplements are needed only when there is a genuine disturbance — for instance when pathologic organisms overgrow and unbalance the intestinal flora. Onion, garlic and yoghurt are everyday examples of pre- and probiotics: if your food already contains plenty of these, you are taking them in reasonable amounts, and the billions of organisms already living in the intestine do not need daily topping up. Just having a bottle on hand, or a suggestion from someone, is not a reason for daily supplements.

There is also a delivery problem. Many commercial probiotic preparations are largely killed in the stomach and are not processed to survive the gastric juices. That is by design: the stomach is the first checkpoint, and its acid destroys elements harmful to the body while passing important ones on to the intestine. The same logic applies to food, which is why the emphasis is on natural, organically available food rather than artificial, chemically synthesised, packaged products — better avoided as far as possible. Even surgical patients in the recovery phase can heal through healthy food alone rather than tonics.

The antibiotic problem

India is described as one of the few countries where antibiotics are available over the counter, so people self-medicate readily for a cold, cough, diarrhoea or constipation. Two issues follow: they do not know the correct dose, and they do not know the correct duration. Every antibiotic's dose, weight-based amount, frequency and duration is identified through research for a reason — to kill the infecting bacteria. But antibiotics also kill the normal, needed organisms in the intestine and shrink their population. That is precisely why the prebiotic–probiotic–postbiotic concept was created: as supplements to help patients whose gut organisms are affected by antibiotics, especially those on them for a long time. These medicines belong under supervision and guidance, taken in a scientific manner — not as a fashion alongside every other supplement.

Detoxes, cleanses and fasts that backfire

Several popular "gut-cleansing" practices are, in this surgeon's experience, actively dangerous.

These practices have led to surgery. Colon cleansing, deep enemas, bitter-juice "detoxes" and liquid-nitrogen food gimmicks have all put patients on the operating table with infection, ulcers or intestinal rupture. A healthy person needs none of them.

Colon cleansing and enemas

Doing colon cleansing or deep enemas without supervision is a very wrong concept and can be severely harmful. The surgeon has operated on patients whose colon-cleansing sessions led to intestinal infection, ulcers, and in some cases rupture — an illness that then required surgery. The intestine is well designed to cleanse itself; with proper eating and exercise, bowel movements stay regular and no external flushing is needed. Colon cleansing is done legitimately only under supervision, with medication, as preparation for endoscopy or colonoscopy — the practice likely spread when people began doing it without any such need.

Juice detoxes and bitter juices

The idea of living on juice or liquids for a day or two to make the gut "healthy" does not hold up. The body does not detoxify — it starves. Everyone has some reserve, so you may feel fine for a while, but once reserve energy and calories are used up you crash, and immunity is disturbed. The surgeon extends the same warning to kadha and bitter juices — bitter gourd (karela) juice, neem or limda juice — popularised especially during COVID. Bitterness does not mean the juice is carrying out toxins; there are many case reports of the stomach ulcerating and rupturing after consuming such bitter juices, and the surgeon has operated on those patients. Assuming that something bitter will "fix" all the organisms inside is, in reality, harming the body.

Prolonged fasting

Long fasts are called out as one of the most harmful habits for the intestine. Fasting features in many religious traditions, and in some communities people keep themselves completely starved for six to eight days at a stretch. Because the commensal cells of the gut also need feeding, prolonged fasting affects those organisms as well as the body's tissues and metabolism — the longer the fast, the more everything is affected. The advice is to avoid fasting for more than about four hours and to eat frequent meals, so that secretions, physiology and metabolism stay aligned.

Intermittent fasting, in perspective

Intermittent fasting — eating within an eight-hour window and not eating for sixteen — is a scientific concept often adopted as an easy way to lose or maintain weight. No version of it asks you to starve for days; it clearly requires eating after some hours. The surgeon's core point is that everyone's body responds differently, so any such concept must be customised to the individual. In practice, some people respond very well to intermittent fasting and can continue it long-term; others get sudden weight loss, hair fall, dull skin and other changes. Rather than copying a fad diet, adopt it only if it genuinely suits your lifestyle and health.

Red flags: abdominal pain and GI cancer

When abdominal pain is serious

Any pain is never truly normal — if the stomach hurts, it is not 100% fine. Still, transient cramps, like a muscle cramp after activity that eases with rest, are nothing to worry about, because every organ contains muscle that can cramp or become inflamed.

Do not delay these. A sharp pain with an acute, sudden onset that does not relieve after some minutes or after rest should be treated urgently — consult a doctor immediately so it can be diagnosed rather than left.

Why GI cancers slip past detection

Cancers of the abdominal organs are being seen in large numbers. Part of that is a genuine rise, but detection has also improved greatly thanks to CT scans, MRI, endoscopy and screening, catching early-stage disease that previously went unnoticed. The core difficulty is that the intestine and other abdominal organs are hidden, giving no external sign — unlike breast or head-and-neck cancers that can be felt from outside. By the time GI symptoms appear, the stage is often advanced, which is why prevention matters so much.

Three levers for prevention

  • Habit — smoking and alcohol are independently proven to be associated with a high incidence of any GI-tract cancer, whether of the stomach, oesophagus or liver. Avoiding them lowers the risk.
  • Food — smoked food irritates the stomach wall, and countries with a daily smoked-food habit (such as Japan and China) see more stomach cancer; the common Indian example given is the sizzler. Western-style frozen and packaged foods are low in fibre and linked to colon cancer, while previously cooked and smoky food is tied to bowel and stomach cancer. Commercially or chemically created sweeteners are best avoided; naturally available sweeteners like honey and sugar are not directly responsible for cancer, though diabetes is an identifiable factor for GI cancers, particularly of the pancreas.
  • Family history — most colon cancers are hereditary or familial, so anyone with a sibling or family member affected should get their abdominal organs screened.

Screening and the symptoms to watch

India has no organised screening programme. For high-risk people — a family history of colon or intestinal cancer, or relevant habits — an endoscopy or colonoscopy once in ten years is advised (some countries start endoscopy from age 40). Because population-wide screening is hard to achieve, the best defence is recognising warning symptoms: unexpected, unintentional weight loss without dieting; bleeding, which by itself does not mean cancer but does mean a check-up is needed; and a lasting change of bowel habit — for instance, someone whose habit has been one motion a day for thirty years suddenly becoming constipated or shifting to once every two or three days. Many such check-ups come back normal, but catching disease at an early stage allows treatment that can completely cure it.

A word on food gimmicks: liquid nitrogen

Fancy smoky desserts served with fumes often use liquid nitrogen, where one millilitre can expand to a hundred times its volume in fumes. The surgeon recounts a young woman at a wedding who took such an ice-cream in its liquid form before it had finished fuming; a single gulp was enough to rupture her entire stomach and intestine, and she was operated on that same night. Even one or two drops would be enough — a reason for public awareness of how harmful liquid nitrogen can be when consumed rather than allowed to evaporate.

The three things to get right

To keep the GI tract healthy, focus on three things: cut bad habits including smoking and alcohol and give the body enough sleep and rest; keep the diet genuinely balanced, spreading protein, carbohydrate and fat evenly across the day; and make stress relief a priority. Those three keep not just the gut but the whole body's physiology in order.

— Dr. Dhaval Mangukiya, gastrointestinal surgeon
