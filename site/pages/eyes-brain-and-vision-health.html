Your Eyes and the Brain: Vision, Dopamine and Mental Health

Ophthalmologist and researcher Dr. Rohit Shetty argues that the eye is far more than a camera: it secretes its own dopamine, its tears carry the body's chemical secrets, and its health is tightly wound into mood, stress and the brain. This is his account of why modern screens are reshaping our eyes, how to protect them, and where his research is heading next — alongside his more personal, philosophical reflections on visualization and meditation.

At a glance

  • The lens in the eye refocuses thousands of times a day; sustained near-focus on a phone can push it into a kind of spasm, and chronic accommodation drives the eye to elongate — the mechanism behind rising myopia (short-sightedness).
  • Dr. Shetty's team published research showing the eye itself secretes dopamine, which appears to slow eyeball elongation. Sunlight, play and sport raise dopamine — one reason some countries mandate outdoor play for children.
  • Tears carry biomarkers much like blood or urine. His group reports developing a tear-analysis device and has measured dopamine in tears; emotional tears differ chemically from tears of joy.
  • Vision depends on two eyes fusing into one image (binocular single vision) and on depth perception (stereopsis) — abilities that sharpen with training and blunt under acute stress.
  • Chronic eye problems are closely tied to mood; dry eye, low tear secretion and high cortisol can dull the "shine" of the eyes, and Dr. Shetty links persistent, unaddressed eye issues to depression in some patients.
  • Glaucoma — the "silent thief of vision" — builds painlessly over time; a yearly eye check that includes eye-pressure measurement is the main defence.
  • Everyday protection is simple: blink often, avoid constantly washing the eyes, limit screen strain with the 20-20-20 rule, get sunlight and vitamin D, and look after the gut.
  • Dr. Shetty's clinical hopes include tear tests to predict disease and gene therapy for corneal conditions such as keratoconus. His remarks on meditation and the "third eye" are offered as personal reflection, not clinical fact.

Screens, focusing and the myopia epidemic

Every time you look at something — your hand, a face, a screen — a lens inside the eye focuses and refocuses, thousands upon thousands of times a day. Dr. Shetty compares continuous near-focus to exercising a muscle without rest: hold it long enough and it goes into a kind of spasm. Scrolling makes this worse because each new clip forces the eye to refocus on a different object at an awkward angle, well below eye level. He links the same downward, angled posture to the neck and back complaints now common in screen-heavy lives — often described as "tech neck."

Myopia, he says, is becoming one of the biggest "pandemics" of the age. Where once only a couple of children in a class of a hundred wore glasses, now it is the child without glasses who stands out. Evolution gave humans excellent sight, and heavy near-work is pushing eyes in a direction they were not built for.

Why chronic near-focus changes the eye

Myopia, in his account, follows from a chronic state of accommodation. When the eye stays locked on something close for long periods, it secretes biological factors and behaves as though the person needs mainly near vision — so it adapts. That adaptation includes the eyeball elongating, which increases minus (short-sighted) power. His analogy: sit permanently hunched and your posture reshapes to match; the same principle is at work inside the eye. Because the problem is near vision, the condition is aptly named nearsightedness.

The 20-20-20 rule. Every 20 minutes, look at an object about 20 feet away. Dr. Shetty explains this breaks the "accommodative cycle" — the sustained near-focus that drives strain — giving the eye's focusing system a genuine reset. Putting the phone down and looking into the distance relaxes a strained eye.

Dopamine, sunlight and children's eyesight

Dopamine is a neurotransmitter — the chemical that gives the drive to push toward a goal — and it is not confined to the brain. Dr. Shetty notes it appears across the animal kingdom in behaviours like hunting and reproduction, and that even gut bacteria secrete it, which he offers as one root of the phrase "gut feeling." Meditation, good sleep and winning an award all raise it.

What his own research adds is that the eye secretes dopamine too, with dopamine receptors and activity in the eye and a synergistic relationship with vitamin D. In work he says his group published in a research journal, dopamine in the eye appears to stop the eyeball from elongating — and since elongation increases minus power, that makes local dopamine protective against worsening myopia.

What raises a child's eye dopamine

The practical message is aimed at parents: make children play, be active, and spend time in sunlight, because vitamin D from sun exposure pushes dopamine. Dr. Shetty points to Asian countries such as Singapore, where children are required to play two hours in the morning or evening — noting that the benefit of sport here is not physical fitness but its effect on the progression of myopia. He describes his institute as among the first to measure dopamine from tears and, on that basis, to predict which children were likely to progress toward high short-sighted powers such as minus 10 or 11. His first paper on this was published around five years ago, with several more on dopamine in progress.

Play is eye medicine, not just exercise. Dr. Shetty's point to parents is that sport matters for a child's eyes specifically — especially a child who already has a small power — because activity, sunlight and the dopamine they generate act on how the eye grows, not merely on general health.

Tears: a new window into the body

Early in his career, Dr. Shetty set out to treat tears the way medicine treats blood or urine — as a fluid that carries the body's information. The prevailing view was that tears are "just water" and a dead end, the same scepticism once directed at urine before it became a rich diagnostic sample. Over roughly twenty years his group worked toward what he describes as a tear biomarker device — which he calls the "biomarker pathfinder" — able to read dopamine and markers of inflammation from a specialized paper that collects the tear film. He frames tears as a "pen drive" of biology: plug in the right reader and they reveal a great deal.

His stated ambition is to extend tear analysis to proteins specific to Parkinson's and Alzheimer's disease, and later to cancers, so that disease can be predicted from a painless sample rather than a needle. He compares the first version to the first mobile phones — limited at launch, then rapidly capable of far more.

Not all tears are the same

Emotional tears, he says, differ in composition from ordinary ones. When you cry with emotion, the tears carry the neurotransmitters flooding the body at that moment; tears shed watching a sad film can contain a lot of unwanted steroids, while tears of joy carry different chemistry again. He cites an experiment, done in one of the Asian countries, in which students were shown films to make them cry and their tears were analysed — with the tears differing according to whether the emotion was happiness or sadness (sadness raising steroids and stress-related chemicals). In his framing, the tears themselves register your emotional state.

The eye–brain axis: spatial awareness, sports vision and mood

Anatomically, the eye is an outgrowth of the brain. The retina's layers converge into the optic nerve — a cable connecting, in his analogy, the bulb to the switch — and that nerve has the same components as brain tissue, including the myelin sheaths that relay information. What we see travels to the brain, where the limbic system, amygdala and hippocampus add emotion, memory and judgement. Hence his distinction between seeing (what the eye does) and processing (what the brain adds): the raw image means little until the brain identifies the object, colours it and attaches feeling.

Binocular vision and stereopsis

Two eyes send slightly different images that the brain fuses into one — binocular single vision — and the combination is what lets us judge depth and distance (stereopsis). Close one eye for long enough and depth perception falters. Dr. Shetty argues that elite athletes have exceptionally good binocular vision and depth judgement, which is what lets a top batsman seem to "see the ball in slow motion" — not literally slowing time, but gauging it better. He recommends that a young player struggling in sport be given basic vision tests for fusion, depth perception and stereopsis before being dismissed as lazy, since poor stereopsis alone can stop a batsman judging a ball.

Stress can steal your depth perception. Dr. Shetty notes that in an acute phase of stress a person can lose stereopsis — the ability to judge depth becomes slow, producing small misjudgements. He offers this as one possible reason a form slump snowballs in an athlete under pressure.

Training the eyes: adaptation, dark vision and flow

Much of what looks innate is trained acclimatization. Special-forces soldiers see well in the dark not because they are different, but because repeated exposure shortens their dark-adaptation time and tunes the brain to it; the light-sensitive rods carry night vision, and elite forces often insist on 6/6 (20/20) or better vision because a clearer eye gives better rod responses. The eye exercises cricketers and F1 drivers do before performing are, in his view, about building reaction time and acclimatizing the senses. In deeply practised "flow," he suggests, some responses are handled so automatically — he describes reactions that behave as though routed through the spinal cord rather than waiting on the brain — that the body reacts before deliberate thought.

Eyes, stress and emotional health

Because the eye is so directly wired to brain centres, Dr. Shetty sees a strong "brain–eye axis" in emotional wellbeing. A stressed person may feel eye twitching, tiredness and brain fog. He recounts patients with chronic eye problems and untreated binocular-fusion issues — seeing double, misjudging stairs, feeling they might fall — whose dopamine and serotonin were low and whose drive had drained away, and links prolonged, unaddressed eye issues to chronic depression in some cases. He is careful to separate this from psychosomatic illness (claiming an illness that is not present), calling it instead a real, poorly understood and hard-to-research connection.

The same axis, he argues, shows in dark circles and the "shine" of the eyes. Dark circles can follow fat loss around the eye and pigment changes from chronically squeezing the eyes under stress; they also follow simple dehydration — which is why almost everyone steps off a long flight with them, and why he advises doubling water intake in dry, low-humidity environments. As for eyes that "stop twinkling" when someone is sad: under heavy emotional stress, high cortisol worsens dry eye and reduces tear secretion, and the reduced tearing dulls the shine we subconsciously read as sadness.

On the "urine cure" for dry eye. Asked about a folk practice — patients instilling their own urine for severe dry-eye pain — Dr. Shetty is explicit that this is wrong, unhygienic and unsafe, and that no doctor would advise it. He explains only the likely reason people perceive relief: urine contains steroids, and steroids applied to tissue can transiently reduce discomfort. It is an explanation of a mechanism, not an endorsement.

Common eye conditions and how they are changing

Dry eye

Dry eye is Dr. Shetty's example of how medicine must keep updating itself. Cheap lubricant drops that worked reliably in the early 2000s often fail now, because the eye's environment — long hours of screens and blue light — has changed. Newer, more targeted options he mentions include omega-3 drops, trehalose-based drops and cyclosporine, aimed at treating the person more holistically rather than merely wetting the surface. He also flags a modern pattern of exaggerated sensitivity: people who cannot tolerate air-conditioning or bright light, whose eyes water in light, for whom old drops no longer work.

Keratoconus

Keratoconus (from the Greek for cone) is a condition in which the cornea progressively bulges into a cone shape; the name captures the change. Symptoms include blurring and seeing multiple images — one classic description is seeing three moons. Dr. Shetty, who did his PhD on it, says it is increasingly common in young people and is the reason around 90% of youngsters who need a corneal transplant do so. The textbook attributes it to eye rubbing and calls it non-inflammatory — a definition he says is roughly 150 years old. His "disruptive" research instead found a distinct set of bacteria in these eyes and points to inflammation (from long screen hours, dryness and other causes) as one of perhaps a hundred contributing factors, with chronic dryness the one he suspects most.

A gene-therapy hope for the cornea. Dr. Shetty describes his group working toward gene therapy for corneal disease — potentially a single injection or, in future, drops. The approach uses a modified adenoviral vector (with its infective genes removed) to carry a corrected gene into cells, "like a spacecraft," so an erratically behaving cell becomes controlled again. He notes that an existing gene-therapy injection for a form of blindness has cost on the order of crores of rupees, and that his motivation for building an indigenous Indian version is to make such treatment affordable. He voices a hope — framed as aspiration, not a delivered result — that a corneal gene therapy could be a world first.

Glaucoma

Glaucoma is a build-up of pressure inside the eye that damages the nerve connecting the retina to the brain; as it progresses, a person may lose parts of the visual field and see only half an image. Dr. Shetty calls it the "silent thief of vision" because it advances painlessly and unnoticed, and describes it as the third most common cause of blindness worldwide, rising because of under-screening, genetics and lifestyle. Occasional fluctuations in eye pressure are normal and not a concern; it is constantly high pressure that causes damage. Treatment ranges from pressure-lowering drops to surgery in severe cases. His prevention message is a routine eye examination — including a pressure check, much like a blood-pressure reading — at least once a year, especially for a young generation with heavy visual demands.

Everyday habits: what harms your eyes and what protects them

Cautions first, because these are the things people rarely connect to their eyes.

Habits and exposures to be wary of

  • Not blinking enough. Deep concentration cuts the blink rate (normally around 12 to 30 a minute). Because each blink spreads good-quality tears across the eye, Dr. Shetty suggests consciously reminding yourself to blink during screen work.
  • Constantly washing the eyes. A widely-held "healthy" habit that he says is not: splashing water on the eyes every half hour strips away the protective tear film.
  • Smoking and vaping. Smoking changes the eye's blood vessels and ocular surface, and is associated with early retinal changes, a higher glaucoma risk and early cataracts, partly through lower blood-oxygen reaching the eye. Vaping, he says, can in rare, susceptible individuals trigger a sudden vascular event in the retina — a rupture or clot that shocks it — leading to temporary and, very rarely, lasting vision loss.
  • Erectile-dysfunction drugs. Asked whether sexual habits affect eyesight, Dr. Shetty's medical point is specifically about Viagra: it can affect the eye's vasculature, and in people prone to small-vessel problems can cause acute damage or leakage in the macula — in his words, "not very safe" for the eye. (In the programme's opening, he notes such vascular causes rank among common causes of blindness worldwide.)
  • Kajal/surma in children's eyes. A common subcontinental practice with historical roots (once linked to the eye disease trachoma). Done unhygienically, or with unknown ingredients, it can introduce infection and affect the corneal cells — primarily a hygiene issue.
  • Scleral (eye) tattoos. A "complete no." Injecting colour into the sclera — important to ocular-surface health — risks serious complications including rupture of the eye, especially if done by an untrained person.
  • A recurring stye. Often bacterial and linked to oily skin, dry air-conditioned environments or vitamin deficiency, a stye should be seen by an eye doctor rather than treated like a pimple. Home care involves warm compresses (hot fermentation) and lubrication, sometimes an antibiotic ointment; in children it usually comes down to hand hygiene, and a repeatedly recurring stye means something underlying needs attention.

Food, gut and daily protection

On the protective side, Dr. Shetty highlights the gut as an unexpected partner to eye health: the gut microbiome strongly influences immune status, and neurotransmitters such as serotonin, dopamine and GABA are secreted in large part by gut bacteria — while stress hormones like cortisol alter the gut flora for the worse. His practical nutrition points echo mainstream advice: eat a variety of whole foods and avoid processed ones, and learn which foods bloat or upset you as your gut changes with age.

For the eye specifically, he names vitamin D (whose only real source is sunlight, since it needs sun to be synthesized), vitamin B12, omega-3 and fibre as beneficial, and cautions about carbohydrate load. He stresses that carbohydrate is sugar — not only sweets cause type-2 diabetes — and that a fasting insulin test (rarely done routinely) predicts diabetes risk years ahead, with a normal figure around 15 but many youngsters already at 27 or 28. Because diabetes threatens the retina, controlling the carbohydrate-heavy diet matters for the eyes.

Non-negotiable basics. As Dr. Shetty sums it up, every good health conversation lands on the same foundations: move regularly, do some yoga, sleep well, get sunlight, and eat clean, home-cooked, whole foods. His one addition for eye and brain health is to practise visualization each day.

Visualization, meditation and the "third eye"

This part of the conversation moves from clinical medicine into Dr. Shetty's personal and philosophical reflections, and the host's own experiences. It is presented here as their perspective and belief, not as established medical fact. Dr. Shetty himself repeatedly flags several of these ideas as hypothetical.

His anchoring distinction is again between seeing and visualizing. Seeing is the eye's job; visualizing is the brain's — adding colour, form and emotion, drawing on the visual centres in the occipital cortex plus frontal and temporal areas. On the widely discussed "power of visualization," he frames the benefit in terms of mental rehearsal: a batsman or surgeon who runs an action through the mind many times conditions the brain and body to perform it, so limbic centres and the hippocampus prime the nervous system for the moment. He cites cricketers visualizing an innings the night before as a version of this — a mainstream sports-psychology idea rather than a mystical one.

On meditation and turning the eyes upward, his medical note is modest: the upward eye-roll relates to Bell's phenomenon, a protective reflex in which the eye rotates up (showing the white sclera) to shield the cornea, and meditation is, at heart, brain exercise performed with the muscles relaxed. He describes brain-wave states (alpha, beta, theta and others) as the brain's electrical activity, and — quoting a separate guest's account — the alpha state as one in which the brain is more amenable to reprogramming.

Where medicine ends and belief begins. The host recounts a personal meditation experience — seeing a violet or blue circle, and later a six-pointed star, when the eyes are turned upward in deep meditation, which mystical traditions call the "third eye." Dr. Shetty does not present this as a clinical finding: he says the necessary peer-reviewed research has not been done, describes the imagery as a personal "form" through which someone connects to consciousness (and one that varies from person to person), and pins his hope on future functional-MRI and "mind–brain" research centres to study meditative states properly. He notes such research also matters for dementia, Alzheimer's, Parkinson's and depression. Treat this section as informed speculation and lived experience — not as medical advice or evidence.

He extends the same faith-and-mind theme to the placebo effect, recounting the well-known wartime story of saline injected in place of scarce morphine appearing to work, and describing a "theatre of healing" — the touch, listening and reassurance around a treatment — as a real part of why care helps. And he draws a philosophical line under the whole discussion: with less than 1% of the human body truly understood, medicine keeps returning to zero as each answer raises new questions. He also volunteers his personal belief that the eye's finesse is hard to credit to evolution alone and may point to "some divine intervention" — again, an explicitly personal view rather than a scientific claim.

The clinical portions — on myopia, dopamine, tears, dry eye, keratoconus, glaucoma and eye-damaging habits — are general education, not a substitute for a personal examination, diagnosis or treatment. The sections on visualization, meditation and the "third eye" reflect the speaker's and host's personal and philosophical views and lived experience, and are not medical facts or recommendations.
