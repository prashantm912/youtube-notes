Skin Care Myths vs. Facts: What a Dermatologist Wants You to Know

Dermatologist Dr. Jushya Bhatia separates fact from fiction on the most persistent skin care beliefs — from sunscreen safety and SPF numbers to face yoga, home remedies, open pores and the mirage of glass skin.

At a glance

  • Water, sleep and hygiene support healthy skin, but no single habit cures glowing skin, dark circles or acne — skin outcomes are always multifactorial.
  • Sunscreen helps prevent skin cancer and pigmentation. Claims that it causes cancer or disrupts hormones trace back to misread correlations and outdated filters.
  • SPF 30 and SPF 50 differ by only a couple of percentage points of UV blocked; applying enough, regularly, matters far more than the number.
  • UVA passes through window glass, so indoor and in-car exposure counts if pigmentation is your concern.
  • No cream permanently closes pores, erases stretch marks or removes dark circles — anatomy and genetics set the limits.
  • Home remedies are at best relaxing and at worst harmful: keep lemon, raw acids, essential oils and unlabelled creams off your skin.
  • A simple, sustainable routine — cleanser, moisturiser, sunscreen — used consistently for years beats constant product-hopping.

Water, sleep, sweat and hygiene: what daily habits really do

"Drinking more water gives you glowing skin"

Partly true. Water is essential: it is the medium through which molecules and growth factors move and cells communicate, and well-hydrated skin does look more vital. But the outermost layer of the skin is naturally deficient in water and has no blood supply, so the water you drink cannot reach it — that is what a moisturiser is for. Hydrate from the inside and the outside.

Around 2 to 2.5 litres a day is enough, or up to about 3 litres if you sweat heavily. Beyond that, extra water mostly means extra trips to the washroom, not extra glow. Dry skin in particular is not a drinking-water problem: it is a barrier problem (trans-epidermal water loss), best addressed with a ceramide-based moisturiser. As Dr. Bhatia puts it, water is only a part of the puzzle, not the whole puzzle — and what is in your diet alongside the water, such as antioxidants and nutrients, matters too.

"Enough sleep will cure dark circles"

Myth — sleep helps, but it is not a cure. Poor sleep genuinely makes dark circles look worse: late-night screen time increases blood circulation around the eyes, causing puffiness that deepens their appearance; chronically inadequate sleep impairs collagen repair, contributing to fine lines; and sleep deprivation acts as chronic stress, raising cortisol, which darkens the area further.

But dark circles are multifactorial. In brown skin they are often structural — a tear trough, thinner under-eye skin, a tendency to pigmentation — and lines, wrinkles and puffiness all worsen how they look. Correcting sleep alone will not remove them. The key distinction is causation versus association: bad sleep aggravates dark circles that are already there; it is rarely their sole cause.

"Poor hygiene causes skin problems"

Mostly myth. Lack of hygiene can worsen conditions you already have, but it does not cause most skin concerns. Acne, the most common example, is not the result of outside bacteria infecting unwashed skin. That said, if you do not wash your face twice a day, the skin's natural oil, grime and pollutants build up and can make existing acne worse.

The opposite extreme is just as unhelpful: washing repeatedly or using harsh cleansers strips the good bacteria — the skin's microbiome — which is worth preserving. Twice-daily washing with a proper cleanser is enough, even for acne-prone skin.

"Sweating detoxifies your skin"

Myth. The body's detox organs are the liver and kidneys, not the sweat glands. Whatever leaves the body in sweat is present in amounts too small to be meaningful; sweating is a mechanism for releasing heat, nothing more. The same logic applies to "blood purifiers" and detox drinks — your blood is not impure, and a very well-oiled internal mechanism already removes waste.

Exercise itself is genuinely good for skin, just not because of "toxins": it improves blood flow (that instant post-workout flush and healthy glow), releases endorphins and stimulates collagen formation. Exercise for those benefits — not to sweat something out.

Sunscreen: what actually matters

"Higher SPF means far better protection"

Partly true. SPF 50 does protect a little better than SPF 30 — but only a little. SPF 30 can block roughly 95–97% of UV rays, SPF 50 roughly 97–98%. Achieving SPF 50 requires more filters, which can make the texture thicker and less pleasant.

Dermatologists reserve the stronger recommendation for people who need the margin: if you have melasma, dark spots or skin that darkens easily, go for SPF 50. Otherwise SPF 30, properly applied, is fine. One practical caveat: most people under-apply sunscreen, and an under-applied SPF 50 may still protect somewhat better than an under-applied SPF 30. If you apply properly and reapply, SPF 30 is not a problem. Above all, pick a texture you enjoy and stick with it — regular use is what counts, not hunting for a mythical best product.

"Sunscreen causes cancer"

Myth. This rumour grew out of a correlation, not a causation. As sunscreen use rose in Western countries, recorded skin cancer rates also rose — and studies looking into it found the real explanations:

  • More awareness, more screening. Campaigns encouraged people to have moles checked every few months, and technology enabled earlier diagnosis — so more cancers were found, at earlier, more survivable stages.
  • False security. People applied a thin layer of sunscreen and then sunbathed for hours without reapplying, without the correct quantity, and without spreading it well — that is not real protection.
  • More cumulative exposure. Beach holidays, surfing and outdoor childhoods increased total lifetime UV exposure.
  • PABA. A sunscreen filter thought to cause mild irritation and skin sensitisation.
  • Benzene contamination. A contaminant — not an intended ingredient — that has been correlated with cancer was found in some products, and major brands withdrew the affected batches.

Sunscreen prevents skin cancer rather than causing it. Melanoma is described by dermatologists as among the top three deadliest cancers, and in Australia, where sunscreen use is heavily promoted, melanoma incidence has fallen considerably.

"Sunscreens are hormone disruptors"

Myth, in any practical sense. A few older chemical filters — oxybenzone (the most common), octocrylene and octinoxate — showed mild estrogenic and anti-androgenic activity in animal and in-vitro studies. Those studies used very high concentrations of a single chemical; the effect has not been shown in humans at the concentrations sunscreens actually contain. Filters have been detected in human blood, but at levels considered completely safe. One study estimated you would need to apply sunscreen over your full body every day for about 77 years before accumulation approached a concerning level.

These three filters are rare in modern formulations anyway. If you have used them in the past, there is nothing to panic about; if you remain anxious, simply avoid them. Newer filters — Tinosorb M, Tinosorb S, Mexoryl and Uvinul — are absorbed far less into the skin and are not endocrine disruptors, and among older filters avobenzone is known to be safe.

Pregnancy and children: during pregnancy, dermatologists advise a physical (mineral) sunscreen rather than a chemical one. Zinc oxide and titanium dioxide sit on the skin without being absorbed, making them the safe choice for pregnant women and for kids.

Dr. Bhatia also notes a pattern behind such scares: fear-based marketing. First an ingredient is branded dangerous, then the "free-from" alternative is sold to the frightened customer. If in doubt, search the published research and form your own opinion rather than buying the fear.

"Chemical sunscreens are toxic"

Myth. "Toxic" is a word without a scale — it can mean lethal or it can mean mildly irritating — and it does not belong on sunscreen.

"I would not like to use that word, toxic, for sunscreens. They have been incredibly helpful for so many patients — for people who struggle with pigmentation, they have changed their lives."

— Dr. Jushya Bhatia, dermatologist

Dr. Bhatia sees patients who started sunscreen during the lockdown, changed nothing else, and found their pigmentation visibly reduced two, three or five years later. Chemical filters also enable excellent formulations: the gel-based sunscreens dermatologists prefer for oily and acne-prone skin always use chemical filters. Sunscreen is a long game — its rewards show five and ten years down the line, and in South Asian skin, which is prone to melanin overdrive, melasma, pigmented demarcation lines and darkening around the mouth, consistent use makes a visible difference between long-term users and everyone else.

"You don't need sunscreen indoors"

It depends on your concern — but glass does not protect you. There are two kinds of UV rays, and window panes only stop one of them:

UVADark spots, tanning, ageing, collagen damageNo — it passes through car, office and home windows

Modern glass-walled offices, time on the balcony drying clothes, driving (including light reflecting off the dashboard) and short two-wheeler trips — a 15–20 minute school pickup counts — all add real UVA exposure. If pigmentation, dark spots or ageing concern you, apply sunscreen indoors too: once in the morning and again around 1 or 2 p.m., depending on your outdoor time. If you sit in a cubicle with no sunlight at all, or darkening genuinely is not a concern for you, skipping it is fine. For anyone struggling with melasma, even five minutes of exposure can cause darkening — and skipping sunscreen quietly cancels out every lightening product you are paying for.

"Sunscreen causes vitamin D deficiency"

Myth. The skin does synthesise vitamin D from UV light, but the requirement is modest: about 20–30 minutes of sunlight two to three times a week. A brisk 10–15 minute morning walk without sunscreen covers it. For the rest of the day — especially the harsh 10 a.m.–2 p.m. window when the UV index is high — wear your sunscreen.

No sunscreen gives 100% protection anyway; some rays always penetrate, and the exposure your hands receive is enough to trigger vitamin D synthesis. If you are severely deficient, the answer is doctor-guided supplementation, not abandoning sun protection and collecting dark spots. Dr. Bhatia's arithmetic: better to use sunscreen and take a weekly vitamin D supplement than to spend heavily on depigmenting creams later. Patients with melasma or photo-aggravated pigmentary conditions should minimise sun exposure and prefer supplementation. And for mental well-being, time outdoors is genuinely valuable — but it is your eyes that need the break from screens and the natural light, not your unprotected skin.

Tinted sunscreens: who actually needs them

Useful — if the tint is iron oxide. Medically, tinted sunscreens are built around iron oxide, a filter for visible blue light. Blue light comes from indoor lighting, laptops, cameras and phone screens — though the largest source is actually sunlight itself — and studies show it can induce melasma. Iron oxide's natural rust colour happens to match brown, Indian and Asian skin, which makes these formulas doubly practical.

Check the label, though: some products marketed as "tinted" contain makeup pigments and no iron oxide. That is makeup, not added protection. For melasma patients, the recommended approach is layering — apply your regular sunscreen first, then a layer of tinted on top, because tinted formulas are usually silicone-based and feel heavy at the full two-finger dose, especially on oily skin. Iron-oxide-based foundation also adds a little protection along with camouflage, and a tinted sunscreen makes midday reapplication over makeup far easier.

Products and actives: spending smart, reading signals

"Expensive skin care products are better"

Yes and no. Not all expensive products are worth the money. But some cost more for a legitimate reason: genuine research. Breakthrough molecules in dermatology almost always come from large brands, because proving safety and effectiveness takes research labs and years of investment — and ultimately the consumer pays for that. Paying for proven research is reasonable; paying for a label is not.

The competition in India's skin care market has made budget-friendly products genuinely effective, so good results do not require a luxury price tag. Choose products you can afford to use long term — one expensive serum used for a month or two changes nothing. The exception: if you have sensitive skin, rosacea or a condition that limits how many products you can tolerate, well-researched (often pricier) products are the safer bet if you can afford them. When in doubt about which brands are research-backed, ask a dermatologist — it is nearly impossible to judge from the outside.

"Everybody needs a vitamin C serum"

Myth — everybody might benefit, but not everybody can tolerate it. Vitamin C is popular for good reason: it works on multiple pathways at once. As an antioxidant it boosts collagen and protects the skin from free-radical damage, slowing ageing; it is also a brightening agent that reduces melanin synthesis.

The catches: it needs an effective concentration — 1–2% does nothing, 5% is the practical minimum, and 10% or even 20% only if your skin can take it. Sensitive skin often cannot tolerate it, and in acne-prone skin it can trigger breakouts; many patients who self-prescribe it come to the clinic with exactly that. If vitamin C does not suit you, do not force it and do not feel you are missing out — alternative antioxidant molecules exist, and a dermatologist can substitute one in.

"If it tingles, it's working"

Myth — and a dangerous one. Chasing the sting can wreak havoc on your skin barrier. Tingling is a normal, brief property of only a few specific molecules: azelaic acid very commonly tingles, the medication tacrolimus does too, and a few seconds of tingling from vitamin C or an exfoliating acid can be acceptable. But if your moisturiser or face wash tingles, something is wrong — stop using it and get it addressed. A product does not need to hurt to work.

"You should change your products every few months"

Myth. The psychology is understandable: people see drastic improvement when they first start good skin care, then it plateaus, and they conclude the product "stopped working". In reality they have reached the top of the mountain — there is nowhere higher to go, and the goal shifts to maintenance. Stopping the product can send the gains back downhill.

If something suits you — cleanser, moisturiser, serum — keep using it. Switching, say, vitamin C for niacinamide will not transform anything. What can be adjusted is strength: if you have used an active properly for a year with no irritation, a dermatologist may slightly raise the concentration. Skin care is a long-term game played over decades — a marathon, not a sprint.

What creams cannot change: muscles, pores, scars and glass skin

"Face yoga can replace Botox"

Myth — the two do nearly opposite things. Botox works by relaxing overactive facial muscles, the ones that repeatedly stretch the skin until wrinkles etch in. Face yoga is exercise: it tones muscles, it does not relax them.

Facial muscle anatomy also defies the gym logic behind the trend. A biceps originates on bone and inserts on bone, so its belly bulks up when trained. Facial muscles originate on bone but insert into the skin, and their fibres are so thin that there is little to strengthen — and working the upper face vigorously stretches the very skin you are trying to smooth, potentially etching lines deeper over the years. One study Dr. Bhatia cites found that 30 minutes of face yoga three times a week — quite a rigorous routine — produced a slight improvement in muscle bulk, and only in the cheek muscles. The lower face may benefit a little, which is convenient since Botox is used only sparingly there. If you enjoy face yoga, it is not harmful in moderation — but expect no magical results, and it is no replacement for Botox. Nor is Botox compulsory: if you have wrinkles and you accept them, that is not a problem to fix.

"Open pores can be closed permanently"

Myth — and closing them would be unhealthy anyway. Open pores are normal skin anatomy: the openings of your oil glands. Think of the oil gland as a well deep in the skin — oil has to travel up and out, and sealing the opening would clog the skin and breed pimples. Pores look larger for two reasons: oilier skin needs wider openings to accommodate more oil, and with age the collagen around each opening breaks down, letting it slacken.

What helps: salicylic acid keeps oily-skin pores cleansed; prescription-grade retinoids, used with a doctor's guidance, tighten collagen and shrink pores to a certain extent; and procedures such as microneedling radio-frequency or skin tightening can refine them further. What does not: ice cubes and ice-water dunks shrink pores and constrict the surrounding blood vessels for a few hours — fine before makeup or an event, pointless as a daily ritual with permanent expectations. And stop examining your skin from an inch away; truly poreless skin is an Instagram-filter fiction, and some of what people call open pores are actually acne scars, which are a different problem entirely.

"Stretch marks can be removed with creams"

Myth. A stretch mark is a type of scar — collagen fibres that have broken. No cream fully reverses a scar. Enough studies have been done to say plainly that oils, vitamin E, centella asiatica and squalane do not improve stretch marks; millions of dollars have been wasted on them over the years.

What has some evidence: early stretch marks — the purplish-red ones that follow weight change or pregnancy — can be reduced to a degree with tretinoin, correctly diluted. Once marks mature and turn white, tretinoin works poorly. In the red stage, silicone-based gels and mildly anti-inflammatory products may also help a little. Beyond that, the only real strategy is prevention: keep the skin well moisturised (higher moisture content lowers the risk, including before workouts), gain weight slowly, progress weight-lifting gradually rather than suddenly, and take extra care in the third trimester of pregnancy, when sudden weight gain produces the most marks. Genetics matter too — if your mother or sister developed stretch marks, or you already have them on the breasts or thighs, your risk during pregnancy is higher, and some cases cannot be fully prevented.

Caution: tretinoin is a prescription treatment and is not safe during lactation. Use it only under a dermatologist's guidance, at the dilution they teach you.

"Anyone can get Korean glass skin"

Myth. Korean glass skin belongs to Korean genetics. What is achievable is the best version of your own skin — call it Indian glass skin. The "glass" effect is not an inner glow; there is no bulb inside. It is light reflecting off a surface that is smooth in texture and even in tone, and both of those can be worked on.

The realistic route: good hydration inside and out, a good moisturiser, microneedling to refine texture, a depigmenting serum to even the tone, and — non-negotiable — sunscreen. Sustained for years, that gets you close to the best skin possible for you. Genetics set each person's ceiling: melanin production and the way skin reflects light differ between people, so identical results are impossible by definition. The flawless faces on social media are ring lights, camera angles, golden-hour lighting, filters and editing. Aim for healthy skin, not glass skin — and remember that skin colour itself cannot be changed by any cream, powder or beetroot rubbed on the face. Knowing the difference between what can change and what must be accepted is what protects you from scams, and from irritating your skin into more dark spots chasing the impossible.

Home remedies: the safe, the useless and the harmful

"Essential oils are natural, so they must be good for skin"

Myth — dermatologists put them near the top of the do-not-apply list.

"If there is one thing you should never put on your skin, it is essential oils."

— Dr. Jushya Bhatia, dermatologist

Essential oils rank among the most sensitising, allergy-prone ingredients you can apply. They are absorbed through the skin, and in Dr. Bhatia's assessment pose more endocrine-disruption concern than sunscreen filters ever did. In a polluted environment their sticky film also traps airborne pollutants against the skin, oxidising it faster and accelerating ageing. They bring no meaningful antioxidant, brightening or moisturising benefit that a purpose-made serum or moisturiser would not deliver better. The rare exception she allows is kumkumadi oil, derived from saffron, which is known not to irritate — though she suggests saving even that for cleaner air than Indian cities offer. If an oil already demonstrably suits you, continuing is your call; but if you are starting a skin care journey, essential oils are not the route.

Do not use essential oils on children or during pregnancy. Their sensitising and allergic potential makes them unsuitable for both.

"Natural is always better and safer"

Myth — "good for you" and "effective" are different things. Multani mitti (fuller's earth) is a genuinely good clay that helps control oil, but it will not cure a pimple. Honey is natural, but for acne-prone skin it makes no sense. Natural ingredients also vary enormously in strength from batch to batch, whereas a tested, packaged product has a stated, reproducible concentration — which is the more trustworthy of the two. And many brands marketing themselves as "natural" simply are not; flip the pack and read the ingredient list.

With expectations set that way, an occasional curd or clay mask does no harm; expect it to cure acne, reverse ageing or remove dark spots and you will only be disappointed. Compliance is also why real skin care wins: a serum that takes two minutes to apply gets used every day for years; a mask that takes 15–20 minutes to mix, dry and scrub out of the basin does not.

"Areca nut paste removes dark circles"

Myth. There are no studies showing it works, and the ritual — grinding a paste, applying it, waiting — costs time that would genuinely help elsewhere: sleeping ten minutes longer will do more for dark circles than any paste under the eyes.

Dark circles attract more myths than almost any concern precisely because they are hard to treat even medically. No single cream removes them, because the causes differ: a structural groove needs filler; puffiness responds to caffeine-based products; thin skin needs collagen stimulation. Where medicine struggles, remedies multiply — mostly harmless, but not improvements. If a remedy truly worked, Dr. Bhatia notes, a pharmaceutical company would long ago have concentrated the extract into a cream and sold it.

"Coconut oil is a good face moisturiser"

Mostly myth. Coconut oil is highly comedogenic: it can give you acne even if your skin is not acne-prone, and clogged sweat and oil glands can develop superficial bacterial folliculitis — an effect documented in studies, including in South Indians who massage their legs with coconut oil. On acne-prone facial skin it should never be used; it definitively worsens acne.

The genuine exception: coconut oil is one of the safest oils for people with eczema, applied over and above the anti-inflammatory creams their doctor prescribes. For everyone else with dry skin, modern moisturisers — formulated around what the skin barrier is actually made of, in the right ratios of cholesterol, triglycerides and free fatty acids — penetrate better and work better. Oil mostly sits on the surface, so if you love the feel, use it as a final sealing step over a proper moisturiser, not instead of one.

At-home chemical peels: the riskiest shortcut

Do not do it. Chemical peels are a double-edged sword even in a clinic — Dr. Bhatia still applies peels to her patients herself, because an acid on someone's skin demands that much care. The 25–30% AHA peeling solutions sold online, used at home once a week or fortnight, have left patients with burns and darkening at the corners of the mouth and sides of the nose, where no protective sealant was applied.

Indian skin raises the stakes further: deep peels (such as phenol peels) can work well on Caucasian skin, which tends to heal with fresh collagen and few marks, but our skin heals almost any irritation with dark spots. If you want to exfoliate at home, stay with basic acids at 5–7% in toner form — a low percentage used gently and regularly is far kinder than a high percentage hitting the skin in one go. Keep it under 10%, once in a while, at most. Patients who over-peel for a couple of years arrive with skin so sensitised that even simple products burn, and it must be slowly repaired before any real treatment can resume. If you want a genuine peel, have it done professionally, occasionally.

Red flags: how skin care misinformation sells

Two dermatologists who spend much of their working lives correcting misinformation see the same patterns repeat:

  • Fear marketing. First an ingredient is declared dangerous; fear creates urgency; you check your product, find the villain, and buy the alternative being quietly sold. If a claim worries you, search the published research and form your own opinion.
  • The know-it-all remedy account. One person cannot have had every skin problem from head to toe, let alone know the cure for each. Ask: what studies were done? On whom was this tried? Friends who are qualified Ayurvedic doctors say many such accounts are misleading people about Ayurveda itself.
  • Filtered proof. Dramatic before-and-afters are often filters, flattering lighting, or dark spots and tanning that faded on their own with time — yet they draw millions of views and purchase links.
  • Preying on insecurity. Skin colour is a sensitive subject, and people with no one to confide in become targets. Nothing applied to the face can make anyone fairer. Believing one impossible claim makes you more gullible to the next scam.
  • The backlash effect. When harsh experiments backfire, people conclude that no skin care works and abandon it altogether — the opposite of what guided, realistic care would have given them.

Never use an unlabelled cream — even from a trusted source. Jars with no label, including those used in salon "Korean glass facials", have been found to contain steroids (recognisable to a dermatologist by smell alone), excess lead and unlisted skin-lightening agents. These cause real long-term harm.

A routine you can actually keep

If the noise overwhelms you, the floor is simple. Even a two-product routine — cleanser and moisturiser, or moisturiser and sunscreen — is better than falling for the endless trap of miracle products.

  1. Cleanse twice a day with a proper, non-harsh cleanser — enough to remove oil, grime and pollutants without stripping the skin's microbiome.
  2. Moisturise, choosing ceramide-based formulas if your skin is dry — topical hydration does what drinking water cannot.
  3. Sunscreen every morning, in a texture you like — the one you will actually use — with a reapplication around 1–2 p.m. on days with outdoor exposure. A tinted (iron oxide) layer on top helps if you have melasma or spend the day in front of screens.
  4. Stay consistent. Compliance is the most important ingredient: nothing changes from one use, improvements plateau into maintenance, and the visible rewards compound over five to ten years.
  5. Escalate with help, not on your own. Actives such as vitamin C, retinoids, tretinoin and anything above toner-strength acids belong in a plan made with a dermatologist — as do stubborn concerns like melasma, acne, dark circles and vitamin D deficiency.
