Why Lifestyle Diseases Are Hitting Your 20s and 30s — and the Habits That Reverse Them

Health and nutrition coach Shivangi Desai — who reversed her own thalassaemia-minor limitations, raising her haemoglobin from below 8 to 15.1 — explains why diseases of the 50s and 60s now appear in teenagers, and lays out the everyday habits, plate proportions and mindset shifts that prevent and reverse them.

At a glance

  • Diseases are getting younger: back pain expected at 50 now shows up in 19-year-olds, fatty liver in 7-year-olds, type 2 diabetes in 14-year-olds. Period pain, obesity and diabetes have become common — but common is not normal.
  • The root of most lifestyle disease is excess — of carbs, sugar, sitting and late nights. Anything in excess is poison. The three top drivers are insulin resistance, high inflammation and obesity.
  • Belly fat is dominated by visceral fat, which gathers around organs and drives fatty liver, pancreas and even heart issues. It is a disease in its own right, not a cosmetic annoyance.
  • Spot reduction does not work — no set of crunches melts belly fat. The body loses fat overall; diet does roughly 80% of the work, paired with weight training.
  • The three-step belly-fat framework: an early, light dinner followed by a 15-minute walk; weight training, which keeps burning calories for the next 24–48 hours; and 7–8 hours of sleep, ideally starting by 10–11 pm.
  • Balance the plate: 25% salad, 25% seasonal vegetables, 25% protein and only 25% carbs — most Indian plates are half carbs or more. Add protein, fat and fibre (the PFF formula) to every meal.
  • Muscle is the most anti-ageing organ in the body and the difference between a 40-year-old who looks 60 and one who looks 20. Walking and basic yoga are good but not enough — muscle needs training.
  • Reversal journeys generally begin showing results within three months — the intestinal lining renews itself roughly every two months, the liver in about eight weeks, the skin in about 28 days.
  • Most people fail not from lack of knowledge but from starting with "how" instead of "what" and "why". A SMART goal, a 30-day habit target and an emotionally attached reason outlast motivation.

We are ageing — the diseases are getting younger

Conditions that once belonged to the 40s, 50s and 60s are now routine in the 20s and 30s. Coach Shivangi Desai sees 19-year-olds with the back pain of a 50-year-old, seven-year-olds with fatty liver and 14-year-olds with type 2 diabetes — ages at which, as she puts it, these illnesses simply should not exist.

Part of the problem is normalisation. Obesity, diabetes, PCOD and painful periods that need painkillers are treated as ordinary facts of life. They are common now, but they are not normal — when period pain demands a painkiller every month, something is wrong.

The three top causes

  1. Insulin resistance — created chiefly by excess carbohydrates. Every carb, whether roti, rice or a spoon of sugar in tea, is converted into sugar in the body. The carbs themselves are not the villain — everybody should eat them — but the quantity is, especially when almost no movement uses that energy up.
  2. High inflammation in the body.
  3. Obesity, with gut-related conditions woven through all three.

Underneath all of it sits one word: excess. Excess carbs, excess sugar, excess sitting, excess late nights. Anything in excess is poison. The other failure is skipping fundamentals in favour of complex biohacks — biohacking itself is fine, but only after the basics are in place.

Every lifestyle-related condition — diabetes, fatty liver, hormonal imbalance, period and thyroid disorders, PCOD/PCOS, infertility, heart-related issues, constipation, acidity — is preventable, and the majority are also reversible through lifestyle change.

Belly fat: a fixed deposit, not a jiggle to crunch away

There are two kinds of body fat. Subcutaneous fat sits just under the skin, is easier to lose and is not especially dangerous. Visceral fat — which is what generally accumulates at the belly — is dangerous: it gradually gathers around the organs and sets off fatty liver, pancreas-related problems and even heart issues. Rising belly fat, obesity and high body fat should be treated as a disease, because they damage everything from gut health to hormonal health to mental health. Belly fat also drives hormonal imbalance, since the gut is the centre of the body — and every disease begins in the gut.

The fixed-deposit analogy

Stubborn fat at the belly, arms, hips or thighs is the body's fixed deposit. You only break a fixed deposit when every other account is empty. The body works the same way: it draws energy first from the bloodstream, then from liver and muscle stores, and only when those warehouses are empty does it turn to stubborn fat. That takes time — which is why the answer is consistency and patience, not a mirror check after one week at the gym.

"Do these crunches for 15 days and your belly will go"

Myth. The fat on the abdomen is like a jelly — crunches just make the jelly wobble; movement is not the same as loss. Crunches strengthen the core muscles, which is their real job. The body is not biased towards one area: it loses fat overall, and a flatter belly arrives as a side effect. The same goes for arm exercises for arm fat or hip thrusts for hip fat — spot reduction does not work. Diet plays roughly an 80% role, and the diet-plus-weight-training combination is what actually shrinks the belly.

The three-step framework

  1. Fix dinner first. Dinner plays the most important role in belly fat — it is both the culprit and a potential best friend. Make it early: ideally three hours before sleep, or two if three is impossible (and that does not mean eating at 10 pm to sleep at 2 am — that gap invites a dangerous extra meal). Make it light: most people, busy all day, descend on dinner and eat the whole day's food in one sitting, which is a mistake. Then, 30–40 minutes after dinner, add a 15-minute walk at a comfortable medium pace — very effective for belly fat and for blood-sugar balance.
  2. Add weight training. It is perhaps ten times more powerful than cardio for losing belly fat. Cardio burns calories during the session; weight training keeps burning them for the next 24–48 hours. Muscle is the most metabolically active tissue and demands calories constantly — which is why muscular people can eat a lot without gaining. Learn the basics — squats, push-ups, planks, dumbbells, barbells — at the gym or at home; countless videos are available to follow.
  3. Sleep — absolutely underrated. Seven to eight hours, asleep by 10–11 pm if possible, can deliver better results than diet and exercise. Sleep is a detox process: while toxins release and hormones rebalance, overall fat is cut down.

Rebalancing the plate: the 25% rule and the PFF formula

The problem is not carbs; it is a plate that is half carbs — if not more — topped up with papad, extra pickle or chivda for taste. The ideal plate divides into quarters:

PortionWhat goes there
25%Salad — and start the meal with this fibre
25%Any seasonal vegetable
25%Protein — vegetarian sources such as paneer, tofu, sprouts and dal count fully
25%Carbs — at this proportion they are not a problem

The PFF formula: protein, fat, fibre

Check every meal for three things — protein, fat and fibre. This formula works for diabetes, PCOD, fatty liver and insulin resistance alike. Around 20 grams of protein per meal solves half the battle on its own, because the real national problem is not just obesity — it is being under-muscled.

Fat is not the enemy

Fat has been wrongly demonised. Desai keeps the classification simple: unhealthy fat versus healthy fat. Unhealthy fat means refined oil above all — hidden even in "healthy" sautéed salads and vegetables — plus processed, packaged foods like chips and packet snacks. Her advice for belly fat, PCOD, hormonal imbalance or gut issues is blunt: throw the refined oil out of the house and switch to cold-pressed oils.

Healthy fats are the omega-3 carriers — walnuts, chia seeds, flax seeds, almonds, mixed seeds — plus good-quality ghee and cold-pressed oils. Anyone with hormonal imbalance should add healthy fat to the diet first. Simple practices: a spoon of ghee or virgin coconut oil with warm water on waking (the "fat first" concept), five soaked almonds and two soaked walnuts in the morning, roasted seeds with an evening snack, a little ghee on the roti or khichdi. These small habits improve blood sugar and hormonal balance.

Starving is not a strategy. A calorie deficit is one thing; going hungry is another. Fasting can be good, but staying hungry backfires — food is the body's only source of nutrition, and cutting it off simply builds nutritional deficiency. Crash diets shed 10 kilos along with your hair, hormonal balance and mood.

Muscle: the most anti-ageing organ you own

If sleep is the best anti-ageing tool, muscle is the best anti-ageing organ. The difference between a 40-year-old who looks 60 and one who looks 20 is muscle — and muscle also keeps disease at a distance. With age, muscle naturally declines and fat rises even if you do nothing; without deliberate effort to preserve it, the problem compounds.

Women in particular need to hear this. Weight training does not make women bulky — and as one fit 40-year-old put it, a woman's real wealth is not gold or jewellery, it is muscle. Enter your 40s with muscle and the 40-to-50 decade is set up for success. Earlier generations built strength through daily life — hauling water, carrying a child on one hip and a pot on the other, cooking and cleaning by hand. Today the pot is gone, but the dumbbell exists; use it.

"I walk every day, so I'm fit"

Partly true. Walking helps initially: if you were sedentary, a noticeable difference — slightly looser clothes — appears when you start. Long term, it stops delivering; a month of the same walk changes little. Around 8,000 steps a day is a safe figure most people can hit, but walking alone, or gentle yoga that never reaches the advanced level where muscles are genuinely trained, is good — just not enough. Muscle-building work has to sit alongside it.

The six pillars every reversal rests on

Whatever the condition — disease, weight, any health issue — the fundamentals come down to six pillars. Master these before chasing anything complex.

  1. What you eat. The plate proportions and PFF formula above.
  2. How you eat. Eating is one part; absorbing, assimilating and digesting is the other. An orange eaten for vitamin C only counts for what is absorbed — gut health, eating speed (five rushed minutes versus twenty enjoyed ones) and eating while distracted by screens all shape that. Meals used to begin seated calmly; today they are raced through between meetings.
  3. Activity and exercise. Being active through the day — the average person now sits six to seven hours or more, which is wrong — plus exercise that pushes your limits: weight training at the gym or at home. Starting with yoga is also fine.
  4. Sleep. Everything starts from sleep, and it remains the most underrated pillar.
  5. Emotions. In Desai's estimate this plays an 80% role, with everything else making up the remaining 20%.
  6. Environment. A child raised on midnight chips, fridge ice-cream and three hours of nightly news grows into that environment; a child who watched a parent give two hours a morning to pranayama absorbs that instead. There is a saying that if three of your friends have weight gain or a health issue, the same issue is coming for you — mirror neurons mean you become what you watch.

How fast can the body change?

The reversal journey generally begins within three months, though severity, duration and body profile vary case to case. Biologically the body is built for renewal: the intestinal lining changes roughly every two months, the liver in about eight weeks, the skin on a cell turnover of about 28 days, and the whole body over about a year.

Why people fail — and the mindset that fixes it

Delete "fatafat" from the dictionary

Most weight-loss attempts fail because of one word: fast. Quick fixes — the "lose 10 kilos in a month" promise on the back of a rickshaw — do shed weight, but they also shed hair, unbalance hormones and wreck moods. The goal itself is usually wrong too: losing weight for a wedding or to fit into clothes, rather than for organ health, for playing with grandchildren in your 60s and 70s, or for touring the world without a knee replacement.

Failure strategy vs. success strategy

The failure strategy starts with "how" — how do I lose my belly? how do I reverse my diabetes? — before "what" and "why" are decided. Ask "how" first and the mind replies with a story from the past: you took a year's gym membership last year and quit in a month; nothing will come of this; go to sleep. The goal quietly dies.

The success strategy starts with what: design a SMART goal — specific, measurable, achievable, relevant and time-bound. "By 31 December 2025 I will lose 10 kg and reach 60 kg" is a SMART goal; "I want to be slim" is a wish, and wishes change daily. Put the goal where you will see it — bedroom wall, bathroom mirror, phone wallpaper. The brain's reticular activating system (RAS) means what you focus on, you attract: decide to buy a Mercedes and suddenly every road is full of them — not because more were bought, but because your focus moved. Decide on the goal and the nearby gym, and the salad next to the chips on the dining table, start becoming visible.

Build the habit before chasing the outcome

The goal should not even be weight loss at first — it should be 30 continuous days of working out and eating right. Weight loss is the outcome; the habit comes first. The familiar 21-day figure traces to a book by a plastic surgeon in the 1960s, who observed that patients took 21 days to accept their changed face as their own — the origin of the theory that a habit takes about 21 days to form. Either way: consistency over intensity, always. A daily push-up or surya namaskar beats an unused annual gym membership; slow, simple change is sustainable for some 95% of people, with only a small minority wired for massive action.

Your brain hates change — repetition wins

Interlace your fingers, then shift every finger one position over. It feels odd — like holding a stranger's hand — because the brain resists even an inch of change, and changing a whole body and mindset is far bigger than an inch. The fix is repetition: do the new clasp ten times and it starts feeling better. The same muscle memory that lets an amnesiac still drive a car is why a workout habit, once genuinely built, pulls you back by the third missed day.

Separate yourself from the disease

Deep healing begins with an identity shift. You are not diabetic; your body has diabetes — the way a phone or a cup is yours without being you. Seeing the condition in the third person lifts you above the problem, and solutions only appear from above the problem, never from inside it. What you focus on grows: endlessly discussing the illness feeds it. The first coaching session therefore delivers two things — hope that the condition is reversible, and the instruction to act as if you are already fit.

Find your why, then start with what's easy

The person who starts a diet every Monday and quits by Wednesday has not found a why that is emotionally attached — "my friend lost weight" will never hold. A why like being a role model for your child, succeeding at work, or travelling the world will. Then pick the easiest entry point among exercise, sleep and food and start there — walking if movement feels easy, adding a salad if food does, or simply sleeping on time. The brain's existing neural pathways make familiar routes effortless and new ones costly, so build the new path where the gradient is gentlest rather than changing everything at once. And for the toughest phases of life, gratitude grounds you — being grateful for the smallest things, from today's food to waking up this morning.

Quick answers to the most-asked questions

Does green tea reduce belly fat?

Myth. Green tea only reduces green tea. Unless you climb a mountain to pick the leaves yourself and walk back down, the belly stays.

Will apple cider vinegar every morning and before meals melt belly fat?

Myth. Not at all — and ACV does not suit everyone. In acidity it may feel temporarily agreeable, but for belly fat it definitely does not work.

Which is worse — crash diets or long-term inactivity?

Both are bad, but crash diets edge it: you eat five times a day and exercise once, so what you eat carries more weight than what you skip in the gym.

Sleep, sugar or stress — the biggest health killer?

Everything is connected — skip sleep and stress rises; add sugar on top and it compounds — but stress is the biggest problem overall; in the conversation it was noted that some 95% of doctor visits are because of stress. To manage stress: sleep properly and stop sugar. And do not stress about stress — worrying about it is more stressful than the stress itself.

Are mood swings and fatigue signs of a lifestyle disorder?

Yes, 100%. Humans are naturally wired to be happy; unhappiness is learned. When gut health goes wrong — a constipated gut gives a constipated laugh — the first signal of any illness lands on your mood.

One small habit everyone should start today?

Sleep on time — by 10 pm, 11 at the latest. Poor sleep instantly ruins your mood, and a ruined mood ruins everything else.

One daily habit that silently causes lifestyle disorders?

Looking in the mirror and hunting for what is wrong first.

How do I keep simple habits like drinking water alive during the office grind?

Use a free reminder app as an assistant in your phone, and keep a separate bottle — four family members drinking from one pot means nobody knows their intake. Aim for 2 to 2.5 litres a day; a one-litre bottle should empty at least twice.

One belief people need to unlearn about their body?

That your body is your enemy. The body is self-healing — every pain, craving and disease is trying to teach you a lesson. Listen to it. The body is the most magical machine on the planet: however much AI grows, it cannot be replaced.
