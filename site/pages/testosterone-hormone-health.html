Testosterone and Men's Hormone Health: An Endocrinologist's Guide

Endocrinologist Dr. Ambrish Mithal explains what low testosterone really looks like, how to test for it properly, how to support it through lifestyle, and where treatments such as TRT, steroids and shilajit fit — alongside the cortisol, adrenaline and conversion biology that shape male hormonal health.

Spotting low testosterone: the symptoms men overlook

Low testosterone is a global problem, not only an Indian one, according to Dr. Mithal. As people live longer and metabolic conditions such as diabetes and obesity become more common, testosterone problems rise too. He is cautious about calling it a "crisis" — what has genuinely changed, he says, is that the deficiency is now recognised far more often than before.

He divides the picture into two very different groups. Severe deficiency is usually obvious; the subtle, gradual form is the one that gets missed.

Severe deficiency is visible

In severe cases — whether they begin in younger or older age — the signs can be recognised on sight: absent facial hair, a different, more feminine skin texture, and a body structure that is not typically masculine, with little muscularity. Testis size is an important marker, and other organs may be smaller depending on when the deficiency began. Where onset is very early in life, development of the testes and penis may not complete; in later-onset severe cases there is some shrinkage of both. Dr. Mithal notes these classic cases are rarely missed — a family member, friend or general physician usually notices and refers the person on.

Subtle deficiency hides behind everyday tiredness

The harder group, he says, is the one with a slow, non-specific onset. Early symptoms are ordinary and easy to attribute to something else: mild fatigue, a little weakness, not performing in workouts as before, or tiring earlier in the day than one used to. Because these signs fit almost anything, testosterone rarely comes onto the radar. Research has shown that even when a man looks entirely normal, he can still have a testosterone deficiency that shows up only as marked fatigue, low energy and drive, reduced sexual drive, or fewer erections.

Dr. Mithal's rule of thumb: the classic, severe signs are not subtle and are seldom ignored. The skill is catching the quiet decline — if you have unexplained, persistent fatigue and reduced drive, testosterone is worth checking, but only after ruling out the many other causes (see the section on deficiencies below).

Facial hair is mostly genetics

Myth. A thin beard on its own is not a reliable sign of low testosterone. Dr. Mithal stresses that facial and body hair are strongly governed by genes and ethnicity — hair patterns differ markedly between, for example, western Punjab and the North-East. If your family and ethnic group naturally have less facial hair, that is the explanation. Only if your father and uncles have full beards and you alone have sparse growth is it worth asking a doctor.

Aging, weight and who is genuinely at risk

Testosterone does decline gradually with age, Dr. Mithal confirms, but the fall is slow. The real question is whether it has dropped to a level that actually causes symptoms — and that can only be answered by testing, not assumed from age alone. There is an overall downward trend, but no sharp, definite curve like the one seen with weight or diabetes.

Body fat and metabolic health matter considerably. People who are fit, exercise regularly and keep their weight in check should normally have healthy testosterone. By contrast, a large waistline and metabolic derangements — high cholesterol, diabetes or blood pressure, especially at a young age — raise the chance of low testosterone. A high-fat, high-carbohydrate diet increases fat proportion, worsens overall metabolism, and in turn affects testosterone.

Those who are overweight, obese or diabetic have more than double the chance of low testosterone compared with the normal population. Being thin, however, is not a guarantee of safety — multiple factors are always at play.

What testosterone actually controls — and why more isn't better

Clarification. Testosterone's primary sexual role is in desire, not directly in erections. Dr. Mithal explains that erectile dysfunction has a large psychological component — stress, relationships, work and poor sleep. Of ten men presenting with erectile dysfunction, perhaps two will turn out to have a testosterone deficiency. The first feature of deficiency is a loss of desire: a man who was intimate with his partner several times a week may find the thought no longer occurs to him. The erection process itself also depends on local hormones such as prostaglandins, blood flow, vascular health and nerves.

Beyond sexual health, addressing low testosterone in older men carries broader benefits, which Dr. Mithal describes as health benefits rather than merely sexual ones:

  • Muscle. Muscle strength declines naturally with age; where low testosterone is contributing, replacing it helps improve muscle.
  • Bone. If age-related bone weakening (osteoporosis) is being driven by testosterone deficiency, it can be slowed or substantially corrected.
  • Blood. Testosterone is important for blood cells — which is also why excess must be avoided (see below).
  • Energy and fatigue. Maintaining optimal levels reduces fatigue.
  • Mood. There is some connection to mood, though Dr. Mithal cautions the popular "testosterone makes CEOs aggressive" idea is more joke than settled science, and the effect is smaller than people assume.

Caution. Dr. Mithal is firmly against overuse. If testosterone is taken in excess, haemoglobin can rise too high, which is itself a problem. Every hormone has a normal band: too little is a problem, and too much is a problem. A healthy sex life supports wellbeing through relaxation and "happy hormones" but does not raise testosterone; conversely, a genuinely testosterone-deficient man cannot have a healthy sex life until the deficiency is addressed.

Testing testosterone the right way

Dr. Mithal describes clear "blood-test guidance" for testosterone, because it is not as simple as an ordinary blood test to order or to interpret.

When to test

  • Over 50: there is no doubt — get it tested, particularly if any symptom such as fatigue is present.
  • Over 40: a good time to check, especially for Indians, in whom many things tend to happen earlier.
  • Any age with clear symptoms: if there are clear-cut signs, testing at 25 — or even earlier — is appropriate.

How to test

  • Fasting, early morning. The sample should be taken fasting between roughly 8 and 9 am — Dr. Mithal advises 8 am — because hormones such as cortisol and testosterone decline rapidly through the day.
  • Use the right method. The measurement technique has improved greatly; older methods could mislead. He recommends the LC-MS/MS (tandem mass spectrometry) method for accuracy, and advises checking whether your lab uses it.
  • Cost and frequency. The test is not especially cheap — roughly ₹1,000 to ₹2,000 — and once a year is enough if all is well.

Reading the numbers

A healthy level is above 300 ng/dL. If a reading comes below 300, it should be confirmed with a second test before acting. There is effectively no meaningful "unhealthy high" from the body itself — very high testosterone (above around 1,000–1,100 ng/dL) usually means it is being taken externally, or, very rarely, a testosterone-producing tumour.

Dr. Mithal cautions against comparison anxiety: tissue responsiveness differs between individuals, so a man at 400 is not inferior to a friend at 800. If you are healthy, with no fatigue or other problems, there is no need to worry about your testosterone number.

Assessing testis size

On the frequently asked question of size, Dr. Mithal notes wide variation, but a volume above roughly 12–15 ml is fine. Judging deficiency from testis size alone is impractical — the marked, "raisin-like" shrinkage occurs mainly in childhood-onset deficiency where the organs never develop. Lifestyle-related low testosterone causes some shrinkage, but the degree is hard to judge even for doctors, so it is not a reliable self-test.

Raising testosterone naturally: training, sleep and protein

Asked for activities a man can do at home to support his own testosterone, Dr. Mithal's answer centres on movement, body composition, sleep and protein — not on any special food.

  • Move. Some aerobic activity together with strength training is very good — a practical target is around 40 minutes, three times a week (roughly two hours across the whole week).
  • Keep body fat down. Do not let body fat rise; lower body fat and better muscle mass are part of keeping health in order.
  • Sleep fully. Adequate sleep is essential.
  • Get enough protein. Ensure protein intake; extra protein beyond that is not especially necessary unless you train very vigorously. India's protein intake, he stresses, is a large problem.

Myth. No food has been shown to raise testosterone. Dr. Mithal says he does not believe any food directly stimulates testosterone, despite a huge market built on the idea. The direct correlation is with overall protein intake, not with any particular "power" food. Eating non-vegetarian food does not automatically raise testosterone.

On the folklore around organ meat and animal testicles: consuming offal (liver, kidney, heart) likely helps because of its vitamin B12, vitamin D and iron content rather than any testosterone effect. Eating animal testicles — an old practice traced to Roman and Greek times for "vitality" — is not something Dr. Mithal can confirm delivers usable testosterone; he is unsure it does.

The vegetarian protein challenge and everyday deficiencies

Dr. Mithal frames India's protein shortfall as a structural, historical problem. Grain-based diets — wheat and rice, hugely expanded by the Green Revolution — were intended for survival and to end starvation, providing calories but little protein. That legacy sits awkwardly with today's conversations about longevity, testosterone and muscle mass. He suggests a target of at least 1 gram of protein per kilogram of body weight, which very few people reach without conscious effort.

Vegetarian protein sources

SourceDr. Mithal's notes
DairyThe main source of protein in a vegetarian diet.
Beans and legumesGood, though not outstanding; a single bowl of dal supplies only about 2–3 g of protein. Soybean in particular is beneficial when taken separately.
NutsAlmonds and walnuts especially are good sources — but you can only eat a handful.

For those willing to include them, Dr. Mithal calls eggs the best practical protein source — a strong option for growing children in particular — unless excluded for religious or faith reasons, in which case dairy and the sources above must compensate. Vegans, he says, have little margin: protein must be planned separately, calcium taken via fortified soy milk (which many dislike the taste of), and a determined vegan should consult a nutritionist to keep intake balanced.

Reassurance. The worry that daily soy or tofu will raise a man's estrogen is, in Dr. Mithal's view, a very minor issue — he calls it absolutely safe, pointing to populations such as Japan that eat it routinely. The one endocrinology principle he adds is that nothing should be taken to excess; he cites a patient who ate only nuts all day and developed high phosphorus. Balance everything.

The deficiencies to watch in India

Beyond protein, Dr. Mithal names three common deficiencies worth attention:

  • Vitamin D. The most common deficiency in India. It comes from sunlight, not diet, and pollution scatters the UV rays before they reach the skin. He recommends a low daily dose (around 1,000–2,000 IU) over infrequent high-dose injections — the widely misused 6-lakh-unit injection being a prime example of the excess to avoid. Urban dwellers, the elderly, growing children and young adults all benefit, especially in winter. Excess vitamin D is real, which is why the dose matters.
  • Vitamin B12. Its sources are mainly non-vegetarian, so vegetarians have a high chance of being deficient. B12 is important for nerves — severe deficiency can cause neuropathy and affect the brain and blood. A low, multivitamin-level dose taken as a tablet is sufficient; the very high doses many people take are only for clinical deficiency.
  • Iron. Iron levels are frequently an issue in India.

Cut the refined carbohydrate

For metabolic health, Dr. Mithal urges reducing refined carbohydrate and increasing fibre. He calls maida (refined flour) a poison in any form and suggests replacing it with bran-mixed atta or millets such as jowar, bajra, ragi and kodo. Avoid sugar, maida and anything that comes out of a packet — chips, noodles, bread — and, as you age, avoid processed food, whose main appeal is convenience.

Treatment versus abuse: TRT, steroids and shilajit

What TRT is, and who it is for

Testosterone Replacement Therapy replaces what is genuinely low in the body. Dr. Mithal's criteria are specific: a testosterone level below 300 ng/dL, confirmed twice on an 8 am fasting sample measured by LC-MS/MS, together with symptoms of deficiency. Both the low level and the symptoms must be present — a low reading alone, with no complaints, is observed rather than treated. Roughly 15–20% of men over 50 may qualify on levels, though not all need treatment; it is very uncommon in the young. Importantly, once TRT is started it generally has to be continued for life. In India the treatment is not expensive — an injection roughly every two weeks can cost a couple of hundred rupees, though gels and patches cost more.

Not proven at normal levels. The growing trend — particularly abroad, and increasingly in metros such as South Delhi — of men starting TRT after 40 to chase youth is, Dr. Mithal says, weakly supported by science. Endocrine Society and European guidelines reserve it for the genuinely deficient. He would decline to give testosterone to a man with normal levels: adding a hormone that is already normal is an unknown, with potential harm that may only surface later, and no study shows benefit in that situation.

Steroids are a different, riskier story

Anabolic steroids used for bodybuilding are testosterone-like substances taken in random, uncalibrated doses — the same underlying science as TRT, but as excess and abuse. (Growth-hormone effects come from separate peptides, not these steroids.) Dr. Mithal has seen the consequences repeatedly: a healthy man who stopped after competing found "everything shrank," with the fallout reaching into his marriage. He lists the serious risks:

  • Cardiac. Sudden cardiac deaths in gym-goers; cardiologists report many more cardiac events among those using these drugs.
  • Kidney. Any excess taxes the kidneys.
  • Prostate. Overdosing raises prostate risk — the prostate (which controls urine flow) can enlarge, and even prostate cancer can occur, as a few misbehaving cells are driven to grow.

He advises anyone under 25 not even to consider injecting, and warns that recovering to a normal life after reaching that level is almost impossible. Anyone who has used steroids needs a qualified endocrinologist to help reset their systems (post-cycle therapy). His consistent message: before starting anything of this kind, see an endocrinologist for proper supervision — not a gym instructor.

Shilajit and unproven remedies

Unproven. Dr. Mithal does not believe in shilajit or similar preparations, describing them as extracts thought to be testosterone-like compounds with no established effect. His principle is to trust only what randomised controlled trials have proven — and he would gladly use shilajit if a proper trial demonstrated it worked. He finds it striking that people demand rigorous safety data of every new pharmaceutical, yet use traditional preparations with no published data on faith alone. Given that proper testosterone treatment is inexpensive, he sees no need to rely on unproven substitutes.

The wider hormone picture: cortisol, adrenaline and gynaecomastia

Cortisol is a life-saving hormone, not a villain

Contrary to its popular image, Dr. Mithal calls cortisol a life-saving hormone. Secreted by the adrenal glands (above the kidneys), it provides morning energy and the capacity to handle stress; cortisol-deficient patients deteriorate badly — weakness, loss of appetite and weight loss — and the deficiency is a recognised disease. As a drug, cortisol is used in every ICU. But, as with every hormone, the normal band matters: being over-stressed with chronically raised cortisol harms the body, and overusing it as medication carries significant side effects.

Nuance. High stress does not always equal a high measured cortisol level. Dr. Mithal separates the constant, severe stress of serious illness — where ICU patients reliably show high cortisol — from ordinary day-to-day work stress, which does not necessarily translate into a measurably higher cortisol level around the clock.

Adrenaline and the value of calm

Adrenaline (a catecholamine) comes from the adrenal gland too, but is an acute, fast response where cortisol is slower. A sudden threat — a gun on the street — sends adrenaline sharply up; studies show surgeons' adrenaline rising steeply during complex operations, then falling afterwards. Adrenaline raises heart rate and breathing. Practices that reduce variability, such as meditation, help lower adrenaline and keep the system calm; more broadly, reducing stress leads to greater hormonal stability. Dr. Mithal agrees that the "break" the brain gets during deep meditation could plausibly benefit the hormonal system.

How hormones really work

Endocrinology once held that hormones came only from a handful of ductless glands — thyroid, pituitary, adrenal, testis — releasing products into the bloodstream to act at a distant site. That understanding has changed: hormones also act locally, at the tissue level. Testosterone is made in the testes, but small amounts may be produced elsewhere and are not captured by a blood measurement — so a "normal" blood level does not tell the whole story. Locally, testosterone converts to dihydrotestosterone (which influences hair) and, in tissue, to estrogen — the latter capable of producing breast tissue in males.

Gynaecomastia: reassurance for teenage boys

Teenage "man boobs" are common and, in the vast majority, not a disease. As puberty begins, testosterone production rises — and estrogen rises alongside it, with some testosterone converting to estrogen while tissues are especially sensitive to these hormones. The result is usually nominal breast development that resolves on its own; in overweight boys, accumulated fat plus gland tissue can make it more pronounced. Actual gynaecomastia — marked by a painful nipple — is uncommon and may need surgery.

Things should normalise after puberty, by around 21–22. If significant development persists beyond that, a doctor's opinion is worth seeking — in about 90% of cases nothing abnormal is found, but a small percentage reveals a genuine hormonal problem. Where persistent tissue is simply bothersome in an otherwise healthy young man, Dr. Mithal's advice is first not to treat it as an illness, and, if it truly distresses the person, to have it removed surgically. He adds a practical caution: working only the chest to hide the area, without balancing back exercises, can pull the shoulders forward and create posture problems later.
