Reversing Heart Disease: The Case for Prevention Over Surgery

Heart disease is spreading through India faster than almost anywhere, and much of it starts silently in early adulthood. Cardiologist Dr. Bimal Chhajer — MBBS, MD in Cardiology, an AIIMS alumnus and founder of SAAOL Heart Care — explains how blockages build, the warning sign people wave away, why so much surgery is unnecessary, and how the disease can be halted and even reversed by attacking every cause at once.

At a glance

  • Coronary heart disease is described as an epidemic in India, now with the largest number of heart patients in the world. Roughly one crore people die in India each year, and about 35 lakh of those deaths are cardiac.
  • The root cause is the deposition of fat inside the coronary arteries — the three small tubes, three to four millimetres wide, that supply blood to the heart muscle. Left unaddressed, that root problem keeps growing.
  • Blockages usually begin in early adulthood, around 18 to 20 years, and grow slowly — often about 2% per year. Disease is typically labelled only once a blockage crosses 70 to 80%.
  • The warning sign most people ignore is chest pain, breathlessness or choking on exertion — especially after eating — that eases with rest. It signals an advanced blockage and warrants a scan.
  • Cholesterol and triglycerides are the two forms of fat that build the blockage; controlling them, plus blood pressure, sugar, smoking, obesity and stress, is central. There are about 13 to 14 interrelated causes.
  • A CT coronary angiogram is a non-invasive, widely available, low-cost way to measure blockages without admission or catheters — unlike invasive angiography.
  • Blockage reversal is described as proven, on the condition that every cause is controlled completely and simultaneously — moderate changes are said not to work.
  • In an emergency, India's biggest problem is not reaching a hospital within the critical one-hour window; the doctor recommends keeping a small emergency medicine packet ready.

How a blockage forms — and turns into a heart attack

The heart pumps blood to the whole body but takes three tubes for its own muscle. These are the coronary arteries, roughly three to four millimetres wide. When a layer of fat settles inside them, that is a blockage. Blockages usually start at the beginning of adulthood: while a child is still growing, dietary fat is used up in growth, but once height stops increasing — say at 18 — the extra fat is no longer needed and begins to deposit inside the arteries.

How fast it builds depends on how badly a person lives. Live a little wrong and disease may appear at 60; live worse and it arrives at 50, 40, 30 — and patients in their twenties are now being seen. The speed is set by how quickly fat is deposited, which in turn depends on how much fat is eaten, on a strong genetic tendency in Indians to make cholesterol and triglycerides internally, and on aggravating factors such as high blood pressure, high sugar, smoking, lack of exercise, obesity, high stress and a diet short on fruit and vegetables.

A blockage growing past 70 to 80% produces angina — chest pain, breathlessness or choking on walking. But a heart attack can arrive before that, and it means a sudden 100% blockage. The blockage itself may only be at 50 or 60%, but the membrane covering it stretches, thins and bursts; chemicals are released that make the blood clot, and the clot completes the block to 100%. This is the same protective clotting chemical that stops bleeding from a cut — useful everywhere else, but inside a coronary artery it becomes the damage.

Why location decides severity. A coronary artery narrows like a tree, thick at the base and thinner toward the tips. A blockage near the very end kills only a small area of muscle (a minor attack); one near the origin kills a large area (a major attack). The left main artery supplies about 70% of the heart before dividing; if it blocks completely and at once, the remaining 30% cannot sustain the person — this is the "collapsed on stage, dead in a minute" heart attack.

Silent heart attacks

Some attacks give no clear signal. A very small (minor) attack can pass unnoticed. A massive one can kill before the person understands what happened — "went to sleep and never woke up." Long-standing diabetes is a third route: microangiopathy damages the small nerves so sensation is lost, and a heart attack can occur without the patient feeling it. All three are called silent heart attacks. The uncertainty of when a membrane will burst depends on its strength: some people carry 90% blockages for years without an attack because the membrane is strong, while others rupture at 40 to 45% because theirs is weak. The one thing common to both is that the blockage was still growing.

Inflammation is increasingly thought to weaken the membrane and make it rupture sooner, which may help explain the rise in young heart attacks, especially after COVID — viral inflammation is considered a likely contributing factor. Anti-inflammatory treatment, including colchicine now being used in the United States, is being tried to reduce that risk. The exact reasons are not fully known, but the numbers have suddenly increased.

The warning sign most people ignore — and how to check

In roughly 50 to 60% of cases where the blockage has reached 70 to 80%, the body gives a sign: the person notices chest pain, breathlessness or a heaviness in the chest when walking, climbing stairs or moving about — particularly after a meal — which settles once they stop and rest. Instead of acting on it, people explain it away as gas or something they ate wrong. That symptom means the blockage has already advanced, and a heart attack may not be far off.

Take exertional chest symptoms seriously. Pain, breathlessness, choking or chest heaviness that comes on with walking or stair-climbing — often worse after eating — and eases with rest should prompt a CT coronary scan to see where the blockage is and what percentage it has reached. If it is more than 50%, the person is a candidate for a future heart attack.

CT coronary angiography versus the old invasive test

The traditional treadmill or stress test only detects trouble late: it checks whether the ECG stays normal at rest, on walking and on running. A normal result on running means only about 30% of the artery is open — in other words, blockages up to about 70% can pass a stress test. Many people reassured by years of "negative" stress tests could still have a 70% blockage.

Invasive angiography, by contrast, requires admission and threading a catheter half a metre to a metre inside the body, which itself can cause harm and carries a consent form for the risk. The non-invasive alternative is the CT coronary angiogram: the patient lies on a table, is given a dye injection that reaches the heart in about ten seconds, and a five-second X-ray shows how much blockage is in which tube and its location — in and out in around fifteen seconds. The machines, made by companies such as Philips, GE and Siemens, are highly accurate; over 300 are now installed across India, including in smaller clinics, and the cost has been brought down to roughly ₹7,000 — against far higher prices abroad.

Who should get scanned

The first group is anyone with a family history of heart disease, which usually means genetically high cholesterol and triglycerides (familial hyperlipidaemia). The second is anyone whose routine lipid profile or other risk markers are out of range — high cholesterol, triglycerides, sugar, blood pressure, obesity or smoking. If at least a couple of these — especially high cholesterol and triglycerides plus sugar and blood pressure — have been present for some years, a CT scan is justified even at 20 or 25. In the United States, a child with an affected parent is advised to have a first lipid profile at age two, and if high, is marked genetic early.

The real causes: cholesterol, triglycerides and the risk-factor list

The blockage is built from fat, and fat has two names in the blood: cholesterol and triglyceride. The more of these there are, the more naturally they deposit. Smoking makes the arterial wall sticky so cholesterol catches faster, raising the speed of blockage — a man due for a heart attack at 50 might have it at 45 if he smokes. Obesity contributes through more fat intake, insulin resistance and diabetes, and raised blood pressure, all of which feed the process. Diabetes causes microangiopathy, roughening the small vessels in the heart wall so cholesterol grips more quickly — like dust settling faster on a chipped corner than a smooth floor. Stress is singled out as especially important: the adrenaline released in acute stress can precipitate a heart attack immediately, and chronic stress indirectly worsens sugar, blood pressure, smoking, food habits and exercise.

The disease is described as having multiple causes — about 13 to 14 in all, a few major and several minor — that are all interrelated. The more of them a person carries, the faster the blockage grows, which is why young patients with four, five or six factors at once progress rapidly. Solving the disease means addressing every cause, not one or two.

Reading a lipid profile

The total report is called the lipid profile. Cholesterol comes only from animal food — meat, mutton, chicken, fish, egg yolk and, because it is an animal product, milk (its fat can be removed, so double-toned milk allows a little consumption; egg white is fine because the yolk holds the cholesterol). Plant foods contain no cholesterol at all.

On targets, the doctor's rule is that lower is better, because the harmful thing should be kept low:

  • He wants total cholesterol kept near 130, and below 130 for reversal.
  • Below about 150, disease will generally not progress.
  • Above 150, the disease is being pushed to grow.
  • Above 180, it grows fast.

A number of 200 is not treated as merely "borderline"; the aim is always the lowest end of the range. Triglycerides are oils — every kind of cooking oil is a triglyceride, and the differences between oils are described as minimal, so all are treated as equally best avoided by a heart patient. Because triglyceride is a plant product, oils are marketed as "100% cholesterol free," which the doctor calls a way to confuse buyers, since cholesterol was never in a plant oil to begin with.

LDL is not present in any food — it is formed in the body from cholesterol and triglyceride, so reducing those (especially cholesterol) lowers LDL automatically. HDL, the "good" cholesterol, is also not eaten; the body raises it through walking, reduced stress and eating green vegetables and fruit. The practical inputs a person controls are therefore cholesterol and triglyceride, both reducible through diet — including cutting oil and the nuts and seeds that oil is pressed from, such as almonds, cashews, walnuts, groundnut and flaxseed, which are themselves loaded with oil.

Why most heart surgeries are said to be unnecessary

The doctor's position is that a large share of angioplasties and bypasses being done in India are avoidable, driven by financial incentives. Private hospitals are described as setting targets for doctors, and a common consultation line — "you have an 80% block, you could die in two minutes, you cannot even reach home" — pressures frightened patients into immediate operations. He notes that angiography reports read 70, 80 or 90, never 71.5 or 83.9, because the percentages are approximate, not exact.

He walks through why the procedures do not address the root:

  • Angioplasty is essentially cleaning a blocked pipe by pushing the fat aside. The early balloon method failed because the blockage recoiled elastically once the balloon was removed.
  • Stents — small metal springs left to hold the artery open — were the next claim, but chemicals deposited inside them and blockages returned faster than in the original artery. Gold, radioactive and biodegradable versions followed, and are described as failing in turn, because the body treats a stent as a foreign body it does not tolerate.
  • Bypass surgery grafts a tube from the leg, arm or chest in place of the blocked one — plumbing, not a permanent cure. The new tube can itself block, the chest must be opened and the heart stopped while a machine runs the circulation, and complications (including effects on the brain) are described as many.

Crucially, none of these stop the underlying process of blockage formation, so they are framed as temporary arrangements. Cardiologists, he says, rarely discuss cholesterol or triglycerides with patients and move straight to operating.

The stent-pricing story. A Delhi lawyer filed a public interest litigation after an RTI revealed stents imported for around ₹10,000 were being sold for ₹1.5 lakh, often several per patient. In 2017 the government capped stent prices to around ₹30,000 and below. In some private hospitals the total cost stayed the same, because the fee for placing the stent was raised to match — illustrating the doctor's wider point that incentives, not need, drive much of the practice.

He contrasts this with Canada and England, where doctors are not similarly incentivised, far fewer surgeries are done, and patients with 80 to 90% blockages are commonly asked to wait, take medication and change their lifestyle. A saying he quotes from medical science: if the doctor is paid the same whether or not they operate, a large share of operations would never be required.

The human body is nature's finest machine — do not tamper with it. Nature has no replacement: a flower cut from the plant cannot be rejoined.

Reversing heart disease: lifestyle, a zero-oil diet and the 14 factors

Two goals are distinguished: prevention (stopping the blockage from growing) and reversal (shrinking what has already formed). Reversal is presented as proven. The American physician Dr. Dean Ornish is credited with first demonstrating on angiography that blockages reverse: in a study of 42 patients, each had a baseline angiogram, and half followed everything — a year of oil-free food, exercise, yoga and stress management — while the other half were given medication and a book. On re-angiography after a year, those who followed the programme had reversed their blockages, and the work was published in leading journals including The Lancet. The stated condition for success was that moderate changes do not work — everything must be done completely and together.

To do this precisely, the doctor uses a "safety circle" listing all 14 causes as spokes of a wheel — cholesterol, triglyceride, blood pressure, sugar, food, zero-oil cooking, yoga, walking, stress, smoking and the rest. Each is rated red, yellow or green: red means the blockage is growing, yellow means it has stopped, and green means it is reversing. The aim is to get all 14 into green. He compares it to a drum with 14 holes — to fill it with water, all 14 must be plugged; half measures will not hold.

Responsibility is split three ways. The family cooks zero-oil food, gives daily fruit and vegetables, removes cream from milk, and stops non-vegetarian food and nuts. The patient does daily walking, daily yoga, daily stress management, weight loss, and quits smoking and alcohol. The doctor supplies the medicines — for cholesterol, triglyceride, blood pressure, sugar, angina and blood thinning — since allopathic medication is retained, not abandoned. The organisation's name, SAAOL, stands for Science and Art of Living: it keeps everything from medical science except three things it considers aberrations — angiography, angioplasty and bypass surgery — and combines that with yoga, meditation, cooking classes and stress management.

Why oil is the target

Oil is a triglyceride and it deposits, so its external supply should stop. The body's small oil requirement is already met invisibly: roti, rice, dal, fruit and vegetables each carry a little fat, which is enough. Indian Council of Medical Research guidance is cited that fat should be about 10% of calories — for a 1,600-calorie intake that is 160 calories, about 18 grams of fat, which comes from ordinary food without added oil. Cooking oil, he argues, adds no taste of its own (spices, salt and chilli carry the flavour) and is one of the major causes of heart disease. Ghee is described as "milk oil" — an animal fat that additionally contains cholesterol — and the belief that it gives strength is tied to an era of hard physical labour that no longer applies.

Oil-free cooking is workable. Spices are dry-roasted and onions, tomatoes and masala are cooked using splashes of water instead of oil to build the same gravy, into which any vegetable, dal, poha or upma can be added. The doctor reports thousands of zero-oil recipes, best-selling recipe books, and a one-minute oil-free cooking video that went widely viral. His stated restrictions for a heart patient are simple: no oil, no non-vegetarian food, fat-free milk products and no nuts.

The "magic diet" for weight

For weight, foods are ranked by calories like currency notes. The lowest-calorie foods — salads and vegetables — are the "₹20" foods; most fruits are "₹100"; grains such as roti, rice, dalia, bread, upma and oats are high-calorie foods that stop weight coming down; fried foods are "₹500"; and oil is "₹1,000," because one gram of oil holds nine calories. A normal person needs about 1,600 calories a day; eat to fullness on the low-calorie salads and vegetables and the body draws on its own reserves, so weight falls without counting or hunger. The doctor reports losing 7 kilograms in a month himself, and thousands of viewer reports of larger losses. Zero-oil eating is recommended not only for heart patients but for everyone, since blockages begin in early adulthood and everyone likely has some.

Natural bypass: activating the heart's spare circulation

The body is described as keeping a spare set of tubes for the heart, just as it keeps a second kidney. The main coronary arteries send tiny branches — like grass roots — into the heart muscle, and thousands of these capillaries and collaterals are joined together, forming a service lane alongside the highway. If activated, a new channel can take over below a blocked tube. Athletes activate this naturally by running for long periods, forcing double blood through the vessels; the doctor calls the result a natural bypass, the same mechanism that kept ancestors' vessels open when survival meant running from animals every day.

To do this without hours of running, an EECP machine developed through decades of American research is used. Jackets are wrapped around the leg muscles — which hold about 70% of the body's muscle in the thighs, buttocks and calves. Guided by the ECG, the jackets loosen when the heart pumps (systole) so the leg muscles fill with blood, then squeeze during diastole to push that extra blood back — and because the heart itself only receives its own supply during diastole, the surge reaches the coronary vessels. Over an hour the machine delivers roughly 4,200 extra pulses into the tubes; across about 40 hours of treatment, the heart's second set of vessels is switched on. It is described as FDA-approved and insurance-covered in the United States, non-invasive, and not very expensive, and is offered at the organisation's clinics for serious cases as an additional natural bypass.

If a heart attack strikes: the emergency response

A heart attack begins when the membrane over a blockage bursts and a clot forms within about a minute, converting the blockage to 100%. Within ten seconds the affected muscle loses its supply, causing pain while sitting still, breathlessness, choking, sweating and sometimes vomiting — an acute, worsening version of the angina felt on exertion. The only thing that helps at that moment is dissolving the clot in time, and the window is roughly one hour: if blood does not reach the area within that hour, the muscle dies.

India's central problem, the doctor stresses, is that patients rarely reach a hospital inside that hour — symptoms are dismissed as gas, doctors are called late, and queues and delays consume the time. He contrasts this with the United States, where a 911 call tracks the address, dispatches the nearest ambulance, records an ECG en route and alerts the nearest hospital to ready the operating theatre, with no signature or money required, so the clot is dissolved with little damage to the heart.

Emergency medicine packet. Because reaching care quickly is so difficult in India, the doctor describes keeping three tablets ready to chew at the moment of a suspected heart attack, to thin the blood and buy time until hospital: clopidogrel 300 mg (two 150 mg tablets), aspirin (sold as Disprin) 325 mg, and atorvastatin 80 mg. He suggests keeping these made up as a packet, notes there is little downside if taken unnecessarily, and calls them a potential emergency lifesaver — but this is general information from the interview, not a personal prescription. Anyone at risk should confirm suitability, dosing and contraindications with their own doctor in advance.

His overriding message is that a heart attack is a slow-building process, not a two-day emergency: a person can usually take six to twelve months to control the causes and avoid bypass or angioplasty, provided the blockage is reversed rather than allowed to grow. Once reversal is achieved, the chance of a heart attack is described as ending. On common fears — young people collapsing during sport or at the gym — the point made is that exercise is not the culprit; a growing blockage will rupture whatever the person is doing that day, so the answer is to stop the blockage growing, not to stop moving. Quitting smoking is called entirely realistic, best done in one go and only when the person's own mind decides, not on someone else's insistence. And with more women now presenting after menopause removes hormonal protection, the same precautions apply to them.
