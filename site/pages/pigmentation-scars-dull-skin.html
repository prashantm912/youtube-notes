Pigmentation, Acne Scars, and Dull Skin: A Dermatologist's Guide

Senior dermatologist Dr. Anil Ganjoo explains what actually drives melasma, why acne can be controlled but never truly cured, how sunscreen has become a science of its own, and what genuinely improves acne scars, dull skin, and stretch marks.

Acne is controlled, not cured

Acne cannot be cured — it can only be controlled, and it runs its own course. It is a chronic problem that tends to relapse even after the skin has cleared, so patients are counselled that this is a long journey the doctor and patient travel together. The window in which a person is prone to acne stretches from the start of puberty — which now sets in early, around 10 or 11 for girls and 13 or 14 for boys — until roughly 21 to 23 years of age. A patient who first presents at 18 may need treatment, on and off, for another six or seven years.

Relief can come within a few weeks for some and take a few months for others. Once active acne clears, maintenance matters because relapse is common. The key to preventing relapse is the topical retinoid group — retinoic acid, adapalene and related molecules — which is the only class that treats microcomedones: invisible lesions sitting quietly in the skin that produce fresh acne the moment they get a chance. After active treatment, patients are kept on a topical retinoid for a long stretch — anywhere from six months to several years — which is what actually prevents the acne from returning.

Teenage acne versus adult acne

Teenage acne is a physiological change. As the body shifts hormonally from childhood through adolescence into adulthood, the male hormones stimulate the oil glands to produce more sebum; the excess oil picks up a secondary infection, inflammation follows, and acne forms. It is very common in the teens.

Adult acne is the acne that appears for the first time after about 25 years of age, and it affects more women than men. It usually signals hormonal change underneath — most commonly polycystic ovarian disease (PCOD/PCOS), which is increasingly common because of lifestyle factors: lack of exercise, poor food, and the metabolic problems that follow. A properly trained dermatologist keeps an eye out for this and treats the hormones at the same time as the acne itself.

Lifestyle is part of the prescription. Because a poor routine drives excess oil, patients — especially teenagers glued to junk food, screens, and indoor life — are encouraged to get outdoors, exercise, and eat a diet rich in greens and protein with less carbohydrate and fried food. Sleeping, eating, thinking, and exercising well all reduce the hormonal push behind acne.

Diet and supplements: what the evidence supports

"Milk and sugar make acne worse"

Fact. It has been proven beyond doubt that sugary, high-glycemic foods and milk have an insulinogenic effect: they induce insulin and insulin-like growth factor, which in turn stimulates the androgens (male hormones) that drive the sebaceous glands and produce acne. Patients are not told to stop entirely — moderation is enough. The practical advice is to avoid whole milk and extra sugary foods rather than cut them out completely. Fried food and gluten are far less clearly proven, so patients are told to avoid whatever they personally find aggravates their skin.

"Whey protein is harmless muscle fuel"

Myth. Whey — a cow's-milk-derived protein taken by many gym-goers — is insulinogenic. It stimulates insulin-like growth factor, which stimulates androgens, which stimulate sebaceous activity and induce acne. It also promotes androgenetic hair loss and darkening of the skin. Every acne, hair-loss, or hormonally driven patient is now asked whether they take whey. The recommended swap is plant or vegan protein, or better dietary protein, rather than these supplements.

"Soy aggravates acne"

Myth. Soy and soy milk are mildly estrogenic, and estrogen tends to help acne rather than worsen it — which is why acne and hair often improve during pregnancy, when estrogens are high. Estrogen-containing substances are not considered bad for acne.

High-dose vitamin B12

High doses of vitamin B12, particularly the injectable form, are known to aggravate acne — patients who never had breakouts can erupt after starting it. The exact mechanism is unclear, but the effect is real. History-taking includes asking about B12; where it is the cause, it is withdrawn, and the eruption can take a month or two to settle after supplementation stops.

Supplements that help

  • Zinc. It has a direct role: a strong anti-inflammatory action that suppresses the inflammation around hair follicles and sebaceous glands, plus a sebum-suppressive effect that lowers gland activity. Supplementation with zinc is a good idea.
  • Vitamin D. Deficiency has an indirect, hormonal influence — it carries a hyper-androgenic potential and PCOS worsens with it; PCOS patients are generally deficient. Supplementing them with vitamin D does help.
  • Probiotics. The microbiome is a balance of good and bad bacteria in the skin and gut. A disturbed gut microbiome can aggravate acne (and conditions such as psoriasis and vitiligo). Correcting it helps acne — but only when clinically needed, for example in people with disturbed gut function or a long history of antibiotics. Giving probiotics to everyone is going overboard.

Antibiotics, cleansers, and the skin barrier

Antibiotics: judicious use only

Antibiotics in acne have become controversial. Acne is now understood as an inflammatory disorder rather than an infective one, but Propionibacterium acnes drives a great deal of that inflammation, so reducing it is an important part of treatment. Even when an antibiotic is given, it is largely for its anti-inflammatory effect. The current view is to use fewer antibiotics to limit resistance, but they do help while significant inflammation is present. The concept of duration has changed: rather than the older three-to-six-month courses, the antibiotic should be withdrawn as soon as the inflammation is gone.

Never self-prescribe topical antibiotics. Many patients arrive already applying clindamycin gel bought over the counter. Used injudiciously or inadvertently, antibiotics cause resistance — a problem for the individual and for society at large. Antibiotics should never be used on one's own.

Choosing a cleanser

Acne-prone skin already has a barrier defect, and the medications used to treat acne — benzoyl peroxide, antibiotics, retinoids — dry it further. So these patients need a very mild cleanser: one without harsh surfactants, that does not foam heavily, and that does not carry too many active ingredients like the salicylic acid and glycolic acid products people pick up over the counter. The aim is a gentle cleanser that suits the skin and restores the barrier, rather than something irritating that peels the skin and worsens the acne.

Dry skin can still be acne-prone

Acne is not only an oily-skin problem. Some patients with dry skin have more severe acne than others with oily skin, because whether a person gets acne depends on individual sebaceous-gland activity and genetic predisposition. Many patients develop dry skin after treatment starts, and applying a heavy, oily moisturizer over dry skin can itself trigger a breakout. Such skin should be moisturized carefully with light, water-based or gel-based, non-comedogenic moisturizers — nothing oily.

Melasma: more than surface pigment

Melasma is genetically predisposed — people with a genetic tendency will develop it — and then aggravated by environmental factors. These include hormonal influences (pregnancy, puberty), certain drugs, UV radiation, pollution, infection or trauma, and even hair dye on the scalp. It commonly becomes prominent around 25 to 40 years of age.

It was once thought to be simple hyperpigmentation, but much more is now known to be happening beneath the surface. There is a vascular component, with upregulated blood vessels; a disturbed skin barrier; and a deranged dermo-epidermal junction — the layer between the epidermis and the dermis. Because that junction is disrupted, pigment "falls" into the dermis (pigment incontinence), which adds to the discoloration. This deeper understanding has changed how melasma is treated, and there are now therapies aimed at the vascular component and lasers in addition to older options.

How melasma is treated

Treatment follows an established algorithm, with topicals as the first line:

  1. Topicals. Combinations are usual to start — a modified Kligman formula of hydroquinone with a very mild steroid and retinoic acid. The original Kligman formula had many side effects; the modified version has fewer. Started judiciously, it gives a quick early response and can then be withdrawn, with the patient maintained on non-hydroquinone, non-retinoid, non-steroid topicals plus sunscreen. Tranexamic acid is a newer molecule that, added in, produces a faster and better response.
  2. Chemical peels. The second line if topicals do not deliver.
  3. Lasers. The last and least-used option, because lasers can cause rebound hyperpigmentation — very common in melasma. They must be used very carefully, though newer, more sophisticated lasers now treat melasma without triggering rebound.

Over-the-counter creams with kojic acid, alpha arbutin, niacinamide, or licorice extract can help control melasma but rarely treat it fully — even dermatologists find it difficult, combining tranexamic acid, topicals, and peels to bring a stubborn case under control and then maintain it. The bigger danger is when patients instead pick up over-the-counter steroids for a quick effect; prolonged steroid use leaves the facial skin already deranged and much harder to treat.

Glutathione

Glutathione is an antioxidant with a mild pigment-lightening effect, useful both for evening general skin tone and in melasma, especially combined with tranexamic acid and topicals. Oral glutathione is considered the best and safest form and does work, though not uniformly in every patient — some respond, some do not — and it is not a drastic treatment that suddenly changes colour. The intravenous (IV) route can sometimes be harmful, occasionally causing fatal reactions, so caution is needed; topical glutathione is not thought to work. Similarly, oral tranexamic acid works better than topical, but tranexamic acid delivered by mesotherapy or with a dermaroller does help.

Sunscreen is a science of its own

Sun protection has evolved into a complete science. It is no longer just about UVB and UVA: several other parts of the electromagnetic spectrum — visible light, blue light, and infrared rays — also aggravate melasma. Modern broad-spectrum sunscreens aim to cover the whole spectrum. Physical sunscreens, particularly iron-oxide formulations, cover most of it and are the safest choice, though good chemical filters can be combined with them to broaden coverage further.

A very high SPF is not automatically a better sunscreen. SPF 30 already gives about 95 percent protection against UVB; going to 50 adds roughly 2 percent, and pushing toward 100 adds only another percent or so, while making the product more expensive, thicker, and less cosmetically acceptable. So it is a mistake to chase SPF alone — the other factors matter more.

Which sunscreen, and how to use it

  • Pharma over cosmetic. Pharmaceutical sunscreens spell out their full protective profile and are more likely to have been properly tested; the guidance is to be a little skeptical of cosmetic-company sunscreens that do not.
  • Not "foreign is better". European or Korean sunscreens are not inherently superior. A well-formulated Indian pharma sunscreen that lists all the right factors — non-comedogenic, not too thick or heavy — is preferable to a foreign product chosen simply because it is foreign.
  • Ingredients to avoid. Oxybenzone and octinoxate are actively avoided: they are now proven to have harmful effects, some carcinogenic and some endocrine-disrupting. Newer sunscreens are already being formulated without them, and many now add anti-pollutant ingredients, since pollution aggravates acne and pigmentation and can itself be carcinogenic and endocrine-disrupting.
  • Reapplication. Reapplying every two to three hours is recommended but rarely practical; for most people two to three times a day is enough. Patients with severe or photo-aggravated melasma and those working outdoors are told to reapply more often. A higher SPF and some longer-lasting chemical formulations extend wear.
  • Comfort drives compliance. Simply writing a prescription is not enough — the patient has to actually use it. Lightweight, water-based, and lotion-based options exist, with different formulations for summer and winter and for north and south India, so the sunscreen can be matched to the patient's skin and climate.

Two common complaints have real explanations. If someone tans despite using sunscreen, a chemical sunscreen — which works by absorbing and then re-emitting the sun's energy — can itself induce a little pigmentation; a physical sunscreen is the better choice in that situation. And heat matters: infrared is a recognized aggravator of melasma, so patients are told to protect against heat as well as sun and to avoid steam, scrubbing, abrading the skin, and unnecessary home remedies.

Oral sunscreens

Oral sunscreens have a large role, and not only in melasma — oxidative stress is an important factor in the pathogenesis of several conditions. Because an effective topical sunscreen has to be applied as a thick layer very frequently — conditions few patients can meet — oral sunscreens are used to supplement the topical one, working synergistically against oxidative stress. For darker Indian skin, the natural pigment already protects against much of the sun's harmful effect, so indoor sunscreen is less essential; but people with melasma or light-aggravated conditions are told to apply it in the morning regardless, and to reapply and cover up when going out.

Dull skin needs a holistic fix

Dullness has many causes. It can come from the skin itself — too much sun, dry skin, or products that cause dullness — or from within: poor health, low mood, and poor sleep all show on the skin, because even mental state matters for skin looking good and glowing. So the approach is holistic; a single cream will not fix it.

The starting point is correcting the diet and adding plenty of fluids, juices, soups, and greens. Local treatment then follows, with topicals such as niacinamide, azelaic acid, vitamin C, and retinol to reduce dullness. Procedures add further: laser toning, chemical peels, and skin boosters that absorb water and hydrate the skin, improving texture and turgor. Sometimes even good sleep alone produces better-looking skin.

New skincare can dull the skin at first. Skin can look dull after starting a routine if the products are harsh and drying, or if actives like retinol cause peeling. This initial dullness settles as the skin adjusts.

Acne scars: prevent first, then treat

The most important time to target acne is while it is active: treating it aggressively then prevents scarring, because once a scar has formed it cannot be removed. A scar can be made to look better, but it cannot be erased completely — once it happens, it is there for life. When a young patient presents, the parents must be counselled that failing to treat the acne now can cause serious problems later.

Who scars, and the warning sign to watch

Scarring depends less on how severe the acne looks than on whether the person is a "scarrer". Someone with only mild, grade 1 or 2 acne can develop severe scars if a low-grade, smouldering, long-lasting inflammation is happening underneath. Conversely, a person with grade 3 or 4 acne and thick nodules may scar little if their inflammation does not persist. Persistent inflammation is the single most important predictor that scarring will happen.

The practical signal is persistent post-inflammatory redness or erythema. When it appears, it should be treated as early as possible with anti-inflammatory and vascular agents — vascular lasers, IPL, and Q-switched lasers with a vascular component, plus topicals such as tacrolimus, niacinamide, and azelaic acid — used extensively during that erythematous phase, or scars will follow.

Resurfacing is then done with fractional carbon-dioxide laser, fractional erbium-YAG, or microneedling radiofrequency. Treatments have to be combined and the scar morphology understood well. It is a long journey: multiple sessions spaced every one to three months, often over six months to two years. The scars do not fully finish, but they are made to look considerably better — and they do not simply go away on their own, which is why treating the acne itself is the way to avoid them.

Home microneedling

Home microneedling with a dermapen has some science behind it — it induces fibroblasts to produce collagen — but not to the degree of an in-clinic microneedle radiofrequency, where needles are placed at the right depth and electrical energy heats the fibroblasts. It will help to some extent, but it cannot match the clinic treatment.

Fresh wounds and keloids

A fresh wound should first be cleaned — with water initially if it came from the roadside or anything dirty — then treated with an antiseptic and an antibiotic. A deep cut or gash should be referred to a doctor immediately, as it may need suturing to avoid a permanent, bad scar. Once a wound has healed, a silicone-based gel (and newer molecules) helps reduce scar formation, especially in people prone to scarring or keloids.

Keloid scars are very difficult to treat, particularly presternal ones over the chest. Many modalities now exist: triamcinolone injections, fractional laser followed by triamcinolone, radiofrequency needling to contract the scar from within, botulinum toxin injections around the scar, and vascular lasers such as pulsed-dye laser or IPL to reduce redness. Even so, many patients remain unsatisfied and keloids recur, because the tendency to form them is present from birth; with age they sometimes flatten and sometimes grow larger.

Stretch marks

Stretch marks are a difficult condition because the deficiency runs deep — it is not only the skin but the subcutaneous tissue that is broken, which is very hard to repair. Resurfacing techniques help: carbon-dioxide laser, microneedle radiofrequency, PRP, and subcision radiofrequency done with needles. The combination of microneedle radiofrequency, subcision RF, and PRP is the most rewarding, but the marks improve rather than resolve completely — patients have to live with some of it.

Prevention and early care matter. During pregnancy and while marks are developing, emollients, olive oil, and anti-inflammatories help, and olive oil is a good way to help prevent them. But if the skin has stretched, there is every possibility that stretch marks will appear.

Laser hair removal is not just running a laser

Laser hair removal is not for everyone, and it depends heavily on the hair. The laser needs pigment to act, so very thin or lightly pigmented hair — hair not visible across a table, that the patient can easily bleach — is better left alone, though newer lasers can treat thin hair and Q-switched "bleaching" lasers are also available.

Crucially, facial hair often signals a problem underneath. It usually reflects hyperandrogenism — deranged androgen hormones or sensitive androgen receptors — which only a clinician can identify, sometimes with tests. Running a laser alone will not work; the patient must be treated holistically by a properly trained doctor who addresses the internal cause. Paradoxical hair growth (hypertrichosis) can result from inexperienced hands using the wrong fluence, parameters, or cooling, or from hormones stimulating growth from within.

Correct the hormones first. PCOD is the commonest cause of excess facial hair in women. If it is not corrected, the laser will not work — as with a fire, putting it out only on the surface lets it burn again. The hormonal imbalance must be treated before or alongside laser hair removal.

Timing and expectations matter too. The right time to start is about two years after menarche, when hormones have settled — age itself varies, since some girls start periods at 10 and others at 16. Roughly seven to eight sessions give an 80 to 90 percent reduction, spaced to the growth cycle: about every six weeks for the face at first, then every two to three months. After that, maintenance touch-ups are needed for several years — perhaps once in six months to a year if hormones are well controlled, but far sooner (every two to three months) if PCOD or another imbalance is driving growth from within.

Quick answers

Can sleeping on one side cause acne?

Yes — there is an entity called friction acne. Repeatedly rubbing the face against a handkerchief or hand, resting the face on the hand while studying, or pressing a mobile phone to one side can all cause it.

Collagen supplements

Although there is not much science involved, oral collagen is believed to help. The idea is that it breaks down into amino acids that reach the skin and are reconstituted as collagen; there is thought to be some science to it.

Growth factors, exosomes, and PDRN

  • Growth factors with microneedling. Definitely make a difference — mesotherapy with growth factors, and PRP (which is growth factors), both help, and synthetic growth-factor products on the market do too.
  • Exosomes. An immature science as yet, but promising; they are said to contain genetic material capable of regenerating dying cells, inducing regeneration of skin and hair.
  • PDRN. Works in a similar, regenerative way and seems promising.

A home remedy that has some basis

Washing the face with milk, which contains lactic acid, acts as a mild chemical peel — Cleopatra bathed in milk. Beyond that, avoid harsh products, keep the skin clean and let it breathe overnight, and support it with a healthy lifestyle, healthy food, and plenty of fluids and juices.

Three things to know about your skin

Healthy skin comes from health within. A healthy lifestyle is extremely important; a good amount of sleep is very important; and diet and exercise complete the picture. If these few things are built into daily life, the result is healthy, glowing, shiny skin.
