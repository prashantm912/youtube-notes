Choosing Hair Care Products: A Dermatologist's Guide to Shampoos, Conditioners, Oils and Styling

Dermatologist Dr. Manjot Marwah, in conversation with dermatologist Dr. Aanchal, untangles the product aisle — which sulfates and silicones matter, why hair botox beats keratin, how to read a label, how hot is too hot, and why most wavy-haired Indians are really curly-haired and would be healthier for accepting it.

At a glance

  • Hair has three layers, like an avocado: the cuticle (skin), the cortex (flesh, holding the main nutrients) and the medulla (seed, holding the bonds). Healthy hair depends on protecting the outer cuticle.
  • Hair ages just as skin does — thinning, frizz and texture change, not only greying. Care can delay it; repeated chemical and heat treatments accelerate it.
  • Of the salon smoothing treatments, keratin is the worst and hair botox (nanoplastia) is the least damaging. "Formaldehyde-free" on the label does not mean formaldehyde-free in practice.
  • Strong sulfates dry and frizz hair, but mild sulfates are necessary cleansers — not all sulfates are villains. Silicones are good ingredients that build up if not washed out.
  • Conditioner goes on the lower two-thirds of the hair, never the scalp. It does not cause hair fall — that is an appearance created by already-shed hairs slipping free in the shower.
  • Reverse conditioning — oiling or conditioning before shampoo — is one of the best habits for dry, frizzy, coloured or chemically treated hair.
  • Hair burns badly at 233°C. Keep irons at 120–150°C, keep dryers below 120°C and never apply high heat to wet hair.
  • The three most common hair care mistakes are all overdoing: overstyling, overwashing and overoiling. Balance is the whole game.

How hair is built — and why its texture changes with age

Hair has three layers. Picture an avocado: an outer coating, a middle flesh and a central seed. The outer coating of the hair is the cuticle; the middle layer, which carries the main nutrients, is the cortex; and the centre-most part, the medulla, holds all the bonds. As long as the avocado's skin is intact, the fruit stays fine — peel it, and it spoils. Hair works the same way: keeping hair healthy means protecting the cuticle, and it is the cuticle that most hair care products target.

The cuticle is not a smooth surface. It is made of many cells overlapping one another like scales, which is why combing and shampooing call for care. At birth those scales are perfectly smooth. With time — combing, sun exposure, simple ageing — they gradually chip and peel. That is hair ageing, and it is why hair turns frizzier with age and why so many people say their texture has changed. Protect the cuticle and the texture holds.

Hair ageing is far more than greying: people notice hair that was once dense turning thin, straight hair turning frizzy — changes at multiple levels. It is completely normal, just as skin ageing is, and a good hair care routine can only delay it, not prevent it. How fast it arrives depends on how the hair has been treated. Someone who has straightened heavily or had repeated keratin and cysteine treatments — increasingly before the age of 18 — has spent the hair's reserves early. When hormonal issues, PCOS, stress or an illness arrive around 30 or 35, that hair no longer has the strength to withstand them: it ages fast, frizzes fast and starts withering, with the cuticle continually flaking away. The result is hair that looks weak, with no shine, more frizz and more breakage. Many people notice exactly this after an illness such as typhoid — hair fall followed by a texture that never quite returns.

Keratin, cysteine or hair botox: the smoothing-treatment verdict

Ideally, none of these treatments should be done — all are chemicals and all damage hair. But since people will do them anyway, the honest question becomes which is the least damaging: a matter of controlled damage.

The least damaging option: hair botox

Hair botox — a brand name; the treatment is also called nanoplastia — is the most superficial of the three. It lasts only one to two months and will not make hair poker straight; some wave remains. What it does is tame frizz: it settles the parts of the cuticle that have become rough and raised, adding a slight lamination and glow effect that makes hair look shinier and more manageable. Tangling drops, so breakage drops with it. For someone with heavy breakage and split ends at the lengths who does not want to keep a curly routine or their natural texture, hair botox is the safer route.

The worst option: keratin

Myth. Keratin was marketed as a protein you add to your hair to strengthen it, and many people still believe it is actually good for hair. It is in fact the worst of the smoothing treatments. Hair looks shinier, so people assume it is getting better — but its strength is falling, and breakage and hair fall increase.

The formaldehyde loophole. When a salon treatment gives off fumes and a strong smell, that is formaldehyde being released. Labels say "formaldehyde-free", but this is not what it sounds like: up to 0.2% is permitted, which does not mean it cannot harm hair, and manufacturers add formaldehyde precursors — "formaldehyde-releasing agents" — that are not formaldehyde on paper but release it on contact with heat. If the fumes in the salon are irritating your eyes, the product will irritate your scalp too. These treatments should never touch the scalp. And if you ever see smoke coming off your hair in a salon, stop immediately — that is permanent damage. Smoke should never come from hair.

Shampoo, decoded: sulfates, clarifying washes and reading the label

Are sulfates the villain?

Partly true. Sulfates are in every soap and cleanser; their job is to clean, and they do it brilliantly. There are different types: the strong sulfates can leave hair very dry and frizzy and should not be used, but the milder ones are necessary — a shampoo has to actually clean the hair. Not all sulfates deserve the villain label; only the strong ones are the issue.

Silicones — and the curly-girl backlash

Myth. Silicones are not bad. They sit mainly in conditioners, not shampoos, where they are added generously because they create that smooth slip when water hits the product. Silicones got their bad name from the curly-hair community, for a specific reason: a non-water-soluble silicone does not rinse out, so it builds up on the hair. If you use a heavy conditioner, mask or leave-in products, you need a good cleansing shampoo to wash that build-up out — otherwise hair starts to feel stiff. This is why one shampoo for the whole week may not be enough: a heavy-product user can pair a regular wash with a clarifying wash at the end of the week to clear everything out. Two or three shampoos in rotation, varied by season and product load, is reasonable for someone taking hair seriously.

Who actually needs a clarifying shampoo

Clarifying shampoos contain somewhat harsh surfactants because their job is a proper deep clean. On the hair lengths, they belong only to heavy users of leave-in products. On the scalp, they suit people who gym hard or sweat a lot — especially in humid, coastal areas. People with dry hair should stay away from clarifying shampoos altogether unless they are using a lot of product.

Matching shampoo to coloured, grey and chemically treated hair

  • Colour-treated or bleached hair: choose a low-pH shampoo — around pH 4.5 — sold specifically for colour-treated hair. Bleached golden hair turns an odd orange with the wrong shampoo, pushing people into repeated re-colouring; the right pH keeps the colour true and makes it last.
  • Grey coverage: a simple hydrating shampoo is fine. A high-sulfate shampoo washes the grey-covering colour out.
  • Keratin, cysteine or hair-botox-treated hair: once chemicals or heat have touched hair, it is damaged, and compensating takes very hydrating products. Keep the shampoo mild in its cleansing, but make the conditioner, hair mask, serum and pre- and post-shampoo products strong and hydrating.
  • Oily scalp: a clarifying shampoo or one with somewhat stronger surfactants, paired with a lighter conditioner — which can even be skipped on very short hair.

Two-in-one shampoo-conditioners

Mostly skip. A combined product does neither job well — a product built for one purpose does that purpose better. If your hair is short, your scalp is not turning oily and it suits you, fine. Otherwise shampoo first to clean the scalp properly and wash out build-up, then apply a hydrating conditioner. One genuine exception exists at the ingredient level: products formulated around cationic surfactants that clean while hydrating (see the label guide below), or products explicitly formulated as two-in-ones.

You do not have to shampoo the full length every wash

For someone who uses no leave-in products and oils perhaps once a week, Dr. Marwah's advice is to shampoo the full length only once a week; on the second wash of the week, shampoo the scalp only and simply condition the lengths. That hair does not need much cleansing, and extra shampoo only dries it further. Judge by hair type whether the need of the day is cleansing or hydration, and combine products accordingly.

Baby shampoo for adult hair fall

Myth. Many adults with hair fall reach for baby shampoo assuming it is milder. The tear-free formula means its pH is quite basic, which will actually make adult hair frizzy — it is not that it cleans especially gently. Baby shampoo is designed for babies: thin hair, low oil production, skin-focused cleansing. On adults it will not clean the scalp properly and it will not control hair fall.

Should you change shampoo if you have hair fall?

Sometimes — but for a specific reason. If a shampoo is making hair very frizzy and badly tangled, to the point where combing is a struggle, those tangles themselves cause hair fall, and the shampoo should go. A new shampoo can feel fine for the first wash or two and show its true colours by the third — heavy tangling and clumping mean its charge is not suiting your hair. Shampoo and conditioner also have to work as a pair. Hair care products help most with breakage along the length; where hair is snapping mid-shaft, product choice genuinely matters, though nutrition matters too. What no shampoo does is fix hair fall at the root level.

How to read a shampoo label

The top five ingredients tell most of the story, because ingredients are listed by concentration — higher on the list means more of it. Comparing two real shampoo labels side by side:

What to check The better label showed The weaker label showed
First ingredient Aqua (the base — same in both) Aqua
Surfactant (second ingredient) Sodium cocoyl isethionate — a long molecular name means a large molecule that penetrates the hair less, so it is less damaging Sodium laureth sulfate (SLES) — the second-strongest sulfate class: strongly clarifying, not hydrating
Natural ingredients Aloe vera, coconut oil, coconut acid, tocopherol (vitamin E), turmeric root extract — listed high up, so genuinely present Absent from the top of the list
Silicone position Lower silicone reliance; more natural oil phase Dimethicone as the fourth ingredient — a strong silicone in high concentration

The weaker formula cleans and coats with silicone — the bare minimum — with no real hydration to expect. The better one carries emulsifiers and a balanced mix of cationic and anionic ingredients that help the shampoo work better. Two shortcuts for non-chemists: long molecular names tend to stay on the surface rather than penetrate, which is exactly what you want from a shampoo; and beware of natural ingredients listed at the very bottom — placed there for marketing, in amounts too small to matter. One word worth searching for on a label is trimonium: it marks a cationic surfactant, an excellent shampoo ingredient that cleans while hydrating.

Conditioners, masks and the case for reverse conditioning

Conditioner is for the length, not the scalp

How much conditioner you need depends on hair length. The scalp secretes oil that travels only so far down the strand — the most common hair type of all is an oily scalp with dry hair — so the longer the hair, the drier the ends and the more they need conditioner. Apply it to the lower two-thirds only. On the scalp it causes build-up, boils and folliculitis, and makes hair look flat and the scalp turn oily fast. People with very short hair can skip it entirely.

Conditioner protects hair from combing friction, towel-drying and daytime exposure to dust and pollution. The simplest way to put it: what sunscreen is to skin, conditioner is to hair — everyone should use it.

"Conditioner increases my hair fall"

Myth. Conditioners contain silicones, and silicones create slip. Hairs that have already shed but remain entangled in the surrounding hair slip free the moment you run your hands through conditioned hair in the shower. Those hairs were going to fall that day anyway; the conditioner just releases them all at once, creating an appearance of hair fall rather than actual hair fall. The conditioner takes the blame for hairs that were already gone.

Reverse conditioning: the pre-shampoo step

Reverse conditioning means conditioning or oiling before the shampoo, and India has practised it for centuries in the form of pre-wash oiling. It suits frizzy hair, dry hair, hair with lots of split ends, and coloured or chemically treated hair that needs extra protection. The science: shampoo washes away the hair's oils. Pre-coating the lengths doubles the oil layer, so the shampoo removes only the unnecessary excess while the necessary oils stay behind and shield the hair from the shampoo itself. One rule — keep it on the lengths, not the scalp. Match the product to your hair type: very dry hair can take butters; hair that is less dry can use a hair mask instead.

Conditioner vs. hair mask

They are not the same product in different packaging. The real difference is cations — ingredients that leave a charge on the hair. Keeping hair negatively charged prevents frizz, and masks carry more of these cationic ingredients than conditioners do. A hair mask is the right tool for frizzy, damaged, static-prone hair and split ends. Once a week is standard; if you wash it out thoroughly, twice a week is fine with no harm — Dr. Marwah uses one twice weekly herself because her hair is both dry and coloured.

Leave-in conditioner vs. rinse-off conditioner

The difference is silicone content: a leave-in should carry less silicone, a rinse-off conditioner more. Silicone behaves like cement or putty — it sticks to the hair and smooths it, but too much left in place makes hair feel stiff and changes its texture over continuous use. A silicone-heavy leave-in demands more frequent washes to clear it; it cannot simply be left to accumulate. Use products for their labelled purpose unless one is explicitly formulated as a two-in-one.

Pre-wash masks vs. post-wash masks

Formulations should differ. Hair care products carry an oil phase of roughly 3–15%, and a pre-wash mask should sit at the high end, rich in natural oils — coconut, argan, seed butters. That richness is exactly why it belongs before the shampoo: used after washing, a high-oil mask is hard to rinse out and can leave hair looking greasy, if not that day then by the second day. If you would rather not buy many products, reduce the quantity or simply keep a mild conditioner and skip the mask.

Oils, serums and heat protection

Which oils actually work — short chain vs. long chain

Going by the chemistry, oils split into short-chain and long-chain. Short-chain oils penetrate inside the hair — coconut oil is the classic, which is why it has been famous for hair for centuries. It does not suit everyone, though: for some people coconut oil leaves hair stiff, almost a silicone-like effect, so it is a matter of testing. Long-chain oils are larger molecules that cannot enter the hair; instead they coat and laminate the cuticle, protecting it — argan oil, rice bran oil, sunflower oil. These are good too, just different. The one useless oil for hair is mineral oil — absolutely no vote. Beyond that, no oil is bad; each does its own job.

Scalp oiling cautions. Dermatologists advise against heavy oiling of the scalp — it can worsen oily dandruff, and olive oil in particular encourages fungal overgrowth. If you do oil, one to two hours before the wash is enough; overnight oiling on the scalp raises acne. Keep pre-wash oil on the lengths.

Three ways to use oil

  1. Pre-wash: apply before shampoo. Overnight is fine if acne is not an issue and it stays off the scalp; otherwise even ten minutes before shampooing works well.
  2. During the wash: stir a little oil into your conditioner in the shower. This raises the conditioner's oil phase yourself, making it work better.
  3. Post-wash: a curly-hair technique — about 1 ml, no more, worked into damp hair to seal the cuticle.

You do not need all three; they are simply the three legitimate routes.

Hair serum: silicone in a bottle

Every serum on the market is silicone-based. Because silicone is that putty-like coater, applying serum to dry hair makes it clump and stick where it lands. On damp hair it dissolves with the water, spreads easily and coats every strand — so damp hair is the way. Serum suits straight hair, styled hair, colour-treated hair and heat-damaged hair. Dimethicone, the workhorse silicone, appears in nearly every product from conditioners to serums to sprays and gives hair a protective coating.

Heat protectant spray vs. serum

The structures are quite similar — both are built on dimethicone or other silicones — but the formulation differs meaningfully. Serum is thick, and putting an iron over such a dense product can effectively burn the hair more; it is also hard to spread evenly over the whole head without using far too much. A heat protectant spray delivers small droplets that distribute far more evenly without weighing hair down, making it easy to apply heat afterwards. Some sprays add targeted extras such as antioxidants to help manage damage, but the main protective work in both is the coating. If you use heat styling tools, a heat protection spray is definitely worth the investment.

Heat, drying and wet hair: the numbers that matter

Wet hair is far more liable to damage than dry hair. Hair kept wet for 15–20 minutes absorbs water, its breakability rises and kinks form in its structure. Damage to wet hair tends to run the full length of the strand, and taking wet hair straight to high temperature causes "bubble hair" — the strand literally bursts. The basic aim: do not leave hair wet for long.

Air dry or blow dry?

Hair burns very badly at 233°C. Working back from that: styling irons run from roughly 120°C to 230°C, and the safe operating band is 120–150°C — especially for thin hair, and regardless of how premium the iron is. A hair dryer should stay below 120°C, held about six inches away, moving the air in the same direction the cuticle lies; blowing against the cuticle raises frizz and flyaways. The goal of the dryer is simply to shorten drying time, not to cook the hair — holding it close and hot against wet hair, brush in hand, is where the trouble starts.

Because prolonged wetness increases breakability, diffusing hair partly dry beats leaving it air-drying for ages, especially in a cold place; in a hot climate where hair dries fast on its own, air drying is fine. Thin hair can air dry without problems, but thick hair that stays damp until evening — often tied up wet, which makes it worse — is better blow-dried. With a diffuser there is no need to wait for hair to be 60% or 80% dry; even dripping wet is workable, since curly styling products are being set anyway. What matters is how much heat is reaching the hair.

Ranking the hot tools

  • Crimpers are the worst — the plate is held at temperature against the hair for time, producing the most burns and damage.
  • Straightening irons on wet hair cause maximum damage; straightening must always be on dry hair.
  • Curling rods are still somewhat less damaging — but keep them at 120–150°C, particularly on thin hair.

Technique matters as much as temperature: work in small sections — thick sections overheat the outer hair while the heat never reaches the inner hair — use quick strokes, do not linger in one spot and do not repeat over the same area again and again. One practical tip: keep a damp cloth beside you and touch the tool to it before each new section. A tool sitting idle between sections heats up beyond its setting, and temperature control on many tools is imprecise; the damp cloth knocks the temperature back down before it touches hair.

Air wraps: worth the money?

The heavily marketed air-wrap stylers are low heat, not no heat — on paper the science is sound, since they work mostly with air. Dr. Aanchal, who bought one herself (not sponsored, and after much deliberation — it is expensive), draws the line clearly: if you already style your hair regularly with hot irons, an air wrap is definitely better and the damage is genuinely lower, and the glossy finish is lovely. But if you have never styled your hair and imagine the purchase will get you started, it will not — the learning curve is real, knowing which way to roll takes training, and it is slower than an iron, not as easy as claimed. It is a styling tool, not a curling iron: the curl hold is weaker. Try one before buying, use it with mousse, and buy only if you style on a regular basis or your profession demands styled hair daily. Otherwise it is a headache, not value for money.

Curly hair, frizz and the tools that tame them

If your hair is wavy, it is curly

Dr. Marwah is emphatic on one statement: in India, wavy hair is curly hair. Her estimate is that 70–80% of Indian women have curly hair but have not accepted it — they sit with buns, over-comb, and push toward straightening because the curl was never acknowledged. Given the choice, moving toward a curly routine instead would leave their hair healthier.

Structurally, curly hair differs from straight: its disulfide bonds attach differently and even the strand's shape is different — more conical. In its properties, curly hair is drier, and although it looks denser, studies show its density per square centimetre is lower than straight hair. Dryness is the big differentiating factor — which is also why texture drifts wavier and curlier with age: ageing hair is drier hair.

A curly routine, start to finish

  • Pre-wash: reverse conditioning, to hydrate the hair before cleansing.
  • Wash: a hydrating shampoo. If the scalp is oily, use a separate product for the scalp, but keep the shampoo for the lengths hydrating.
  • Post-wash layering: conditioner, then leave-in products in layers. The known methods go by their initials — cream and gel; cream, oil and gel; gel followed by oil — and every head suits a different layer. Dr. Marwah's own hair takes just cream plus oil: after washing she applies cream, then scrunches in a single millilitre of oil on wet hair — nothing like a heavy Indian oiling — which is enough to give her curls definition for the whole day.
  • Refresh daily: by next morning the frizz returns, so curly-haired women refresh almost every day — wetting the hair and reapplying styling product, without washing.

Scrunching with product in the hair does not worsen frizz. Scrunching dry, product-free hair does — you can literally hear the crunch, and that is a problem.

Frizz and flyaways: the toolkit

Most of frizz control is already covered above: reverse conditioning matched to your hair type, oils used during conditioning, and a leave-in product when frizz is severe — serum for straight hair, layered products for curly. Two additions complete the kit:

  • Silk or satin at night. For frizzy hair, a satin bonnet or a silk or satin pillowcase is close to essential — the usual cotton and linen pillowcases can be a little harsh on hair and even skin, and flyaway-prone hair wakes up visibly better off silk. Dr. Marwah never travels without her satin bonnet. Buy good quality, keep it clean and change it regularly.
  • The right brush. The highest-rated brush in the world for frizz — tried and confirmed to work — is the boar bristle brush. They are somewhat expensive and harder to find in India, but a good investment for frizzy hair.

Combs and brushes generally

Mostly hype. Wooden combs are widely promoted for static and frizz, but the dermatology textbooks note the catch: even a small chip in the wood withers hair and worsens split ends, a problem a good-quality plastic or fibre comb does not have. Nobody seems to benefit from wooden combs as much as the claims suggest. They are also porous and genuinely hard to keep clean — scalp flakes and oil lodge in the wood and it starts to smell. If you are choosing wood for environmental reasons, fair enough; and on very dry, frizzy hair it may still help — but on an oily scalp, skip it.

  • Detangling brushes: good ones have bristles arranged in a chequered pattern of columns, so hair detangles directly through them.
  • Blow-dry brushes: must have vents, so the air passes through and the brush's temperature does not climb.
  • Wide-tooth comb vs. paddle brush: a wide-tooth comb is always better in the shower — it is what curly-haired women detangle with there. A paddle brush detangles a little better and gives a different finish; between the two it is a matter of your styling preference — a wide-tooth comb leaves bigger, clumpier curls.
  • Whatever you use: check that the protective silicone tips on the bristles have not come off — a damaged brush multiplies damage — and do not over-comb.

Split ends, dandruff, children's hair and other frequent questions

Split ends cannot be repaired — only cut

A split end is hair ageing in miniature: the strand has literally split, from multiple causes, insufficient conditioning among them, and reversing it is next to impossible. International products now claim to reverse split ends, but the studies are not fully convincing — a silicone or a conditioner probably does the same thing temporarily. The honest protocol: focus harder on hair care and oils to prevent new ones, and if the split ends are few, simply cut them off. Afterwards, keep the hair conditioned and protected from friction and they will not return in force.

  • Split-end trimmer devices — the gadgets run across the hair in social media reels — did not convince either doctor on trying them. A split often sits too close to the shaft for the device's combing mechanism, which is simply not precise enough; it appears to snip mid-strand fragments rather than genuinely remove splits, and nothing about it is backed by science. And even where the jutting-out half is cut, the remaining strand is still damaged.
  • Burning split ends off — practised by some who do not cut their hair, including in communities where hair is traditionally uncut — is even more damaging. Heavy use of conditioning products is the better answer there.

Myth. Split ends do not stop hair from growing longer. Hair grows from the root, at the scalp, so splits at the tip make no difference to growth — it is simply that growth naturally slows after a period of time. Conditioning will improve the situation regardless.

Dandruff: think leave-on lotion, not shampoo

Dr. Aanchal generally does not prescribe anti-dandruff shampoos: they dry out the whole head of hair, and because they carry ingredients like salicylic acid and glycolic acid they also wash out hair colour and change hair quality — patients routinely find their hair frizzy and unmanageable on them. The preferred route is an anti-dandruff leave-on lotion applied to the scalp only, keeping the treatment off the lengths. There are also leave-on products that work by balancing the scalp microbiome — options to take through a consulting dermatologist. Where a dandruff-prone, product-heavy scalp needs cleansing, a clarifying shampoo can help somewhat.

Hair sunscreen: real, but niche

Hair sunscreens are valid — they will protect hair. The catch is cosmetic and practical: sunscreen filters are usually white, transparent ones are both rare and less effective, so a hair sunscreen spray shows white on the hair. Meanwhile the dimethicone and silicones already in your leave-in products carry some SPF value of their own — unquantified in studies, but present — so ordinary leave-ins are already protecting hair to a degree. The deciding logic is sun exposure: sportspeople training in the sun genuinely damage their hair (the naturally sun-bleached golden streak many athletes develop is the visible sign) and should use one; otherwise you do not need it.

Children's hair care

Most good shampoos are safe for children too — quality shampoos do not contain the bad sulfates, and children need no extra ingredients. If choosing feels confusing, products labelled child-friendly are fine, and curly-hair product lines are usually safe for children as well since they carry fewer harsh chemicals. The bigger issues are habits, not products:

  • Tight, high-traction school hairstyles are the real hazard. When hair is combed hard and tied very tight every morning, traction alopecia shows up frequently — short broken hairs at the margins and a visibly enlarging forehead. Avoid the tight pull.
  • Oiling for school: apply only over the surface of the hair, not the scalp — children sweat and often cannot wash frequently enough to clear scalp oil.

The three most common mistakes

Asked for the three biggest hair care mistakes in practice, Dr. Marwah's answer is one word applied three ways: overdoing. Overstyling, overwashing, overoiling. Care taken too far becomes damage; there needs to be a balance. And her three habits she wishes everyone would adopt: reverse conditioning, proper scalp cleansing, and managing wet hair with care.

Home remedies and hair colour: what holds up

Home remedies that actually work — because pH does

For skin, Dr. Marwah is no believer in home remedies; for hair, they genuinely work, because hair responds to pH. Certain acids, used diluted, control frizz and boost conditioning — in fact, anything with an acidic pH helps control frizz. The working list:

  • Apple cider vinegar rinse: one capful diluted in a mug of water, used as a rinse after cleansing. It gives a conditioning effect and a tamed look.
  • Aloe vera gel: mixed into water it works as a styling spray for a light gel effect between washes; many also use it directly as a conditioner. A perfectly fine trick.
  • Rice water: a very good conditioning effect with no silicones and no chemicals — a solid option for anyone wanting the natural route.
  • Beer (the controversial one): it does help reduce frizz — its pH is acidic — and some companies even market around it. To rinse with, not to drink.

Set your expectations correctly: home remedies for hair are usually harmless and give mild conditioning, but they will not change hair quality, thickness or texture. A methi (fenugreek) mask is the weak entry on the list — perhaps slight conditioning, but there is not much science behind the strength claims, and neither doctor could pin down what it is actually supposed to do. One genuine niche use: a patient allergic to hair colour successfully used ground methi seeds with ground coffee as a DIY hair colour.

Henna or hair dye?

Black henna is not the safe option. Black henna is effectively hair dye — it contains as much PPD as a dye, sometimes more — and can be just as harmful. Do not treat it as the natural alternative.

Ammonia-free hair colour is usable if you do not get allergic reactions. Know the warning signs of a reaction: itching, bumps, swelling, redness or darkening of the skin. Even PPD-free and ammonia-free colours prescribed by doctors can trigger reactions in sensitive patients — there is currently no dye on the market that can be handed over with full confidence that no one will react to it. For genuinely reaction-prone people, pure leaf henna is the only safe route, provided the orange tint is acceptable; mixing in indigo darkens it. The trade-offs of the gentler chemistry are real: PPD-free dyes give weaker colour, and ammonia-free colours fade sooner and need patience — the colour builds up over about three washes, looking somewhat orangish at first before settling.
