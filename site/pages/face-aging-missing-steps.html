Two Missing Steps in Face Aging: Daily Sunscreen and Cellular Energy

A PhD who formulates skincare for a living names the two anti-aging steps most people skip — wearing sunscreen every single day, and restoring the energy skin cells lose as they age.

At a glance

  • Studies have shown that using sunscreen once a day for a year can help reverse facial aging — and once daily protects against several hallmarks of aging, even though most people never reapply.
  • Any sunscreen counts; the formulator's recommendation is to stay above SPF 30.
  • She has not found peer-reviewed evidence that sunscreen causes cancer, and considers the chemical filters — regulated as over-the-counter drugs and reviewed by the FDA — to slow skin cancer and melanoma.
  • UVA and UVB are only about 5% of the solar spectrum; roughly 45–50% is visible light and the rest is infrared. Most North American sunscreens address only UVA and UVB.
  • The familiar A-B-C of skincare — vitamin A (retinoids), a broad-spectrum sunscreen, and vitamin C — is missing an E: energy, which declines in skin cells with age.
  • A newly patented sunflower sprout extract is claimed to lower inflammatory signals, raise protective proteins, and increase NAD+, with early visible changes in the first two weeks.

Missing step one: sunscreen, every single day

Asked for the number-one skincare step people miss in North America and around the world, the formulator's answer was unequivocal: sunscreen, one hundred percent. Her case rests on outcomes, not on any single product.

Do this daily. Many studies have shown that simply using sunscreen once a day for one year can help reverse aging of the face. Any sunscreen will do — her recommendation is to keep it above SPF 30. Patients and consumers rarely reapply, but even a single daily application protects against several hallmarks of aging.

The benefit is visible in the clinic. People who use sunscreen come in looking younger, are easier to treat, and respond better to injectable treatments; their skin bounces back faster, and in the clinicians' view they develop fewer skin cancers and melanomas. Because sun protection works slowly, the payoff is a gradual reversal of aging rather than an overnight change.

Does sunscreen cause cancer?

No evidence, in her view. The formulator has not seen published records or manuscripts in peer-reviewed journals showing that sunscreen causes cancer. The chemical sunscreen agents that worry people have been thoroughly reviewed by the FDA, which regulates them as over-the-counter drugs.

The fear centers on the chemicals in sunscreen being absorbed through the skin or into the blood and causing cancer there. She regards that as scare science and points anyone wanting a deeper dive to the interviewing physician, Dr. Rajani. Her own read of the data is the opposite of the rumor: sunscreen appears to slow skin cancer and melanoma. Once-a-day application is genuinely good protection against all elements of the sun.

Melanoma is described here as one of the deadlier cancers, and the internal question of long-term systemic effects is one that remains under ongoing study. The point stands that the published evidence has not shown sunscreen to cause cancer.

Sunlight is more than UV — and most sunscreens know it

The sun does not just emit UVB and UVA. Those rays are only a small slice of what reaches the skin, which is why protection aimed solely at them leaves gaps.

Most sunscreens sold in North America and the United States can only address UVA and UVB. The formulator's own line is built to target visible light and infrared light as well, which is where she sees additional, very gradual reversal of aging.

Missing step two: restoring the skin cell's energy

Pushed for a second overlooked step, the formulator moved past the standard advice. Physicians typically recommend an A, a B, and a C: vitamin A in the form of retinoids, a broad-spectrum sunscreen that protects against UVA and UVB, and vitamin C. Her addition is an E — for energy.

Energy in the skin cells declines as we age. Many patients seek NAD+ injections precisely because NAD+ supports the mitochondria, helping build muscle and supply the extra energy the body needs. The same logic is now being applied to the skin: her DEJ Daily Boosting Serum is designed to regenerate skin cells by boosting their energy levels, which she frames as rejuvenation of the skin.

The mechanism echoes a tool already familiar in skincare — red light. Photobiomodulation drives ATP production through cytochrome c oxidase, and pairing it with skincare is a way of maximizing results. Delivering that energy boost from within a topical formulation, rather than only from an LED mask, is what she describes as being among the first to do.

A new patent: sunflower sprout extract and the NAD+ pathway

The energy idea led to a patent granted in January for a sunflower sprout extract for skincare. Gene expression assays were used to demonstrate its effects.

  • It downregulates inflammatory mediators — the cytokines that cause inflammation in the skin.
  • It upregulates more of the skin's protective proteins.
  • It increases NAD+, feeding the NAD+ pathway that many people currently chase through injections.

Applied to the face, the extract is claimed to raise NAD+ and improve ATP. The visible sequence she describes: within the first two weeks, improvement in skin radiance, texture and touch; then firmness and lift, which take longer because skin cells need more time to produce elastin and collagen.
