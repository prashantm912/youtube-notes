The Science of Healthy Weight Gain: Building Muscle, Not Just Mass

Dietician Suman Agarwal lays out a scientific system for the chronically underweight — the calorie mathematics, the training that makes food count, the everyday habits that silently block weight gain, and how to use protein and creatine without harming yourself.

At a glance

  • Healthy weight gain is mostly muscle gain: the goal is to raise fat, muscle and body water together, not just the number on the scale.
  • Around 70% of underweight youngsters are anxious, and the mind–stomach connection suppresses their appetite. Managing anxiety is part of the treatment.
  • The base calculation: 31 calories per kilogram of ideal body weight, plus 500 calories for gain. Protein starts at 1 gram per kilogram of ideal body weight — more protein actually causes weight loss.
  • A weight-gain diet is high-carbohydrate and medium-protein. Carbohydrate quantity is left open; protein quantity is what gets capped.
  • Two silent blockers: undiagnosed lactose intolerance (swap milk for curd or paneer) and excessive walking, which burns off the surplus.
  • Eating alone will not add healthy weight. Without weight training, the surplus becomes fat gain, not weight gain.
  • Never train on an empty stomach when gaining: eat carbs within 30 minutes before a workout and a carb–protein combination within 30 minutes after.
  • Protein powders are supplements, not steroids — cap them at one to two scoops a day, buy verified quality, and get the rest of your protein from food.

Start with the mind: why anxious people stay underweight

Among the teenagers and young adults who seek help gaining weight, roughly 70% share one trait: an anxious mental makeup. The mind and the stomach are directly connected, so anxiety makes them eat less. It also speeds up the breath and the heartbeat, which raises metabolism — some clients are so lean that body-composition machines cannot trace any fat at all. Very lean people also tend to be fidgety, constantly pacing and tapping; this non-exercise activity thermogenesis quietly burns calories all day, and the only real fix is bringing the anxiety level down.

Modern connectivity feeds the problem. A phone that buzzes every minute — social media, email, messages — never lets the mind settle, and this rising anxiety is seen in both underweight and overweight people, though more often in the very thin. The prescription is behavioural: exercise, sleep, pranayama or meditation, time with family and friends without the phone, and no phone at the table while eating. Approaching problems calmly, with the attitude that everything can be handled, matters too.

Exercise is singled out as the biggest lever against anxiety — and walking does not count. It means strength training, running, floor exercise, swimming, Pilates or yoga: activity that activates muscle and raises the heart rate. The payoff shows up at night. During sleep the resting heart rate should stay under 65, and ideally the minimum should dip to around 45–48 within the first four hours of sleep — that is the sign of very good recovery, and it simply does not happen on walking alone. A hard training session even two days earlier still shows up as a lower minimum heart rate at night.

  • Sleep matters most: a poor night leaves the mind irritated, the heart rate elevated and the metabolism running high the next day.
  • Assuming sleep from 11 pm to 7 am, the minimum heart rate should arrive before 3 am, with the overnight average between 60 and 65.
  • Stimulants keep the heart from resting: avoid tea, green tea, coffee and alcohol after five or six in the evening.
  • During waking hours, the heart needs at least two to three hours in a relaxed or restorative zone — not the stress zone.

Tip: if you can afford a tracking ring or band, tracking your own heart rate and sleep is genuinely eye-opening — even scrolling reels measurably elevates the heart rate. For students who cannot, the habits above still apply without any device.

The mathematics of healthy weight gain

Everything starts from the basal metabolic rate — the calories your body burns doing nothing. For women this is generally between 1,200 and 1,400 calories; for men, between 1,800 and 2,500. It depends on how much muscle you carry: more muscle means more energy burned and more food needed to maintain it. This is also why the very lean feel the cold so much — with little muscle and little fat, the body has almost no insulation.

The target itself comes from ideal body weight, which is a genuinely complicated calculation. It combines wrist measurement, age and gender to determine frame size (small, medium or large, each with sub-categories), and from the frame comes a weight range about 5 kg wide. Suman Agarwal uses her own software for this, compiled from established references, with an app planned; until then, a rough estimate of your ideal weight is a workable starting point.

From there the arithmetic is fixed:

  1. Calories: 31 calories per kilogram of ideal body weight for an adult. Someone whose ideal weight is 60 kg needs 60 × 31 = 1,860 calories.
  2. Surplus: add 500 calories for weight gain — 1,860 + 500 = 2,360 calories a day.
  3. Protein: start at 1 gram per kilogram of ideal body weight (60 g in this example) and increase gradually.
  4. Fat: around 25–30% of total calories, cut to about 20% if cholesterol is high.

The macro logic is the mirror image of weight loss. Weight loss runs on high protein and low carbohydrate; weight gain needs high carbohydrate and medium protein. Giving a weight-gain client extra protein backfires — it causes weight loss rather than gain, along with bone loss and hair loss. Protein stays at what the person needs, no more.

Success is measured on every marker, not just the scale. A weight-gain client should see weight, body-fat percentage, fat mass, muscle (fat-free) mass and body water all rise together — someone carrying only 2–4 kg of fat mass needs to be taken to at least 10–12 kg. Girls need a minimum of 20% body fat and boys a minimum of 12%. Muscle gain drags water with it too: every 100 grams of new muscle adds roughly 100–200 grams of cellular water.

Before you start: get a blood report. Some thin people arrive with high cholesterol (which forces fat down to 20% of calories) or even diabetes, and the diet has to be built around those numbers.

Why weight gain stalls — and the training that fixes it

The milk problem

At least 80% of people who come in for weight gain drink milk faithfully, often two glasses a day since childhood — and it is exactly what is holding them back. While some 98% of people digest milk fine, the 1–2% who cannot break down lactose are heavily over-represented among the underweight. Undigested milk means the protein passes straight through unused, and the resulting indigestion kills whatever appetite they had. The fix is simple: swap milk for curd, paneer, chaas or lassi — fermentation partially breaks down the lactose, making them far easier to digest. If you drink milk daily and stay thin, this is the first thing to change.

The walking problem

Very thin, anxious people tend to walk a great deal — and walking causes weight loss. A weight-loss client is told to walk for hours; a weight-gain client is told to barely walk at all: a maximum of 20–30 minutes a day, or none. Replace it with swimming, weight training, Pilates or yoga.

The appetite problem

"I have to force food into myself" is the standard complaint, and the cause is twofold: no real training, and milk that will not digest. Weight training or swimming grows muscle mass, growing muscle demands food, and appetite opens on its own. Fix the milk and add the training, and the hunger follows. For those who refuse the gym, alternatives count too — swimming, sport, Pilates, floor exercise, athletics — but some form of muscle training is non-negotiable. Eating alone, without training, produces fat gain rather than weight gain.

The training week

The weekly structure for weight gain is deliberately modest:

  • Three training days, with a gap between them: 15 minutes of warm-up cardio, 30 minutes of strength training, 15 minutes of stretching.
  • One sport day, if you play one.
  • The remaining days are full rest — at most 15–30 minutes of walking.
  • No long cardio sessions; the only low-impact cardio is that 15-minute warm-up.

The 30-minute windows

Weight-gain training is never done on an empty stomach. Within 30 minutes before the workout, eat carbohydrates — the strength has to come from somewhere. In the evening, fruit works; in the morning (when fruit or juice on an empty stomach is not advised) have toast, dates or a few nuts. A protein smoothie beforehand is equally fine for those who tolerate it. Training in the evening is preferable to early morning for this group.

The logic: carbs eaten before the session convert immediately to blood glucose, so the body fuels the workout from glucose instead of raiding its scarce fat and muscle. Then, within 30 minutes of finishing, take a carb–protein combination — a smoothie of fruit, a little fat (ghee, butter or flaxseed powder, or the fat already in a curd smoothie) and protein powder — so the session's stimulus goes straight into muscle gain.

This is the exact opposite of the weight-loss protocol, where training is done nearly empty and nothing but water is taken for the first 30 minutes afterwards, precisely so the body attacks its fat stores. For weight-loss clients who need a protein shake, it is given for muscle recovery just before sleep, or an hour after dinner like a dessert, because the body repairs itself most during the first four hours of sleep, while the next four hours of REM sleep restore the brain and file the day's memories.

Carbohydrates: fuel the gain without going skinny-fat

A carbohydrate has one job — energy — at 4 calories per gram. Modern life has automated away most of the movement that once used that energy, which is why excess carbs now turn to fat, and in anyone with insulin resistance that fat settles on the belly. The result is India's familiar skinny-fat pattern: a protruding stomach on an otherwise thin frame, hard fat that looks fit from a distance. Two habits produce it — no strength training, plus a heavy intake of sweets, chocolates, chips and fried namkeen.

For genuine weight gain, carbohydrate is the one macro that is not rationed. Clients are told the exact quantity of protein to eat, but for upma, poha, rice or bread the instruction is: eat to your appetite. The quantity to control is protein, not carbohydrate — with the crucial condition that the high-carb diet is paired with weight training, or it becomes fat gain.

What not to eat — for gain or loss alike

Junk calories build bellies, not bodies. The standard rule given to every client, gaining or losing: avoid desserts, ice creams, aerated drinks, chocolates, fried food and papad — Sundays included. Take a day off work, not off your diet.

  • Western desserts (cakes, pastries, cupcakes, donuts) are sugar, hydrogenated fats and refined flour — empty calories with no nutrition.
  • Aerated drinks are a genuine killer. Nimbu pani is a far better swap for a sweet drink, and plain water is best of all.
  • Ice cream is often not really milk but hydrogenated fats and chemicals; even honest versions are concentrated fat and calories.
  • Papad is heavy in salt, fat and soda, and so moreish that it is always over-eaten on top of a full meal.
  • Commercial fried snacks (bhujia and packaged namkeen) are fried in oil reused until it turns carcinogenic. If you must buy fried food, buy it where you can see it being freshly fried. Healthier versions of these snacks are now available online for the chatpata craving.
  • Sweet cravings should go to natural sugars instead — fruit, dates, fresh juices — which bring vitamins along with the fructose.

Understanding the glycemic index

The glycemic index measures how quickly a food releases glucose into the blood. Fast release means high GI; slow release means low GI. Starchy, fibre-poor foods sit at the top: polished white rice, potatoes, pasta, anything made of maida (pizza, breads), and sugar — with honey and jaggery scoring just as high, despite their healthy reputation among diabetics. Among vegetables, potato releases sugar immediately while yam is slower, which is why athletes carb-loading the night before an event choose yam. Wheat and other whole grains carry fibre, so their glycemic load is lower; kodo millet is particularly low, red and brown rice sit below white.

More useful than any single food's number is the glycemic index of the meal. Combining a high-GI food with protein and fibre pulls the whole meal's load down: white rice becomes acceptable when eaten as palak paneer with rice, alongside a vegetable dish that is more gobi than aloo. The same trick tames dessert — if you must have it, take it immediately after a meal built on protein and low-GI carbs, and the sugar's uptake into the blood slows considerably. Fruit follows the same rule.

Apple and pear bring soluble fibre, which is good for both the heart and diabetes. For anyone with digestive issues — common among the underweight — a grain trial helps find what suits: a week on wheat, a week on rice, a week on a millet, watching for less bloating and better energy. Bloating itself is worth learning to recognise: the uncomfortable, over-full, heavy feeling where the stomach distends and breathing feels laboured. It often traces to protein the gut cannot yet digest, and the route out is building up easily digestible proteins first — curd, paneer, moong dal, egg whites, chaas, coconut water — so digestive strength and the gut microbiome slowly improve until harder proteins (wheat, millets, chole, rajma) become manageable.

Protein: food first, powder second

Protein is needed for hair, blood, skin, hormones, digestive juices, nails and the antibodies that fight disease. Muscle is also the single most important marker of aging — less muscle, faster aging — and muscle loss begins after 40 (around 45 in men, earlier in women and faster still after menopause), which is why protein needs rise with age, in pregnancy (an extra 15 g a day) and lactation (an extra 25 g), and during recovery from injury, fracture, long illness or cancer, where doctors themselves advise raising protein.

Count all of it — food carries hidden protein

People assume their protein equals their protein powder plus their dairy. In reality protein hides throughout the diet, and a proper calculation counts everything:

  • One 7-inch roti from 25 g of atta: about 2.5 g protein.
  • A serving of rice: about 1.5 g.
  • A slice of bread: roughly 1–2 g, more for larger slices.
  • 100 g of vegetables (cucumber, tomato, spinach, methi, carrot, beans, mushroom): about 1 g.
  • A handful of nuts (17–20 g): about 4 g.
  • A 200 ml katori of medium-consistency dal: 9 g.
  • A cup of curd: 6 g.

So a 60-gram protein target is not "two scoops of powder and done" — much of it is already in the food. Real meals should carry the load: chicken, paneer, dal, curd. That is why powder is called a supplement — it assists the diet, it does not replace it. The maximum ever prescribed is two scoops a day; one is preferred, and for weight-gain clients specifically, very little powder is used at all if food (especially for non-vegetarians) can supply the protein.

The safe range — and what excess protein does

Protein is kept between 0.8 and 1.2 grams per kilogram of ideal body weight for people with healthy kidneys. Only heavily muscled actors, whose muscle mass is confirmed on a body-composition machine, are taken to 1.4–1.5 g/kg — and 1.5 is never crossed. Excess protein is not harmless:

  • Avascular necrosis (AVN) — bone death from blocked blood flow, which is irreversible. One client had taken five scoops of whey a day (120–150 g) during his growth years; boys grow in height until 21–22 (girls until 16–17), and radiologists and doctors link excessive protein in the growth period to AVN.
  • Hair loss from heavy excess.
  • Kidney markers drifting — creatinine begins to rise.
  • Fat gain — protein carries 4 calories per gram like any carb, and the excess converts to fat and settles in the liver.

Get it calculated. You would hire an architect to build a house, and your body is the most important house you live in. See a nutritionist or dietician at least once or a few times to have your exact protein requirement worked out rather than structuring it yourself.

Choosing a powder

Whey isolate is the easiest protein to digest because the lactose has been removed — the natural choice for the many weight-gainers who are lactose intolerant. Plant proteins are perfectly acceptable if they suit your body: commercial soy protein is fine (manufacturers remove the trypsin inhibitor that makes raw soybean hard to digest — never grind your own soybeans to save money), and pea or vegan isolates work too. Some people get skin trouble even from whey isolate and do better on plant protein; gut sensitivity varies.

Quality is the real risk. Cheap powders may carry poor ingredients — seed oils, stabilisers, sweeteners — can be adulterated, and cheap whey or plant protein is a known trigger for acne: save on the powder and you will spend at the skin doctor. Practical filters:

  • Divide the tub's price by its number of scoops and judge the per-scoop cost — genuinely good protein is not cheap.
  • Read the label, check reviews online, and ask your nutritionist.
  • Buy from verified, reputable websites — never from local dealers.

Protein powder is not a steroid

Parents fear protein shakes because they equate them with steroids. They should know: protein supplements contain no steroids, and 20–25 g a day has no side effects. Steroids are a different substance entirely — lab-made versions of testosterone or human growth hormone that temporarily multiply protein absorption far beyond natural limits. The first guidance given to every client is not to take them: the long-term side effects reach the kidneys, the hormones, the pituitary and other glands, and using them at all demands professional supervision with pre- and post-cycle support stacks. They are common in the film and modelling world, which sets an unnatural benchmark for ordinary boys — but a shirtless-screen physique is an occupational requirement, not a life goal. Unless your work demands it and you can manage the side effects, recovery and monitoring it entails, the distinction to learn is what is natural and what is not.

Fats, oils and cholesterol targets

Weight gain needs fat as well as muscle, and dietary fat comes in three families, all of which belong in the diet in combination:

Fat typeMain sourcesEffect on cholesterol
MUFA (monounsaturated)Olive oil (purely MUFA)Raises HDL (good cholesterol) without raising LDL
PUFA (polyunsaturated)Peanut, sunflower, safflower and other cooking oils (each a MUFA–PUFA mix)Raises HDL without raising LDL
SFA (saturated)Coconut oil, ghee, butter; hidden in paneer, curd, milk; lard is purely SFARaises both HDL and LDL; pure sources like lard and coconut oil raise LDL
Trans fatsMargarineRaises only LDL (bad cholesterol)

Practical oil strategy: cook eggs in olive oil (or peanut oil, if your HDL is already fine) and rotate your main cooking oil every three months — three months of peanut oil, three of sunflower, and so on. Peanut oil is common and well-suited to India, though a less-refined source beats a highly refined one. Use ghee and butter sparingly rather than not at all — cholesterol already arrives hidden in eggs, milk, curd and paneer — but there is no need to fear them: ghee has a direct link to weight gain, and dietary cholesterol is required for vitamin D absorption. The condition is that you exercise regularly and can burn it off, and that your cholesterol is not already high.

Blood targets to aim for: HDL above 50, LDL below 100, triglycerides below 100, total cholesterol below 200. The skinny-fat, candy-and-namkeen pattern shows up as the opposite — high LDL, low HDL, high triglycerides. Very health-conscious eaters (balancing fish and chicken, using peanut or avocado oil, limiting butter, eating soluble-fibre foods like dal and oats that reduce bad cholesterol) typically show triglycerides of 50–75, HDL of 50–65 and LDL of 80–90. Triglycerides rise with sugar, fruit and high-carbohydrate intake, which is why the fruit-heavy weight-gain diet assumes yours are normal.

Putting it together: the eating day, mass gainers and creatine

Rules of the eating day

  • Three balanced main meals — protein, carbs, fat and fibre in each — plus a compulsory evening snack as the fourth meal. Long gaps between meals are not allowed.
  • Add inter-meal calories: a juice or a banana between breakfast and lunch, and again between the evening snack and dinner if wanted. A banana is about 75 calories; a carton juice, 100–150.
  • Choose calorie-dense versions of everything: thick dal rather than thin, hung curd or Greek yogurt rather than loose dahi, rice (about 85 calories per pressed half-katori), potato, paneer (50 g is roughly 125 calories), parathas.
  • No soup and no salad — they fill the small stomach of an underweight person before the real food arrives.
  • Fruits: six to eight a day including juices (a glass of juice counts as three fruits, each fruit counted as about 50 calories), provided triglycerides are normal. Fresh juice at home; a tetra pack outside beats nothing at all.
  • Go easy on nuts, tea and coffee — all three kill appetite. Favour liquids like juices instead.
  • Milk stays off the menu; protein comes from curd, paneer, eggs, dal, chicken and fish.

A sample college day

  1. Before leaving home: a fruit-and-curd smoothie.
  2. Tiffin: paneer toast or a cheese sandwich, plus a banana.
  3. Lunch: rajma rice or dal rice with a vegetable (aloo sabzi is encouraged — it is energy-dense) and curd for a double hit of protein.
  4. Before heading home: another fruit, or a fresh juice if available.
  5. Evening snack: curd with granola, or upma, poha or a sandwich.
  6. Training: weight training after the snack — without it there is no point to the rest.
  7. Post-workout (only if chasing muscle gain on top of weight gain): two to three tablespoons — not scoops — of whey isolate blended with banana, blueberries and almond milk. Isolate, not concentrate, which can trouble the gut.
  8. Dinner: paneer sabzi with a little dal and roti or paratha; or pasta with chicken; or fried rice (not oily) with chicken or tofu and vegetables — any cuisine works if protein and carbs are balanced.
  9. After dinner: a fruit or a small traditional sweet such as sandesh or a pista laddoo — not Western desserts.

Mass gainers

A mass gainer is essentially a high-calorie shake — high in carbohydrate with protein and fat — given to the very sick, to people recovering from injury, and to those who need to gain weight. It works, with monitoring: watch that body-fat percentage and cholesterol are not climbing and that the weight is not rising too fast.

Creatine

Creatine is built from three amino acids — arginine, glycine and methionine — and deserves a positive reputation. It speeds the cell's ATP energy conversion, so you get more energy in training (a boost you can feel within a week or two of starting); it improves muscle synthesis, so muscle gain is faster; and studies show benefits for the aging brain and body — it slows the muscle loss of sarcopenia and may help prevent or reduce age-related neurodegenerative disorders. Weight-gain clients in their twenties are routinely advised creatine, usually alongside very little protein powder.

  • Dose: 3–5 grams a day. Men can take 5 g; women are advised 3 g; beginners with low muscle mass start at 3 g. More is not better — excess of anything is bad.
  • Form: monohydrate — the most common and the best studied.
  • Take it daily, including rest days and weekends — it accumulates in the muscle, and skipping days drains the tank. When a tub runs out or you travel, a two-to-three-week gap before restarting is fine, and it can be taken lifelong.
  • Water: drink noticeably more, especially when starting — creatine sits in the muscle and pulls water in.
  • Digestion: if it upsets the gut, take it after a heavy, carb-rich meal.
  • For women: creatine will not make you bulky — women's lower testosterone means even excess protein does not build large muscle — and the benefits apply fully, provided the weight training happens.
  • Kidneys: creatine can raise the creatinine reading on a blood test; do not panic at that number alone — ask for the appropriate kidney-profile test to understand what it means. Anyone with kidney, gut or other health issues, and the elderly especially, should start only after asking a doctor or nutritionist.
