Male Sexual Health: Cutting Through the Myths, Anxiety and Misinformation

Few areas of health carry more shame, silence and marketing noise than men's sexual health — where a one-rupee pill can be repackaged into a lakh-rupee "cure" and a frightened patient will pay anything. Urologist and andrologist Dr. Vijayant Govinda explains what the evidence actually supports, why most sexual problems are rooted in anxiety and lifestyle rather than defect, and how to find safe help instead of a quack in a tent.

At a glance

  • Sexual problems can be medical or surgical. A urologist — and, for men's health specifically, the sub-specialist andrologist — is the qualified place to seek help, not a roadside sexologist or hakim.
  • A regulated doctor cannot ethically inflate a cheap, effective treatment into a lakh-rupee package; exploitation thrives because frightened, anxious patients will pay almost anything for a quick result.
  • Most sexual difficulty is performance anxiety, not physical failure. Men can only have sex when they are relaxed, and first-time sex is difficult for almost everyone.
  • Size matters, but it is not the only thing that matters. A modest size with good timing and technique can outperform a large one that lasts thirty seconds.
  • Foreskin hygiene is genuine medicine. An unhealthy, non-retracting foreskin is worth treating; circumcision has real but often overstated benefits, and the online literature is heavily biased by its authors.
  • There is no single "correct" sexual timing — a good time is one that satisfies both partners, and premature ejaculation is best defined by loss of control rather than by the stopwatch.
  • Fathers talking openly to sons through adolescence is, in this andrologist's view, the single most important piece of sexual education — and unsupervised gym steroids in teenagers can permanently stunt growth.
  • Compulsive masturbation and pornography, unregulated Viagra and unproven shilajit are the common traps; diet, sleep, exercise and stopping porn do more for sexual health than any quick fix.

Finding safe, science-based help

Sexual problems can be medical or surgical, and urologists have extensive experience managing them. Within urology, those who take a special interest in men's health are called andrologists — the next step after a urology qualification, for a doctor who wants to go deeper into this field specifically. Seeing an andrologist means getting correct information and correct ideas about sexual health, rather than the surrounding noise where one voice praises Viagra, another condemns it, and a third simply says to take shilajit.

The credibility test is straightforward. People readily seek a cardiologist for the heart, yet for the penis they will crawl into a hakim's tent. A legitimate doctor maintains patient privacy — a good practitioner would never display a famous patient's photograph, precisely because that patient would never want it known he was treated for erectile dysfunction. A clinic advertising itself under a railway bridge, or leaning on photographs with celebrities, does not look like a legitimate medical practice. What actually matters is how many patients are cured, how properly, and how affordably.

The economics of exploitation

The scale of exploitation follows directly from how patients arrive. A Viagra tablet costs very little. But quacks blend that same molecule into gold ash (swarn bhasam), silver, or a sweet syrup (sheera), and a one-rupee pill becomes a ten-thousand-rupee monthly treatment — and some sexologists in Delhi charge as much as one lakh rupees a month. For a lakh rupees a cardiac stent can be placed and a heart attack treated in intensive care; the andrologist's point is that a penis does not need a lakh-rupee cure.

The reason patients pay is emotional, not rational. Most men spending on this do so under acute stress and anxiety — pressure from an impending marriage, a partner, or the fear of failing to perform. A patient may arrive saying his wedding is in ten days, that he is desperate, even suicidal, and that he will pay anything to be fixed. At that moment he would hand over whatever is asked. A doctor registered with the medical council cannot commit such fraud, because the government regulates the profession; but many outside that system do, in large numbers.

Why don't people with cardiac failure go to these hakims? Because they can't put in a stent.

Dr. Vijayant Govinda, urologist and andrologist

The underlying problem, the andrologist argues, is that a man overvalues the organ itself while undervaluing the things that keep it healthy. He will pay a fortune for a quick fix but neglect hygiene, exercise and lifestyle — the factors that actually determine outcomes.

Performance anxiety and what sex actually involves

A large share of Indian men have had no early, open sexual experience. Many studied in boys' schools with limited opportunity to meet women, never went out to clubs, and formed their entire idea of sex from pornography and web series. Layered onto chronic masturbation, poor exercise and deficient general health, this leaves them confused about what sex even is. It should be no surprise, then, that first-time sex is difficult — nobody scores a century on the first innings.

Performance anxiety is essentially stage fright: the same nervousness that comes with a first debate, a first driving lesson or a first exam. And sex cannot happen inside fear. It is a primal response — a man can only have sex when he is not scared, because sex is meant to be a relaxing, leisurely activity. This is one reason a first sexual experience so often involves alcohol: its base effect is anti-anxiety, dulling the fear enough to let a person perform. Even then, first-time sex is rarely good sex, because sex has to be learned — a person is handling a living being, not a hand.

Masturbation is not a rehearsal for sex. With a hand a man can go fast or slow, tight or loose, finish in seconds, with no one watching and no one to satisfy. Sex involves another living person with her own body, views and desires. Treating the two as equivalent sets up failure — and then, in that anxious moment, men are robbed of large sums for treatments they do not need.

There is no rule that says "first night"

Society has manufactured the idea that sex must happen on the wedding night. There is no need. A couple who have married have decades ahead, are financially and emotionally invested, and are building a family together — so the sexual part can be delayed until both partners are comfortable. Developing intimacy first, taking it slow, perhaps starting with a kiss, is far more sensible than chasing penetration on day one as if it were a ritual to be completed.

The woman is an active partner

A persistent myth holds that the man performs and the woman merely receives — that she only has to "serve," while the man does the work. This is false. The female partner has just as much of a role as the male in reducing anxiety, arousing her partner and charging the encounter sexually. Where a woman treats sex only as a marital duty — not exploring her own body, not helping her husband become aroused — the encounter suffers for both.

Sex is more than penetration

School textbooks reduce sex to an erect penis and a vagina, but the erect penis is the last part of the process. Before it come several stages, and arousal itself has many components: hygiene, smell, physical appearance, physical intimacy and attraction. A person will never manage sex with someone they are not attracted to. Yet nobody teaches this. Sex has been collapsed down to the single act of achieving an erection and penetrating, while everything that should precede it is ignored — including the fact that pornography, where so many men learn, is mostly not sex at all. Oral sex, treated as almost normal on screen, remains uncommon in India for educational and hygiene reasons and simple lack of exposure.

When circumstances kill arousal

Cohabitation does not happen by default in India, and the andrologist sees this repeatedly. A soldier may be home fifteen days every four months; an industrial worker may live in one state while his family lives in another. In a short, crowded visit — with a pregnancy to attempt, children to see, land and family functions to attend — sex occupies a narrow window with no time for arousal to build. Meanwhile the husband, living in an urban environment, absorbs one set of expectations while his wife in the village has had no such exposure. His arousal, shaped by pornography, is missing at home, and erectile difficulty follows. These are not always the cause, but they are real and important parts of managing sexuality.

Breaking the vicious circle

Anxiety feeds a self-perpetuating loop: sex does not happen, the man becomes anxious and self-monitoring, and the anxiety prevents it happening again. During sex itself he stays trapped in a running commentary — "is it working now, is it working now" — and that very self-checking makes it fail. The common reassurance, "don't worry at all, it will happen on its own," is generally poor advice on its own.

There are several ways to break the circle. The simplest is short-term medication — sildenafil or tadalafil — used to have satisfactory sex a few times, after which a man gets over the block and becomes used to it. For those who prefer not to take medication at all, the advice is breathing exercises, more intimacy, becoming fully aroused first, and not hurrying.

Penis size: what matters and what doesn't

Much male anxiety is size-related — it is the oldest joke among boys. Size is a very big deal in sex, and it is important; but it is not the only thing that is important. Sexologists and social-media influencers have swung to the opposite falsehood, insisting size is unimportant. It cannot be unimportant; that reassurance is offered mainly to comfort those who lack it.

An average size is defined for a reason: at that size children are conceived, sex tends to be good, and a partner will most probably reach orgasm. Just as there is an average height, or a standard airline seat designed for a standard person, there is a functional average. As the andrologist states them, average erect length is roughly four to five inches, with an average girth of about one and a half to two inches. Below the average, sexual problems become more likely, because the vagina — a stretchable organ — is receptive to size and needs that stretch, and orgasm arises from both vaginal and clitoral stimulation. The claim that a partner can simply be rubbed to climax is a misconception; sex, ultimately, is penetration, so a penetrative size matters.

Size without timing is wasted

The counter-point matters just as much. A penis of six inches, thick, attached to a timing of thirty seconds is still a waste.

Partly true. A large penis with no staying time is as useless as a small penis — while a small penis with good timing can actually be a winner. Where size is modest, it can be compensated with better timing, more vigour and technique. What is false is the blanket claim that size never matters and that every woman prefers only kissing, licking and oral: many women reach orgasm through penetration with good size, good timing and vigorous sex, and for a relationship that can be a genuine game-changer.

Three kinds of "small"

When assessing a man who reports a small size, the andrologist looks at three things: whether it is actually small; in which state it is small — flaccid or erect; and whether the concern is really about visible appearance. The patients themselves fall into three groups.

  • Medical (pathological) small penis, or micropenis. Usually due to hormonal problems, injury, or cosmetic defects such as hypospadias, previous surgeries or strictures. These are genuine medical problems with medical and surgical solutions. Treatment cannot make it very large, but it can make it functional — enough to marry, have sex and have children — often supplemented by improving timing and using oral stimulation. The andrologist notes that Indian women tend to be loyal and cooperative, and where a husband is decent and the marriage offers mental, economic and family security, most partners will find ways to make it work.
  • Psychological small penis. Here the size is normal or average, but the man is convinced it is small and projects that insecurity onto his wife, insisting he is inadequate even as she reports she is content. These are tricky patients: they will not accept reassurance, they demand a medicine or a magical cure, and many go on to surgery — implants, plasty, grafts — after which complications can create new problems.
  • The in-between group. Average size, but a real difficulty satisfying a partner who complains. The complaint may have several causes — reduced sensation after childbirth, or low timing being confused with size (two quick thrusts and discharge cannot register as "size"), or comparison with a previous partner. These men usually need to improve hardness and timing rather than length.

What treatment can and cannot do

For the in-between and appearance-driven cases there are both medical and surgical options: PRP injections (marketed as the "P-shot") said to aid penile growth; fat or fillers to add thickness; and ligament release that lets the penis hang lower and look better. Often a partner is not concerned with erect length at all but wants more to hold during foreplay or oral stimulation, where thickening the flaccid or semi-erect organ helps — comparable, the andrologist notes, to breast augmentation, the breast also being an organ that contributes to arousal.

"Turtling" is an appearance concern, not an erection problem. Some men have normal erections but shrink markedly when bathing, in cold, or clothed — and, for example, in a shared army barracks bath, mockery can spread among scores of soldiers and become its own source of anxiety. Here the wish is for the organ to hang rather than retract, addressed with treatments such as "Gro-Tox" (botulinum toxin) or PRP. These are medical treatments, injections and surgeries — precisely what a hakim, who offers the same syrup for erection, timing, size and early discharge alike, cannot provide.

Foreskin, hygiene and circumcision

Most men never discuss the anatomy of a well-functioning penis, so problems such as a tight foreskin (phimosis) come as a surprise. A foreskin that does not open, or sex that becomes painful, is not normal — sex should not be painful. Around 70% of the world's men are circumcised, whether at birth or through ritual practice in various communities, and circumcision has become a heavily debated subject, with some countries arguing it should be delayed until an age of consent.

Read who published the study

Much of the online information is heavily biased, so it is important to look behind the picture at who produced it. Pro-circumcision literature emphasising its advantages tends to come from certain countries and authors, while strongly negative papers tend to come from child-rights campaigners and researchers elsewhere. Interpreting this — differentiating genuine signal from advocacy — is itself a skill; search for almost any claim and its opposite is also available.

On the andrologist's reading, some circumcision claims hold up and others do not: cosmetic appearance of the glans is improved, and there is some truth to effects on sensitivity and timing, but the claim that circumcision reduces penile size is untrue, and the claim that it increases sensitivity is untrue. Benefits exist, but not to the degree often advertised — roughly half of what is claimed.

Where the evidence is clearer

Several protective effects are, in his account, well established: circumcision does not cause cancer; it reduces HPV; it lowers a female partner's risk of cervical cancer; and it reduces sexually transmitted infections. A non-circumcised penis stays moist and can accumulate pre-ejaculate and smegma with inflamed skin, raising infection risk, whereas the circumcised surface has better hygiene and its mucosa toughens. Penile cancer is almost absent in circumcised men and is seen more where the foreskin is retained and hygiene is poor — though it remains rare, on the order of one in a million.

The determining factor is hygiene, not religion. A healthy foreskin retracts easily, is free of cuts and redness, has normal sensitivity, no early discharge and no pain during sex; it should be opened and cleaned daily with clean water, leaving no smegma. An unhealthy foreskin — painful, non-retracting, impossible to wash, with smell, smegma or redness — offers no benefit in being kept, and in such cases circumcision is advised and genuinely beneficial. This applies regardless of faith: where disease is present, the benefit is available to anyone.

Basic hygiene comes before any debate about sensitivity. Young men with a permanently closed foreskin often want to discuss sensitivity and pleasure, when the immediate issue is washing. A sexually active adult will encounter a partner's secretions and must be able to retract and clean daily — the andrologist compares failing to do so with kissing and then never brushing your teeth.

Two different meanings of "sensitivity"

Confusion about sensitivity comes from conflating two things. The first is touch sensitivity — hyper-sensitivity, where the slightest contact feels like a current, with rubbing, burning, pain and redness. This is not good; it is a driver of premature ejaculation and arises from fine pain-fibre receptors that tend to disappear after circumcision, so circumcised men usually have less hyper-sensitivity. The second is sensitivity to sex and orgasm — the sensation during penetration leading to climax. This is broadly the same in circumcised and uncircumcised men; if it were drastically different, the majority of the world's circumcised men would not enjoy sex, which is not the case, though there may be a slightly fuller or "wetter" enjoyment when the foreskin is retained. Historically, in Victorian-era English literature, circumcision was even promoted as a way to curb masturbation in young boys.

The andrologist's own position is neither pro nor anti: ritual circumcision is a community's own practice on which he offers no comment; where disease is present it is not in question; and cosmetic circumcision purely to improve sex, in the absence of disease, is a separate conversation to be had individually with a patient.

Premature ejaculation and timing

Average sexual timing is strikingly short — many men reach orgasm very quickly — and around this a whole anxiety builds, a feeling of not being "man enough." But timing cannot be judged from the man's perspective alone; the woman's perspective matters too. A good sexual timing is one that satisfies the partner, and that depends heavily on who the partner is: some women reach orgasm with penetration in a short time, others need lengthy stimulation, some respond to slow stroking and some to rapid. A man whose timing is three or four minutes may be entirely successful with one partner, where anatomy and rhythm match, and struggle with another — so he may have premature ejaculation with one and not the other.

Why the same man varies

Several ordinary scenarios illustrate that timing is contextual rather than fixed. A man may satisfy his wife at home yet finish quickly with an outside partner because of heightened excitement, or perform well in a decade-old routine but not with a new, more demanding partner. A second marriage after the loss of a first can bring hyper-sensitivity or greater excitement, along with increased frequency of sex. A couple separated by distance, meeting once a month, may find the first encounter very quick and later ones normal as they settle into a rhythm. All of this makes any single definition slippery.

The textbook definition holds that a man able to hold for one minute does not have premature ejaculation — a definition the andrologist rejects. In his view, if a man cannot satisfy his partner according to both partners' wishes, he has premature ejaculation and warrants treatment. One minute may suffice for the purpose of conceiving a child, but it is not the right target. The ideal is control: a man should not discharge until he wishes to, holding perhaps ten, fifteen or twenty minutes until his partner and he are satisfied. Premature ejaculation, defined simply, is discharging before you wish to.

Shame does lasting harm

Discharge during foreplay — even at a touch — is common and, in a young and highly excited man, forgivable; a second or third encounter often goes well. The danger lies in humiliation. Shaming a man once can plant a lifelong conviction of premature ejaculation in his mind. The andrologist is critical of screen portrayals that mock such moments, and of real-world encounters engineered to demean.

Do not take a paid encounter as a verdict. Many men have a first experience with a sex worker whose aim is only to finish the job quickly, not to make love — and who may plant the idea that the man is "small" or has premature ejaculation, precisely to end the encounter faster. Men return convinced they are broken. The andrologist's counsel is that this is a tactic, not a diagnosis; in a real, relaxed relationship things are usually fine. Where a patient still insists on medicine, a placebo can serve — sildenafil for genuine erectile problems, an inert tablet where the block is psychological.

Talking to sons: adolescence, growth and the gym-steroid trap

Parents readily monitor a child's height and weight, taking him to a doctor for poor nutrition or to an orthopaedist for bone concerns. But the sexual development of a male child between roughly thirteen and twenty is left unwatched: whether both testes are developing, whether beard growth and other signs of puberty are appearing, whether the penis is growing in length, whether the foreskin retracts and hygiene is developing, and whether conditions such as an undescended testis, hernia or hydrocele are present. Many of these problems develop precisely during this window, when treatment — medication, and stretching and exercises during growth — can still help.

Too often that window closes unused. Boys stay shy, parents do not raise the subject, and a young man first confronts a problem — phimosis, hyper-sensitivity, a small or asymmetric testis, a hernia — at college, when he is too embarrassed to discuss it with a father who never opened the conversation. He turns to friends, who laugh, or to the internet, where he collects unsolicited advice from quacks and years of unnecessary "treatment." By the time such men reach an andrologist at twenty-four or twenty-five, the decade of sexual growth — and the period when treatment would have been most effective — has passed.

A child's first idol and most trusted source is his father.

Dr. Vijayant Govinda, urologist and andrologist

The remedy is ordinary conversation, sustained year on year: asking whether a son has a girlfriend, whether he is drawn to pornography, whether he is having normal erections, and reassuring him that these things are normal — much as a mother speaks to a daughter about periods and breast development. In the andrologist's view this is the single most important part of sexual education, because a father who becomes a trusted friend clears countless doubts and spares his son years of being stuck. He recounts a teenage nephew who could ask him intimate questions but not his own father — a very real and common problem — and argues that an early, open conversation can change the entire outcome of a boy's life.

Steroids in the teenage gym

A growing and separate danger is anabolic steroid use in adolescents. Seventeen-year-olds, not yet fully mature, take up the gym and — often without any parental awareness — are given steroids there. The consequences during this developmental stage are serious: there is a phenomenon of "steroid imprinting," in which starting steroids around seventeen for bodybuilding causes testicular function to decline, sperm count to fall, penile growth to arrest and bones to stop lengthening, while aggression becomes imprinted on a still-developing brain and educational focus is derailed. Few parents discuss this or give their sons the confidence to talk openly about it.

Building real sexual health: lifestyle over quick fixes

Prevention and treatment both begin with lifestyle rather than pills. Preventing erectile dysfunction and premature ejaculation means managing the risk factors — not smoking, keeping the heart healthy, maintaining a healthy body weight, exercising regularly. Pelvic floor (Kegel) training genuinely helps erections, and penile massage can help too. The trouble is that everyone wants a quick result: as with losing weight, real change may take months, not days.

The case for stopping masturbation and pornography

The andrologist's blunt claim is that the most useful single step a man can take to improve his sex life is to stop masturbating.

Myth. Daily masturbation is a harmless "everyday activity" or stress-relief "multivitamin." The older, reasonable message was that occasional masturbation is not unhealthy and need not cause guilt — but that has been pushed to an untenable extreme. Uncontrolled, daily masturbation can blunt sexual drive and reduce penile hardness and fullness. Masturbation trains a dry hand, which never matches the sensation of intercourse, producing what is termed "death grip syndrome," and Western literature increasingly flags related problems.

Beyond the physical, the andrologist argues that masturbation to pornography is the opposite of natural sex. Its stimulation is visual and endlessly novel — the user scrolls constantly, cannot focus on one partner, and comes to need ever more extreme scenarios — while the body barely moves, the pelvic floor slackens over time, and the man becomes unable to be aroused by a real woman. It also removes the reward that motivates effort: if climax is available instantly from a hand, the drive to date, exercise, build a presentable body and pursue a real partner falls away. He points to elite athletes who practise abstinence before major events, and warns that pornography-and-masturbation addiction can escalate like any other, hollowing out the brain's pleasure response much as heavy alcohol use does — he describes young men whose careers and motivation collapsed. The damage, importantly, is reversible with time — often four to ten months — alongside improvements in lifestyle, sexual health and motivation.

Testosterone: the "DESI ILAJ" of diet, exercise, sleep and, if needed, injection

Science does not yet know precisely what a "normal" testosterone level is. Older reference ranges were drawn from a single study spanning ages eighteen to eighty, yet an eighteen-year-old's level is not an eighty-year-old's; later age-specific figures were derived largely from Western populations and may not translate to Indians. As a rough rule of thumb the andrologist offers an age-adjusted target — on the order of 600 for a man around thirty, with perhaps as little as 200 acceptable at seventy — while stressing these numbers are not well standardised. The right level also depends on the person: a sedentary IT professional may do well at 300–350, a moderately active man wanting sex several times a week around 500, and a high-level athlete may need more. The "sweet spot" is not settled, and testosterone replacement therapy is an evolving field.

His practical prescription is a mnemonic — "DESI ILAJ," standing for Diet, Exercise, Sleep and Injections (testosterone replacement) — with diet, exercise and sleep doing most of the work:

  • Diet. Testosterone is a steroid built from cholesterol, which the body makes itself, so the aim is a good balance of quality fats and avoidance of inflammatory ones. Egg yolk, desi ghee, olive oil and rice bran oil are cited as healthy, alongside adequate multivitamin and fat-soluble vitamin (A, D, E) levels. Dry fruits help, without overthinking which — a balanced diet with everyday peanuts, cashews and almonds is fine. The andrologist is scathing about fads that repackage cheap staples as premium (peanuts recast as costly pecan nuts, makhana as "fox nut," inexpensive ashwagandha displaced by pricier horny goat weed), recounting a rickshaw-puller told to eat pecans when milk, desi ghee, more protein and some exercise would help far more. Eat according to your means; do not chase fads.
  • Sleep. Sleep repairs the hormonal system, and a regular sleep cycle matters most — going to bed at the same time sets the body clock, which anticipates and times hormone release, testosterone included. Erratic or night-shift schedules damage that clock. Good sleep means a fixed schedule with adequate morning sun exposure — five to ten minutes of sunlight on waking helps activate the pineal gland and supports vitamin and testosterone production — plus six to seven hours of clean, uninterrupted sleep, undermined by late-night phone scrolling ("blue screen syndrome").
  • Injections. Testosterone replacement has its place, but it is not magic. If a man takes injections and changes nothing else, the problem returns the moment they stop — the andrologist's point being that he is "not Jesus Christ" handing out holy water; exercise, diet, sleep and stopping masturbation must change alongside.

Myth. Shilajit is a proven testosterone booster. Everyone is promoting it — often bodybuilders whose physiques come from injections, not from shilajit. At best it is a multi-mineral supplement whose mineral content may do something, but good-quality shilajit is hard to find and unregulated: companies advertise a "fulvic acid" content the andrologist disputes even exists in the claimed form, products may be adulterated, and it is, in his words, among the easiest things to sell as snake oil.

Viagra is a disease medicine, not a lifestyle drug

Marketing sells "fauladi" (steel) power, but the penis is not meant to be an iron rod, and never is for anyone — that image is borrowed from the veined, ripped bodybuilder and wrongly projected onto sexual function. The only way to force that kind of rigidity is a drug such as Viagra, and chasing it recreationally is the core mistake.

Myth. Recreational Viagra is a harmless way to be "stronger." Sildenafil and related PDE5 inhibitors were introduced to treat a disease — erectile dysfunction — just as one would not take a fever medicine without a fever, or blood-pressure medicine without high blood pressure. Taken without genuine need they reset expectations wrongly: after experiencing an artificial peak, normal, healthy sex begins to feel inadequate, and a man comes to crave the drug. The andrologist reports common downsides — headache, body aches, weakness, loss of motivation, and a marked shrinkage of the organ once the effect wears off, sometimes for several days — which then tempts men into further medication and a worsening cycle. Men whose Viagra stops working may be pushed by successive practitioners toward steroids and other agents, escalating without anyone bringing them back to a healthy baseline.

Verified medical channels are coming. The andrologist notes moves — already visible in some countries — to require that anyone giving out medical advice online hold a medical degree and provide their registration, and expects similar verification in India. The broader message: take sexual-health information from qualified, accountable sources rather than from marketing.

Why male fertility is falling

Sperm counts are falling and infertility is rising in India, mirroring a worse situation already seen in the West — a shift reflected in the rapid growth of fertility businesses. The andrologist dismisses simple single-cause explanations such as ambient temperature (hotter countries would then be less fertile, which is not the pattern) and instead points to a cluster of modern factors:

  1. Late marriage and late childbearing. The average age of having a first child has climbed steadily, and many now delay past thirty; the older the age, the harder conception becomes.
  2. High-stress work. Modern work keeps the body in repeated high-cortisol states — he cites a pilot landing several times a day, a surgeon operating, a podcast host recording — with cortisol taking days to subside between episodes, unlike the slower rhythm of older agricultural life.
  3. Poor and contaminated diet. Pesticides, fertilizers, high-fructose sugar, and plastics and microplastics in the food chain.
  4. Radiation and pollution exposure. Cell-tower and solar radiation, sound pollution and constant electronic exposure.
  5. Poor water quality. The everyday drinking water supply.

His advice follows directly: have children on time, which in turn means marrying rather than indefinitely postponing family life. Left unchanged, he warns, the trend points toward the low-fertility pattern seen elsewhere, with more couples turning to adoption.

The bottom line — and a caveat about who has the time

Stripped of the marketing, the andrologist's core message is simple: diet, sleep and exercise are not that complicated, and sex is not that complicated. Leave pornography and the penis tends to perform again; exercise, sleep and diet manage testosterone; and the further a man retreats into his own head — thinking, comparing, self-monitoring — the less he enjoys sex. The more he compares, the less pleasurable it becomes.

He is candid, though, that this "light version" of life assumes free time most people do not have. An employee earning a modest salary on a long shift with a long commute, without a proper weekly day off, is closer to bonded labour than to leisure — and even doctors, he says, are "glorified labour." Those with a home, a car and time to exercise are, in effect, the well-off; two kinds of people can readily exercise, the one with little work and the one with so much work that others do it for him. The practical takeaway is not guilt but perspective: fix the fundamentals where you can, distrust quick fixes and marketing, and seek qualified help rather than a syrup that promises everything.
