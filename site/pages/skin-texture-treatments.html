How to Improve Skin Texture: Six Dermatologist-Backed Treatments

Dermatologists Dr. Shah and Dr. Maxfield break down why skin develops texture — pores, fine lines, growths, a thickened surface layer and even invisible facial hair — and order the fixes from gentlest topicals to in-office resurfacing.

At a glance

  • Skin texture rarely has a single cause — fine lines, enlarged pores, sebaceous filaments, oil-gland changes, skin growths and facial hair all contribute, which is why it is hard to treat and sometimes even to diagnose.
  • Chemical exfoliation with alpha hydroxy acids is the over-the-counter starting point: glycolic acid is the strongest, PHAs like gluconolactone the gentlest.
  • Retinoids normalize the skin's thickened top layer while supporting collagen — the single best pick if you only do one thing for texture.
  • Double cleansing with an oil-based cleanser helps dissolve the buildup inside pores and sebaceous filaments.
  • Topicals will not take you all the way: microneedling or micro-infusion, chemical peels, ablative lasers and neuromodulator injections deliver the bigger changes, especially for growths that topicals cannot touch.
  • Removing facial hair — including the fine vellus hairs you cannot see — makes skin look smoother and reflect light better, though dermaplaning can trigger breakouts in some people.

What causes textured skin — and how it shows up

Texture is usually several things at once rather than one problem. The main contributors:

  • Fine lines and wrinkles. Driven by muscle dynamics, while the subtler ones come simply from time.
  • Oil glands. These cause texture in two ways: sebaceous hyperplasia, where the glands become enlarged, or pores, where the oil gland sits at the base. Pores themselves are a combination of skin structure and that oil gland.
  • Skin growths. Moles and seborrheic keratoses — small growths you can see as individual dots and feel when you run a finger over the skin. Some rough spots can even be precancerous lesions called actinic keratoses.
  • Facial hair. Both the coarse terminal hairs you can see and the fine, near-invisible vellus hairs.
  • A thickened stratum corneum. The very top layer of the skin becoming too thick — the same layer that thickens into a callus on your hand — is a major contributor to dullness.

In practice, patients experience texture as poor radiance — the uneven surface stops light reflecting back, so skin looks dull; as enlarged pores that can sit so deep they resemble acne scars; as visible or palpable dots and rough spots; and, perhaps most commonly, as makeup that cakes in certain areas, never lays smooth, and wrinkles and creases around the eyes.

The treatments below run from least invasive to most invasive — because topical treatments alone will not take you all the way.

The topical foundation: exfoliation, retinoids and cleansing

Exfoliation — choose your acid by molecule size

Exfoliants come in two forms. Chemical exfoliants, such as alpha hydroxy acids, dissolve the bonds between skin cells; physical exfoliants — the classic apricot scrub — are particles you can feel rubbing against the skin. The dermatologists recommend physical scrubs for the body but generally not for the face.

Within chemical exfoliation there are two groups: alpha hydroxy acids (AHAs) and beta hydroxy acids like salicylic acid. Salicylic acid is great for pores but not for the surface texture layer, so for texture the AHAs are the focus. Their behaviour tracks molecule size: smaller molecules penetrate deeper, work harder and irritate more; larger molecules sit on top and exfoliate only the uppermost layer, making them gentler on sensitive skin.

Alpha hydroxy acid Molecule size Character
Glycolic acid Smallest Penetrates deepest — the heavy hitter, most effective but most irritating for sensitive skin
Lactic acid Small–medium In between glycolic and mandelic
Mandelic acid Medium–large Milder than lactic
PHAs (e.g. gluconolactone) Largest Sits on top of the skin; may be less effective, but great for sensitive skin

Gentle over-the-counter peels using these ingredients are typically used once or twice a week. If you are acne-prone or texture is your main concern, a low concentration used daily can make a difference — but do not use so much that you over-irritate your skin barrier, which can make your skin worse. In the office, the same ingredient families power chemical peels that are considerably stronger and vary widely in depth; the deepest ones essentially resurface the entire face — it peels off in the process — and can produce profound texture results.

Retinoids — the number-one pick

While exfoliators break the bonds between skin cells, retinoids increase skin cell turnover directly. The whole family — retinol, retinaldehyde and adapalene over the counter, tretinoin and tazarotene on prescription — increases turnover and normalizes skin thickness. Rather than thinning the skin, retinoids thin the overgrown stratum corneum while thickening the rest of the skin, including the dermis and the remaining epidermis, to a normal extent.

Because wrinkles also contribute to textured skin, retinoids do double duty: they address the surface layer and support collagen against the deeper contributors. In the dermatologists' view, tazarotene is the most effective of the family for texture — though expect peeling when you start. If you pick only one thing to improve texture, a retinoid is the recommendation.

Double cleansing — clearing out the pores

Cleansers with exfoliating acids like glycolic or salicylic acid will not do much heavy lifting on their own. Where cleansing genuinely helps texture is in removing buildup inside pores — especially sebaceous filaments. Massage an oil-based cleanser or cleansing balm into the skin for one to two minutes: because like dissolves like, the lipid-soluble base helps break up dead skin cells and dissolve the oily buildup deep in the pores. Follow with a water-based cleanser to wash it all away. This deep clean keeps pores clear and helps minimize how they contribute to texture.

Microneedling and micro-infusion

Even after mastering topicals, texture often calls for something procedural. Among at-home devices, microneedling is one of the best-studied technologies — and the most important consideration is safety. That is why the dermatologists prefer micro-infusion, which uses a sterile, single-use needle head.

Micro-infusion does two things at once: the micro-needling itself mechanically interrupts the skin's structure, and the healing after that deliberate trauma directly stimulates collagen; at the same time it helps the serum ingredients you use absorb more deeply, making them more effective. The result helps fine lines and wrinkles as well as the surface-level dullness caused by a thickened stratum corneum.

The more advanced version is in-office microneedling, where — after your skin is numbed — thousands of needles treat the skin far more aggressively.

In-office procedures: resurfacing lasers and neuromodulators

Ablative lasers — the only fix for growths

Resurfacing lasers such as erbium target the top layer of the skin. Erbium is an ablative laser whose chromophore — where it absorbs — is water; like a CO2 laser, it disrupts the skin and triggers profound healing after the deliberate trauma. Even as a first in-office venture into texture treatment, it can deliver tremendous results.

The unique advantage of these lasers — erbium, CO2, fractional or fully ablative (the latter being a genuinely aggressive procedure) — is that they remove all surface texture. Chemical exfoliants and retinoids tend not to shift things like seborrheic keratoses or sebaceous hyperplasia; ablative, semi-ablative and fractionally ablative lasers clear all forms of texture, including the growths that build up with age and do not respond to topical skincare.

One of the dermatologists recently had a CO2 laser treatment himself — something once considered taboo for his skin tone during his training. At the right, lower settings it proved workable, and he was happy with the results for both texture and tone. The lesson: outcomes depend not only on the technology but on the person operating it, especially for in-office lasers and peels.

Neuromodulators — the under-discussed glow

Botox and the other neuromodulators — Dysport, Xeomin, Daxxify — paralyze facial muscles, letting everything relax so that fine lines and wrinkles, especially the surface-level ones, start to melt away. Patients, family members and the dermatologists themselves notice a radiance — light visibly reflecting off the skin — within about a week or two of treatment, fading once the product wears off.

I think this is such an under-talked-about benefit of Botox — and we're guilty of that too. It really creates a tremendous glow that lasts for a few months.

— Dr. Shah, dermatologist

Whether the glow is entirely down to the muscle effect is unclear — there may be more to it. Botox can temporarily improve the appearance of pores, and Daxxify has its own data on overall skin glow, though the effect shows up with most if not all of the botulinum products. Some practitioners micro-inject small doses across the entire face for an all-over relaxed look rather than treating only dynamic-wrinkle areas.

Nor is it a lifelong commitment: the theoretical schedule is every three months, but one of the dermatologists has gone over a year without any neuromodulator — full facial movement and fine lines returned, exactly as expected. You can do it more or less frequently as you choose.

The bonus tip: facial hair removal and dermaplaning

Beyond the obvious coarse terminal hairs — beard, eyebrows, sideburns — the face carries fine, usually blonde, near-invisible vellus hairs. These deflect and break up light, reducing the skin's radiance. Remove them and the surrounding skin looks smoother and reflects light better: one of the dermatologists notices this every time he has his eyebrows threaded — the skin immediately around the area looks visibly smoother. After hair removal, moisturizers seem to layer better and tinted sunscreen stops pooling around the hairs. The effect is anecdotal rather than formally studied, but both dermatologists believe it is real.

The same logic explains why dermaplaning works. Whether with an over-the-counter Tinkle razor or a scalpel wielded by an aesthetician or in a dermatology office, dermaplaning removes the vellus and terminal hairs plus the top layer of the stratum corneum. Think of the hairs as the fuzz around the edges of a zoomed-in photo: remove them and everything looks crisper and cleaner.

Dermaplaning caution: anecdotally, a lot of people break out after dermaplaning. Possible reasons include the wrong type of blade, regrowing hairs becoming ingrown and forming acne-like lesions, and aftercare — the skin barrier is exposed after the procedure, so keep the area clean and wash your face regularly. If you are new to dermaplaning, be cautious: you do not want to end up with worse texture than you started with because of post-procedure breakouts.

Building it into a routine

You do not need to do everything on this list. If you want to go after texture fully, here is how the dermatologists would layer the treatments in, starting with the core basics:

  1. Start double cleansing — every night, beginning immediately.
  2. Add a retinoid and an exfoliant — a retinoid five nights a week and an exfoliant the other two, cycling roughly three retinoid nights followed by one exfoliant night.
  3. After several months of consistency, add micro-infusion — every two weeks.
  4. Once that is mastered, add an in-office procedure — a chemical peel, an erbium or other ablative laser, or in-office microneedling, once or at most twice a year.
  5. Neuromodulator injections as desired — theoretically every three months, but even once a year works.
  6. Facial hair removal any time — with one caveat: if you are getting waxed, stop your retinoid at least three days beforehand.
