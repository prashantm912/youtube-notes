Bowel and Stool Health: What Your Poop Reveals and the Habits That Keep Your Gut Clear

Gastroenterologist and hepatologist Dr. Shubham Vatsya explains how to read a bowel movement, which stool changes are genuine alarm signs, why so much "gut trouble" is really a gut–brain problem, and the simple, unglamorous habits — fibre, fluids, movement, curd and sleep — that do most of the work.

At a glance

  • A healthy stool is formed, solid to semi-solid, leaves you with a feeling of complete evacuation, and takes only a few minutes — the best sign is a bowel movement you are not obsessed with.
  • Bowel frequency varies hugely. Going once or twice a week can be normal in low-fibre Western diets; missing two or even three days is not a problem in itself. Constipation is defined by whether it disrupts your daily life, not by a number.
  • Around 60–70% of people pass normal stools; roughly one in three has some gut issue. Most gut problems are lifestyle problems first.
  • Three visual stool alarm signs need a doctor: more than three watery stools in 24 hours, visible red blood, and black tarry stools (with no iron supplement).
  • Because 90–95% of the body's serotonin is made in the gut, mental health and bowel health are tightly linked. Symptoms that vanish during sleep point to a gut–brain disorder such as IBS rather than a dangerous disease.
  • IBS is a disorder of the gut–brain axis, not inflammation. Four normal tests — CBC, CRP, faecal calprotectin and coeliac serology — support the diagnosis and rule out inflammation.
  • Food-allergy panels and gut-microbiome tests are described as among the biggest scams in the market; only lactose intolerance and gluten sensitivity have real, testable science behind them.
  • Curd is the original natural probiotic; fruit, vegetables and fibre are prebiotics; whole eggs are an exceptional protein. Added sugar, colas, fruit juice and flavoured yoghurt are hidden drivers of fatty liver and metabolic problems.

Reading your stool: what a healthy bowel movement looks like

"Getting fresh" in the Indian sense is passing stool — not bathing or washing the face — and it should feel satisfying, leaving you sure you have emptied completely. Many people are distressed precisely because they never feel fully evacuated. A healthy stool is formed, solid to semi-solid, and comes with a clear feeling of evacuation.

Best sign. A good bowel movement is one you are not obsessed with: you sit, you push, you are done in a few minutes, and you do not think about it again. Photographing and analysing your stool, or sending pictures to a doctor to be praised for it, is the opposite of healthy.

Posture: why squatting helps

The last part of the large intestine is the sigmoid colon, below it the rectum, then the anus. Many people cannot empty properly because the rectum and sigmoid colon are not aligned; the rectosigmoid angle needs to straighten. Squatting straightens it naturally, which is why the traditional Indian squatting position clears stool so effectively. If you use a Western toilet, place a stool under your feet to reproduce that angle.

Practical toilet advice. Do not take your phone or a newspaper into the toilet. If you actually want to empty properly, spend that time with yourself — and on a Western toilet, use a foot stool to open the passage.

Frequency: there is no single "normal"

Bowel habits differ by diet and culture. In the US or UK, where diets have historically been low in fibre, going once or twice a week and feeling fine is normal. In India, people panic if two days pass — but not passing stool for two or even three days is not, by itself, a problem. Medically, constipation comes in three forms: normal-transit, slow-transit, and pelvic dyssynergia. The working definition that matters is practical: constipation is a lack of bowel movement that interferes with your routine and productivity.

Undigested food in stool

Seeing rajma (kidney beans), tomato, corn or red chilli come through looks alarming but is normal. The fibre in these foods was never meant to be digested and absorbed by humans — we lack pectinase, the enzyme (present in cattle) that breaks down pectin, the major structural component of fibre. Fibre exists to bulk the stool, not to be absorbed. What you can see simply passed through; what you cannot see was in there too. Chewing more, or crushing the chilli, mostly just makes it invisible.

Colour and smell

Stool smells because it is decomposing waste. Its brown colour comes from bile: bile (made in the liver, stored in the gallbladder, released to absorb fat) leaves a pigment called stercobilinogen in the leftover stool. As the stool is passed and exposed to oxygen, stercobilinogen converts to stercobilin, which is yellow-brown. Bright green stool after a plant-based or "vegan meat" burger is simply chlorophyll; black stool can come from an iron supplement.

Wind and smell

Myth. The idea that loud farts relate to how "tight or loose" the anal muscles are is wrong, and so is the claim that smelly wind always means a bad gut. Passing gas is a sign of bacterial fermentation of the food you eat. Foul-smelling, sulphur- or methane-rich gas can indicate dysbiosis — an imbalance where bad bacteria outnumber good — but not always. If it is troubling you, the fix is to raise your natural probiotic intake, especially curd.

Three natural steps to a clear gut

Before any medicine, the specialist prescribes three things and asks patients to hold them for three months. If your gut is not clearing and you are not doing these, you already have a lifestyle issue.

  1. Adequate fibre. Two apples a day, a guava, an orange, and banana if it suits you (bananas can worsen clearing in some people). Aim for the recommended 25–40 g of fibre per day — far more than most people now eat amid processed and delivered food. Taking fibre (a salad) about 15 minutes before a meal regulates gut speed and blunts the blood-sugar spike that follows the meal; then eat protein, and then carbohydrates and fats.
  2. Fluids. Fibre works by pulling water into the stool, so it fails if you are not drinking. Three litres a day is not a big ask — a glass holds roughly 300–500 ml, so ten random glasses gets you there, and that is only good for the body.
  3. Movement. At least 10,000 steps of brisk walking a day. If you will not give your body this time, and your gut is not clearing, the problem is already lifestyle.

These three are the bare minimum for anyone: mind the vegetables, drink water, keep a balanced diet, and move.

Red flags: when bowel trouble needs a doctor

The first step with any constipation is not "eat fruit and drink water" — it is ruling out an underlying serious disease. A careful history looks for these alarm signs. The reassuring pattern is a long-standing, episodic problem in a young person; the worrying pattern is something new, in an older person, with bleeding or anaemia.

Seven history red flags

  • Recent onset. Symptoms that started only days or weeks ago are a red flag. A problem present for more than six months, where the person cannot even remember being symptom-free, is generally not a red flag.
  • Age at onset. Harmless gut issues typically start young — in the 20s and 30s. New onset above age 50 is a red flag.
  • Anaemia. Haemoglobin below 12 in men or below 11 in women is a red flag, because a core job of the gut is absorbing iron and vitamin B12; both iron and B12 deficiency are very common in India, so a normal basic CBC is reassuring.
  • Bleeding. Fresh red blood dripping after passing stool, blood mixed into the stool, or black tarry stools (in the absence of any iron supplement) are red flags.
  • Family history of GI cancer. A first-degree relative — mother, father, sibling — with gastrointestinal cancer (colon, stomach or oesophagus) is a major red flag even if you are healthy yourself, and triggers formal screening guidelines.
  • Progressive vomiting. If the intestine is obstructed, waste collects and comes back up: progressive vomiting is a serious red flag.
  • Night symptoms. If constipation or diarrhoea disturbs your sleep and makes you get up repeatedly at night, add it to the list — because most functional gut symptoms vanish while you sleep.

Three visual stool red flags. More than three completely watery stools in 24 hours; visible blood (dripping red, or red stool mixed with blood); and black, tarry stools with no iron supplementation. Any of these means you should be checked — they can signal that you are unwell rather than just uncomfortable.

IBS and the gut–brain axis

Once red flags are excluded, most remaining "gut issues" live in the mental space. The reason is chemical: 90–95% of the body's serotonin — the neurotransmitter behind mood, stress, anxiety and depression — is made in the gut and then acts in the brain. Sleep is the highest form of relaxation and the biggest heal: while you sleep, the brain rests and other functions are regulated, and gut–brain symptoms disappear. That is why symptoms that wake you at night are treated as a red flag, and symptoms that vanish in sleep point toward a gut–brain disorder.

What IBS actually is

Not inflammation. Irritable bowel syndrome is a disorder of the gut–brain axis — a very common one in younger people — and its full form is often misquoted. In IBS there is no gut inflammation: the gut is in distress, but there is no disease of the gut wall. It comes in three patterns: IBS-C (constipation-predominant), IBS-D (diarrhoea-predominant) and IBS-M (mixed, alternating between the two). In this specialist's clinic, IBS accounts for a large share of cases.

Treatment starts with exercise

The first home treatment for IBS is exercise: roughly 45 minutes, three to five times a week, by any method you enjoy — sport counts as the highest form of involvement for the mind. There is an inverse relationship between mental and physical fatigue: the body tires when worked and recovers, while an overworked mind keeps running. Tiring the body deliberately balances physical and mental burnout. Exercise is also the actual treatment for seasonal affective disorder, which can flare during sharp temperature changes — a natural antidepressant and anti-anxiety measure. Sleep quality, screen time and substance use are all part of the same picture.

When there is deeper trauma. Some people with severe IBS have a history of genuine childhood trauma — sexual abuse, domestic violence, physical abuse. Here the gut symptom is downstream of a real mental-health injury, and healing needs a gut specialist for the biology and a mental-health professional for the rest. Left unaddressed, such distress can become dangerous, which is why active, combined care matters.

A classic feature of these patients is that stress episodes flip the gut: constipation the morning of an important interview, diarrhoea before a big presentation. The history is typically long — 20 or 30 years — and episodic, with completely normal stretches in between and a return to symptoms under stress.

Lactose, gluten, eggs and the food-allergy myths

"I need a food-allergy test"

Myth. Commercial food-allergy panels have no science behind them and no trained doctor advises them — described here as one of the biggest scams in the market. Only two food problems are real and testable at ground level: lactose intolerance and gluten sensitivity.

Lactose intolerance, explained

Lactose is a disaccharide made of glucose and galactose, broken apart by the enzyme lactase, which sits on the microvilli lining the small intestine. In some people, lactase falls with age below a critical level, so lactose is no longer split and absorbed. Undigested lactose is osmotically active: it raises osmolality in the intestine, pulls in water, and causes bloating and diarrhoea after drinking milk.

Crucially, curd, cheese and paneer are usually fine even for the lactose-intolerant, because overnight fermentation converts their lactose into lactic acid — so the lactose is no longer there. The real test is simple and rarely offered: a stool pH below 5.5 confirms lactose intolerance (the bacteria have produced lactic acid). If someone reacts to curd, paneer, cheese, lassi and buttermilk too, it cannot scientifically be called lactose intolerance, because those foods contain no lactose.

Do not villainise milk — especially for children. Making milk a "cool" enemy is a mistake. Given how much malnutrition and how many rickets cases India has, children need the calcium. Adults naturally reduce milk over time; children should not be made to avoid it.

Wheat, gluten and coeliac disease

Wheat is a complex carbohydrate containing gluten, and not everyone needs to avoid it. But a roti-predominant diet drives a sharp post-meal sugar spike and, long-term, weight gain and fatty liver, so cutting or diluting pure-wheat roti is sensible. Mixing in millets lowers the glycaemic index and adds fibre (more than wheat):

Fact. Coeliac disease is a genuine, gluten-induced immunologic injury to the small intestine: the body makes antibodies against gluten that damage the intestine. The only treatment is a lifelong gluten-free diet — there is no medicine. It is called an "iceberg disease" because only the tip is visible; a published paper puts it at around 1% of Indians (roughly 1.5 crore people). It is more common in wheat-eating North India, and it is the hardest disease to diagnose but the easiest to treat once caught. It can also cause infertility and stunt children's growth: in one case, a mother and her 14-year-old daughter — who had not touched four feet in height — were both diagnosed, and within a year the daughter reached 5 feet 3 inches. Children not catching up on height should see a gastroenterologist (and an endocrinologist). A separate, less common condition, non-coeliac gluten sensitivity, also warrants a gluten-free diet.

Myth. Consciously avoiding wheat does not make you gluten-sensitive — there is no science to that. Going gluten-free (as some athletes have said publicly) is not the same as having coeliac disease.

Eggs and vegetarian substitutes

A whole egg is the only food carrying all nine essential amino acids. Each egg has about 6 g of protein absorbed almost completely — no other food matches that absorption. The yolk adds B12, vitamin A (eyes), vitamin D, choline (a boon for fatty liver and brain health), and lutein and zeaxanthin (retinal health, and protection against diabetic blindness). The recommendation is two to three eggs a day as a lightly fried omelette — in olive oil, mustard oil or desi ghee depending on budget.

For those who will never eat eggs, protein can come from dairy, soya, paneer and cheese. Soya is a good vegetarian protein and does not cause "man-boobs" or raise oestrogen. Paneer and cheese differ in use:

Curd, probiotics and a well-fed microbiome

Trillions of bacteria live in the gut, good and bad, normally in balance — a state called eubiosis. When bad bacteria outnumber good (dysbiosis), problems follow. A probiotic is a food containing live good bacteria; a prebiotic is a food that feeds good bacteria so they grow.

Curd as the original probiotic

Curd is the original, natural probiotic — live Lactobacillus, the best good bacteria — and is treated here as a superfood. Fruits and vegetables (and fibre generally) are excellent prebiotics. Other good probiotics include buttermilk (chaas), paneer, cheese, home-made kombucha and kanji (fermented carrot). Matcha and green tea are prebiotic, not probiotic — their catechins are antioxidants. The consistent caution: do not add sugar, which fuels bad bacteria.

Butyrate and "leaky gut"

Good bacteria produce butyrate, the nutrition for intestinal cells. Butyrate maintains the tight junctions of the gut lining — the permeability barrier. When good bacteria are looked after, butyrate keeps that lining repaired and tight, so even the toxins made by bad bacteria cannot cross into the blood. That is the scientific basis of "leaky gut": lose the good bacteria and their butyrate, and bad-bacterial endotoxins pass into the blood, triggering an immune response that can show up as skin problems and acne. This is the real mechanism behind the gut–skin axis.

Diarrhoea: curd, not antibiotics

Myth. "Take an antibiotic for loose stools." About 99% of infective diarrhoea is viral and self-limiting; an antibacterial drug does nothing against a virus, and a viral course runs 3–5 days. The recovery needs good bacteria, which are more sensitive to antibiotics — so an antibiotic kills the very bacteria you need and slows recovery, sometimes causing post-infective IBS. The correct approach is hydration plus a probiotic, with curd the ideal one. Take antibiotics only when a registered medical practitioner prescribes them in writing; casual self-medication breeds resistant "superbugs".

The supplement and microbiome-test scams

Supplementation is described as a major scam in India, where nutraceuticals are unregulated while pharmaceuticals are tightly regulated — so labels often do not reflect what is inside. Sweet "probiotic" drinks are marketed as health products but carry a lot of sugar and preservatives. Two tests in particular are called out as the biggest scams in the market: food-allergy tests and gut-microbiome tests. The microbiome is still so poorly understood, even by researchers, that a consumer test cannot meaningfully guide you.

The microbiome as a personal footprint

Your microbiome is a footprint, much like your genome — largely your own, but able to change over time. What shapes it includes the mode of birth (vaginal versus caesarean), what is given before the first breast milk, exclusive breastfeeding, exposure to soil in childhood, and later smoking, alcohol and antibiotic use.

Two newborn cautions. Giving honey before the first milk is scientifically wrong and dangerous: honey can carry Clostridium botulinum, causing infant botulism, which can be fatal. Discarding colostrum — the first breast milk, richest in vitamin A and vital for immunity — is the biggest mistake for a baby's long-term gut health. A healthy newborn is recommended to have nothing but mother's milk for six months, with top feeds added from the sixth month and breastfeeding continuing up to 18 months to two years.

Playing in soil early is protective — the hygiene hypothesis holds that early microbial exposure builds a stronger immunity, which is one reason locals may tolerate foods that make unexposed visitors ill. In adulthood, you can do your best, but what is lost may not fully return: there is no reliable way to "reboot" the microbiome. Gut dysbiosis is being studied as a link to fatty liver, diabetes, Alzheimer's, Parkinson's and other neurological disorders.

Habits, the liver and the bigger picture

Sugar, the "legal drug"

Sugar is called out as being as bad as nicotine and, in these terms, worse than alcohol — a legal drug and an acquired taste. "No added sugar" is described as a big scam, with three hidden sugar bombs: sauces and ketchup (a spoonful can be loaded), colas and fruit juices, and flavoured yoghurts. Fruit is good, but fruit juice has the fibre stripped out, leaving pure fructose that goes straight to the liver and converts to fat. These feed insulin resistance, fatty liver, metabolic syndrome, PCOS and skin problems, and accelerate ageing. The single easiest change: stop putting sugar in your tea.

Black coffee

Black coffee is used here as an appetite suppressant — its caffeine dampens appetite at the level of the brain while adding almost no calories (the calories come from the sugar and milk you add). Three to four cups is described as good for the liver, brain and heart. Two cautions: it raises blood pressure only transiently, and you should not make it your first drink on waking, because it raises cortisol, which is already high in the morning — build the habit gradually rather than shocking the stomach with acid.

How caffeine and nicotine move the bowel

Intestinal movement runs on the vagus nerve, which releases acetylcholine acting on muscarinic and nicotinic receptors. Caffeine (tea, coffee) and nicotine (cigarettes) act on the nicotinic receptors, stimulating movement — which is why a cigarette or tea can trigger a bowel motion. The catch is desensitisation: with repeated use the dose the intestine needs keeps rising, and stopping can leave you unable to pass stool. That is a physical sign of addiction, distinct from the psychological "chai-sutta break" habit. (Long-term cannabis use is separately linked to amotivational syndrome, hallucinations and, in heavy addiction, dangerous agitation.)

Fasting: the three Fs

The science here is summed up as three Fs — Fibre, Fermented food, Fasting. Intermittent fasting is essentially the traditional idea of time-restricted eating: eat between sunrise and sunset, keep dinner early, and do not lie down straight after eating. On the details, 12-hour versus 16-hour fasting makes no difference by itself — what matters is the total calorie count; a 16-hour fast followed by 4,000 calories in an 8-hour window is not really fasting.

Myth. "Some people faint if their stomach is empty too long." In a normal body this does not happen — it is a temporal association. Counter-regulatory hormones (glucagon from the pancreas, cortisol and adrenaline) keep glucose normal during a fast. Only genuine starvation of the order of 72–96 hours would seriously drop blood glucose.

The liver: resilient, but not invincible

The liver is the most resilient, self-healing organ — a transplanted third of a liver can regrow to full size — but it is silent until badly damaged. Fatty liver progresses through steatohepatitis and fibrosis to cirrhosis, the stage at which roughly 80% is irreversibly damaged. It matters early: a raised SGPT (a marker of liver swelling, usually from fat) at age 24 is linked to about twice the normal risk of a heart attack by 36. Fat, not alcohol alone, is what the liver "understands"; both obesity and alcohol cause fatty liver.

Liver "detox" syrups are a scam. The liver detoxifies you — you do not need to detox it. The way to help it is simply to stop adding toxins (alcohol, excess fat, fried and processed food) and let it work. The liver also naturally makes glutathione, the antioxidant behind "skin glow"; sulphur-containing vegetables such as cauliflower, radish, ginger and garlic help it make more, and the choline in egg yolk helps clear fat from the liver.

Street food and hepatitis

Street food is largely a matter of luck and sanitation. The real danger is faecal contamination — for example, unwashed hands contaminating panipuri water — carrying Hepatitis A and E, the food- and water-borne viruses that can cause acute liver failure. Hepatitis E causes the most deaths in pregnancy. When someone in a household has Hepatitis A or E, they should use a separate washroom for about 12 weeks and be strict about hand-washing and nail-cutting, because the virus is shed in stool and can spread through door handles and surfaces.

A note on anatomy

The small intestine is the longer one — about 6 to 8 metres, with a surface area likened to a tennis court — but it is called "small" because of its narrow diameter, suited to churning food; the large intestine is shorter but wider, to form and pass stool. Its three parts absorb different things: the duodenum takes up iron, calcium and folate (and is the site damaged in coeliac disease); the jejunum absorbs almost everything — water, fats, carbohydrates, protein; and the ileum handles bile acids and B12. About 9 litres of fluid enter the small intestine each day (from diet, and from pancreatic juice and saliva), of which the small intestine reclaims around 7.5 litres and the large intestine around 1.4 litres, so only about 100 ml is lost in normal stool. The good bacteria mainly live in the small intestine.

The unglamorous conclusion

Health is created in boredom, with boring things done over and over again. Calm down instead of chasing excitement every day; build healthy connections with those close to you and your family; get away from screens and scrolling; read more books; eat fermented foods and good fibre; eat your fruits and vegetables well; have whole eggs; have black coffee; and sleep well.

— Dr. Shubham Vatsya, gastroenterologist and hepatologist
