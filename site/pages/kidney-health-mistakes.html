The Everyday Mistakes That Quietly Damage Your Kidneys

Kidneys do far more than make urine, yet they are the vital organ most people ignore — and their diseases are almost silent until they are advanced. Nephrologist Dr. Shoeb Khan explains what kidneys actually do, the handful of everyday habits that destroy them, and the simple checks that catch trouble early.

At a glance

  • The kidneys are one of the body's five vital organs (with the brain, heart, lungs and liver) and receive about 20% of the heart's output — more than the brain. They balance body acidity, control blood pressure, regulate haemoglobin and maintain bone health; urine is only a by-product.
  • Worldwide, the top three causes of kidney disease are uncontrolled diabetes, uncontrolled blood pressure and the irrational abuse of painkillers — and all three are preventable.
  • Kidney disease is dangerous precisely because it has almost no early symptoms; problems typically surface only when roughly 15% of function remains.
  • The right amount of water is however much keeps your urine clear or pale yellow — there is no single fixed figure for everyone.
  • Kidney stones form when calcium or uric acid becomes over-concentrated in urine, either from high intake or too little water.
  • Unregulated painkillers, certain antibiotics, long-term acidity medicines, energy drinks and mercury-laced fairness creams are all quiet threats to the kidneys.
  • Dialysis is a life-saving bridge, not a cure; a transplant is the closest thing to restored normal life.
  • Once a year, check your blood sugar, blood pressure and a three-part kidney report card — structure, function and filter.

What your kidneys really do

The most important thing to understand is that a kidney's job is not simply to make urine. It works like a factory: a factory's purpose is not to pour pollution into the air or river but to produce something of high value, and urine is just one by-product of everything else the kidneys do to sustain life.

They maintain the body's acid balance — if blood becomes too acidic, it is dangerous and can be fatal. They govern haemoglobin: how much blood the body carries is decided by the kidneys, through a hormone called erythropoietin. Many people are treated for low haemoglobin with iron injections, tablets, syrups, multivitamins or even transfusions, only for a simple, inexpensive test to reveal that the real problem was failing kidneys and a raised creatinine. The kidneys also control blood pressure through hormones such as renin, and they maintain bone health — some people discover their kidneys have failed only after a fracture, because it was the weak bone that led to the fall, not the fall that caused the fracture.

Blood pressure in particular is often thought of as a heart problem, but it is regulated by the kidneys. Very high blood pressure in someone under 30, or a previously well-controlled patient who suddenly needs three or four tablets, strongly points to a kidney problem.

Roughly one in every thousand people is born with a single kidney and lives an entirely normal life. Yet in a survey most people would not even count kidneys among the vital organs — they think life depends on the heart, brain, lungs and liver, and overlook the kidneys, which deserve exactly the same importance.

The three preventable causes of kidney failure

Everywhere in the world, the number one cause of kidney disease is uncontrolled diabetes, the second is uncontrolled blood pressure, and the third is the irrational abuse of painkillers. All three are preventable. Among patients on dialysis, a large share are there because of uncontrolled blood sugar, another large group because of uncontrolled blood pressure, and a smaller group — mostly women — because of painkiller abuse. Very few people genuinely need dialysis for reasons outside their control.

High blood pressure silently tears the filters

Think of the kidney's filter as a fine net with tiny holes, holding back pressure. Up to a reading of 120 it copes; around 130 it is straining; from 140 it starts to get damaged; and at 150, 160, 180, 190, 200 it bursts. Once that happens the kidney begins leaking protein, which shows up as froth in the urine and, on biopsy, as damaged filters that have simply given way. Uncontrolled high blood pressure causes target-organ damage across the body: the brain (stroke), the heart, the eyes (retinopathy) and the kidneys (nephropathy).

Hypertension is silent — you cannot know you have it unless you check. Many people also stay in denial and avoid medication. The response should not be pressure but patience: a home blood-pressure machine costing around ₹1,500, checked morning and evening, lets a person see the problem for themselves. Blood pressure should generally be kept below 130/80.

Do not stop medication because you suddenly feel unwell. A body accustomed to a reading of 180–190 will feel strange when it is first brought down toward 130, just as a heavy smoker feels bad on suddenly quitting. That discomfort is the body adjusting, not a sign the tablet does not suit you — a common reason people wrongly abandon treatment.

Diabetes turns the blood sticky

For the general public, diabetes usually means type 2, which in about 95% of cases arises from one's own habits — obesity leading to insulin resistance. Type 1, where the body makes no insulin, is not a matter of fault and must be managed with insulin.

Picture a glass of water. Two spoons of sugar dissolve and leave it clear — no one could tell. But 15 to 20 spoons in hot water turn it into a sticky syrup. High blood sugar does the same to blood, making it viscous. As sticky blood is pushed from the large arteries into ever-narrower arterioles, it can no longer pass — like a lorry blocking a residential lane — so oxygen and energy are cut off and the tissue beyond it dies. In the retina this dead area turns yellowish, and leaking vessels produce dots and blots of haemorrhage; diabetic retinopathy is the number one preventable cause of blindness. The same sticky blood reaching the feet produces the dark spots of diabetic dermopathy, and the same process is happening in the heart (heart attack), brain (stroke) and kidney — where it is called diabetic nephropathy, for which there is no cure, because the kidney was never at fault.

Controlling diabetes and the viscosity of the blood is therefore essential. The targets everyone should know: fasting blood sugar below 130; two hours after a meal below 180; a three-month average (HbA1c) below 7; blood pressure below 130/80; LDL (bad) cholesterol below 100; triglycerides below 150; and HDL (good) cholesterol above 40 to 50. Hit these and you are safe; miss them and the dialysis machine is waiting.

Kidney stones and how much water to drink

Everything has a natural state. Calcium's natural state is solid. The kidneys keep it dissolved in the blood and pass it out in urine. When there is too much calcium — or too little water — the urine becomes super-saturated: the kidneys can dissolve no more, so it settles at the bottom as a stone. Uric acid, whose natural state is also solid, behaves the same way. So a stone forms for one of two reasons: too much calcium or uric acid in the diet, or too little water to keep it dissolved.

There are only two ways to clear a stone. The first is to raise the water — drink plenty of it — which is the essence of medical (dissolution) therapy; warmer water dissolves it a little better. The second is surgery, to physically remove it. Prevention depends on knowing the stone type: for uric acid stones, cut down on uric-acid-rich food; for calcium oxalate stones, reduce oxalates — and in every case, drink plenty of water.

Which foods matter — and only for stone-formers

Calcium oxalate is the toughest stone to prevent because oxalates are almost everywhere in vegetables — spinach, potato, tomato seeds, almonds — so vegetarians are especially exposed and cannot avoid them completely. Counter-intuitively, taking a very low-calcium diet can be risky, because dietary calcium binds oxalate; normal calcium intake with plenty of water is better. Uric acid stones are mainly a problem for non-vegetarians, since meat and especially organ meats are high in uric acid, and anything protein eventually breaks down to it. Crucially, someone who does not form stones has no reason to avoid these foods and should not needlessly give them up.

Alkaline water and citrate

The body's pH sits around 7.35 to 7.45, averaging about 7.4, while ordinary bottled water is around 6.9. Mildly alkaline water (pH about 8.1–8.2) helps stone patients. The simplest way to alkalinise is a glass of water with lemon each morning: the citric acid becomes citrate in the body and nudges the pH up. Electrode-based ("Kangen") machines also produce alkaline water, separating out an acidic stream used for face-washing.

How much water — and how

There is no single correct amount. Requirements differ between men and women, adults and children, season to season, and by activity — a labourer outdoors needs far more than someone sitting in an air-conditioned room. The reliable guide is your urine colour: aim for urine that is clear and transparent, which means you are well hydrated; pale yellow is also normal and means you are on the borderline. Early-morning urine can be pale yellow simply because no one drinks while sleeping, but by afternoon and evening it should return to clear. Dark yellow, orange, strong-smelling or reddish urine means you are dehydrated — with a real risk of stones, as viscous blood gets stuck and muscles cramp. In a cool room around 18°C, one litre in 24 hours may be enough; in a desert, six litres or more may be needed. Being slightly over-hydrated is not harmful — the kidneys are wise enough — but under-hydration is bad.

Water temperature matters too. Very cold water (around 8°C) sits in the stomach until the body warms it to about 37°C, and very hot water must be cooled down, so both are absorbed slowly — which is why ice-cold water never truly quenches thirst. Room-temperature water, around 24–26°C, is absorbed fastest. Timing matters as well: the stomach holds roughly 750 ml, so about 500 ml at one time is the practical maximum, and it takes around an hour to be absorbed before the next round. Even 5 litres over a day is easily managed in about ten hours for someone with stones.

You can drink too much water. Following a rule mechanically — hourly alarms, forcing water when you are not thirsty — can cause water intoxication: the sodium level falls, brain cells swell, and function deteriorates. Anything good is only good within its limit. (One useful note the other way: drinking about 500 ml at once can raise metabolic rate by roughly 30%, so water genuinely helps with weight loss.)

Medicines, drinks and myths that harm the kidneys

Painkillers: the third great cause of failure

The commonly abused painkillers are non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) — aceclofenac, diclofenac, ibuprofen and others sold under many names. Their abuse is especially common among women who, exhausted after running a household, reach for a cheap strip from the local shop night after night. Over several years these drugs narrow the vessels, the blood supply to the kidney drops and the kidney shrinks — a condition called analgesic nephropathy. Medicine is both cure and poison, a double-edged sword; overdose is bad and even underdose may not help. A qualified doctor must always write the duration of a painkiller, and long-term use should never be encouraged. When pain is genuine — a fracture — of course take it; but tiredness calls for rest, not a tablet.

Antibiotics: monitor kidney function

Aminoglycosides such as amikacin and gentamicin are effective and cheap against urine infections but, used without monitoring, are toxic to both the kidneys and the ears. In one case a poor patient's creatinine was not checked to save cost, the antibiotic was continued, and by day five his kidneys had failed and he needed dialysis. The COVID era was a hard lesson: drugs such as remdesivir, amphotericin B and vancomycin can all harm the kidneys. Whenever such antibiotics are necessary, kidney function must be monitored — check your creatinine.

Acidity medicines (PPIs)

Proton pump inhibitors — pantoprazole, rabeprazole, omeprazole — are a major problem because of unregulated use. People eat acidic food, develop acidity or ulcers, and keep suppressing stomach acid for the long term. This is one cause of chronic interstitial nephritis, in which urine output stays reasonable but the kidneys shrink and age prematurely — a person of 35 can have kidneys resembling age 75. These drugs are now handed out by almost everyone rather than prescribed by doctors, and their use should be limited to a written, defined duration.

Alcohol, coffee and energy drinks

Alcohol is a diuretic, so it increases urine and can push toward dehydration, sometimes worsening stones. It does not fail the kidneys directly; instead it damages the liver, and liver failure drags the kidneys down with it — the kidney has intimate relationships with other organs (cardiorenal, hepatorenal and pulmorenal syndromes), so cirrhosis can lead to life-threatening hepatorenal syndrome.

Myth. Beer flushes out kidney stones. You may develop a beer belly, but stones do not wash out this way; at most, the extra fluid volume helps a little.

Coffee is also a diuretic and mildly dehydrating, and its caffeine can raise blood pressure — two routes by which it can harm the kidneys over time, so it is best not taken in large amounts. Green tea is high in oxalate, so the same caution applies. Far worse than coffee, though, are energy and soft drinks — described as the biggest addiction of the age, more harmful than alcohol or smoking. Rich in sugar and caffeine and consumed in unregulated amounts, they raise the risk of diabetes, heart attack, stroke and kidney disease, and their high caffeine can trigger dangerous heart rhythms. Their own labels warn against use by pregnant or lactating women and children, yet schoolchildren buy them freely; caffeine is otherwise a licensed drug a pharmacy cannot sell as a 100 mg tablet.

Detox drinks and "blood purifiers"

Myth. Detox juices and blood purifiers cleanse the kidneys. The kidney is itself the body's blood purifier, and it works like a drainage system — you cannot clean a drain by pouring more into it. Extra detox loads simply overload the kidney. Real "detox" means resting the kidney: drinking plain water and avoiding unnecessary excess protein, because the more waste the body produces, the greater the chance of it getting stuck.

Supplements and herbal remedies

A healthy person does not need protein supplements to stay healthy. A normal adult does well on roughly 1 to 1.5 grams of protein per kilogram from a balanced diet; supplements make sense for those with a genuine professional demand, such as competitive bodybuilders who may need around 2 g/kg, or ICU patients fed through a tube. Regular exercise — walking or higher-intensity activity at least five days a week — is important in itself and does not mandate supplements; over-dosing protein in someone who does not need it only burdens the kidney.

Myth. Herbal and natural remedies have no side effects. Anything that has a genuine effect can also have a side effect; something with truly no side effect has no effect at all. Traditional systems such as Ayurveda, naturopathy, Unani, Siddha and homoeopathy are respected, government-recognised sciences, and a patient who believes in a therapy may benefit partly through the placebo effect — the point is to make an informed choice, knowing these too carry risk.

Beware unregulated products laced with heavy metals. Homemade fairness creams containing mercury have caused membranous nephropathy — protein leaking from the kidney, confirmed on biopsy — in people who used them daily for a lighter complexion. Unregulated herbal and cosmetic products with hidden heavy metals are a real danger and should be avoided as far as possible.

Urinary tract infections: who is at risk

A urinary tract infection (UTI) divides into two types. An upper UTI is an infection inside the kidney (pyelonephritis) and is dangerous — usually seen in diabetics, in women, in people who hold their urine, and in those with kidney stones. It can cause kidney failure, may require a plastic stent (a DJ stent) to drain pus, and at its worst causes urosepsis with a dangerous drop in blood pressure, demanding intravenous antibiotics against aggressive bacteria such as E. coli and Klebsiella. A lower UTI is very common and comparatively milder; many women get one once or twice a year.

The main risk factors are uncontrolled blood sugar (the leading cause), being female, holding urine, and stones. School and college girls are especially affected: faced with dirty toilets they avoid drinking water and hold their urine all day, combining dehydration with prolonged holding. Urine is sterile, but if it is held for more than about six hours, bacteria begin to grow and multiply into an infection, and repeated holding thickens the bladder wall (cystitis), which is slow to resolve. The simplest prevention is to pass urine when needed and never hold it back.

Cranberry juice has been widely studied and genuinely helps prevent UTIs: its compounds coat the bladder lining so E. coli cannot stick and is flushed out instead. Roughly one to two glasses a week, or about 50 grams of cranberry daily, works as prevention — not as a treatment for an active infection. Where cranberry is hard to find, strawberries are an equally good and more available alternative.

Finish the full antibiotic course. A single dose may end the burning and foul smell, but that does not mean the infection is gone. An inadequate dose or duration lets the UTI return like a sleeper cell and breeds antibiotic resistance — cycling through stronger and stronger drugs toward a "superbug." With few new antibiotics in the pipeline, resistance is a serious, growing danger.

When kidneys fail: dialysis, transplant and donation

Dialysis is one of the great inventions of the century and has kept millions alive — but it is maintenance, not a cure or a solution. It is an artificial support, like living by candlelight when the power is out: workable, but the quality never matches the real thing. On dialysis, creatinine (normally about 1.0 in the blood) never comes back to normal and stays roughly between 5 and 15, so the still-impure blood slowly poisons the brain, heart and lungs; a 20-year-old on dialysis can resemble an 80-year-old. Survival falls sharply — of 100 people who start dialysis, only about 20 may be seen five years later.

A transplant, by contrast, restores life. Creatinine can fall to about 1.0 within two days, clean blood again reaches the brain, heart, lungs and liver, and performance returns close to normal — though never quite as it was before, like a car repaired after an accident. Success rates are high: of 100 transplants, about 98 do well at one year, 90 at five years, 80 at ten years and 50 at fifteen years — an average graft "half-life" of around fifteen years.

Coconut water and bananas are excellent — but dangerous for kidney patients. For healthy people they are a fine source of electrolytes, antioxidants and potassium, which even helps keep arteries supple and blood pressure low. But for a chronic kidney disease patient with a high creatinine, the potassium can rise dangerously (hyperkalaemia) and stop the heart. Such patients should stick to plain water. Interestingly, for kidney-stone patients coconut water is beneficial, as it alkalinises the urine — alongside mint water, sugarcane juice, barley water, cranberry and pineapple juice, which help flush stones.

Transplant also attracts illegal rackets, which demand stronger policing, ethics committees and more ethical surgeons and physicians. Beyond legality, a mismatched purchased kidney survives poorly: a well-matched kidney from a relative lasts far longer, whereas an unmatched organ is like fitting a cheap door to a luxury car. Donation is remarkably safe — the donor undergoes a full checkup and lives a normal life on one kidney; the first transplant was performed in 1954, and donor deaths are extremely rare. A donated kidney weighs about 150 grams and is now removed laparoscopically, with the donor walking by the second day and often discharged by the third.

People hesitate to donate for two reasons: resentment toward the recipient, and a mistaken fear that giving a kidney will leave them weak — which it does not. Deceased (cadaveric) donation faces a further hurdle of social and religious stigma in India, unlike in countries such as Spain where donation is the default. Brain death — where the brain will not recover although the heart still beats — is certified through a careful process (including an apnoea test) taking around 12 hours in some states, after which a consenting family can donate the organs. Just as people are eager to receive donated blood but reluctant to give it, organ donation needs the same shift in attitude, which will come with greater awareness.

Catching trouble early — and eight rules for kidney health

The biggest symptom of kidney disease is that it has no symptom.

Dr. Shoeb Khan, nephrologist

Because one kidney alone can sustain a normal life, no symptoms and a normal creatinine appear even after half of function is lost. Like a phone battery, warning signs usually begin only when about 15% is left, and serious symptoms appear when kidney function (GFR) falls to around 5 — by which point the blood is effectively poisoned, sending scattered signals from the skin, heart, lungs, brain and stomach. A few earlier clues are worth watching:

  • Persistent froth in the urine. The earliest specific sign is protein (albumin) leaking through a weakened filter. Normal froth clears on flushing; froth that remains means protein is leaking and the filter has widened.
  • Swelling, especially around the eyes. A sign the kidneys are weakening.
  • Falling performance. Do not simply blame it on ageing — get tested, as creatinine or blood pressure may be raised.

The kidney's "report card" has three parts, each worth checking once a year: structure, via an ultrasound (around ₹1,000, radiation-free, which also reveals stones, fatty liver, gallstones and more); function, via GFR calculated from creatinine (normal about 125, aim for around 100, with below 60 marking failure); and filter, via a simple urine analysis (around ₹100) showing protein, red blood cells, pus cells and crystals. Adults should do this yearly; for children, a blood-pressure check is enough.

Finally, the eight rules — drawn from WHO guidance — for protecting your kidneys:

  1. Drink enough water that your urine stays clear.
  2. Keep protein moderate. A vegetarian or South Indian diet tends to be around 0.5–0.6 g/kg per day and a non-vegetarian diet around 0.8–0.9 g/kg; the balanced Indian diet, with fibre, carbohydrate, protein and minerals, is among the best.
  3. Exercise — walk at least 30 minutes a day, or do higher-intensity activity, five days a week.
  4. Avoid addictions, above all soft drinks and energy drinks.
  5. Avoid unregulated painkillers.
  6. Sleep adequately, six to eight hours a night; rest is the best natural therapy.
  7. Check blood sugar and blood pressure once a year — every month if you are already diabetic or hypertensive.
  8. Do the kidney report card — structure, function and filter — at least once a year.

A closing note on treatment: the biggest everyday mistake patients make is not complying with prescriptions — often because they do not trust the doctor, which places the responsibility on the doctor's credibility and patience. Generic medicines are perfectly acceptable, but verify with your doctor that you have been given the right one. And diet change should be a gradual modification, not an extreme overhaul the whole family cannot sustain.
