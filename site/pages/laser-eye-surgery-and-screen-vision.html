Laser Eye Surgery, Screens and Vision Myths

Eye surgeon Dr. Rahil Chaudhary breaks down what modern screen habits are doing to our eyes, which widely-shared eye "remedies" are useless or harmful, and how today's vision-correction and cataract procedures work — with plain guidance on when a symptom is worth a doctor's visit.

At a glance

  • Short-form content and heavy screen use are driving a myopia (short-sightedness) "pandemic": roughly 60% of Indians now need glasses, and Dr. Chaudhary expects that to approach the 90% already seen in China, Korea, Singapore and Japan within a decade.
  • Many popular eye "fixes" do nothing: blue-light filter glasses, most eye exercises, cucumber slices, carrots as a cure, and washing the eyes repeatedly with water.
  • The single worst traditional habit, in his view, is putting kajal or surma in a child's eyes — no benefit, and a real risk of allergy and infection.
  • Vision-correction surgery (LASIK, SMILE, SILK, WaveLight, ICL) is described as painless, quick and among the safest of all procedures — but it is not a one-size-fits-all treatment, and 20–30% of people still need glasses.
  • Cataract is universal: everyone who lives long enough develops it, and surgery replaces the clouded natural lens with an artificial one.
  • Glaucoma is the "silent thief of sight" — symptomless, irreversible once vision is lost, and best caught by an annual check-up after 40.
  • Some threats cause permanent blindness: methanol in illicit liquor, acid injuries, and staring at the sun or strong concert lasers.
  • The recurring advice: don't self-medicate or copy YouTube remedies — get an eye examination and let a qualified doctor find the cause.

Screens, myopia and everyday eye strain

Nature built the human eye for distance vision, Dr. Chaudhary explains. When you look at a screen, the eye's muscles must work continuously to focus on a near object; hold that near-focus for hours and the eye adapts, and in young eyes a spectacle number (short-sightedness) sets in. He calls this part of a global "myopia pandemic" driven by excessive screen use — accelerated sharply after Covid, when people were "glued" to their screens.

The numbers he cites are stark. In countries such as China, Korea, Singapore and Japan, almost 90% of people now carry a spectacle number. In India it is around 60%, and he expects it to reach roughly 90% over the next decade. When he was a schoolchild, perhaps 20–30% of a class wore glasses; he now expects that to become the large majority. He dates the steep rise to around 2020, when short-form reels took off, and says cases have grown "exponentially" between 2020 and 2025 — naming short content as one of the major culprits, because it keeps viewers hooked for hours.

Air-conditioning and air quality

Air-conditioning lowers a room's humidity, and low humidity produces dry eyes — more so if the AC air blows directly onto the eyes. If the unit is positioned so the air does not hit the eyes and you have no dry-eye problem, he considers it harmless, and sleeping in AC does not affect eye health. Pollution and high AQI cause two main problems: dryness, because particulate matter disturbs the tear film (the outermost layer of the eye), and allergies. Children are more sensitive than adults, though both are affected, and he sees these complaints surge in his clinic when pollution is high.

The 20-20-20 rule. For anyone spending prolonged hours on a screen, every 20 minutes take a 20-second break and look at something 20 feet away. Looking into the distance relaxes the focusing muscles that near-work keeps contracted, easing strain and headaches. Because concentration also reduces blinking — which stops tears spreading properly and causes dryness — he recommends using a basic lubricating eye drop a few times a day during screen work.

Distance, brightness and font size

Keep any screen at roughly one arm's length, ideally working on a larger desktop screen with an increased font size — the bigger the font, the less the strain. He is unconvinced by an absolute "safe distance" formula (such as doubling the screen's diagonal): an arm's length is comfortable and practical, and holding a screen far enough to be "safe" would defeat the purpose. On brightness, the goal is a comfortable level: setting brightness to zero forces the eyes to strain and lean in, which is unhealthy, while overly bright settings are also not recommended. Reading in dim light will not damage the eyes but will cause uncomfortable strain.

Blue-light glasses: what they do and don't do

Blue-light filter glasses and zero-power "computer" glasses have, in his account, no scientific benefit for the eyes — a point he attributes to published research from the American Academy of Ophthalmology. The idea that screen blue light damages the eyes is a myth; far more blue light comes from being outdoors in sunlight. What blue light genuinely affects is sleep: at night, screen blue light suppresses melatonin and disturbs the circadian rhythm, so blocking it can help you sleep. But he adds that this is now handled by the night-mode or "orange" blue-light filters built into phones — switch those on and you don't need separate filter glasses. (A red bedroom lamp, by contrast, has no established link to sleep; that connection is to blue light, not red.)

Myths and habits to unlearn

Kajal and surma in children's eyes

The worst traditional habit. There is no advantage to it, Dr. Chaudhary says; the belief that it sharpens sight, enlarges the eyes or wards off illness is superstition (the "nazar" or evil-eye belief). Applied repeatedly, it causes allergies, and sharing a kajal stick between children historically spread trachoma, an eye infection — now nearly eliminated but once common precisely because of shared sticks. If protection from the evil eye is the concern, he suggests a mark elsewhere rather than the child's eyes.

Eye make-up

Eyeliners and kajal can contain chemicals, nylon and rayon fibres; sensitive people react with red, itchy, puffy or swollen eyes. He sees this often in very young women who applied make-up overnight and woke with swollen lids. His advice is not about budget versus premium products but about individual sensitivity: if a product does not suit you, change its composition or brand and stay away from it. Crucially, remove eye make-up thoroughly at the end of the day, or the residual chemicals can cause long-term dryness and allergies.

Washing the eyes with tap water

In India, tap water can be contaminated and introduce infection; in developed countries where tap water is filtered it is safer. If you must wash the eyes, use drinking water. He also warns against the habit of washing the eyes repeatedly through the day in the belief it keeps them clean — over time this washes out the eye's natural oils and can cause chronic dryness. The eye's own tears already wash it; no additional rinsing is needed for eye health.

Rubbing the eyes

Excessive eye-rubbing is genuinely dangerous: it can reshape the round cornea into a cone, a condition called keratoconus (kerato = cornea, conus = cone). It does not look obviously abnormal from outside, but vision becomes distorted, glare appears, the spectacle number changes and quality of life suffers. Children with untreated eye allergies who rub constantly are also at risk.

Foods and "natural" cures

There is no superfood for the eyes. Carrots, papaya, coloured fruits, leafy greens and nuts are generally healthy — some contain nutrients such as lutein and zeaxanthin that are good for the eyes and may delay some diseases — but they are not a cure or treatment for eye disease. A spectacle number, once present (whether −0.75 or −5), cannot be reduced to zero by any food, drop, yoga, massage, herbal remedy, walking on grass or kajal; if it must be corrected, the only route is a surgical procedure. Vitamin A is the one real dietary link: a deficiency causes night blindness, and this specific problem can be prevented (or, if diagnosed, treated with a proper medicinal dose) rather than by eating carrots in bulk.

Eye exercises and massage

For a healthy eye, the figure-of-eight and "look this way, look that way" routines shared on YouTube are useless — no benefit at all, mostly "feel-good" videos trading on fear of the unknown; they do no harm but confer no advantage. Genuine, prescribed exercises do exist for specific weak-muscle problems: pencil push-ups, cat-card and Brock-string exercises can strengthen convergence muscles when weak focusing muscles fatigue and cause strain or blurring. Daily eye massage or vigorous rubbing, however, is dangerous for the keratoconus reason above.

Wearing someone else's glasses won't raise your number. Borrowing another person's spectacles will not permanently harm the eyes or increase your number. It does cause temporary strain and headache, because it disturbs the eye's focusing mechanism and the eye muscles tire trying — and failing — to correct the focus.

Red eyes, dark circles and what symptoms signal

Red eye and conjunctivitis

Redness comes from dilated, engorged blood vessels carrying more blood to the surface. A drop containing naphazoline constricts those vessels and instantly whitens the eye — but this is symptomatic relief, not a cure. Conjunctivitis itself can be bacterial, viral, fungal or allergic, and each must be correctly diagnosed and specifically treated; repeatedly reaching for a whitening drop only masks the problem, and chronic use brings tolerance, then inflammation, allergy and dryness. Allergic conjunctivitis is becoming very common because of pollution and high AQI.

"Eye flu" is a viral conjunctivitis — highly contagious, spreading through fomites when a person touches an infected eye and then a surface or another person's hand. Anyone with a red, infective eye should keep their distance, maintain hygiene, wash hands, avoid handshakes and not share towels.

Dark circles

Dark circles have many possible causes: dehydration, insufficient sleep, fatigue, excess caffeine, alcohol or smoking, a nutritional deficiency, an eye allergy, naturally thin under-eye skin, or heavy sun exposure. Because the message differs from person to person, the fix depends on the cause — pigment or thin skin may respond to a little laser or a de-pigmenting cream; sun-driven darkness to sunglasses and sunscreen; sleep or nutritional causes to addressing those directly. The recurring advice: see an expert to analyse the cause rather than self-treating from YouTube. Cucumber slices offer only a mild soothing and hydrating effect — no real role in removing dark circles.

Don't self-diagnose vision changes. The eye is an optical system, so almost any eye disease first shows up as blurred vision — but the cause could be a spectacle number, cataract, diabetic retinopathy, ARMD, glaucoma and more. Blurring, pain, watering, burning, itching or redness are the body signalling that something is wrong; the specific reason must be found, because the treatment for each differs entirely. Asking a friend what they used, or buying a drop over a pharmacy counter, is the wrong approach.

Age-related conditions: cataract, presbyopia and glaucoma

Cataract — universal and inevitable

Everyone who lives long enough develops cataract, Dr. Chaudhary stresses — like grey hair or wrinkles, it is a change of ageing, as the lens proteins change and the lens loses transparency. The only difference is timing: it may arrive at 40, 60, 80 or 90. Its usual age is after 60, but smoking (which he calls one of the biggest reasons for early cataract), heavy sun exposure, an unhealthy lifestyle, diabetes and genetics are pushing onset into the 40s and 50s. Early signs to watch for include blurred or "foggy" vision as if a veil has descended, difficulty in low light, a changing spectacle number, and glare while driving at night — an oncoming truck's headlights feeling "blinding." These build up very gradually.

Presbyopia — the reading-glasses number

The need for reading glasses around 40–45 is presbyopia (not hypermetropia, though the two are often used interchangeably and both add a "plus" number). The lens inside the eye is naturally flexible: distance muscles relax and the lens thins for far vision, while for near work the muscles thicken the lens to focus diverging light. By about 40 that flexibility fades, so near objects no longer focus and a supportive plus-power reading correction is needed. This happens to everyone. People who already have a minus (short-sighted) number develop it later, or barely notice it, because the existing minus partly neutralises the emerging plus — which is also why someone who has had SMILE or similar surgery (bringing the eye to zero) will need reading glasses at the normal age, having lost that incidental advantage.

Glaucoma — the silent thief of sight

Glaucoma (kala motia) is dangerous precisely because it has no symptoms or signs: the patient does not realise anything is wrong. It slowly kills peripheral vision while the centre stays clear, so by the time interference is noticed and a doctor diagnoses it, significant vision has already gone — and what is lost cannot be restored, only what remains can be saved. Biologically, fluid is continuously produced and drained inside the eye, maintaining a pressure of about 15–20 mmHg. If production rises or drainage falls, pressure builds and damages the optic nerve, whose fibres slowly die. It is more common after 40 and runs in families, so from around 40 — or earlier with a family history — get checked yearly. Treatment is simple: a pressure-lowering drop at night, taken lifelong, or surgical correction. He frames a routine annual eye check after 40 as essential, alongside conditions such as diabetic retinopathy and age-related macular degeneration (ARMD) that also cluster after 40.

Removing your glasses: LASIK, SMILE and ICL

A spectacle number arises because the eye elongates as it grows (the eye is about one inch; roughly every 1 mm of elongation adds about −3 of power). Surgery does not shrink the eye. Laser procedures (LASIK) instead use a laser to reshape the front surface — the cornea, or transparent "black" of the eye — removing a little tissue to change its curvature so light again focuses on the retina. The lens is untouched; the surgery corrects the eye's focusing mechanism at the cornea.

The procedure itself

Modern refractive surgery is, in his description, a "10-minute walk-in, walk-out procedure": no pain, no injection, no stitch, no blade, no cut, no flap, no bandage and no hospitalisation. Only numbing drops are used; light falls on the eye for about 10 seconds, vision is clear by the next morning, and normal activity resumes in about three days. A device called an eye speculum gently holds the lids open so you cannot blink, and you simply keep looking at a fixation point (a balloon or a green light, depending on the machine). The process is around 90% automated — the doctor positions the patient and performs small steps, but automation is what delivers the precision a human hand cannot. The doctor remains present and talking to the patient throughout.

On the "Final Destination" fear. Dr. Chaudhary is emphatic that the dramatised surgery scene many people fear is the opposite of reality — an exaggerated, fear-inducing depiction. These procedures are bloodless (the cornea has no blood vessels — the same reason LASIK is bloodless), painless, and among the safest of all surgeries. Anxiety is natural because the eye is delicate; for very anxious patients, an anti-anxiety tablet or brief sedation is available. He counts refractive surgery among the highest anxiety-reducing procedures once done — many patients' only regret is not having done it sooner.

Choosing between laser types — and ICL

LASIK is not a one-size-fits-all treatment; it has many variants (aspheric, wavefront-guided, topography-guided, ray-tracing-guided), plus lenticule-based procedures (SMILE, SILK) and surface procedures (PRK, epi-based). The correct choice depends on the patient's reports, which is why he urges going to a facility that offers all options. Laser has limits: it generally cannot correct numbers above about 8, cannot be done on a cornea that is already thin (laser thins it further), can worsen pre-existing dry eye, and is unsafe on an irregular or conical (keratoconus) cornea. In all these cases, an implantable lens — the ICL — is placed inside the eye instead, adding the power of your glasses without removing anything. This differs from cataract surgery, which removes a clouded natural lens; ICL simply adds a lens. It too is painless, uses only drops, and gives clear vision the next day.

ICL is very popular in Japan, where roughly 90% of correction is done this way — a legacy of unqualified cosmetic centres botching early laser cases and instilling public fear of lasers. In India the split is roughly 60% laser to 40% ICL. He names Rakul Preet Singh, Priyanka Chopra and Nick Jonas among those reported to have chosen ICL.

Super-vision and pricing

A newer LASIK technology he calls WaveLight Plus InnoVize uses artificial intelligence and ray tracing to correct the eye's optical irregularities beyond a normal 6/6 (20/20) — potentially to 6/5, 6/4 or even 6/3, so a person can see at 12 feet what a normal person sees at 6. He is candid that this "eagle-eye" enhancement has no real practical advantage for someone with normal vision (barring a niche case like a hunter or bird-watcher), and that operating on an eye that already sees a perfect 6/6 purely to gain "super-vision" is not recommended and should not be done. As a rough guide to cost, he cites approximately ₹1.4 lakh (both eyes) for WaveLight, ₹1.2 lakh for SILK, ₹90,000 for Contoura and ₹40,000 for standard LASIK. His consumer advice is not to economise on this once-in-a-lifetime procedure: choose the best technology available.

The largest number he has corrected in one patient was −32.5 (for which he holds a Limca record), in a woman of about 24; thick glasses at that power also cause a "minification" effect, making the world look smaller even with correction.

Ortho-K and contact lenses

For those who cannot have surgery — under-18s, or people who swim or play cricket — there are non-surgical options. Ortho-K uses a specific hard lens worn overnight that temporarily alters the corneal shape, so for 8–10 hours the next day the person sees without glasses. The alternative is ordinary contact lenses, which Dr. Chaudhary frankly dislikes for regular long-term wear. They feel wonderful at first, but the cornea has no blood vessels and takes its oxygen directly from the air; a contact lens sitting on top compromises that exchange, causing corneal changes and oedema. Worn daily for years, they lead many patients to chronic redness, itching, allergy, watering, dryness and foreign-body sensation — indeed a common reason patients come in for LASIK. Occasional wear is fine, and daily-disposable lenses with good hygiene are far better than monthly lenses.

Cataract surgery and specialised eye procedures

Cataract surgery removes the clouded natural lens and implants an artificial one (an intraocular lens). Like LASIK, the technique has advanced through generations — from the older large-incision, stitched ECCE, to sutureless phaco and MICS, to AI-based robotic cataract surgery, and in 2025 to a system he refers to as "Unity" robotic cataract surgery. He frames the whole progression as a question of energy: a hardened, whitened lens must be broken up and removed, and every advance reduces the energy delivered into the eye to achieve that — protecting the surrounding tissue and making the procedure safer and gentler. It is already painless (done under drops); the newer goal is minimum energy for the same result. In the modern method the lens is softened to a semi-liquid state and suctioned out through an opening so small it self-seals, needing no stitch.

Two choices drive both the outcome and the cost: the technology (phaco, AI-robotic or Unity) and the lens design. A monofocal lens gives clear distance vision but needs reading glasses for near work; an "extended depth of focus" lens adds intermediate (mobile/computer) range; and a trifocal or multifocal lens covers near, intermediate and distance for effectively full freedom from glasses — restoring, he says, an "18-year-old's" vision. High-end cataract surgery with a premium lens can run around ₹1.5 lakh per eye (and up to ₹1.75–1.8 lakh), while basic cataract surgery is available free through camps and charitable institutions in India.

Sub-specialties within eye care

Ophthalmology has branched into seven or eight super-specialties, he notes: cataract and refractive; glaucoma; retina (including diabetic retinopathy); paediatric (squint and children's eye problems); oculoplasty; and neuro-ophthalmology (the eye's nerve). Oculoplasty is plastic surgery of the eye — correcting a drooping lid (ptosis), tear-drainage problems, and cosmetic work including Botox and "cat-eye" lifts. Eye injuries are generally very safe to treat, but every surgery carries its own set of complications weighed against its benefits.

Eyelashes, eyebrows and lumps

Eyebrows and eyelashes exist for function, not just looks — the hairs act as a filter against dust entering from above or the side. Eyelash oils such as castor oil are popular; special prostaglandin drops can genuinely produce fuller lashes, but should not be used continuously for years, as any drop has long-term side effects. He warns against mascara and, especially, lash extensions: gluing material onto the natural lashes can block the meibomian gland openings that secrete the oil layer protecting the tear film, leading to dry eye. A stye is a painful, pimple-like bacterial infection at the root (follicle) of an eyelash; a chalazion is a painless lump from a blocked oil duct where oil collects. Styes are linked to hand-to-eye contact (one reason hand-washing matters for eye health) and to eye-rubbing during screen strain.

Injuries and hazards that can cost your sight

Methanol in illicit liquor

Alcohol harms the whole body, including the eyes. The specific, catastrophic danger is illicit "country" or moonshine liquor: ordinary alcohol is ethanol, but poorly-made liquor can contain methanol, which is toxic. Methanol poisoning damages the optic nerve — to which it is particularly sensitive — and can cause permanent blindness. Patients arriving after such consumption have a characteristic presentation, and the vision, once lost, does not return.

Chemical and physical injuries

Acid — as dramatised in the film Gangaajal — melts the tissues it contacts, causing a painful, torturous blindness. Dr. Chaudhary sees real cases from industrial settings (sulphuric or hydrochloric acid) and treats them by neutralising with bases, washing, and further treatment; a large splash can be impossible to recover from. Eye emergencies more broadly include trauma (a blow, ball or punch), chemical splashes (acid or alkali), road-traffic accidents, and firecracker or spark injuries around festivals — each handled according to its cause. Well-fitting protective goggles work very well because they keep a filter in front of the eyes.

Sunlight, welding and lasers

Staring directly at the sun — whether during a misguided "sun-gazing" habit or an eclipse — can burn the retina, causing solar retinopathy: a permanent dark shadow in the vision that cannot be treated (the same reason a camera must not be pointed at the sun, which damages the sensor). Welding light injures the eye similarly. Strong lasers at concerts and DJ events can damage the retina if they enter the eye directly for even a few seconds, so avoid looking toward them; a brief flicker is not a problem, but sustained exposure can cause harm.

Cannabis and whitening drops

Cannabis (ganja/bhang) turns the eyes red through blood-vessel dilation and congestion, but has little direct effect on eyesight from occasional use; chronic, regular use harms the eyes (and much else). The visible redness is simply what is happening throughout the body, made obvious in the eyes. People use naphazoline-type "eye-cool" drops to constrict those vessels and restore a white appearance — but, as with any chemical instilled daily, regular use brings tolerance and eventually inflammation, allergy and dryness.

Sunglasses earn their keep. Beyond comfort against glare in sun, snow or by water, UV-protective sunglasses reduce sun-driven dark circles and cut the UV exposure that brings on early cataract and age-related macular degeneration. A comfortably-fitting eye mask for sleep is fine, but one worn too tight can put pressure on the cornea overnight and cause temporary blurring on waking — the cornea regains its normal shape once the pressure is removed.

Eye care across a lifetime

Children

Regular examinations matter most in childhood, because many eye problems can only be sorted out then. Dr. Chaudhary advises checks at birth, at about 3 years, and at 5 years, then yearly from 8 to 18 while the body grows and the number changes. Several conditions must be caught early or vision is compromised for life: amblyopia (lazy eye), where an uncorrected number or a congenital cataract means the eye never learns to see clearly and stays limited once development completes around 8–9 years; retinopathy of prematurity, which must be diagnosed and treated at birth; and squint. Warning signs a parent can watch for include a child who cannot see the blackboard, is falling behind academically, squints or narrows the eyes to see, sits very close to the TV, has redness, itching, watering, a whiteness in the eye, a turned eye, or bumps into things. A five-minute check with an eye doctor — increasingly done in schools using a Snellen chart — can settle it; the right specialist is a paediatric eye doctor.

Adults

From roughly 20 to 40, the number usually stabilises and problems are fewer — a relative window. Refractive surgery requires a minimum age of 18, a stable number (a small quarter-diopter shift is acceptable, as checking itself carries error), no other eye problem, no pregnancy or breastfeeding, and no medicines such as steroids or immunosuppressants; after thorough tests the doctor decides which procedure — if any — suits. He tells patients plainly that these procedures benefit only about 70–80% of people; 20–30% must continue wearing glasses, and forcing an unsuitable candidate into surgery is exactly how complications and regret arise. From 40 onward, return to an annual check-up for cataract, glaucoma, diabetic retinopathy and ARMD.

Choosing a doctor, and avoiding quacks

Because LASIK has become a "household word," unqualified operators ("quacks," or jholachhap) also perform eye surgery, and a botched case then spreads the false impression that "the surgery itself is bad." His guidance: go to a facility where every option is available (so the doctor can choose what suits you), check the surgeon's credibility and qualifications (in ophthalmology the relevant degree is an MS — "Master of Surgery" — built on MBBS, then super-specialty and fellowship training), and take a second and even third opinion, looking for the opinions to match. He closes on a note of reassurance about access in India: doctors are readily available and consultations can cost as little as ₹50, ₹20 or nothing at charitable clinics — so if there is an eye problem, see a professional rather than self-medicating.

On the future. Dr. Chaudhary is optimistic about emerging technology, while careful to mark it as unproven. Eye transplantation has historically replaced only the cornea, but he notes that a full eyeball transplant (using a donor eye) has now been performed for the first time, with encouraging early results. He also points to Neuralink-type brain interfaces — still in FDA trials — which could bypass a diseased eye entirely by sending a camera's signal directly to the brain's visual cortex, potentially treating forms of blindness that are untreatable today. These are described as hopes and early trials, not available treatments.
