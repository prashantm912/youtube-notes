Eating Less, Eating Right: Portions, Cravings and the Foods Quietly Harming Us

Sports and celebrity nutritionist Ryan Fernando argues that most modern illness is a slow verdict of how much and how carelessly we eat. His method: eat less, learn your own body, and treat every plate as a question — is this worthy to enter me?

At a glance

  • The human race is simply eating too much. In earlier centuries we ate less and died of communicable disease; today we die of lifestyle diseases, of which food is the true villain.
  • There is no single "healthy" diet. Lean, muscular, fat and acne-prone bodies all exist; conforming to someone else's version of health — especially a camera-ready celebrity's — is a false measure.
  • Portion control comes before food choice. A rough guide: eat to the size of your closed fist, three times a day, and match your intake to your body and how much you actually move.
  • Gluten and dairy are the two foods most often quietly causing trouble — acne, bloating, fatigue, gut damage. The fix is an elimination trial, not a lifelong verdict handed down without testing.
  • It is not "which oil" but how much oil. One gram of oil is nine calories; the copious quantities used for taste are the problem.
  • Sleep is the new diet. Sleep 7–8 hours for 45 days straight and watch junk-food cravings fall away.
  • Cravings are a signal — of stress, poor sleep, a disrupted gut, or a vitamin and mineral deficiency — not a moral failing. Fix the root and 99% of cravings disappear.
  • Your body is the most expensive real estate you will ever own. Most people live in it like tenants, not landlords.

Why we eat too much

Fernando's central diagnosis is quantity. The Industrial Revolution pushed humanity to a point where you either have enough money to eat or you do not, and disposable income has grown so much that food is now a smaller share of spending than it once was. He points to India's consumption surveys: in 1991 roughly 56–58 rupees out of every 100 went to food; by 2022 that had dropped to about 40 rupees. Of that 40, around 10 rupees 80 paise now goes to processed foods and beverages — the junk — while only about 3 rupees 80 paise each goes to fruit and to vegetables, and spending on pulses and legumes has fallen 5–6% since 1991. India is eating more, but the wrong food is rising and the nutritious food is falling.

The cultural roots run deep. For most of the last several centuries food was scarce — failing crops, famines, invaders — and we were healthier for eating less. As wealth grew, it was displayed either as jewellery or as food: feeding a guest generously became a show of prosperity, love and gratitude. Leaving food on the plate became an insult, because others had gone without. Fernando's reading is that hosts never registered a guest's actual capacity to eat, and food became the "Maharani" invited into the house who was never told when to stop.

He adds an analysis of the "eat for two" tradition given to pregnant women. With historically high child mortality during pregnancy and childbirth, encouraging an expectant mother to eat a little more was a hedge for the family's survival — a message grandmothers then amplified. He is careful to flag this as his own interpretation rather than something from a textbook.

The stakes are national. Fernando warns that rising diabetes, hypertension and cancer will burden the economy: a country with the youngest population could also carry the highest lifestyle-disease load — "the sickest youngest population" — dragging down GDP through medical and hospital costs.

How much to eat: portion control first

Before what to eat, Fernando insists people learn how much. He frames the body's energy need through basal metabolic rate (BMR) — the "engine idling speed" that burns fuel even when you are standing still at a traffic signal. A bigger engine needs more petrol: a heavier or more muscular person needs more, a lighter or sedentary person needs less. Because giving an exact serving size is genuinely difficult without measurement, he offers a simple visual rule.

Eat to your heart's content — and do you know the size of your heart? Close your fist. That is the amount you put on your plate, three times a day.

Ryan Fernando

The catch, he notes, is that fist-sized does not mean calorie-safe: a small piece of a rich sweet can be dense with calories and near-zero in nutrition, while cucumber at the same weight is barely 20 calories — though even three cages of cucumber can upset the gut. So portion and quality both matter. He also blames commercial serving sizes: a restaurant thali or biryani portion is built by a business to feed you and stay profitable, not by your mother for your body, and the "don't waste food" rule keeps us finishing it.

At home, the quiet danger is the second and third helping taken without thinking. His childhood model was food served at the table and then cleared, rather than left within arm's reach for endless refills. His self-check for whether portions are creeping up: track your weight year on year — through college and each year of your job — and ask honestly whether it keeps climbing.

Reading a typical Indian thali

Fernando walks through a common thali — rice, roti/paratha, sabzi, palak paneer, dal, fried batata vada, gulab jamun, onion, and a glass of chaas — starting with a single question: who are you? A labourer or delivery worker climbing stairs in the sun would need it all. Most podcast viewers are "desk jockeys" using brain and fingers, not muscles, so the same plate is too much carbohydrate. His edits for a sedentary person, or one in weight-loss mode:

To measure progress he favours a scale that reports body-fat and visceral-fat percentage and a "metabolic age," not just weight. He is wary of BMI as a blunt height-to-weight ratio, preferring fat percentage — an Indian meal, he suggests, should sit around 15–20% fat for men and 20–25% for women, with alarm bells at roughly 25% body fat in men and 35% in women. Having seen visceral fat on a heart during a medical-college autopsy — yellow-to-black and, in his words, ugly — he uses these numbers to make the invisible visible.

Gluten and dairy: the sensitivities most people miss

Fernando's own story anchors this. He suffered adult acne, bloating and irritable-bowel symptoms until, around 2012–13, genetic and food testing showed him to be lactose- and gluten-intolerant. As a Goan raised on fresh-baked bread, he had noticed his skin clearing whenever bread was unavailable — on camp, or while studying in Scotland — but never connected the dots until a structured four-week elimination trial dried up his pimples and stopped new ones. Reintroducing bread brought loose motions and a fresh pimple; the pattern was unmistakable. Novak Djokovic's account of discovering his own gluten sensitivity, published around the same time, reinforced the link between gluten and both skin and athletic fatigue.

He positions himself not as an anti-wheat crusader but as a judge weighing each food for each person: gluten "makes its case," and testing decides whether it is the culprit or innocent. He is emphatic that no one is paid by a "wheat lobby" or "milk lobby," and that culture — what grandmother taught you to eat — is the hardest thing to change, which is exactly why he argues for eating scientifically.

Three levels, not one

  • Gluten intolerance — eating gluten produces a clear, painful reaction: loose motions, headache, even a bleeding nose over a few days. This is his own case.
  • Gluten sensitivity — subtler: you stop gluten and simply feel better, lighter, more energetic, without ever having named a "problem."
  • Celiac disease — a severe allergy where gluten can lead to hospitalisation or, at worst, anaphylactic shock. Fernando notes India's celiac population is relatively small.

How gluten damages the gut lining

He explains "leaky gut" through a security metaphor. Gluten is a large protein — a "VIP" molecule — that the body struggles to break down. It calls up zonulin, a molecule in the gut that begins prying open the gut lining, letting gluten pass while other nutrients queue normally. Repeated over years, these gaps widen, so partially undigested food molecules slip into the bloodstream. The immune system, meeting whole molecules where it expected broken-down ones, escalates to a permanent "red alert" — which is one route to autoimmune conditions doctors may label without a clear cause. He references autoimmune and genetic conditions (rheumatoid arthritis, alopecia areata, HLA-B27-linked ankylosing spondylitis, IBS) as reasons not to keep the immune system needlessly inflamed by food it reacts to.

The practical move is an elimination trial. Fernando repeatedly recommends keeping a food diary and eliminating a suspect food for a set period — often about 20 days up to 90 days — then observing skin, gut and energy. His rationale for ~20 days: the gut lining turns over roughly every 11 days, so the food must stop long enough to let one cycle heal and a second confirm it. Testing (blood, genetic, microbiome) makes the verdict precise, but he stresses that people without any symptoms need not test — "enjoy life," and investigate food only if a problem appears.

Oil, sugar and artificial sweeteners

Oil: quantity, not type

Reframe. The debate over which oil is "good" misses the point, Fernando argues — the real culprit is how much oil we now use. Ten thousand years ago food was hunted, grown and roasted on stone; extracting oil from seeds for tadka, basting and frying is only two or three centuries old. One gram of oil is nine calories, and the copious amounts used to create a rich mouth-feel are what harm us. He does concede genuine individual variation — his own genetic testing suggests he responds poorly to monounsaturated oils (olive, peanut, groundnut) and better to sunflower and safflower — but the headline advice is to cut the volume: use an air fryer, oil sprays, better cookware, even the rural "garam-paani tadka" that tempers spices in water instead of oil. He predicts that oil and ghee packets will one day carry a health warning much like cigarettes.

Consumption of ghee and oil in copious quantities is injurious to your health.

Sugar: start with children

Fernando's sugar strategy begins at the source: he did not let his son taste sugar until age three, and disagrees with the tradition of feeding infants oil and salt early. His argument is about thresholds — introduce sweet and salt too young and a child develops cravings and a rising tolerance, especially urban children who no longer sweat all day in the sun and so need less added salt. Once a child gets the "nasha" (addiction) of a chips packet — engineered by food scientists to fire every pleasure centre — it is very hard to undo. For teenagers he advises education about excess sugar and sharing one dessert among friends. For adults, a three-month "detox" from sugar resets the palate, so that far less tastes plenty sweet.

Artificial sweeteners

Suspect. The jury is still out, Fernando says, but he is wary. A paper or two suggest sweeteners affect the gut microbiome, and on his own continuous glucose monitor certain sweeteners made his body perceive sweetness and release sugar from the liver in anticipation — a feedback loop with no actual sugar present. His rule of thumb: molecules not found in nature — acesulfame, sucralose, aspartame — he treats with suspicion; those found in nature — monk fruit, stevia — he is more comfortable with. Better still, address why the craving exists at all: it is often a magnesium, vitamin B12 or B6 deficiency, or stress and hormonal imbalance. Correct those and, in his experience, cravings fall away.

Sleep, breathing and the craving cycle

Cravings, in Fernando's telling, are physiological messages rather than weakness. Ten thousand years ago a sweet craving sent you hunting for fruit; today it is answered by a button on a delivery app, and the modern diet is already nutrient-poor from farming practices, so the body is on the back foot before stress even hits.

He links overeating to breathing. Shallow, short breaths reach only the top of the lungs and keep the sympathetic ("fight or flight") nervous system switched on, raising heart rate and pushing the brain to seek more fuel; a deep sigh reaches the bottom of the lungs and engages the calming parasympathetic system. Poor sleep compounds this: research he cites shows people who slept badly crave more sugar the next day, because faster heart rate reads, evolutionarily, as "a tiger is chasing me" — except, as he puts it, the tiger is Netflix.

Fact — his strongest single takeaway. Sleep is the new diet. Fernando's challenge: sleep 7–8 hours for 45 continuous days and see whether junk-food cravings stop.

He adds a nuance from a sleep study (he attributes it to Uppsala University in Scandinavia): junk food did not change how long people slept, but it degraded sleep quality. The deep, slow-wave sleep (SWS) that occurs in the first three hours of the night is when growth hormone is secreted, feeding stem-cell repair — what popular language calls "beauty sleep." Alcohol and junk food eaten at night cut into that SWS. His personal workaround is to place a cheat meal on a Thursday or Friday before a heavy leg workout, protecting Saturday and Sunday sleep. He frames the weekend "off-roading" — partying and eating badly — as damaging the one vehicle you can never replace.

Smarter snack swaps

The classic danger window is around 5 p.m.: evolution primes us to eat before sundown, work stress spikes brain glucose demand, and delivery apps make oversized portions one tap away. Fernando notes a 500 p.m. snack is often 300–400 calories, while an hour's walk burns only about 180 — so this is where weight battles are lost. His general defences are to drink more water and electrolytes, check for magnesium deficiency and insulin resistance, and pre-portion a homemade trail mix (salted pistachios with raisins) into a zip-lock so you don't eat five days' worth in one stressed sitting. Below are his run-through of common cravings and their swaps.

Two behavioural tricks recur. For sweet or fried cravings he suggests chewing gum or brushing teeth after a meal — clearing food from the teeth and the act of chewing both signal satiety to the brain (the traditional reason drivers and film crews chew supari/gutka to suppress hunger). And he ties craving control back to exercise: only about 3% of urban India works out, yet a workout releases endorphins and shifts the internal dialogue so strongly that the freshly exercised brain itself starts refusing junk — "you are not worthy to enter my body." He recommends consistency, roughly five days a week, over sporadic effort.

Supplements, fasting and reader questions

Supplements: convenience, not a cure

Fernando's line is that "supplements are a nutritional convenience for your nutritional indiscipline" — indiscipline you either chose (poor eating) or had thrust on you (food that is less nutrient-dense than a generation ago). Ideally a doctor governs supplementation, but he notes doctors rarely prescribe something like magnesium for cramps, tight muscles or sugar cravings, so knowing your own blueprint through testing helps you target what to take. He warns equally against self-prescription: too much zinc, for instance, can switch the immune system off, so there is an evolutionary "right amount" best obtained from food. Having once headed marketing for two large supplement brands, he is pointedly pro-food, treating supplement and diet as partners rather than a supplement as a single knife to a problem.

Fasting: housekeeping for the body

He describes fasting as a municipal clean-up system — like the sanitation that keeps a city clean — invoking the Nobel-recognised discovery of autophagy. He cites a "leading fasting doctor" for the claim that a roughly 30-hour fast is a sweet spot said to kill cancer cells, and notes intermittent fasting windows of 12–18 hours and one-meal-a-day patterns have shown improved biochemistry, blood pressure and even telomere-related aging in cases he has seen on continuous glucose monitors. His firm caveat: fasting should be done under a doctor's or dietitian's guidance, monitoring weight, blood work and blood pressure, and whatever meal you do eat must be nutritionally well-constructed.

From the audience

Fernando answered several reader questions; the recurring theme is "look beyond the obvious and get guided."

  • Constipation after surgery and painkillers: take a doctor's advice before any multivitamin, since you don't know what damage the painkillers did; prefer nutrition from colourful fruit and vegetables and consider a microbiome (poop) test to reset the gut.
  • Trouble gaining weight: some people are simply born with genes that neither build muscle nor store fat easily. If sleep, skin, digestion and energy are all good, that is health; a weight-gain plan should combine enough protein and carbohydrate with weight training, adding calories gradually so you gain muscle, not fat.
  • Children craving chocolate: habits set young, so before 11–12 make better versions at home (dry-fruit chocolate laddoo, almond/coconut-milk shakes) and use non-food rewards; parental control over commercial, high-sugar chocolate is a right up to age 18.
  • New food allergies as an adult (e.g. broccoli): a gut stressed by years of sugar and chemicals becomes reactive, and foods show a "seasonality" — eating one thing constantly can trigger a temporary reaction. He does a yearly food-intolerance test and rotates foods accordingly.
  • Grade-2 fatty liver without alcohol or obesity: fatty liver can come from stress, overeating, pollution or genetics, not only sugar, fat and alcohol. It is reversible — start walking and work with a nutritionist to lower calorie load and use certain foods and herbs.
  • Understanding low glycaemic index: GI runs 0–100 for how fast a food releases glucose; white rice and white bread release it fast and high, an apple slow and low. Favouring low-GI foods supports diabetic control.
  • Feeling weak on a vegan diet: vegans need not quit, but must balance plant protein and test vitamin and mineral levels — especially B12 (found in animal food) and vitamin D — at least every quarter to six months, supplementing to top up deficiencies.
  • Which fasting for weight loss: fasting is powerful, but do it under a family doctor or dietitian's guidance so you avoid extreme deficiency and eat well-constructed meals when you do eat.

Food sensitivities, supplementation and fasting can carry real risks; consult a qualified doctor or registered dietitian before changing your diet, starting supplements, or fasting — especially if you have a medical condition, are pregnant, or manage a chronic illness.
