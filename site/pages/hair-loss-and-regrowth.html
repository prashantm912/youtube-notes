Hair Loss, Thinning and Regrowth: What Two Hair Doctors Want You to Know

Dermatologist and hair transplant surgeon Dr. Abhishek Pilani, with counselling insight from Dr. Priyanka Pilani, explains why baldness is arriving in the early twenties, how the daily habits that damage hair build up, how to read the warning signs, and which treatments — and myths — are worth your money.

At a glance

  • Genetics decides whether you are prone to baldness; lifestyle decides how fast it arrives. As Dr. Abhishek Pilani puts it, genetics loads the gun and lifestyle pulls the trigger.
  • Male pattern loss follows a hormonal path: testosterone converts to DHT, which binds sensitive follicles and miniaturises them over years.
  • A sound routine rests on three things — scalp hygiene, follicular strength, and the integrity of the hair shaft. Wash four to five times a week with a pH-balanced shampoo and always condition the strands.
  • Heat and chemicals are the two red flags: blow-drying, straightening, styling products, colouring and keratin treatments damage hair cumulatively, not overnight.
  • Losing 50–100 hairs a day is normal. Early thinning shows up on your pillow and clothes long before relatives notice a visible bald patch.
  • Serums that claim to regrow hair or stop hair fall are protective at best. Onion, aloe vera, rosemary, beer and curd remedies support texture but do not reverse thinning.
  • Real options exist for every stage — medical (minoxidil, finasteride), non-surgical (PRP, exosomes), transplant, and nutritional support — but they must be prescribed for the individual, not bought from a one-size-fits-all online kit.
  • Hair loss is deeply emotional, not merely cosmetic. It is common, it is solvable, and the right move is to see a qualified doctor rather than self-diagnose online.

Why baldness is arriving younger

A decade ago, patients seeking help for baldness and thinning were mostly in their thirties and forties. That has changed completely: people in their early twenties, even at 21 or 22, now present with recession, thinning and miniaturisation, and some reach advanced grades of baldness at 18 or 19. The genetics of the population have not changed in a single generation. What has changed is how people live.

"The genetics has loaded the gun, but the lifestyle has pulled the trigger."

— Dr. Abhishek Pilani, dermatologist and hair transplant surgeon

Chronic stress and raised cortisol, poor nutrition, poor sleep, late nights, blue-light exposure, alcohol, smoking and hormonal imbalances all collectively accelerate the process. The same lifestyle shift is driving other conditions to appear earlier too, from heart problems to early-onset diabetes. Hair is simply one visible reflection of what is happening internally — whatever is going on inside the body tends to show on the skin and hair.

The DHT mechanism, simplified

Testosterone is the male hormone. An enzyme, 5-alpha reductase, converts it into a more potent form called dihydrotestosterone, or DHT. Hair follicles carry DHT receptors; when DHT binds to them, it triggers miniaturisation. The follicle's growth phase shortens, so a thick hair is shed before it completes its cycle, and each successive hair grows thinner until the follicle stops producing visible hair altogether.

Hormones work like a lock and key: the hormone has to combine with its receptor to have an effect. Crucially, susceptibility is not about how much testosterone you have — it is about how genetically sensitive your follicle receptors are to DHT. Someone with a high testosterone level but low genetic sensitivity will keep their hair, while someone with low testosterone and high sensitivity will thin and bald. Testosterone still does its other work — muscle, sexual health — through its own pathway; only the hair-thinning effect runs through DHT.

The predisposition is polygenic and can come from either side of the family — father, mother, uncles, aunts, grandparents — and it can skip a generation, which is why someone whose father has thick hair can still bald early. Boosting testosterone through weightlifting or sport does not accelerate loss unless you are already genetically predisposed. Taking exogenous testosterone or growth hormones, however, does enlarge the pool available to form DHT and can push things faster in those who are predisposed.

The everyday routine that protects your hair

A good routine maintains three things: scalp hygiene, follicular strength, and the integrity — the tensile strength — of the hair shaft. Dry, frizzy, easily broken hair usually traces back to how it is handled day to day.

Shampoo: frequency and what to avoid

A common fear is that frequent shampooing causes hair loss. It does not. Normal daily shedding is 50–100 hairs. When you skip washing for four or five days, the shed hairs accumulate and all come out together at the next wash, creating a panic in the sink — but that is normal shedding, not shampoo damage. Shampoo four to five times a week with a pH-balanced product. An oily scalp with dandruff may need more frequent washing; otherwise three to four times a week is fine. The right shampoo is the one that suits your own scalp and hair type, which differs from person to person.

Turn the bottle around and read the label. For daily use, avoid harsh chemicals: sulfates (used for foaming), parabens and other high-preservative content, formaldehyde-releasing preservatives, and drying alcohol. These leave hair dry and frizzy over time.

Conditioner: for the strand, not the scalp

Conditioner does not cause hair loss. Where shampoo clears oil, dust and microorganisms from the scalp to maintain hygiene, conditioner seals the cuticle of the hair strand and moisturises it, protecting the body of the hair and keeping it healthier and shinier. Apply it along the strands — not on the scalp — leave it for two to three minutes, and rinse with water. Almost everyone benefits from conditioning after every shampoo; done this way it prevents the dryness and brittleness that lead to breakage.

Water temperature, drying and combing

  • Use lukewarm water. Extremely hot water strips proteins from the hair shaft, leaving it dry, dehydrated and frizzy. If you want a hot shower, a hair cap that keeps the water off the hair is fine.
  • Be gentle with wet hair. Wet hair is fragile. Do not rub it aggressively with a towel or drag a fine-tooth comb through it immediately. Dab it dry softly and let it rest.
  • Comb with a wide-tooth comb. Combing with light pressure can massage the scalp and support blood circulation through the dilated capillaries there, but never comb wet hair, which breaks easily.

Serums and internal care

A hair serum forms a protective layer around the shaft against dust, pollution and toxins, which are especially high in metro cities and can inflame and damage hair. It can be used daily, on the shaft rather than the scalp, and is a sensible habit for city dwellers. One caution: any serum that claims to reduce hair fall or grow new hair is overstating itself — it is simply protecting the hair from daily damage.

Externally good habits are only half the picture. Hair is built from keratin, so the body needs adequate protein, good amino acids and multivitamins, alongside sound sleep and controlled stress. Excessive blow-drying and chemical application should be minimised. That basic combination — internal nutrition plus gentle external care — is the foundation for anyone.

Heat, chemicals and other everyday damage

The two red flags are excessive heat and excessive chemicals. Damage from either is an eventual, cumulative process — like smoking, one exposure will not ruin your hair, but repeated exposure over years surely will. Heat and chemicals break the protein bonds in the shaft, make hair fragile, shorten the growth phase and speed up shedding and thinning.

Heat styling

Blow-drying and straightening apply direct heat that breaks the hair shaft. If you blow-dry frequently, use a heat protectant spray to limit — though not eliminate — the damage. Daily straightening carries the same risk for the same reason.

Chemical colouring

Colouring damages hair, and repeated or multi-tone colouring is worse because it layers colouring, bleaching and re-colouring, each step burning the shaft, damaging the cuticle and, over time, thinning the follicle's output. The practical rule is low-frequency colouring. When you do colour, choose safer options and read the box: avoid ammonia, PPD (paraphenylenediamine) and formaldehyde-type preservatives, which can cause dermatitis and follicle damage. Mehndi is often adulterated, so a good, known brand matters more than the "natural" label. A colour-protecting shampoo helps the colour last and reduces stripping, and colour done well by a good salon or a trusted person, once in a while, is acceptable.

Dreadlocks, tight braids and hard water

  • Dreadlocks keep the hair pulled for long periods, causing traction alopecia, and make scalp hygiene almost impossible — oil, dirt and microorganisms build up under them, producing dandruff and odour. Acceptable occasionally for styling, not as a long-term look.
  • Tight braids cause traction alopecia; oiling the hair and then tying it tight adds odour and dust collection. Loose braids are the safer practice, including for children whose parents tie their hair.
  • Hard water — high in mineral content, common in the Gulf and in India's tier-2 and tier-3 cities — is harsher on the scalp and accelerates thinning (the underlying cause is still genetic). People moving from soft-water to hard-water regions often notice a difference. Shower filters and water softeners help.

Reading the warning signs

Thinning is gradual, and people often fail to notice it has begun. Many arrive convinced they went bald over three to six months, when in reality the process started years earlier. Male pattern baldness is graded on the Norwood scale, from one to seven.

  1. Grade 1: slight temple recession and a rise in daily shedding. If you can count more than about 150–250 hairs falling in a day, thinning has likely started.
  2. Grade 2: the temples recede further, creating a visible M-shaped hairline.
  3. Grade 3: a bald spot appears at the crown, which begins progressing while the hairline recedes.
  4. Grade 4: the crown expands and the temples recede toward merging, though a thick band of hair still separates them.
  5. Grade 5: that separating band itself starts to thin.
  6. Grade 6: the regions merge and hair is lost across the top.
  7. Grade 7: only the sides and back remain, in a horseshoe pattern.

Grade 1 is the stage that gets ignored, because it is something you experience privately rather than something others point out — relatives and friends only remark on it at grades three or four, when it becomes obvious. Watch for more hair on your pillow when you wake, on your shirts, and in the shower during frequent washing.

Female pattern thinning and hormonal causes

Hair thinning is not a male problem alone. More than half of women face it at some point. In women it usually presents as diffuse thinning rather than a bald patch — a Christmas-tree parting that widens from the centre, graded on the Ludwig scale. Because it can be camouflaged, it is less visible than male baldness, but it affects women just as much internally.

The common triggers are hormonal, driven by drops in the protective hormone estrogen:

  • Postpartum: during pregnancy hormones keep hair luscious, but two to three months after delivery, the loss of estrogen produces noticeable shedding. This is a telogen phase and usually self-limits. If excessive hair fall continues beyond four to five months, see a dermatologist to check for and correct any deficiencies.
  • PCOS and thyroid issues: hormonal imbalances that show up as scalp thinning.
  • Perimenopause and menopause: falling estrogen again drives thinning. Care focuses on measuring diet, protein and nutrition properly and, where appropriate, using non-surgical therapies to sustain the follicles.

Alopecia areata is different

Not genetic — autoimmune. Alopecia areata, which public figures such as Jada Pinkett Smith have spoken about, is an autoimmune condition in which the body reacts against its own cells. It is not inherited in the usual sense and not acquired from anyone else; no one in the family may have it. It can be triggered by stress or other internal issues, and most cases are controllable. It is also less common than its celebrity profile suggests.

Myths, trends and home remedies

"Straight hair falls out more than curly hair"

Myth. Neither straight nor curly hair is inherently more prone to falling. The whole cascade of fall, thinning and baldness traces back to the hormonal and genetic DHT process. Curly hair may break more easily while combing because of its shape, but that surface breakage does not damage the follicular root.

"Silk pillowcases and loose braids save your hair"

Healthy practice, not a cure. Silk or satin pillowcases cause less friction than cotton, which reduces mechanical breakage — a genuinely good, harmless habit. Tying a loose braid at night similarly avoids tangling and pulling. But this only protects hair from external breakage; it does nothing for the genetic miniaturisation that causes real thinning.

"Plucking one gray hair makes two grow back"

Myth. Plucking does not multiply gray hairs. What it does do is damage the follicle — pluck the same one repeatedly and it may stop growing hair at all. On graying more broadly: once hair grays you cannot reverse it, only delay the process. Early graying is often driven by stress and deficiencies (including thyroid and B12), and no miracle medicine restores natural colour.

"Beer, onion, aloe and other home remedies regrow hair"

Mostly myth — supportive at best. Applied topically, these do not regrow hair or control thinning:

  • Curd, egg yolk, amla packs and beer shampoos are not absorbed as nutrition through the scalp. Beer shampoo's barley protein gives a temporary voluminous, shiny look, but its drying alcohol eventually leaves hair dry and frizzy.
  • Garlic is very harsh on skin. Onion has some antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties from its sulfur and allicin content, but does not drive regrowth or control hair loss.
  • Aloe vera and rosemary products are largely marketing gimmicks for hair-loss purposes — they may support hair quality, texture and moisture, but not regrowth or thinning control.

These same ingredients can genuinely help when eaten rather than applied: amla supplies vitamin C and antioxidants, egg provides biotin and protein, and both work well as internal nutrition. Random oil applications carry real risk — some have caused eye damage and hospitalisations, and inflamed, dermatitic scalps are a frequent clinic finding. Consumer logic: buy an onion or aloe product for the pleasure of the bath if you like it, not for the health of your hair.

"There's a beard growth serum that works"

Mostly marketing. Most beard products are styling and hygiene items — perfumes, gels, combs, colouring agents — not growth treatments. The one scientifically grounded option is topical minoxidil, a vasodilator that improves local blood circulation; a known side effect is excessive facial hair growth, which is why it can be tried for the beard. It must be used under medical supervision, because minoxidil solutions are usually alcohol-based and can cause dryness, dermatitis and irritation, especially in people with seborrheic dermatitis or dandruff. A beard transplant is another route: it draws from the same donor zone at the back of the scalp, is done under local anaesthesia with negligible pain, and works best structured subtly following the natural direction of growth.

"Not washing your hair is good for it"

Myth — and a pattern to recognise. Scalp hygiene matters, and skipping washing lets oil, dirt and microorganisms build up. Many such "trends" gain traction precisely by contradicting sound advice for the sake of going viral. Lice (pediculosis) are a related hygiene issue: the infestation is highly contagious, spreads quickly, and requires that the whole household be treated, not just the affected person.

"An online medicine kit can fix it"

Not tailored, so not reliable. Filling an online form and receiving a combined kit of medicines, shampoos, conditioners, serums and lotions — often a mixture of allopathy, ayurveda and homeopathy promoted by celebrities — sets unrealistic, one-size-fits-all expectations. Every body and biology is different. Without examining the scalp, checking follicular strength, identifying deficiencies and reviewing lifestyle, one form cannot prescribe well, and combinations of medicines may even be contraindicated for some people. Get evaluated in person by a doctor.

Treatment options when care is not enough

Treatment falls into four categories, and most patients need a combination matched to their grade of baldness. There is no universal protocol — a doctor should evaluate which combination gives the best outcome for the individual.

1. Medical

  • Minoxidil is a vasodilator that improves blood circulation, available topically and orally. The oral form has more efficacy but a greater chance of side effects — water retention, palpitations, low blood pressure — so it must be prescribed and monitored by a doctor.
  • Finasteride / dutasteride help delay deterioration. Someone who would lose noticeable hair over two years might instead sustain it for four to five. It is effectively a lifelong commitment, and while side effects are widely discussed, both the effect and the side effects are reversible on discontinuing. Have it checked by a doctor rather than self-diagnosing the risks online.

2. Non-surgical therapies

These are retention and strengthening therapies that help follicles withstand ongoing internal damage. PRP (platelet-rich plasma) uses your own blood: it is drawn, the platelet-rich fraction with its growth factors is concentrated, and it is injected back into the scalp. The injection takes roughly 30 seconds to a minute, is not especially painful, and the technique is scientifically established. Exosomes are nanomicelles that can reactivate dormant follicles, and QR678 is another growth-factor therapy. All are used in a customised, tailored approach — every treatment does not work for everyone.

3. Hair transplant

A transplant restores volume where areas have already balded — crown, temples or the whole top. It is a two-part procedure: follicles are extracted from the donor area at the back (the head is shaved or taken to size zero for extraction) and implanted at the front. For fully gray hair, the scalp is coloured during extraction so the follicles are visible; otherwise gray hair transplants well.

Choose a doctor, not a shortcut. The law allows the procedure to be performed under a doctor's supervision, and problems arise when unskilled technicians who do not understand hair anatomy, direction and angulation do the implanting, or when clinics hasten the work into mega-sessions to finish in a day. Wrongly done transplants — including beard transplants where the hair angle and density look unnatural — make up a large share of the corrective cases these surgeons see. It is a time-consuming, tedious procedure; let the doctor invest the time.

4. Nutritional therapy and smart supplementation

Vitamins compete with one another for absorption when taken all at once — an iron tablet and a calcium tablet taken together, for instance, compete, whereas iron taken with vitamin C is better absorbed. Fat-soluble vitamins such as vitamin D need dietary fat to absorb, are not required daily, and can accumulate to toxicity if overused. The better approach is cyclical nutritional therapy: protein and amino acids for follicular strength, iron and minerals for scalp oxygenation, and other vitamins and antioxidants for growth and repair — in the right proportion at the right time, rather than one megadose multivitamin.

Practical guidance on supplements:

  • Start with a blood report. Identify actual deficiencies and review your diet before supplementing; you may already have sufficient levels. Protein is the most common deficiency, especially among Indian vegetarians.
  • Do not copy someone else's stack. Every biology is different; get individual guidance rather than a friend's regimen or a random combined tablet.
  • Collagen is worthwhile, with attention to dose and source, as adulterated products are common — a good affordable brand is fine.
  • Omega-3 / fish oil acts as an antioxidant and anti-inflammatory; algae-derived omega-3 is the vegetarian option. Antioxidants counter the oxidative stress that pollution and toxins create in the body, which otherwise shows on skin and hair.
  • Whole foods — good nuts, fruits and vegetables, soaked nuts and seeds (chia soaked to avoid bloating, plus flax and pumpkin) — supply antioxidant and anti-inflammatory support.

On the horizon, molecules such as PP405 work differently from a minoxidil vasodilator or a finasteride enzyme-blocker — they act as a messenger that reactivates dormant, "sleeping" follicles. Early data showed good safety and, in about 30% of patients, roughly 20% hair growth and improved density in four to eight weeks. The Instagram claim of a grade-7 bald head regaining full hair in eight weeks is AI-generated and not real. Any new molecule must pass long trials — animal studies, then monitored human trials — because side effects can appear four, five or ten years later.

The emotional weight, and when to see a doctor

Hair loss is not only a cosmetic issue; it is deeper and very emotional. Dr. Priyanka Pilani, who has counselled well over fourteen thousand patients, describes how it stays at the back of the mind through interviews, social gatherings and meetings, quietly draining energy. Patients fix their hair repeatedly before walking into a room, check every reflection, avoid photographs and withdraw from socialising. Personalities shift — the way people interact, their energy, their willingness to be seen.

"It isn't only a cosmetic issue. It's a lot deeper, and it's very emotional."

— Dr. Priyanka Pilani

Patients tend to move through a predictable arc: panic at the first clump of hair on the pillow, then overthinking fuelled by endless online searching and misinformation, then confusion after consulting several clinics with conflicting advice. The counselling that helps most is simple and honest — this is a common problem, you are not alone, and it is solvable. Family support matters: parents of a young boy should listen to his insecurities rather than dismiss them, and young girls often need even more support, because thinning ties into beauty standards and identity and they tend to suffer it more silently.

Skip the self-diagnosis

Reading symptoms on Google or asking an AI chatbot tends to breed hypochondria — people start fearing symptoms they do not have. Even AI tools include a disclaimer to see a doctor, because physical examination is an essential part of any diagnosis. Warning signs for anything going wrong in the body usually appear early, so the sensible response is to visit a doctor for clarity rather than to diagnose and medicate yourself. The encouraging message from both doctors is that hair and skin are, at root, very basic: eat well, sleep well, keep a good routine, and if a problem persists despite that, a doctor has solutions.
