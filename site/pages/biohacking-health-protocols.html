Functional-Medicine Biohacks for Sleep, Testosterone and Gut Health

Practical, root-cause protocols from functional-medicine practitioner Palak Midha for sleeping deeper, raising testosterone naturally, repairing the gut, lowering toxic load, and reversing the lifestyle diseases that cluster in Indian adults after 40.

Start with sleep: the foundation of everything else

Asked where a beginner should start biohacking, the advice is to invest where you already spend the most time. A gym session is only 40–45 minutes; sleep is roughly a third of your life. That makes the bedroom the highest-leverage place to begin.

Clean the air you sleep in

The first, cheapest change is a HEPA filter in the bedroom — distinct from a general air purifier. Even in high-pollution cities, filtered night air gives you good-quality air for the third of the day you spend asleep. Snake plants and peace lilies were also suggested for the sleep room.

Hack the light

  • White light (including CFL bulbs and TV) signals "sun is out" to the brain and suppresses melatonin. Switch bedroom bulbs to orange, and move the TV out of the bedroom to avoid a sedentary, screen-in-bed pattern.
  • Orange or red blue-light-blocking glasses in the evening strengthen melatonin production. Red is the stronger signal — useful only when you intend to sleep soon, not before a late meeting.
  • For night bathroom trips, a dark-red night light keeps melatonin production from shutting off.

Fix the last meal, not just the bedtime

Stop eating about three hours before sleep. A carbohydrate-heavy Indian dinner — dal (around 35g carbs) plus rice (around 35–40g) totals roughly 80g — spikes blood sugar and then drops it. If your blood-sugar regulation is poor, the resulting dip can wake you as a survival response when the brain runs low on energy. Reducing the carbohydrates and adding protein and fat to dinner, plus a 15-minute walk afterward to stabilise the glucose spike, addressed this.

Elevate for sleep apnea

To reverse a parent's sleep apnea, the upper body was raised about 10–15 degrees so the nasal and throat passages stay clearer while lying down. If you can't tilt the mattress itself, placing two bricks under the head-end legs of the bed works the same way. This was combined with the light and dinner changes above.

Melatonin is described as mildly habit-forming — the one supplement said to reduce your own production over time. It was used only as a low, doctor-prescribed dose (around 2–3mg) for older parents whose natural melatonin had declined with age, and taken situationally (e.g. during travel) rather than as a permanent nightly aid for younger, healthy sleepers. Decisions like this were made with a functional-medicine doctor, not self-started.

Raising testosterone naturally

Motivation, drive and the energy to compete are framed as testosterone-driven. For men who want to raise it naturally, the priority order starts with sleep, then recovery-aware training.

  • Sleep first. It is named the single biggest lever, ahead of any supplement.
  • Train to your recovery, not your ego. A wearable sleep/recovery tracker (a WHOOP band was worn by the whole family) shows how much strain the body is ready for each morning. Pushing hard on a day of poor sleep or dropped HRV raises cortisol and breaks muscle down faster than it builds — a pattern linked to gym heart attacks and to "yo-yo" muscle building.
  • Don't over-train. Breaking muscle daily is inflammatory; heavy lifting was done three to four times a week, not every day.
  • Muscle mass is the currency of anti-aging for both men and women, and especially important for women around menopause.

Ashwagandha is cited as having 27 human clinical trials supporting testosterone and fertility benefits — with strong caveats on quality, covered in the supplements section below.

Reversing the common Indian disease stack

A longitudinal aging study in India (2017–18) is cited for the most common conditions among Indians over 40: hypertension, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, cancer and chronic kidney disease — described here as generally reversible through lifestyle before medication. The approach for each: change the lifestyle first, then add a targeted supplement.

Hypertension

The emphasis is the potassium-to-sodium ratio rather than salt alone: swap table salt for mineral-rich Himalayan pink salt and raise potassium through green leafy vegetables. Choose walking over high-intensity interval training, to work through the parasympathetic nervous system. Magnesium before bed relaxes the arteries so blood supplies more easily past areas of plaque; omega-3 is preferred as a gentle blood thinner over pharmaceutical thinners.

Vitamin K2 — taken as a full-spectrum A/D/K formula with K1, K2, MK-4 and MK-7 — is described as helping reverse arterial calcification by directing the LDL-and-calcium plaque away from the artery walls and toward bone and teeth, where calcium belongs. Calcification deposited in arteries causes plaque; in kidneys, stones; in joints, pain.

Diabetes

  • Cut carbohydrates and balance macros — don't drop the poha or rice, but add a protein and fat source (paneer, two eggs) so the meal isn't carb-dominant.
  • Walk 15 minutes after every meal to normalise the post-meal glucose spike (observed directly on a continuous glucose monitor worn for a year).
  • Raise protein as protein synthesis declines with age; older parents were given pre-digested hydrolysed isolate protein to avoid bloating, plus essential amino acids to protect against muscle breakdown.

Cardiovascular disease

Reframe. Cholesterol is described as a vehicle, not the culprit — LDL carries cholesterol out to patch inflamed, damaged artery walls, and HDL brings it back. The real problems named are arterial inflammation, high insulin, and high homocysteine.

Homocysteine is called the "silent killer." Protein contains the amino acid methionine; if you are deficient in B6, B9 and B12 — and 80% of Indians are said to be B12-deficient — the body cannot clear methionine well, so homocysteine rises, damages arteries, and can cross the blood-brain barrier. The suggested support is the methylated forms (methylcobalamin, methylfolate) which donate the methyl group that lowers homocysteine.

Eyesight and mitochondria

Eyesight is treated as a marker of mitochondrial health — the eyes carry heavy sensory input and dense energy-making cells, so clearer vision without glasses is described as an early sign of improving mitochondrial health. Reported personal practices: reducing an over-strong prescription in small (0.25) increments to remove eye strain, deliberate distance-viewing (a problem attributed partly to close-range "junk light" and desk work), and morning sunlight in the eyes. Reported change: from around -1.25 to -1 over time. A trampoline routine is also recommended for eye exercises, heart health and lymph/blood flow.

Gut health: the root system for skin, mood and immunity

Much of the skin, mood and immune picture traces back to the gut. The work has two parts: rebuilding the gut lining, and shifting the bacterial and fungal balance across the whole body.

Remove what damages the gut, then rebuild the lining

First remove gut-damaging inputs: gluten, dairy, vegetable/seed oils, processed food, and plastics/microplastics (don't drink from plastic bottles). Then rebuild the lining with L-glutamine and bone broth. Fluoride is flagged as a neurotoxin that also harms the thyroid — hence avoiding fluoride toothpaste.

Feed the good bacteria

  • Prebiotic fibre feeds beneficial bacteria: cooled potato or rice (cooling converts starch to resistant starch), or slightly green/raw banana.
  • Introduce vegetables and salads gradually — a weak gut will bloat if you suddenly load fibre. Strengthen the gut first with collagen and bone broth.

Eczema, dandruff and skin flares

Root cause. Eczema, dandruff and similar flares are treated as connected to fungal overgrowth and a compromised (leaky) gut turning autoimmune, not as isolated scalp problems.

A personal example: an infant's eczema reportedly cleared within a week after the mother cut all dairy and added omega-3 (via breast milk), and did not return. The general suggestion for eczema is to stop dairy products and support with omega-3, which helps flaky, dry skin because cell membranes are built from fat (DHA also serving the brain). A separate testimonial linked pigmentation and vitiligo improvement to liver support (see below).

Respect antinutrients

Legumes such as rajma and chana are soaked, pressure-cooked and sometimes sprouted specifically to reduce antinutrients — a plant's chemical defence — and improve digestibility; raw kidney beans can be fatal. The trade-off is that heavy processing also strips much of the nutrition, leaving mostly taste and emotional comfort. On high-stress, high-performance days, heavy fermentable foods like rajma-chawal are more likely to trigger bloating and low mood.

Liver, detox and toxic load

The liver is described as the common thread across many conditions, and one reason Indian environments carry a heavy load of pollution, pesticides and toxins.

Reversing fatty liver

Fatty-liver fat is attributed to carbohydrates (especially fructose), not dietary fat. The protocol: cut fructose (sugar is around 50% fructose; swap for monk fruit or stevia; watch dates and flavoured yogurt), cut overall carbohydrates (don't eat rice and roti together), and add protein. For regeneration, 400mg of silymarin from milk thistle plus NAC (N-acetylcysteine) is said to help liver cells — even damaged ones — regenerate. A parent's grade-3 fatty liver reportedly normalised (SGOT/SGPT) over about three years, alongside lifestyle change. Liver health is linked here to pigmentation, vitiligo, digestion, fatigue, blood sugar and cancer risk.

The infrared sauna detox protocol

Attributed to a post-9/11 firefighter detox protocol, an infrared sauna is said to release about 10× more heavy metals and pesticides in sweat than a conventional sauna, because you sweat at lower temperatures via radiant plates rather than rocks. A cabin starts around ₹1 lakh; a low-EMF unit is preferred. Sessions of roughly 20–25 minutes were used for parents and for people with the disease stack above, with reported improvement in joint mobility.

Before entering the sauna, an activated-charcoal capsule was taken to bind toxins as they are released. The reasoning: the sauna pulls toxins out of cells, but if detox pathways are sluggish, a portion is reabsorbed — charcoal binds the released toxins so they leave via the stool rather than recirculating.

Pollution and antioxidants

Aging and pollution are linked through reactive oxygen species (ROS). The argument is that with pollution, pesticides and toxins all rising, food-based antioxidants alone (e.g. "pomegranate has antioxidants") are not enough. A "stop aging" supplement is described as delivering, in two capsules, antioxidant loads comparable to the resveratrol of 15 glasses of wine, the quercetin of 20 apples, the EGCG and polyphenols of 10 green teas, and the antioxidants of 12 pomegranates. Resveratrol is associated (via David Sinclair's work) with telomere length and longevity. The claimed first biomarker of reversing aging is improved mitochondrial health and energy — and, in turn, needing less sleep once toxic load drops.

Choosing supplements wisely: quality, dose and salt form

Two "red flags" of the supplement industry are named: under-dosing (filling a capsule with 100mg of active plus filler when it could hold 650mg of a proper therapeutic dose), and using cheap forms. The recommendation is to read the salts on a label you trust, then buy the same salts elsewhere if a given brand feels expensive — treat quality supplement labels as an education tool.

Ashwagandha as a case study

  • Use root only — leaf contains alkaloids that can cause liver toxicity over time.
  • Extraction method matters: milk extraction was preferred over cheap solvents like ethanol.
  • Standardise the active compound: withanolides at around 5% (the standardised KSM-6/KSM-66 material named), versus 2–3% in cheaper products.

Magnesium is described as required for the body to absorb and use vitamin D and K — without enough magnesium, those don't work. A founder-led, bootstrapped, research-driven brand model is contrasted with investor-funded companies that compete on ad spend and marketplace bidding rather than R&D, and a reported 70% customer retention rate is cited.

Several "advanced" practices carry explicit self-dosing warnings. High-dose vitamin D (e.g. 10,000 IU every two days for a week) was described with "I don't recommend people do this themselves." Methylene blue is "not for everyone" — it cannot be combined with SSRIs, and a gene test was suggested to check tolerance; spirulina or chlorella were offered as gentler mitochondrial alternatives. NAC given to a child was described as "I don't recommend" doing directly because of sudden absorption. Foundational, well-studied options (turmeric/curcumin for inflammation) are recommended before advanced peptides.

Fasting, keto and Ozempic — used with caution

Not a fan of fasting. For diabetics, a 12–14 hour window is called a good protocol, but blanket fasting is discouraged. Skipping a meal while dosing up on caffeine with no food drives a fight-or-flight, anxiety-building spiral that ends in a 5–6pm crash and a binge. Fast only strategically — on a genuinely relaxed day with no demanding agenda, ideally replacing the dopamine of food with another source — and stop the fast the moment it turns to suffering. High performers who need brain energy generally should not fast on their hardest days.

Keto and metabolic flexibility

After roughly 10 years in ketosis, the goal is metabolic flexibility: run on ketones (brain fuel) for podcast and research days, and add a little carbohydrate at night for better sleep. Muscle recovery suffers in strict keto, so when the goal shifts to building muscle, carbohydrates return.

Ozempic and GLP-1 drugs

GLP-1 medications are a peptide, and peptides (BPC for joints; CJC/ipamorelin for growth hormone, which declines after 30–35) have been used for years. GLP-1 suppresses appetite, which can help someone with strong cravings change habits for a few months — but only alongside adequate protein (1.2–1.6g per kg) and muscle training. The warning is against staying on it and losing muscle ("Ozempic face"): muscle takes years to build, is very hard to rebuild at older ages, and its loss sets up future knee pain. People 5–6kg overweight who take it without changing lifestyle are told to expect problems later.

A simple place to start

Asked for three things anyone could do to make their health 5–10% better, the answer targets India's biggest problem — insulin and blood sugar.

  1. Cut carbohydrates and eat eggs at every meal. Eggs are called the safest non-vegetarian protein source available in India — treat them almost like medicine (bhurji, omelette, five to eight a day). A whole hen can form from one egg, so it "provides everything."
  2. Treat fruit like nature's candy, and pair it. You'd eat two or three toffees, not a bowlful — apply the same restraint to fruit. Don't eat fruit alone; eat a small portion after a meal, and pair sweet fruit (mango, watermelon) with avocado or coconut first to blunt the blood-sugar spike. This approach reportedly reversed a parent's pre-diabetes.
  3. Walk after meals. Do not underestimate a post-meal walk — on a glucose monitor, walking brings blood sugar down within about three hours, and high blood sugar/insulin is described as steadily damaging nerves.

A bonus: don't starve out sweetness — replace sugar with stevia or monk fruit. A keto ice cream made from egg yolks and stevia (sweetened with seasonal fruit such as half a mango) is offered as a "zero-consequence" everyday dessert.

The closing message is not to give up or become resigned to decline at 40 — most of the disease stack is framed as reversible with consistent lifestyle change, and starting somewhere matters more than doing everything at once.
