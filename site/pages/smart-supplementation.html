Smart Supplementation and Everyday Biohacking

Nutritionist and supplement formulator Palak Midha on what biohacking really means, how to buy a supplement without being fooled, the deficiencies that quietly drain Indian health, and why most of the results come from free habits — not expensive powders.

What biohacking actually means

Biohacking here is defined narrowly: you look at the biomarkers that naturally decline with age — growth hormone, melatonin, thyroid — and intervene to raise them back up. The framing is that the body is designed to decline after 30 so it eventually makes room for new life; biohacking identifies which markers are falling and works on those specific ones, aiming to give a 40-year-old the biomarkers of a 25-year-old. It is described as re-engineering, not rehabilitation, and it is mainly intervention.

Her own example: at 25 she wore glasses at −2.5; at 40 she no longer needs them. The reasoning given is that eyesight weakening is an early sign the body's energy is running out, because the eyes carry a very high density of mitochondria to power sensory input. Screen exposure and blue light add oxidative stress (reactive oxygen species) to the eyes; working on the mitochondria plus an antioxidant to cancel that oxidative stress is offered as the way to protect vision.

The 80/20 rule of biohacking

The advanced end — whole-blood plasma exchange, young-donor blood, stem cells, peptides — is real but described as the last 1–20% of the game, requiring a lot of money, and unnecessary for most people. Around 80% of the benefit comes from small, ordinary lifestyle changes. The advice is explicitly not to chase the elaborate morning-routine stacks of well-known biohackers; start with basics, and add one thing at a time (an electrolyte packed for a trip, for instance) so habits build gradually rather than feeling overwhelming.

Metabolic flexibility and thyroid

After 30, thyroid can test "in range" while the T4-to-T3 conversion quietly weakens — and that conversion happens in the liver. If free T3 sits low in its range (she cites a 2–4 example), a doctor may call it normal, but she argues a level nearer 3.5–4 is where the body burns fat efficiently, the way children do. A liver burdened by hair sprays, mould, alcohol, and indoor and outdoor pollution can drag T3 down even at 25. She reports eating 2,300+ calories a day with high muscle mass as evidence of a well-functioning conversion, and describes training the body toward metabolic flexibility — the ability to run without a constant carbohydrate supply.

Rethinking the Indian plate: carbs, gluten and protein

The central diet argument is that Indian meals are too carbohydrate-heavy for sedentary, desk-bound lives. Fifty years ago people ate three parathas or three rotis and burned it as farmers and labourers; after office work replaced field work, the food never changed. A labourer burning it off may need three rotis; a desk worker does not.

Leaving gluten first

Her single biggest diet lever is dropping gluten. The account given: wheat was genetically modified around the 1960s (she credits the Nobel-winning agronomist Norman Borlaug) to prevent famine, which hardened its gluten so people could survive on it but, in her framing, not thrive. Replacing roti with easier-to-digest, gluten-free options — oats, besan (gram-flour) chilla, gluten-free paratha — is described as winning "20% of the game." She is less opposed to bread, which is easier to digest through its yeast processing, than to roti; roti is acceptable only if you can take a 25-minute nap or a walk afterward.

The reframe on roti itself: if the meal already has potato sabzi for carbohydrates, dal, and a protein source such as paneer with a little butter, the roti adds little beyond a satisfaction habit — and satisfaction can come from many foods.

Balance every meal, and put protein in each one

Every meal should be read as macros: is the protein complete, are the fats there, can the hormones be built? A carb-forward breakfast chilla is improved by adding one or two eggs. Testosterone, neurotransmitters and "happy hormones" are all built from protein, so protein belongs in every meal — not just once a day. A common Indian objection, that eggs twice a day cause "heat," is answered with cooling foods like gond katira or aloe vera to balance, and with drinking enough water first.

The "good amount" trap and fruit

Morning carbohydrates are legitimate — overnight the brain runs and liver and muscle glycogen deplete, so you wake depleted — but the amount must be balanced. Three dates are carbohydrates; so is one. The example given: a second slice of bread past what refills glycogen converts to sugar and then fat, and a protein bar can hide the carbohydrate load of four dates, of which the body burns one and the rest push toward diabetes. Moderation is the repeated key.

Fruit is treated as sugar the body cannot distinguish from other sugar, carrying only trace micronutrients. The recommendation is to use fruit as a flavouring or dessert rather than a full bowl, and to pair a craved seasonal fruit with fat and fibre — coconut or MCTs — to blunt the insulin spike. The influencer with six-pack abs "because I eat fruit" is called misleading: they also burn it off with three workouts and 20,000-plus steps a day.

The deficiencies India overlooks

A recurring theme is that supplementation was built into medical routines in many Western countries as a norm from the late 1960s, while awareness in India lagged. Several deficiencies are singled out as common and consequential.

  • Vitamin D. In Germany, D3 drops are given to newborns on day one. Brown skin converts sunlight to vitamin D poorly, clothing blocks it further, and lying on a beach long enough to make it risks pigmentation because darker skin has more melanocytes and burns rather than reddens. The practical conclusion: most Indians cannot rely on sun for vitamin D.
  • B12. Vegetarian diets lack it; the traces in plant foods cannot fill a years-long deficiency. It is also often sold in a poorly absorbed form — with compromised stomach acid and widespread bloating, B12 may never cross the stomach and needs to be absorbed in the mouth, which is why a chewable is preferred. B12 is recommended alongside B9 (folate) because the two work together.
  • Iron and anaemia. Anaemia is called a big problem in India. Girls abroad get iron support as periods begin; here, fatigue, hair loss, pale complexion and shortness of breath go unaddressed. Standard iron medication is faulted for using the wrong iron salt and for being given without cofactors.
  • Omega-3. Missing from Indian diets. Chia and flax provide ALA, but the body's conversion to usable omega-3 is inefficient, so an extra omega-3 capsule is advised — described as the biggest anti-inflammatory.
  • Magnesium. Depleted by stress and immobility. It is described as acting like a magnet that helps the body absorb other nutrients, so a magnesium shortfall drags down other micronutrients too.

Iron caution: the headaches and constipation many people get from iron supplements are attributed to the wrong iron salt, or to iron given without its cofactors. Iron is recommended with B12 and vitamin C. Low iron in a 12–13-year-old — when study load is rising and the brain needs the most oxygen — is flagged as an under-recognised cause of poor school performance.

Buying a supplement without being fooled

The claim that supplements are "fake" is rejected in favour of a sharper point: the real variable is dosage. Reference is made to a 650 mg capsule; some brands fill it only partway (e.g. 200 mg) and tell you to take three, so the bottle appears to hold 90 pills for the money — when a single properly dosed pill would do. Good dosing is researched and, ideally, backed by human clinical trials rather than only animal studies, with the research published on the maker's website.

Red flags and what to check

  • Suspiciously cheap. Quality supplements are not cheap; an overly low price is a warning.
  • Whole powder vs standardised extract. With ashwagandha, a plain dried-plant powder or crude extract can carry the herb's toxic compounds and burden the liver. A standardised, clinically studied extract such as KSM-66 (cited with 200-plus clinical trials) is preferred. Similarly, look for the better salt form — methylcobalamin rather than plain B12.
  • Absorption and certification. Reputable ingredient makers enhance absorbability and hold certifications; that is part of what you are paying for.
  • The ingredient list. A supplement should contain the labelled actives and little else. If it says B12, B6, B9, it should be those plus a natural preservative — she notes using rosemary extract as a preservative — and nothing more. Anything extra is filler.
  • The capsule shell. Plant-cellulose shells are preferred over cheaper fish-gelatin shells; a green dot suggests, though does not guarantee, a plant shell.

Why form changes the dose: vitamin C

Of 100 mg of ordinary vitamin C, only roughly 20–25% is absorbed and the rest leaves in urine, because it is water-soluble. Wrapping the molecule in a phospholipid (a small fat coat) makes a water-soluble vitamin behave like a fat-soluble one, so it is retained and delivered into the cell instead of flushed — the reason a liposomal 100 mg can do the work of a standard 500 mg.

One label distinction is dismissed as a myth: "nutraceutical" and "dietary supplement" are the same thing. What changed over time is that pushing a vitamin such as vitamin C to a very high dose moves it from general wellness into actually helping with disease — which is how the field moved toward thyroid and autoimmune support.

Doctor vs nutritionist: much of medical education does not cover supplement dosing, so an MBBS doctor may not be the right source for how much of a given vitamin, mineral or micronutrient your body needs. The advice is to see a nutritionist for those specifics rather than following recommendations blindly — something she estimates would solve most of the confusion in the market.

Dosage, timing and the toxicity question

Before adding anything, the recommendation is to evaluate whether your body actually needs it — blood tests first, then the right amounts — and to respect the RDA (recommended dietary allowance), while noting reference values shift over time.

When to take what

  • Vitamin D3 — morning. Reasoned simply as the "sunshine vitamin." Evening is acceptable, but morning is her recommendation; it does not need to be taken at night to work.
  • Liver support — empty stomach in the morning, so toxins mobilise well.
  • Other supplements — with food, when stomach "agni" (digestive fire) is stronger and absorption is better.
  • Magnesium and omega-3 — night. Magnesium before sleep relaxes the arteries and veins so blood flows more easily; melatonin, a sleep hormone, is taken only at night and only situationally.

On toxicity

Water-soluble vitamins are largely flushed, but any vitamin taken in excess that the body cannot clear can become toxic; the genuine toxicity risk sits with the fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E and K. Two safeguards are offered: water-soluble vitamins like C are taken in short bursts (a few tablets around travel, flu season, oxidative stress or a party) rather than daily for life; and a cycling protocol of roughly three months on, two months off is used for fat-soluble vitamins.

Her vitamin D targets run well above conventional Indian practice: she suggests keeping levels around 80 ng/mL, cites research pointing to about 60 ng/mL for an average person, and mentions functional-medicine groups running studies above 100 ng/mL — while India still often treats 30 as normal, a level at which she says low energy already begins. These are her positions and ongoing studies, not settled guidance; upper-range vitamin D and any high-dose regimen carry real toxicity risk and should be checked with your own clinician and bloodwork. The same caution applies to the strong reversal claims she describes for conditions like fatty liver and vitiligo, which are presented from her clinical experience rather than as established cures.

Common supplement myths, cleared

"Multivitamins are an expensive placebo"

Myth. They are general wellness rather than a cure, and she does not take one herself — but for someone deficient in a given B vitamin they work. B1 (thiamine) helps convert carbohydrates to energy; very low selenium is linked to autoimmune conditions. The caveat is the salt form: a good multivitamin uses folate not folic acid, and methylcobalamin not plain B12. She calls it "ten drops in the sea" — helpful, but not a substitute for correcting a real deficiency at dose.

"Women should not take protein powder"

Myth. Protein builds hair, skin and neurotransmitters, and she cites a study finding a large share of pregnant women deficient in protein. For women and their hormones, it is a firm yes.

"Skin-care supplements don't really work inside-out"

Partly true. Lifestyle and nutrition do most of the work, but collagen is backed by research: after 30 the body loses roughly 1–2% of its collagen a year and the quality it makes degrades. Supplemental collagen improves skin across the whole body, with visible facial effects taking around five months.

"Face yoga is just a viral fad"

Partly true. It does not fix skin health directly, and it will not lift fully sagging skin at 50. But for a puffy face, or the early cheek-fat-pad droop that begins around 30, pressing the underlying muscle puts it under tension so it strengthens and holds the fat pad against gravity — results taking six to eight months. Gua sha helps a puffy face; she skips her morning gua sha because she avoids inflammatory foods and takes magnesium at night, so she does not wake puffy.

"Is a liver-detox supplement really necessary?"

Necessary in India, in her view, because of low quality standards and high air pollution. The generic formulation she points to combines NAC (N-acetyl cysteine) at around 900 mg — a dose she links to reversing fatty liver — with milk thistle for scar-tissue regeneration; she notes a juice cleanse does not substitute for that dose. The bigger point: bloating is an early sign to act on, because it signals low stomach acid, which means even good food is poorly absorbed. Her first product was apple cider vinegar for exactly this reason. If ACV worsens bloating or acidity, betaine HCl or Swedish bitters are suggested instead.

The free biohacks — and where to start

The most emphasised interventions cost nothing. The order she recommends for someone who feels hopeless with a chronic condition is diet first, then movement — mindset last, because mindset change is a lifetime process, not a quick switch.

Morning

  • Water first. Overnight the lymphatic system clears waste and dead cells; drinking water on waking pushes it out. This is called the single biggest biohack.
  • Next level: a pinch of pink salt under the tongue, then water — for cellular hydration. Himalayan pink salt is cited as carrying around 82 trace minerals, and the water then moves directly into cells via calcium voltage-gated channels. A dehydrated body is linked to infections and skin issues; the salt can also simply be added to water.
  • Rebounding. A few minutes on a trampoline supports lymphatic drainage and detox, strengthens the calves (which is why calves ache in the morning for some), reverses blood flow briefly, and is a good heart exercise. For older or frailer people, gentle one-leg-at-a-time foot movement is the scaled-down version.

Sleep, light and stress

Sleep is called 80% of the game and the biggest gift you can give the body. Gradually switch home bulbs to orange or red, and hold late family conversations in that warmer light, to protect melatonin. Stress is framed physiologically: it leaches minerals from the bones and pulls nutrients from the hair (the body protects the heart over the hair), and much of it is lost through urine — magnesium especially. A "mother hormone" decides whether the body shifts toward cortisol or toward good hormones; chronic stress pushes it toward cortisol, contributing to hair loss, hormone imbalance and the belly bulge.

Abundance is not a sin — it's a frequency.

— Palak Midha

The remedy offered is genuine recovery, which she says the Indian work-and-social setup rarely allows: alternate weekends between winding down and socialising rather than stacking stress on stress, learn the art of delegation in both work and health, and keep "sacred boundaries" so you are not anyone's permanent emotional dumping ground. Mind and body are treated as one system — stuck emotions are linked to physical conditions, and mental clarity is described as the precondition for physical healing.

A simple starting stack

Asked for her top supplements for India, she names, in order: liver support (low quality standards, high pollution), B12 (diets lack it, and it is often sold in the wrong form), and magnesium (because of stress) — with protein as a fourth, since the Indian diet is low in it. For a young person on a tight budget wanting just one thing to begin with, she recommends a D3 + K2 combination from any brand, with D3 around 5,000 IU and K2 around 200 mcg, because many hormones are built from vitamin D. Home remedies are not dismissed but are called insufficient at dose — one turmeric capsule can concentrate the extract of close to a kilogram of haldi, more than kitchen quantities deliver — so the guidance is to eat well and add supplements on top, per your lifestyle.

On the "fancy fats" like bulletproof or butter coffee: she used bulletproof coffee (coffee with MCT oil and a little creamer) for about ten years for steady, crash-free ketone energy, and recently stopped. Coffee is called a double-edged sword — it gives a mental edge but also leaches minerals — so the advice is to drink it deliberately, not on autopilot, and not late in the day if you need to sleep.

The through-line she returns to is that biohacking is easy and mostly free, that no supplement or diet works without the mind at peace, and that the single daily practice she credits with transforming her own health is happiness — approached, like everything else, one small step at a time.
