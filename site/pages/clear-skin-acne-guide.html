Clear Skin, Explained: Acne, Serums, Diet and Lifestyle

Dermatologist Dr. Rajal walks through what actually controls acne and pigmentation — how each active ingredient works, a minimal routine you can keep, why sunscreen matters differently for Indian skin, and how sleep, stress and diet quietly decide your results.

At a glance

  • Acne splits into non-inflammatory lesions (whiteheads and blackheads, the pre-acne signal that skin is acne-prone) and inflammatory ones (papules, pustules, nodules, cysts). Even dry skin can be acne-prone.
  • Fungal acne is over-diagnosed. Its lesions look uniform, cluster in the T-zone and appear suddenly — but it needs an expert to confirm, not self-treatment with antifungals.
  • Fewer products, used correctly, beat a crowded shelf. Correct your two most prominent concerns first; more products mean more irritation and dilution.
  • Active concentration has a ceiling: higher is not better. Niacinamide gives diminishing returns above 10–15%, and over-strong vitamin C or acids irritate — and on Indian skin, irritation leaves dark spots.
  • Oily skin still needs moisturiser (at night), and every Indian skin type needs sunscreen — the goal here is even tone and fewer dark spots, not just wrinkle prevention.
  • Diet, sleep and stress are as important as the routine. Skin that "isn't responding" is often a sleep, stress or nutrition problem, not a product problem.
  • Most home remedies only moisturise and lightly exfoliate. A few help; scrubs, lemon, toothpaste and baking soda actively harm the skin.

Understanding acne: types and fungal acne

Acne is grouped into two families of lesions. Non-inflammatory acne is the comedones — whiteheads and blackheads. Inflammatory acne is everything else: papules, pustules, nodules and cysts. A pustule is the small yellow pus point on a slightly red base. Most people picture only the angry red lesions when they say "acne", but whiteheads and blackheads are pre-acne lesions: developing them means the skin is acne-prone, and the right precautions can help prevent eventual breakouts.

Acne-prone skin usually reveals itself in the history — teenage or early-adult breakouts that leave behind small scars. For that skin, a routine that reduces sebum production and dead-skin buildup is what keeps it in check.

"I can diagnose my own fungal acne"

Myth. Fungal acne is not easy to diagnose and is far less common than it is made out to be. It looks monomorphic — the lesions all look similar — whereas ordinary acne is polymorphic, mixing papules, pustules, whiteheads and blackheads. In fungal acne you see no comedones, just multiple tiny pustules, mostly in the T-zone, appearing suddenly.

It tends to follow specific triggers: overuse of antibiotic creams, prolonged oral antibiotics, creams that keep damaging the skin barrier, a hot and humid climate, or seborrhoeic dermatitis and dandruff. Because self-diagnosis leads people to reach for antifungals that will not help, anyone who suspects fungal acne should have a dermatologist confirm it before treating it.

Actives decoded: what each ingredient does

A serum is a watery, concentrated formulation of an active ingredient, carrying few emulsifiers, solvents and preservatives — so the active sits at a high concentration in close to its strongest form. Creams and moisturisers, by contrast, are thick with emulsifiers, thickeners and emollients. That is why a serum follows the cleanser: it delivers the active efficiently.

Salicylic acid

Preferred for acne-prone skin because it is lipophilic — oil-loving — so it concentrates in the oil-forming glands. Apply an even layer across the face and it is absorbed more where the active oil glands are, reducing both sebum production and dead-skin buildup. In a cleanser, 1% is typical and 1–2% is enough; a 1% salicylic acid serum works well too.

Niacinamide

A good pairing with salicylic acid in a serum. Beginners should stick to 5%; after roughly six to eight months of consistent skincare, up to 10% is reasonable. Higher is not better — the benefit is only incremental up to about 10–15%, and beyond that the irritation potential climbs while the added benefit shrinks. There is no need to go above 10%. For oily skin, niacinamide also appears in moisturisers alongside glycerin, hyaluronic acid and zinc PCA to help reduce oiliness and repair the barrier.

Hyaluronic acid

An almost omnipresent ingredient because it is a humectant: it pulls water to the skin's surface, giving a mild plumping effect, and holds water within a formula. It also softens the irritation potential of other ingredients and can improve their penetration, which is why it turns up in cleansers, serums, moisturisers and even sunscreens. Useful, but on its own it is not an acne treatment — acne-prone skin still needs a salicylic acid step rather than hyaluronic acid alone.

Retinoids and retinol

Retinoids are vitamin A analogues, and vitamin A is central to skin-cell differentiation and to forming a healthy barrier. They exist along a strength ladder, from mildest to strongest: retinyl esters, then retinol, then retinaldehyde, then tretinoin (retinoic acid). The milder forms need more conversion steps to reach active retinoic acid, so they are the best tolerated but slower to act; tretinoin acts fastest but irritates most.

Retinol sits in the middle — effective, science-backed and well tolerated, which is why it is so popular. It increases collagen synthesis and epidermal cell turnover, so skin looks more youthful and plump, texture improves, and because the upper dead-skin layer is shed faster, surface dark spots, freckles and uneven tone improve too. It is not only for acne; it helps fine lines and wrinkles as well, and is worth starting in the late 20s at the first signs of ageing. Begin around 0.1% — enough for most people; 0.5% is available, but at that strength it is better taken under prescription.

Vitamin C

Overhyped, and not for everyone. Vitamin C is genuinely multi-purpose — an antioxidant with anti-ageing benefit, a brightener that reduces melanin by lowering tyrosinase activity, and a photoprotective agent that reduces UV damage. That range is why marketing has pushed it so hard that patients feel a routine is incomplete without it.

But the concentration must land in the 5–10% range: below 5% shows little improvement, while 15–20% (which some products now push) can be very irritating. L-ascorbic acid is the most potent form and also the most irritating — and on Indian skin, anything that irritates can leave a dark spot. Gentler derivatives such as 3-O-ethyl ascorbic acid, sodium ascorbyl phosphate or magnesium ascorbyl phosphate convert to ascorbic acid in the skin, reducing irritation at the cost of some potency. Crucially, vitamin C is a maintenance-and-brightening ingredient, not a fix for acne, melasma, open pores or dark skin — correct those first. If you have a real concern, treat it before reaching for vitamin C.

Over-the-counter vs prescription strength

Pharmacy (prescription) products are often formulated stronger because they are dispensed with a doctor's guidance — how to use it, what to combine or avoid, how to prepare the skin, what aftercare and which sunscreen to pair. A stronger formula can irritate if those instructions are missing. Over-the-counter cosmeceuticals are bought off the shelf without that guidance, so they cannot carry such high concentrations — this limits irritation and liability but also means results take longer to appear.

A simple routine, morning and night

For oily, acne-prone skin, the morning starts with a good cleanser containing salicylic acid or glycolic acid. Slightly drier skin with acne can use a face wash with mandelic acid or lactic acid instead — the aim is the same, reducing oil secretion and dead-skin buildup. Do not assume only oily skin gets acne: dry skin can have an active sebaceous gland too, so dry and acne-prone skin coexist.

How to actually use a cleanser

Wet the face completely first — cleanser goes on damp skin, not the other way round. Apply a little, spread it gently to cover the whole face (no vigorous rubbing or massaging), and leave it on for about 30 seconds so the active ingredients have time to work, then rinse. Rubbing it off within a second or two, as many people do, wastes most of the effect.

The order of steps

  1. Cleanse on damp skin, leaving it on ~30 seconds. For oily, acne-prone skin this is the first thing in the morning; very dry skin can skip morning cleanser and rinse with plain water.
  2. Serum — a thin layer of 1% salicylic acid, or a salicylic-acid-plus-niacinamide combination, across the whole face.
  3. Sunscreen — a gel-based sunscreen suits oily, acne-prone skin, and that completes the morning.
  4. At night, cleanse thoroughly, apply an oil-reducing serum, and here you can add a retinoid — a prescription retinoid or an over-the-counter retinol. Follow with a moisturiser.

"Oily skin doesn't need a moisturiser"

Myth. Every skin type needs moisturiser; the choice of formula is what changes. Oily skin can use it at night (and skip it in the morning, going straight to sunscreen after the serum), because moisturiser repairs the barrier, soothes the skin and reduces inflammation. Dry skin suits emollients and occlusives — glycerine, shea butter, cocoa butter, a little squalane, ceramides. Oily skin does better with glycerin, hyaluronic acid, niacinamide and zinc PCA, which help reduce oiliness while repairing the barrier.

Sunscreen for Indian skin

The goal of sunscreen differs by skin type. In fair skin the priority is preventing fine lines and wrinkles; in darker Indian skin those are less prominent anyway, and the real targets are uneven skin tone, dullness and dark spots. There is also a pollution angle: the physical filters such as zinc oxide form a barrier that helps protect the skin from the oxidative stress and particle damage that pollution causes. On that basis, every Indian person benefits from sunscreen, regardless of the older Western argument that darker skin does not need it.

When you can skip it

There are a few genuine exceptions. If you have a lot of active acne, keeping the number of layers minimal matters more, so sunscreen can be dropped in favour of physical protection — an umbrella, and avoiding the sun between about 11 a.m. and 3 p.m. In a fully enclosed space with no window and no sunlight, it can be skipped. And if the skin is irritated — contact dermatitis or a flare unrelated to the sun — it is better to repair the barrier and heal first. Even so, making sunscreen a fixed daily habit rather than a UV-index decision is what keeps people consistent, and consistency is what produces results.

Pigmentation is the exception to the exception. For melasma, photo-contact dermatitis or stubborn dark spots, sunscreen is non-negotiable even indoors — a thin layer still applies. Most visible-light exposure comes from sunlight itself, and for melasma even a few minutes of exposure can cause darkening, quietly cancelling out the lightening products you are paying for.

Covering up on two-wheelers

Covering the face with cloth while riding is good, but a helmet with the visor down gives added protection — for safety and for the skin — so a helmet is a must no matter what. It does not cause hair fall; it may exacerbate dandruff if worn too long, which is why cleaning the cushioned foam inside (there are sprays for this) matters, especially for people already prone to dandruff. No amount of scalp irritation justifies skipping the helmet.

Pigmentation and the eye area

Melasma

Melasma is a hyperpigmentary disorder in which the skin makes excess melanin. It can be driven by sun exposure and hormonal imbalance, has a genetic predisposition, and can be triggered by oral contraceptive pills or hormone replacement therapy. It typically appears on sun-exposed areas — cheeks, forehead and upper lip — and is a resistant condition that demands diligent skincare and sun protection to improve. A melasma-focused routine centres on reducing the darkening and protecting against UV, with SPF 50 and a preference for a tinted sunscreen. The specific lightening actives involved are best prescribed and supervised, because used in the wrong concentration or overused they can actually darken the skin.

Dark circles

No single cream fixes them. Dark circles are multifactorial: thin under-eye skin, prominent blood vessels, genuinely increased melanin, fine lines and wrinkles, and a tear trough — the groove and shadowing under the eyes — that makes them look darker. Indian facial structure is prone to this, and it worsens with age as the orbital rim widens and the eyes look more sunken.

Creams can only help the darkening component: vitamin K may reduce the prominence of blood vessels, and hyaluronic acid, peptides and retinol improve skin quality. Many people who complain of dark circles do not actually have dark under-eye skin — it is the hollowing and shadowing, a structural problem no cream can change. For a tear trough, filler placed around the complex lifts the area so the groove looks less prominent, and skin-booster injections can improve under-eye skin quality. If puffiness is the cause, a cool, caffeine-containing product (or chilled green tea bags) reduces the appearance. Realistic expectations matter: dark circles can be improved, rarely erased, and a little concealer helps for hereditary cases.

Dark lips

Lip colour is proportional to the melanin in the rest of the body, so a duskier complexion naturally comes with darker lips — genuinely pink lips are not on the table, and no product delivers them. Hot beverages, sun exposure and smoking darken lips further. A lip balm with UV filters helps stop sun-related darkening from worsening. Lips that have darkened over time (acquired darkening, as opposed to lifelong dark lips) can improve somewhat with skin-lightening agents and a few laser sessions, and lip blushing — tattooing a pink pigment into the lips, lasting one to two years — can make them look pinker.

Kajal and the eye area

Use sparingly, never overnight. Kajal can block the meibomian glands, which open along the waterline, causing styes and dermatitis — worse now that long-wear 24-hour kajals use a thick, oily base to keep micronised particles in place, which clogs the glands. Keep it off the waterline; if you want the look, apply a little on the outer corner on skin only, or use a dark brown or dark eyeshadow for a smoky effect instead. Do not wear it daily, and never sleep in it — overnight it irritates the delicate skin around the eyes, ages it prematurely and makes fine lines more prominent. Remove every trace with a cleansing balm or micellar water before bed.

Scalp, dandruff and hair washing

How often to wash your hair

There is no single answer — it depends on scalp type, how much you sweat and work out, the climate, and the styling products (sprays, mousse, serums) you use. An oily scalp can be washed every day without harm, and washing daily does not cause hair loss. As a floor, wash at least three times a week; a very dry scalp can manage twice a week; but once a week is not enough. On average, two to three times a week suits most people. Teenagers who wash only on Sundays often arrive with itchy, flaky scalps — and because teenage scalp oil trickles down onto the face and forehead, infrequent washing feeds whiteheads and acne there too. (And no, washing your hair does not give you a cold.)

Why dandruff keeps coming back

It is genetic and controllable, not curable. In dandruff the scalp produces a different type of oil — usually the same amount, but higher in triglycerides and free fatty acids, which are more pro-inflammatory. That oil drives inflammation, redness and flaking; the resulting dead-skin buildup encourages fungal overgrowth, and the fungus feeds on that sebum. Because the genetic makeup behind the oil type cannot be changed, dandruff needs long-term management, which is why people feel let down when it returns after a course of shampoo.

Anti-dandruff shampoos work on several fronts: an antifungal such as ketoconazole reduces the fungal overgrowth (and with it the flaking, redness and itching), while salicylic acid and coal tar reduce dead-skin buildup. The best results come from a shampoo that also soothes the scalp and calms inflammation. In severe cases with heavy itching and flaking, use it two to three times a week; once it is controlled, once a week or even once every 15 days can keep it in check.

Diet, sleep, stress and vitamin D

A basic routine matters, but the other aspects are just as important — and often the reason someone "doing everything" still sees no results. Diet, sleep, exercise and stress management can matter more than any single product; once they are in order, even a simple cleanser-moisturiser-sunscreen routine is often enough to maintain the skin.

Stress and the skin

There is a definite correlation between stress and acne, eczema, psoriasis and rosacea. The stress hormone cortisol acts on receptors present in the skin — in the keratinocytes, sebocytes and melanocytes — so under stress they become deranged. The lipid layer of the stratum corneum changes quality, so the skin loses more water and looks dry and dull; at the same time, sebocytes are stimulated to make more oil, so acne-prone skin can break out with larger papules and nodules, which is why breakouts so often appear before an important event. Calming habits — exercise, breathing techniques and sleeping on time — all help.

Sleep hygiene

Reserve the bedroom for sleeping — studying in bed disturbs the sleep cycle. Use an alarm clock rather than the phone, so you are not tempted to scroll if you wake at night, and stop scrolling before sleep. Small changes like these meaningfully improve sleep quality, and sleep is where many people are quietly lagging.

Collagen supplements

Not worth it for most people. The evidence for oral collagen powders is weak and often funded by the companies selling them, and the improvements described are minuscule. A high-protein diet plus fresh fruits and vegetables — natural sources of antioxidants — does more for skin quality. Whether collagen supplements improve the outcome of procedures like microneedling is debatable; with a proper high-protein diet, the outcome should already be good. And a collagen powder cannot compensate for a poor diet.

Vitamin D

Vitamin D behaves as a hormone in skin regulation and keratinisation, helping the skin mature into a proper barrier and helping regulate the oil-forming glands. A deficiency tends to aggravate acne, psoriasis, eczema and dry, dull skin — so vitamin D levels are worth checking in acne and, especially, in hair fall. The most common deficiencies behind hair loss are iron, vitamin D and vitamin B12, along with thyroid abnormalities. Vitamin D is even used therapeutically in psoriasis, where calcipotriol creams applied to plaques help balance keratinisation and add an anti-inflammatory effect.

Home remedies and body care

Home remedies have a place, as long as their ceiling is understood: at best they moisturise and gently exfoliate (curd, for instance, has a little lactic acid). They will not reduce dark spots, melasma or acne. Treated as relaxing me-time rather than treatment, they do no harm.

Chilled green tea bags — soaked, refrigerated, then placed on the eyes — genuinely help reduce morning puffiness, thanks to the coolness and mild antioxidant, soothing effect. Multani mitti is worth using for oily skin and prominent open pores, but the right way: mix it with rose water or water and a little glycerine into a paste, and wash it off once it has dried only to about 60–80% (not bone dry), then moisturise. Used like that it makes a good occasional pre-event mask, temporarily pulling out oil so pores look less prominent for a few hours.

The scrubs and pastes to avoid

Physical home scrubs are a firm no. Crushed orange peel or moong dal makes particles of uneven, ragged size that cause micro-tears on the skin surface — and on Indian skin, any irritation heals with a dark spot. Scrubs feel like they work because they remove dead skin and leave skin looking momentarily fresher, but over time they injure the skin, worsen dryness and irritate it further. Toothpaste on acne burns: its harsh alkaline carbonates and bicarbonates (meant to whiten teeth) penetrate and cause worse burns than an acid would. Baking soda on the face or underarms is the same story.

Cracked heels and pedicures

Cracked heels are common, especially in women who ride two-wheelers in sandals, exposing the heel to dust and wind that dry it out. A cream containing urea or lactic acid works best. Do not scrub — foot files and aggressive scrubbing irritate the skin, make it thicken and can lead to fissuring. Instead, soak the feet in warm water for about 15 minutes, gently remove loosened dead skin by hand, then apply a thick layer of a urea- or lactic-acid-based moisturiser.

Pedicures are not necessary, and only fine if done correctly. The pitfalls are heel scrubbing, aggressive cuticle pushing, and cutting nails too close to the nail bed or in a curved shape — which leads to ingrown toenails — plus unsterilised tools that can cause paronychia and nail infections. Choose a hygienic place, and avoid unnecessary scrubbing and deep nail cutting.

The one thing to get right

Faced with many concerns at once, the winning move is restraint: correct the two things that bother you most first, then move on. More products mean more chances of irritation and dilution, and worse results — so go step by step with the minimum number of products.

Be consistent with a very simple skincare routine. Understand your skin type, build something that fits your budget, and stick with it — only consistency shows results in the long run.

— Dr. Rajal, dermatologist

The pressure to adopt every newly launched product is a form of FOMO, not a skincare need. A simple routine kept for years beats a crowded one abandoned in weeks.
