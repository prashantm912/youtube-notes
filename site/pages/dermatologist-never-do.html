Five Everyday Habits Dermatologists Avoid

Dermatologists Dr. Shah and Dr. Maxfield explain why they never exfoliate their lips, pop deep pimples, overuse heating pads, go barefoot in shared spaces, or reuse dirty towels — and what actually works instead.

At a glance

  • Lips lack the dead-cell layer that exfoliation targets — moisturize them instead of scrubbing.
  • Only pimples with a visible whitehead have a clear exit path; popping deep pimples pushes bacteria and inflammation inward.
  • Repeated heat on the same patch of skin can cause a mottled discoloration that is often permanent and raises skin cancer risk.
  • Shoes indoors and bare feet in locker rooms spread the fungus behind nail infections, which are notoriously hard to cure.
  • Dirty towels harbor bacteria linked to acne and styes — wash towels once or twice a week, and clean anything that touches your face.

Lip exfoliation: scrubbing a layer that isn't there

Across most of the body, the outermost layer of skin is the stratum corneum — a sheet of dead, nucleus-free cells called corneocytes. This is the layer that creates dullness, blocks pores, and contributes to acne, and it is exactly what exfoliation removes. Deeper chemical peels go further and break apart keratinocytes in lower layers.

Lips are different: they do not have a stratum corneum. That is why lips hold moisture poorly and dry out faster than any other part of the body — a problem that becomes extreme on Accutane. When you exfoliate your lips, you are scrubbing tissue that has no dead layer to remove.

Dr. Maxfield, a fellowship-trained Mohs surgeon who treats aggressive skin cancers on the lips, notes that under the microscope the wet part of the lip has no stratum corneum, and that in response to environmental insults it can undergo metaplasia — changing to become more resistant. Aggressive exfoliation, whether physical or chemical, applies the same kind of stress. The likely result is a callus-like thickening: just as hands respond to lifting weights or abrasive work by building thicker skin, lips that are exfoliated aggressively and frequently can become thicker, less soft, more textured, and more vulnerable over time — and even more dependent on lip balm, because the lip can no longer hold water.

Skip it. Exfoliating your lips once or twice is probably fine, but making it a regular routine risks permanently changing your lip texture. Most people simply need moisture to soften their lips.

If lip balm alone leaves your lips flaky, the fix is still moisture first. At best, an occasional, exceptional exfoliation can clear loose skin cells as a one-time reset back to baseline — it is not the ongoing solution.

Popping pimples: the whitehead rule and the danger triangle

A pimple is mostly contained within its pore, and that containment keeps bacteria from reaching other parts of the body. A pimple without a visible whitehead has no clear path to the surface — it sits deeper in the skin. Squeezing it can rupture it downward, like a volcano erupting into the skin instead of out of it, forcing bacteria and inflammation into the surrounding tissue.

The consequences run in both directions:

  • Short term: infection, a larger and more irritated pimple that sits deeper and lasts longer — the opposite of clearing it faster. It can progress to cellulitis (inflammation or infection of the tissue) or an abscess.
  • Long term: damage to collagen and elastin, which is how deep acne scars form.

Whitehead or leave it. The dermatologists don't generally recommend popping pimples at all — but if you do, only pop one with a visible whitehead, because it has a clear exit path out of the skin.

Never pop pimples in the T-zone. This central facial area was once thought to drain through a valveless system; valves do exist, but the direct connection to the cavernous sinus and other areas of the brain means infections here can spread to the brain. It is rare, but it has been reported — be careful in that zone.

Prolonged heat on the skin: comfort now, permanent marks later

Heating pads, hot water bottles, and laptops resting on the legs all deliver sustained warmth that feels almost addictive — and dermatologists see the aftermath in the office often. Prolonged, repeated heat can cause erythema ab igne: a mottled, net-like (reticular) redness that can develop into long-lasting hyperpigmentation and even increases the risk of squamous cell carcinoma. Skin cancer can result from this reaction.

The mechanism is subclinical damage: the heat is warm enough to feel pleasant but not painful enough to register as harm, so structural damage accumulates quietly over time. The skin changes can become scarred, contracted, and irreversible — once erythema ab igne is established, nothing can treat it.

Short-term use only. There is no proven safe number of minutes, but do not use a heating pad every single day. Use heat for an acute issue, for a short period, until the problem resolves. For chronic pain such as long-term back pain, a heating pad is not a viable long-term solution.

If you notice mottled, net-like discoloration anywhere you regularly apply heat, that is the heat's doing — stop immediately.

Shoes indoors and bare feet in locker rooms

Studies have looked at what the soles of shoes carry into homes: feces, fungus, and bacteria in amounts the dermatologists describe as unquantifiable. Gym locker rooms compound the problem — warm, humid, and trafficked by countless bare feet, they are a classic place to pick up fungus.

The main fear is onychomycosis (also called a tinea infection of the nail plate) — nail fungus. It can also affect the skin, but the nail form is the bigger worry: it can permanently damage the nail, treatment is extremely difficult with low cure rates no matter what you do, and the treatments are sometimes worse than the disease. Oral medications exist, but many are rough on the liver, so they are reserved for severe cases.

Prevent, don't treat. Remove shoes at the door (or offer shoe covers to guests), never go barefoot in gym locker rooms, and protect your feet at home and in public — because once nail fungus takes hold, it may never fully leave.

Dirty towels and everything else that touches your face

Towels that go unwashed harbor bacteria — and they smell, and then you do. The risks are practical: unwashed towels are linked to acne, and for people prone to styes (bacterial infections of the eyelid margin), a dirty towel makes a noticeable difference. Dirty makeup brushes, mascara spoolies, and makeup sponges raise the same stye risk.

Recommended cadence: wash bath towels twice a week for the optimal routine; once a week is acceptable. Once a month — a bachelor-era habit one of the dermatologists admits to — is not.

For the face specifically, two approaches work:

  1. Single-use disposable face towels (the dermatologists use Clean Skin Club towels): a fresh, clean towel every time you wash your face, softer than a regular paper towel, and no leftover makeup or tinted sunscreen ground into your bath towels. The honest downside is waste — even biodegradable, bamboo-sourced single-use towels generate more waste than cloth.
  2. A rotation of washcloths: keep five to seven hand towels or washcloths on the counter, use them one by one through the week, and launder them weekly. Same hygiene, no disposables — it just requires the planning.

Clean towel, every time. Whether disposable or a weekly washcloth rotation, never dry your face with a towel that has been sitting dirty.

Wash anything you apply to your face regularly, and wipe your phone with an alcohol wipe every now and then — it harbors bacteria that can transfer to the skin and eyelid margin.
