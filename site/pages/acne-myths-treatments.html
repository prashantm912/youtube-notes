Acne: Myths, Real Causes, and Treatments That Work

Dermatologist and dermato-surgeon Dr. Koushik Lahiri, with over three decades of experience, dismantles popular acne myths and lays out what actually causes breakouts — and how they should be treated.

Acne by the numbers

Acne is one of the most widespread conditions on the planet. On a rough estimate, about 650 million people — roughly 9.5 to 10 percent of the world's population — have acne at any given time, making it about one person in ten. Reported prevalence is around 9.8 percent in women and 9 percent in men, though women report to doctors more often, so the gap may not be meaningful. In India, that works out to roughly 15 crore people. Around 80 to 90 percent of all human beings experience acne at some point in life, which makes it the eighth most common disease in the world at any point in time.

Prevalence tracks age: studies cited in the conversation put acne at 80 to 90 percent of teenagers, about 50 percent of young adults, 20 to 30 percent of people aged 30 to 50, and 10 to 20 percent of people over 50. Even newborns can have acne, driven by the mother's hormones; prepubertal acne appears around ages 8 to 9; and the classic teenage form runs from about 12 to 19 or 21.

There is no medical difference between "acne" and "pimple" — acne is the Latin term, pimple the English one. The full name of the common form, acne vulgaris, has nothing to do with vulgarity: vulgaris simply means "common". Dermatologists grade it as mild, moderate, or severe; severe acne is the pustular and cystic form. In a modern practice, pimples are no small matter — they account for about 25 percent of Dr. Lahiri's total patient load.

What does not cause acne

"Acne comes from bad hygiene"

Myth. The single most common misconception. Parents routinely blame poor washing, food habits, or sleep, but acne is not a hygiene problem.

"Pollution is why so many people break out"

Myth. The highest rates of acne vulgaris are found in Europe, which is far less polluted than India and where food habits are not as poor as in America. If pollution were the driver, Delhi would top the charts — it does not.

"Milk and dairy cause pimples"

Myth. The largest consumers of milk and dairy products are Denmark, Canada, and New Zealand, and there is no evidence those countries have the most pimples. Conversely, village children in India with little access to milk, dairy, or chocolate still get acne. There is no two-plus-two link — though Dr. Lahiri flags a high-glycemic diet as a separate consideration.

"Fried food gives you pimples"

Myth. There is no direct relation between acne and fried food, dosa, uttapam, biryani, or any regional cuisine. The caveat comes later: food matters indirectly, through body weight.

"A vegetarian (or non-vegetarian) diet is better for skin"

Myth. Neither diet is superior for skin. Strict vegetarians can have flawless, pimple-free skin; so can meat-eaters. Eat whatever you love and are comfortable with — the only real rule is to avoid foods you are allergic or intolerant to, such as milk in lactose intolerance.

"Being sexually active reduces acne"

Myth. A claim common on the internet, with absolutely no evidence behind it. Sexual activity neither improves nor worsens acne.

"Celebrities have naturally flawless skin"

Myth. Celebrities visit dermatology clinics without makeup and are absolutely normal — they get pimples, hair loss, and post-inflammatory pigmentation like everyone else. The flawless screen image is the product of grooming time, makeup, and knowing when and how to shoot. They do tend to look better than average, but wanting healthy skin is the reasonable part of that aspiration; believing their skin is naturally perfect is not.

What actually drives breakouts

  • Excess oil and hormones. Pimples happen because the body overproduces oil, particularly in the teenage years when androgens (male hormones) surge.
  • Family background. Genetics matter: some families have naturally clear skin, while in others most members carry facial scarring.
  • Stress. A definite and important contributing factor.
  • Wrong products. Unscientific remedies and harsh products applied to the skin add to the problem.
  • Obesity and metabolic syndrome. The recent surge in acne tracks the surge in overweight and obesity. Where one classmate in ten was heavy a generation ago, now 80 to 90 percent are overweight or obese — and this metabolic shift is directly related to acne. This is where diet re-enters: eat healthy to bring body weight down, even though no single food directly causes pimples.

Adult acne can signal a hormonal condition

Acne should largely disappear by the late twenties. When it persists or appears after 25 or 30 — especially on the lower face, flaring before each period — it points to something happening inside the body. In young women, a cluster of signs raises suspicion of polycystic ovarian disease (PCOD/PCOS): significant weight gain (a 14- or 15-year-old weighing 80–90 kg), a receding frontal hairline, lower-face acne, excess facial and body hair (hirsutism), irregular periods, easy tanning, and thinning hair. Many patients are unaware because their periods seem normal — yet in 60 to 70 percent of cases it is the dermatologist who first diagnoses PCOS and refers the patient to an endocrinologist.

Men show a parallel picture: a 100-kg patient who tans easily, with darkened neck and underarms (acanthosis nigricans) and pimples all over, while his lean brother never had a breakout. Postmenopausal women can also develop acne: oestrogen falls away while androgens remain, and the relative male-hormone influence can trigger breakouts after 50 or 55.

Home remedies to avoid

Most pimples are mild to moderate rather than nodulocystic, and they subside on their own even if you do nothing. That is exactly why bogus remedies appear to "work" — the pimple was going to fade anyway. None of the following has scientific support, and several cause real harm.

  • Toothpaste. Formulated for teeth, not skin — the pH is wrong. Left on for hours, its corrosive ingredients can cause contact dermatitis and leave post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (a dark mark, not technically a scar).
  • Apple cider vinegar. Highly acidic. It burns the skin, causing irritant contact dermatitis — redness, itching, burning — which sun exposure then worsens, and the area darkens. Dermatologists see this damage regularly, on faces and underarms alike.
  • Lemon juice. Highly acidic (pH around 4.4–4.5), with the same darkening result. Both acids make a simple pimple harder to treat than it would have been.
  • Urine therapy. Urine from a healthy person is sterile, but applying (or drinking) it defies the body's own logic: urine exists precisely to excrete what the body wants out. There is no benefit.
  • Leech and snail therapy. Built on the false notion that something "impure" in the skin must be sucked out. Ridiculous, bogus, and absolutely wrong — never indulge in it.
  • Semen facials. Absolutely no science behind them. Hype is hype.
  • Neem leaves in hot water. A relic of the pre-antibiotic era. Neem has some very low antibacterial property, but relying on it in a bad situation is a mistake.
  • Multani mitti. A full industry has grown around it, but it has nothing to do with dermatology.
  • Oil cleansing. Ridiculous on its face: acne is caused by overproduction of oil, so adding oil is the opposite of a treatment.

Why you should never pop a pimple

Pimple popping is the commonest thing young people do — and one of the worst. You are not a doctor: a dermatologist punctures a lesion with sterile instruments, uses a comedone extractor, and can inject a steroid where needed, all under trained supervision. Squeezing with bare hands does the opposite. Bacteria lodged beneath your fingernails are delighted to enter an area that is already inflamed, and the extra force drives the infection deeper — a papule progresses to a nodule, then a cyst, ending in scarring and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation. Leave it alone and consult a qualified dermatologist.

Nobody is inspecting your pimple. Part of the urge to pop comes from selfie and screen culture — your own face magnified at arm's length all day. In a world of 7.5 billion people, no one is examining your skin with a magnifying glass. You are far more bothered about it than anyone else.

The steroid cream trap

The most serious danger in Indian skincare is not a myth but a market: topical steroid abuse. Dr. Lahiri has worked for two decades on TSDF — topical steroid dependent (or damaged) face. A 2009 study he was part of found that about 60 percent of steroid abusers were applying the strongest available steroids to the face without any rhyme or reason, and about 90 percent were doing so without a prescription. Unethically sold steroid creams are a business worth about ₹1,000 crore in India, where prescription-only products are freely handed over the counter — even a scribble on a piece of paper, or no paper at all, gets you the tube.

Once started, a topical steroid behaves like tobacco or alcohol: you start taking it, and then it starts taking you. Coming off is very difficult, and the damage can leave permanent scarring that even the best doctor cannot undo. The older exit strategy stepped down from strongest to mid-potency to low-potency steroid; the latest thinking is to stop the steroid immediately, warning the patient that they will suffer for some days but that this is ultimately the way out.

The abuse extends to fairness creams containing steroids — applied by mothers to their own faces and even to babies, who absorb the steroid and visibly gain weight, a criminal harm the buyers do not recognize. Receiving an honor from the American Academy of Dermatology, Dr. Lahiri used his one sentence to say that at that very moment thousands of mothers were buying such creams to get fair.

Warning. Never apply anything bought loose from the local chemist to your face. Your skin is your most precious asset, and steroid-damaged skin can be scarred permanently.

How acne is actually treated

Treatment today is structured by severity. The initial focus is the comedone — the basic lesion. A blackhead is an open comedone whose contents have oxidized and darkened; a whitehead is a closed comedone, a tiny bump yet to become a papule.

SeverityWhat it looks likeTreatment
MildOccasional pimples, comedonesA good non-comedogenic face wash plus a moisturizer — mostly no doctor needed
ModerateLesions with pus — an infectionConsult a doctor; topical antibiotics, escalating to systemic (oral) antibiotics if more extensive
SeverePustular and cystic acnePrescription medicines are compulsory, under a qualified dermatologist — often oral isotretinoin

The moment a lesion contains pus, it is infected — that is the point to see a doctor rather than experiment, because home treatment of an infected lesion sets off a geometric progression of new ones. Beyond isotretinoin, dermatologists use oral antibiotics such as doxycycline and azithromycin. Very oily skin that cannot otherwise be controlled is where oral isotretinoin earns its place, drying the skin so everything can clear.

Isotretinoin: effective, but respect it

Globally, isotretinoin is never dispensed without prescription, chiefly because it can cause deformities in a baby if taken during pregnancy. Depression and suicidal tendency have also been reported abroad; India has fortunately not seen this pattern, possibly because of the cushioning effect of family support and because Indian dermatologists use far lower doses — roughly 20 mg per day against the 60 mg common in the West. Claims that it causes erectile dysfunction or infertility have no evidence, even with longer use.

Topicals and spot correctors

Modern topical options include benzoyl peroxide and the topical retinoids (retinoic acid, adapalene). The newest, trifarotene, is costly but works two ways — it treats the inflammation and erases the pigmentation — which makes it especially useful for back acne ("bacne") and the dark spots it leaves. It is prescription-only, belongs to the retinoid family (it is not a steroid), and should be applied in a very small amount on the face.

Over-the-counter spot correctors are acceptable as long as they contain no steroid; the usual actives — niacinamide, glycolic acid and other alpha-hydroxy acids, salicylic acid — are all accepted ingredients. Anti-acne face washes do work, provided they are non-comedogenic.

The first-line routine for teenagers

Yes, 14- and 15-year-olds should absolutely be treated — but isotretinoin is not the first line. First-line therapy is properly applied topical medicine without steroids, plus:

  1. A non-comedogenic cleanser.
  2. A barrier-repair moisturizer.
  3. Sunscreen of SPF 15 to 30 — more is not needed — based on titanium dioxide or zinc oxide, in a cosmetically acceptable formulation that does not leave a white cast.

Birth control pills: cure, not cause

Myth. Birth control pills do not cause acne — for polycystic ovarian disease they are the treatment, supplying the correct combination of hormones to regulate the imbalance for a few months or years. Parents who panic after a Google search and consult the next-door neighbour instead of the doctor risk leaving the imbalance uncorrected, which can progress to hirsutism and other complications.

Scars and dark marks

What most people call a "scar" — the dark mark left behind — is post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, not a true scar; a real scar is the depression in the skin. All scars are preventable if proper therapy starts at the proper time, and even established scarring is 60 to 70 percent recoverable with lasers, peels, and related procedures.

Skin and mind are connected

Skin and psyche are closely related in a literal, developmental sense: in the womb, the nervous system and the skin develop from the same pluripotent cells. When the skin is not feeling well, the mind is not feeling well — and vice versa. A person who shrugs off blood sugar, blood pressure, or kidney disease may panic over a single pimple or white patch on the face.

Acne strikes hardest in the formative years. Screens intensify it: online classes, Zoom calls, and selfie culture keep your own face in front of you and an audience all day, creating pressure to look fresh every morning. A 45-year-old may not take a breakout seriously, but a 15-year-old is deeply affected — academic grades deteriorate, depression sets in, and sometimes a psychiatrist's help is needed. Roughly 40 percent of clinic patients panic over even one pimple, while another subset with severe nodulocystic acne is not bothered at all. The working definition Dr. Lahiri offers: acne becomes a disease when you are no longer at ease within yourself.

Choosing products and doctors safely

There are two worlds of skincare: prescription products, which belong with a qualified dermatologist, and over-the-counter products. OTC products from big, reputed companies that have served people for years — backed by prolonged research, evidence, and trials over decades — are fine: they may or may not help, but they will not harm. The danger lies in local, unbranded concoctions with no science behind them, and in new companies without any track record pushed by online influencers. Do not be the guinea pig.

Patch test first. Before trying any new product on your face, apply it behind your ear or on the inner elbow overnight. If the skin is absolutely fine after 12 hours, you can use it on your face. If something goes wrong on a patch-test site, it is treatable; a damaged face puts you in real trouble.

Use the prescribed medicine only for the period written on the prescription — continuing it for months without review is where problems begin. And take skin problems to a skin doctor: in India there are at least 10 to 20 quacks for every qualified dermatologist, some without an MBBS at all, running "skin clinics" and even hair transplants on the strength of unrelated degrees.

How to verify a dermatologist

  1. A qualified dermatologist holds a DVD, MD, or DNB in dermatology and is a member of the Indian Association of Dermatologists, Venereologists and Leprologists (IADVL).
  2. Check the doctor's name on your state medical council register — every Indian state has one (in Maharashtra, the Maharashtra Medical Council).
  3. A doctor trained abroad must have cleared the government certification course authorizing practice in India.
