How to Build a Skin Care Routine That Actually Works

Dermatologist Dr. Priyanka Reddy, practicing in Bangalore, explains how to identify your skin type, pick a cleanser, moisturizer and sunscreen that suit it, and layer actives without wrecking your barrier.

Start with your skin type — the mirror test

No test kit is needed to find your skin type: just a mirror, 30 minutes to an hour of patience, and some self-awareness. Wash your face with a gentle cleanser (a mild foaming cleanser if your skin is very oily), pat dry, leave the skin bare for 30 minutes to an hour, then observe how it looks and feels.

  • Dry skin: the skin feels parched and seems to be crying out for moisture; parts of it may look flaky.
  • Oily skin: the face looks shiny — like a glazed donut — all over or in several areas.
  • Combination skin: the T-zone and some areas are shiny while other parts are dry, flaky and thirsty.
  • Normal skin: you feel absolutely nothing — no dryness, no shine.
  • Sensitive skin: not a fixed skin type but a phase — patches of redness, itching, sometimes burning sensations.

Skin type is never constant. It shifts with age, weather and season — summer skin care may differ from winter skin care — and, for many women, around the menstrual cycle: skin often turns oilier with a few eruptions in the week before a period, which is when dermatologists suggest switching to lighter products. Keep a few products handy for these swings, including travel, when flight air can leave skin drier and more reactive.

Cleansing: the step that can make or break your barrier

The cleanser is the first step of the routine, and a wrong choice can break the skin barrier — after which nothing else works well. Contrary to popular belief, the evening face wash matters more than the morning one: it removes the day's accumulated dirt, makeup, pollutant particles and oil.

Dry skin

Keep cleansing to a minimum; dry skin cannot afford to have its scarce natural oils stripped away. In the morning, skip the cleanser entirely and just rinse with water. When you do cleanse, use an extremely gentle, pH-balanced formula — ideally one with ceramides or hyaluronic acid that cleans without stripping and adds hydration.

Oily skin

Very oily skin can use a foaming cleanser morning and evening. Mildly oily skin does better with a gentle ceramide or hyaluronic acid cleanser in the morning and a foaming cleanser with salicylic, lactic or glycolic acid in the evening. A slightly acidic pH is fine for oily skin; dry or sensitive skin needs a neutral, skin-friendly pH.

Makeup removal and double cleansing

Daily double cleansing is overkill for lightweight makeup — doing it every day simply over-strips the skin, especially for dry or sensitive types. Reserve it for water-resistant makeup and thick party or wedding foundations, which must be removed thoroughly, or for professions that demand heavy daily makeup. Water-resistant sunscreen alone rarely justifies it: if the last application was at 3–5 p.m. and the night routine happens at 7–8 p.m., little coating remains after two to three hours. Double cleansing suits oily, acne-prone skin that still feels greasy within an hour of washing.

For removers: micellar water is the practical pick for oily skin — and, for ease of use and rinse-off, arguably for every skin type — while cleansing balms suit dry skin and those who enjoy skin care as a sensorial ritual. Balms leave a heavy residue on oily skin, demand more cleanser to remove, and are best avoided by acne-prone skin.

Makeup and acne: habits matter more than makeup itself

Makeup does not cause acne by itself when used correctly, but it certainly can when misused: layering primer, foundation and compact powder is a recipe for blocked pores. For acne-prone skin the advice is one lightweight BB cream or a very light water-based foundation — or a tinted sunscreen as a full-makeup replacement — and no full-coverage foundation. Cleanse as soon as the makeup is no longer needed; makeup left on for long hours, applied in multiple layers, or applied with dirty brushes will trigger acne.

  • Never dab applicators over active acne. Touching a concealer wand to an acne lesion and returning it to the tube transfers bacteria and literally spreads infection into the product. With active acne, apply concealer with clean fingers — it also blends and covers well.
  • Sponges are bacteria farms. A sponge holds product and moisture together — a breeding ground for bacteria. Avoid them on acne-prone skin and before events; replace brushes at regular intervals, store them away from dust, and never touch up through the day with the same brush. For touch-ups use disposable sponges or an eco-friendly tissue.
  • Reapply compact powder properly. Compacts are only needed for very oily skin or humid climates; dab away excess oil before reapplying.
  • TV actors show the worst-case pattern: full days in thick, layered camera makeup produce severe acne outbreaks underneath. Mitigation there is hydration, a solid routine, makeup-free days, and meticulous removal.

The kajal problem. Kajal is the number one cause of acquired dark circles. Suspended in oil and now formulated for long wear, it is extremely hard to remove; its gritty particles irritate the thin under-eye skin, which darkens easily — and it actually makes eyes look smaller, feeding a vicious cycle of applying more to hide the darkness it created. Prefer a liquid liner on the upper lid, or a black or dark-brown powder eyeshadow along the outer lash line instead of the waterline, saving kajal for occasional glam events followed by a thorough double cleanse.

Moisturizer: the most underrated product you own

Moisturizer is the most important product in the routine — more important, in Dr. Reddy's view, than even sunscreen. Without a healthy, intact barrier, the skin cannot accept and use any other ingredient; skipping moisturizer fails the entire routine, no matter how expensive the rest of it is. That includes oily and acne-prone skin: oily is not the same as hydrated, and even the oiliest skin needs a moisturizer — just the right one.

Humectants and emollients usually come combined; look for moisturizers containing at least ingredients like hyaluronic acid, glycerin, ceramides, squalane, shea butter or NMF. Then match the texture to your skin: gel or water-based and very lightweight for oily skin; cream-based formulas rich in butters and ceramides — with long-lasting 8–12-hour protection — for dry to combination skin.

Do not confuse sealants with moisturizers. This is the number one moisturizer mistake. Petroleum jelly on bare lips does nothing to hydrate them: apply a nourishing lip balm first, let it sit, then seal with petroleum jelly at bedtime a couple of times a week. Very dry skin follows the same logic — moisturizer on damp skin morning and night, occlusive on top; for cracked feet or foot eczema, seal and cover with cotton socks overnight. Cheap moisturizers that are mostly petroleum jelly and beeswax feel smooth but do nothing for the skin, and dry skin only gets worse on them.

Combination skin needs more products

Normal-to-combination skin can manage with one in-between formula, neither super light nor super rich. But a very oily T-zone with very dry cheeks genuinely needs two moisturizers — often two cleansers as well — with a lighter product on oily areas and richer moisture on dry patches, plus a lightweight moisturizer carried along to top up dry areas through the day. Within about two weeks of tuning in, most people learn what to use where.

Two moisturizer myths

Myth. "Applying too much moisturizer makes the skin lazy and stops its natural oil production." There is no such feedback mechanism — the effect runs the other way: stripping oils from oily skin makes it produce more oil to compensate. Moisturizing does not make skin drier.

Myth. "If you drink enough water you don't need moisturizer." Internal hydration and hydrating the superficial epidermal layers are very different things; water intake supports healthy skin but cannot replace topical moisturizer, just as oral vitamin C acts differently in the body than topical vitamin C on the skin. Omega-3 supplements can help retain a little moisture, and in very dry or ageing skin dermatologists may add oral moisturizers — but always alongside, never instead of, topicals.

Sunscreen: how to choose and actually use it

There is no longer any excuse to skip sunscreen: white cast, greasiness and darkening are problems of the past, and the market is now spoiled for choice. The basic decision tree:

  • Pure mineral sunscreen (physical filters only — zinc oxide, titanium dioxide): the mandatory choice for pregnant women, babies and children, very sensitive skin, and anyone who prefers to avoid chemical filters. Modern mineral formulas are light and no longer leave a white cast.
  • Hybrid sunscreen (chemical plus physical filters): fine for everyone else who is open to chemical filters.
  • Checklist: SPF 30–50 (Dr. Reddy prefers 50; anything above is only marginally better), PA rating of at least +++ (++++ is best), and blue-light protection — everyone with phone or laptop screen time has blue-light exposure.

On controversial older filters like avobenzone, benzophenone and octocrylene: the alarming lab findings come from animal studies at very high percentages that no one would cumulatively reach in a lifetime of normal use, so there is no direct endocrine-disruption threat — finish the tube, then switch to newer-generation filters such as Tinosorb S, Tinosorb M and Uvinul A, which are now arriving in India and can offer four-to-six-hour protection instead of the usual two to three, reducing the need for constant reapplication. A pregnant woman who occasionally applies chemical sunscreen has nothing to fear from that accidental exposure; the point is to consciously choose mineral.

Texture and format matter

A sunscreen should form a layer that sits on the skin, not rub in or dribble away, and its texture should let you measure the standard two-finger-length dose — very watery, runny fluids make that quantification unreliable. Watery fluid sunscreens are acceptable for people with only 15–20 minutes of daily sun exposure; anyone with real sun exposure, melasma, stubborn pigmentation or uneven skin tone should use at least a gel or cream base. Ultimately the best sunscreen is one that meets the criteria and that you genuinely like applying and reapplying consistently.

Formats to avoid on the face: sprays, sticks, mists and powders. Sprays make dosing unknowable and risk aerosol inhalation — you will not remember to hold your breath every time, and even small repeated inhalation is detrimental; on the body they are more tolerable. Sticks hide the quantity applied and rely on waxy, pore-clogging bases that can trigger comedonal acne. Sunscreen powders can replace a compact for dusting, but none of these formats can be depended on for real protection — the best reapplication is the original application technique.

Reapplying over makeup

Good modern sunscreens do not pill much. Take one finger-length at a time, dab it on and let it sink rather than rubbing (sunscreen should absorb easily but not vanish entirely), then repeat with the next finger-length and refresh makeup on top — a little concealer on spots that need correcting is enough, since the foundation underneath is still there. It is not an everyday necessity, but with full-day sun exposure it has to be done.

Serums and actives: an ingredient-by-ingredient verdict

Serums are heavily overdone. Nobody needs one for a basic routine — cleanser, moisturizer and sunscreen suffice — but chosen well, a serum is a good addition targeted at a specific concern.

Vitamin C — the most controversial serum

Partly true. Marketing has pushed vitamin C so hard that people believe it is something everyone absolutely needs. It is not: it is a potent antioxidant, but it is not made for all skins, and an unstable formula can cause uneven tone, darkening, and acne breakouts.

  • Right candidates: normal, combination or dry skin with pigmentation concerns but no active acne or sensitivity; people with heavy pollution, light exposure or free-radical damage who need antioxidants. Not for active acne or acne-prone skin, where it is known to exacerbate breakouts and the routine is already full of actives.
  • Forms: L-ascorbic acid is the most potent and fastest acting but highly unstable — buy only a well-stabilized formulation from a top brand and don't take chances. Derivatives (ascorbyl glucoside, ethyl ascorbic acid, magnesium ascorbyl phosphate) are gentler, better for slightly sensitive skin, but far less potent — they must convert to L-ascorbic acid first, which is why users often see no results for a long time.
  • Percentage: start slow — 5%, then 10, then 15–20 gradually for L-ascorbic acid; other forms can start at 10%.
  • Timing: Dr. Reddy has moved vitamin C to the night routine in most protocols because photosensitivity remains a concern unless the formula is well stabilized. The exception: vitamin C combined with ferulic acid and vitamin E — ferulic acid stabilizes it and vitamin E adds synergistic potency, making the trio more photostable — which she still gives in daytime, especially to people working indoors.
  • Role: a quality-improver and glow-giver for otherwise problem-free skin, or maintenance after melasma or acne have been treated — never a first-line treatment. Patients who come with acne, dark spots or melasma get those treated first; vitamin C features nowhere in that phase.

Niacinamide

Partly true. "Jack of all trades, master of none." It pairs well with almost everything and helps a little with everything — oil control, dark spots, skin rejuvenation — but as a standalone single-ingredient serum it is a waste of money and time. Use it as a supportive ingredient: niacinamide with zinc or with salicylic acid for oily, acne-prone skin; with retinol to reduce irritation; with alpha-arbutin or kojic acid for mild unevenness. Good to have, never mandatory.

Retinol and retinoids

Fact. The one ingredient that can never be overhyped — the holy grail of skin care across age groups, from teenage acne through pigmentation to ageing skin. Prescription-strength retinoids must be used only under a doctor's supervision; the widely circulated prescription creams on social media are causing real damage and reactions. Every negative retinoid story traces back to misuse.

  • Over-the-counter retinol is mild, with good anti-ageing benefit (less so for acne) — the right starting point for a beginner's well-ageing protocol, typically from the late 20s, or earlier when genetics, smoking, alcohol, poor sleep or stress accelerate ageing.
  • Start with a tiny quantity once a week (even once in 10 days), progressing gradually to two or three times weekly for a mild retinol; a potent one stays at weekly once. Never exceed twice a week unless it is extremely mild.
  • Never apply it to extremely dry skin: wash, apply a hydrating serum or light moisturizer, then the retinoid, and always seal with a good moisturizer. Don't combine it with an exfoliant, and don't start it while experimenting with other new products.
  • Not the right candidate for now: perioral pigmentation, under-eye dark circles, atopic or sensitive skin — or at least avoid those pigmentation-prone areas in Indian skin.
  • Manage expectations: no ingredient magically erases wrinkles or closes open pores. Retinol boosts collagen and improves texture, softening fine lines and making pores appear less prominent.

Hyaluronic acid

Mixes with anything, so a standalone serum is rarely needed — it earns one mainly for sensitive skin that tolerates nothing else, for dry or combination routines that just need extra hydration, or for people chasing its instant plumping effect on fine lines (and as an under-eye or pre-makeup serum). Apply it to damp skin after washing and seal with moisturizer or sunscreen, and prefer formulas with multiple molecular sizes — small, medium and large — so hydration reaches different layers of skin. It is a short-acting molecule; it will not do much beyond that.

Ceramides

Fact. The one ingredient anybody and everybody can use and genuinely benefit from — dry, oily, combination or sensitive — especially now that lightweight formulations exist. Ceramides repair the barrier, calm skin with mild anti-inflammatory potential, and suit skin that dries or flakes soon after moisturizing, heavy skincare users, and beginners starting actives; they pair beautifully with hyaluronic acid.

Peptides

Partly true. An accessory, not a base. Peptides have collagen-signaling properties, mild anti-ageing and hydration benefits, and pair well with everything — retinol-plus-peptide formulas are a good combination — but their long-term importance is not fully understood, and their standalone benefit is hard to isolate because they are almost always blended with adenosine or antioxidants. Current research says they are safe, good for well-ageing, hydration and elasticity; copper peptides in particular are attracting new research. Reasonable for people in their 30s, or 20-somethings wanting a gentle extra — the upcoming star, not a mandate.

Alpha hydroxy acids (glycolic, lactic, mandelic)

All are gentle exfoliants; glycolic acid tops the list and lactic acid is the mild option for very dry or sensitive skin. Mandelic and citric are fine in a face wash but not as standalone exfoliants. Glycolic acid suits dry skin with pigmentation or ageing concerns and shines on body areas — underarm and knee pigmentation. Limit creamy glycolic formulations to at most twice a week (once is enough), and treat exfoliation as a later, third-phase addition: starting a routine with exfoliation damages the barrier before it is established. Never exfoliate more than once or twice a week — and exfoliating toners used daily (some people twice daily) are doing it all wrong; after a point they strip the protective barrier, not dead skin.

Salicylic acid (BHA)

Fact. The absolute favorite for oily, acne-prone skin — it balances oil and tackles blackheads, whiteheads and active acne, plus uneven tone and open pores, in any format from cleanser to serum to exfoliation pad. Use a low percentage and follow the prescription: it can absolutely dry the skin out, and like all exfoliants, retinol and vitamin C, it is wonderful on protocol and detrimental off it.

Depigmenting ingredients (alpha-arbutin, kojic acid, tranexamic acid, azelaic acid)

The most popular pigment-fighters, now usually sold as combination serums (often with liquorice or other extracts). Dr. Reddy's topical preference runs alpha-arbutin first, then azelaic and tranexamic acid, then kojic acid; tranexamic acid is wonderful but works better orally, prescribed after thorough evaluation of the right candidate. Apply the treatment serum after cleansing, then moisturizer; non-irritating formulas can go twice a day for active pigmentation, while combinations containing exfoliating or irritating components stay in the night routine. Unlike retinol or vitamin C, which can continue for years, depigmenting routines get re-evaluated every three months — upscaling, toning down or cycling ingredients based on results.

Topical glutathione and collagen creams

Myth. Glutathione is too large a molecule to work when applied topically — as are collagen creams and "skin lifting" or firming creams without real actives. In glutathione products, the accompanying kojic acid, alpha-arbutin and liquorice extract do the heavy lifting; the glutathione is added purely for marketing. A cream selling topical glutathione alone is a waste of money.

Layering: how many steps is too many?

The non-negotiables are three: face wash, moisturizer, sunscreen. What fits between them depends on skin concerns and goals — and nobody needs more than four steps, with a fifth only on rare occasions. Anything beyond that wastes time and money and risks overdoing it and disrupting the barrier.

  • Pigmentation: add one serum with melanin-controlling ingredients — alpha-arbutin, kojic acid, azelaic acid, tranexamic acid, or vitamin C in some individuals.
  • Oily skin: a little salicylic acid even in daytime to control oil, ideally combined with niacinamide (and zinc) in one bottle rather than two separate serums.
  • Dry skin: cleanser, hydrating serum, sunscreen — plus moisturizer if very dry, which already makes four steps.
  • Multiple concerns: treat the two most bothersome first, then move on. Never more than two serums or two actives at once — one ingredient like salicylic acid or a retinoid handles several concerns (oil, acne, pores, tone, texture) by itself, so there is no point stacking ten actives.

Piling on layers doesn't build a protective mantle; each layer just displaces and dilutes the one beneath, rendering products ineffective — and no one returns every ten minutes to let each of six steps penetrate. Wash, apply the serum, wait a few seconds, then finish with sunscreen, moisturizer or night cream. Keep it simple and be super consistent: consistency over intensity, any day.

Phase actives in over five to six weeks

  1. Weeks 1–2 (sometimes 3): only cleanser, moisturizer, sunscreen, and at most one non-irritating serum (niacinamide, or an alpha-arbutin/tranexamic acid combination) — nothing else, no matter what.
  2. Week 3 onward: introduce one acid per week. For example, salicylic acid exfoliation weekly once; watch the skin for two weeks, then increase to twice a week.
  3. Then add the retinol — only once the base is comfortable and established.

Actives don't need daily use: retinol, vitamin C and exfoliants can be cycled on alternate nights, with some products used once or twice a week or even once in 10–15 days. Rushing this — buying the trendiest active first, trying every new launch out of fear of missing out — just irritates the skin, and in Indian skin whatever irritates heals with darkening, which is why skin often "looked better before skincare."

What skin care can and cannot do

People who call skin care a scam usually aren't doing it right or aren't consistent. A dermatologist's glowing skin in their late 30s is not the result of one product or one procedure — it is years of consistent, holistic care: suitable products, diet, exercise and lifestyle, compounded. The skin care you do today pays off ten years down the line. And comparisons with grandparents who "never used anything" ignore that their world had less pollution and screen exposure, a healthier ozone layer, more nutrient-dense food, no packaged food, and entirely different beauty standards.

Dr. Reddy's house analogy makes the boundaries clear:

  • Daily sweeping = your home routine. Products handle superficial concerns only. They cannot penetrate deep enough to fix wrinkles, melasma fully, volume loss, under-eye hollows, open pores or acne scars — home products are lower percentage and larger molecular size by design, for safety.
  • Deep cleaning = clinic treatments. Lasers and deeper chemical peels reach depths products cannot: CO2 or fractional lasers for acne scars, dermal-depth treatments for melasma, laser hair removal for permanent hair reduction (which Dr. Reddy calls one of the best gifts, especially for women).
  • Renovation = restorative procedures. Fillers, toxins and threads are optional — you can keep the same house neat for decades without renovating — but they are what actually pause or slow the clock on structural change. Structural problems like prominent sagging, folds and jowls will never be fixed by a cream.

Define your skin care goals clearly — what you want to improve, what you want to accept — and communicate them to your dermatologist so you are both on the same page; unspoken expectations end in disappointment. Nobody is obliged to chase any of this: embracing lines earned by a life of smiling is an equally valid choice.

Three principles to remember

  1. Keep it simple. Skin care doesn't have to be a circus. Have a short-term goal (fix this pigmentation, treat this scar) inside a long-term vision of how you want to age — and take informed decisions on any procedure by asking your dermatologist about short- and long-term effects, case experience and real patient photos.
  2. Consistency, consistency, consistency. Minimum effort done consistently beats intensity. Even with limited interest or budget, a moisturizer and face wash alone, kept up over time, will visibly improve skin. The routine takes two to three minutes a day.
  3. Never underestimate holistic health. The body is one interconnected unit: stress raises cortisol and inflammation, flaring acne, psoriasis and whatever else you are genetically predisposed to. Diet, sleep and stress management are part of skin care — stress is becoming a silent killer.
