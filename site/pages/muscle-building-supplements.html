Muscle-Building Supplements: A Celebrity Trainer's Guide

Kris Gethin — trainer to elite athletes and to actors Hrithik Roshan and John Abraham — on which supplements actually earn their place, how to dose them, how to spot an under-dosed label, and why the powders still only add about 5% on top of training, food, sleep and hydration.

Supplements are the icing, not the cake

Gethin's framing is blunt: all of the common muscle-building supplements together — protein, creatine, glutamine and the rest — contribute roughly 5% of overall gains. The other 95% comes from the consistency and intensity of training, nutrition, sleep and hydration. Sleep alone is worth about another 5%: six hours a night is not horrible, but it is not optimal, and someone sleeping less should also train less to avoid overtraining.

The proportions shift only for people who genuinely cannot eat enough — a client replacing three of six daily meals with shakes because of travel or work leans harder on supplementation, and for that individual it becomes a bigger share. But the rule stands: a supplement is there to supplement the diet, never to replace food. Gethin does not even start clients on supplements until their sleep, hydration and nutrition are in place first.

Age guidance: anything over 18 is fine. Under 18, skip the supplements and focus entirely on food and nutrition. Gethin himself did not start taking supplements until about 25.

Protein powder: what it is and how much to take

Parents often assume protein powder is a performance enhancer or a steroid. It is neither — it is simply protein extracted from dairy such as milk, with most of the fat and the lactose removed, usually just naturally flavoured. The two things to verify are the source (reputable, never black market) and the purpose: it exists to help someone lifting weights hit their protein requirement conveniently.

Convenience and bioavailability are the whole case for it. Eating six meals a day gets boring fast; drinking one or two of them as a chocolate shake, or stirring the powder into plain oats, makes the target achievable. Straight after a workout, when the goal is to repair damaged muscle tissue as quickly as possible, nothing is more bioavailable than a protein isolate — egg whites are second, but few people want to eat eight of them.

How much protein, and how many scoops

  • The baseline for an active person — whether building muscle or losing fat — is 1 gram of protein per pound of body weight.
  • There is an upper limit. Gethin goes up to about 1.5 grams per pound, but only because he trains extremely hard and breaks down a lot of tissue. Requirements also vary by the day's body part: biceps demand less than a back workout.
  • Scoop count (at roughly 25 grams per scoop) depends on the person's size. Three or four scoops is possible, and clients who struggle to eat because of travel may take five or six scoops a day — but they must still eat three real meals, alternating meal and shake so the body gets micronutrients and balanced sustenance, never shake after shake.
  • Someone over 18 who trains can take a scoop straight after the workout, or add one to morning oats in place of egg whites.
  • Even people who never touch a gym can take about one scoop a day. Muscle atrophy comes with age, so older adults benefit from the protein — Gethin's own parents take a couple of scoops daily without working out.

Does protein damage the kidneys?

Myth. There is no scientific truth to the claim that protein shakes load the kidneys or other organs — none whatsoever. The body digests protein powder more easily than any other food.

Hydration is non-negotiable. The body is about 75% water; without enough fluid, neither the body nor the brain functions properly — many headaches are dehydration, not a paracetamol deficiency — and muscles cannot contract adequately. Urine colour is a working test: clear means hydrated, yellow means drink water (though some vitamins also turn urine yellow, so note when you took them). Gethin puts clients on about a gallon a day — roughly three to four litres for a smaller woman, five-plus litres for a bigger man, adjusted for how much the person sweats.

Creatine: the most studied supplement there is

The average sedentary person probably does not need creatine. But for anyone active who is trying to progress — chasing personal bests or playing sport at a decent level — Gethin recommends it, and currently has Hrithik Roshan on it. Creatine helps the body produce more ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the energy source for explosive movements such as sprinting, powerlifting and bodybuilding. Its practical value shows up at plateaus: if you always stall at ten or eleven reps, creatine can help you grind out that one extra repetition — and that last, hardest rep is where the progress lives.

On safety, his position is unambiguous: creatine monohydrate is the most studied supplement of all — not only for performance but for purity and safety — and it is a safe product. The one genuine caveat is hydration: creatine pulls fluid into the muscle cell, so anyone taking it must drink more water or they will become dehydrated.

Monohydrate vs. hydrochloride (HCL)

A small minority of people do not respond to creatine, and the form matters. Monohydrate sometimes causes stomach upset because the particle size cannot absorb through the stomach lining; creatine hydrochloride (HCL) is a more concentrated form that the body absorbs more easily. Neither is universally better — some people simply respond better to one or the other.

Cycling, fluid and the salt comparison

You can stay on creatine year-round, but some people become less responsive that way. Gethin's own cycle is eight weeks on, four weeks off; a four-on, four-off pattern also works. Off creatine, the muscles release fluid — you look a little leaner and push a little less in the gym — and when you return to it, serious trainees typically jump slightly past their previous level. That extra fluid retention is not just cosmetic: it cushions the joints and surrounding connective tissue, letting you push harder with less strain. He compares it to salt in the diet — a minimal version of the same effect, and especially important in a humid climate where you sweat heavily. Creatine also occurs naturally in protein sources such as meat; the supplement is simply a more condensed, convenient delivery.

Glutamine and BCAAs: amino acids against muscle loss

Training hard is the most catabolic thing you can do — you actually lose muscle during the workout itself, and that trauma is precisely what signals growth, provided recovery through nutrition and sleep outweighs the breakdown. The amino-acid supplements exist to tilt that balance.

Glutamine

Glutamine is the most abundant amino acid in the body and also supports gut health. Its role in physique terms is preventing atrophy — muscle loss. Gethin deploys it during hard-training phases rather than year-round, typically about 20 grams, taken before and after the workout and also sipped through the day in bottles of water (often alongside creatine).

Branched-chain amino acids

BCAAs are three amino acids — leucine, isoleucine and valine. Leucine is the real anabolic signaller, but it works more efficiently with the supporting two present. Research and his experience point to two effects: preventing muscle atrophy and providing muscle energy while blunting some of the catabolic response to weight training. Gethin noticed the difference most in endurance work — Spartan-style obstacle races, ultramarathons, Ironman triathlon — where BCAAs in his drinks bottle helped him buffer lactic acid and break down less muscle tissue. He takes BCAAs together with essential amino acids during the workout, and recommends them to anyone over 18 who trains very hard, whether they are 18 or 80.

Antioxidants: protect the body without blunting the workout

Hard-training clients endure a lot of free-radical damage, so Gethin's stack for actors in training includes antioxidants alongside creatine and protein: vitamin C, glutathione, PQQ, hydrogen-rich water, and MyVital C — a human-grade form of carbon C60, a Nobel-prize-winning carbon.

Timing is the critical nuance. A workout is meant to create hormesis — deliberate inflammatory trauma. A bicep curl taken to failure heats and tears tiny myofibrils inside the muscle fibres; the soreness the next day is that damage, and the inflammatory response to it is what tells the body to grow stronger. Taking vitamin C or vitamin E around the workout blunts that response — they are fine at any other time of day, just not around training. Hydrogen-rich water and carbon C60, by contrast, carry very high antioxidant value without blunting the training response, which is why he favours them for fighting free-radical damage in hard-training clients.

Pre-workouts and fat burners: read the label like a formulator

Gethin gives pre-workouts to all of his clients — but only decent ones, and the supplement aisle is a place where marketing easily outruns substance. The discipline is always the same: turn the tub around and read the ingredient panel, then do the research.

Dosage is everything

A long ingredient list means nothing if the doses are wrong. Citrulline, the pump ingredient, has been shown effective in research at 6.5 grams — a pre-workout listing 1.5 grams will simply not have an effect, no matter how impressive the label. The industry has a name for this: powder dusting, or magic dusting — sprinkling twenty ingredients in trace amounts. Five ingredients at proper, studied dosages beat twenty at dust levels.

Look for patented ingredients

A patented ingredient has been studied and tested for purity, safety and performance before being patented. Beta-alanine, for example — the ingredient behind the tingly pre-workout feeling, which buffers lactic acid — has a patented version sold as Carnosine. Part of the buyer's homework is learning the patented versions and effective doses of the ingredients they need.

Fat burners: an edge, not a cheat code

A fat burner supplements an already-correct diet and activity plan — it does not make up for shortfalls, and no fat burner survives a diet of burgers and pizza. Many products are just caffeine and a buzz. A well-built one takes a multi-pronged approach:

  • Blood-sugar stability — ingredients such as chromium or berberine.
  • Craving control and appetite suppressants, for people who are hungry all the time on a diet.
  • Thermogenesis — capsaicin, or its patented version Capsimax, which raises core body temperature.

Timing follows the same logic. Gethin added a fat burner to Hrithik's plan only six weeks into a diet, when the hard part began — rising hunger and cravings — and even then it is worth only an extra couple of percent. Fat loss without one is entirely possible.

Multivitamins and fish oil: insurance for a depleted food supply

Mostly no. Can a diet full of raw vegetables and colourful fruit do the job of a multivitamin? A lot of the time, no — and Gethin says his answer would have been different 60 or 70 years ago. Overpopulation and over-harvesting have depleted the soil, so even healthy food is deficient: an orange our grandfathers ate carried around seven times the vitamin C of one today, and animal proteins raised on that same soil and grass inherit the deficiency.

A multivitamin is therefore insurance — for the sedentary person because of the malnutrition built into the modern food system, and even more so for active people, who need more vitamins and minerals. Many vitamins are water-soluble, so the body simply excretes what it does not use; the worst case is slightly more expensive urine. Choose an organic, food-based product — farm-to-table rather than lab-created pharmacy-to-table. The payoff is systemic: micronutrients, especially minerals, support muscle contraction and relaxation, and a healthier, better-tuned engine simply produces better output, in muscle building, fat loss and immunity alike.

Choosing a fish oil

Much fish is factory-farmed and most people do not eat enough of it, while modern diets are heavy in omega-6 — which makes a good omega-3 supplement important. Quality markers: oil extracted from fresh cold-water fish (Alaska, or off the coast of Chile), and a light colour. A dark, cloudy oil suggests the fish sat for days before extraction and the oil turned rancid. For pure vegetarians, seaweed- and algae-derived omega-3 is an absolutely fine substitute.

The 95%: training, cardio and the mind

Because supplements only top up the result, the conversation keeps returning to what actually drives it.

Train hard, not long

A small muscle group such as shoulders needs maybe 30–40 minutes; a large muscle group, which demands more oxygen and more ATP and therefore longer rests between sets, might take an hour. Someone stretching workouts to 90 minutes probably has not trained hard enough — too much rest, sets stopped short of absolute failure, volume mistaken for intensity. The gauge of a good session is having nothing left in the tank afterwards.

Form comes first, though. A little controlled cheating is acceptable — but only after reaching absolute failure with strict form. Example from a real session: Hrithik performed three seated drop sets of side raises, and only once he could not manage another strict rep did he stand, letting a little momentum from the lower back and traps extend the set on an already pre-fatigued muscle. The many back injuries, slipped and ruptured discs Gethin has seen in Indian gyms trace back to skipping that first step.

“I want you to think of every single fiber as an orchestra, and they all have to sing in a perfect tune in order to get out the music that you're trying to create.”

Cardio while building muscle

Lifters bulking up often drop cardio, thinking it works against the calorie surplus — then their cardiovascular fitness declines, and with it the intensity of their lifting. High-intensity interval work in a calorie surplus does the opposite: it burns calories during the session and keeps the metabolism elevated for hours afterwards (steady-state work like a power walk burns fat only during the activity, though it improves how the body synthesises food eaten afterwards). Better blood flow also transports nutrients to the trained muscles, so recovery improves and training can be more intense and frequent. Lifting weights six days a week with short rests is not a substitute — it never raises the heart rate consistently enough to count as heart-healthy, and the heart is a muscle that needs its own workout.

The one time Gethin avoids HIIT is during a major calorie deficit, because he does not want cortisol spiking beyond what the weight training already causes while he is trying to hold on to muscle — in a deficit, HIIT happens occasionally and only on non-training days.

Getting below 12–13% body fat without drugs

Most people stall around 12–13% body fat — lean enough to look like you train, short of truly ripped. Gethin's drug-free protocol is double cardio. After the morning weight session, the client drinks a protein shake immediately — the workout is already catabolic and the muscle loss must be stopped first — then goes straight into steady-state cardio at around half past nine or ten in the morning. A second cardio session follows at about five in the afternoon: walking as fast as possible while still able to hold a conversation. Deliberately not anaerobic — cortisol was through the roof during the workout and must now stay as low as possible to retain muscle, because the gym happens again in 24 hours. In between: clean eating, stress management, hydration. Training runs five to six days a week, adjusted instinctively — poor sleep or high stress earns a day off; when everything is perfect, a third consecutive training day is fine, followed by rest.

The rest of the plateau-breaking toolkit is a holistic, parasympathetic one: a morning cold shower or ice bath to mobilise fatty acids more easily, serious hydration, early bedtimes, quality sleep, and meditation.

Meditation and mindful eating

Gethin has all his clients meditate, morning and night — clearing stimulation before bed for better sleep, and waking with intention instead of the self-doubt that quietly caps a workout. Presence extends to the gym (no phone) and the table: eating without focus leaves people finishing a meal still hungry and hunting for more. Slow down, put the fork down between bites, chew, and be grateful — mindfulness also tells you when to stop, which matters most on the inevitable junk-food occasions. Chasing growth in mindfulness, the conversation concludes, does more for the pursuit of money and muscle than chasing either directly.
